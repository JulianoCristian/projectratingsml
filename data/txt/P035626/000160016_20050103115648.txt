3. Assessment of Development Objective and Design, and of Quality at Entry

3.1 Original Objective:
The objective of the project, as stated in the PAD, was to design and test, in selected poor urban areas,
management methods and procedures for carrying out small-scale urban infrastructure works, and to
disseminate information on such methods and procedures, aiming at strengthening the private construction
sector and increasing employment in that sector.

The objective was clearly stated. The objective was designed to meet goals of the Country Assistance
Strategy (CAS) which included making public expenditure more efficient, promoting private sector
development and contributing to poverty alleviation. This required a shift of resources to poor urban areas
using (i) sustainable infrastructure schemes; and (ii) adopting more decentralized and participative
planning, lower cost technologies, procurement and management procedures that facilitate private sector
involvement.

The project was a Learning and Innovation Loan (LIL) designed to create new methodologies for
community infrastructure works and to build capacity in order to be able to replicate the experience on a
larger scale. The design and testing of procedures for small-scale urban infrastructure works was a
potentially important undertaking in Gabon at the time. Although the country benefited from oil revenue
and large works were undertaken, a more flexible and competitive system was needed to implement
small-scale works that would be carried-out by small and medium enterprises (SMEs) in low-income areas.
The project proposed an integrated (socio-economic, technological) approach, based on the introduction of
new methods, labeled "TIPPEE" (travaux d'intérêt public pour la promotion de l'entreprise et de
l'emploi), because they promote enterprise development employment through public works. The approach
required: (i) the introduction of standard bidding documents and procurement procedures for small
infrastructure based on open/competitive bidding process and explicit post-qualification selection criteria;
(ii) the use of appropriate technologies for the construction/rehabilitation of small scale infrastructure--such
as concrete cement blocks for street pavement-- for a larger involvement of SMEs in the construction
process; (iii) rapid payment of contractors (typically within a period of two weeks) because of their limited
cash flow; (iv) training and strengthening of government personnel, particularly at the local level, in terms
of contract management, community participation, and maintenance of small infrastructure; (iv) training of
SMEs in the construction sector; and (v) overall involvement of beneficiaries in the design and follow-up of
community infrastructure projects. The approach was to be tested in the integrated infrastructure upgrading
of poor urban settlements, with the objective (upon project completion) of expanding the TIPPEE methods
to the procurement of other priority infrastructure included in the National Investment Budget.

The project was to be implemented by PAPSUT, a technical unit within the Ministry of Plan, benefitting
from a relatively autonomous mode of operation, and relying on a core staff of nine professionals, had
implemented a previous Bank-assisted project. The latter --a Technical Assistance Project that had focused
on the transport and urban sectors-- included the development of an urban strategy endorsed by the
Government and a pilot improvement of community infrastructure aimed in priority at testing the feasibility
of labor-based technologies for urban roads rehabilitation in Gabon, and incidentally trying to set-up
methodologies for a participative and community-based. It did not, however, require a large degree of
outreach to municipal governments, communities, entrepreneurs and others.

3.2 Revised Objective:
The objective was not revised.

3.3 Original Components:




                                                     - 2 -

The components of the project (PROTOTIPPEE ­ Projet Pilote de Travaux d'Infrastructures
Communautaires et de Renforcement des Capacités - Pilot Community Infrastructure Works and Capacity
Building Project) were:

Component A: Capacity Strengthening of Key Stakeholders (Institution Building - US$0.55 Million) This
included technical assistance and support to implement the project based on the use of TIPPEE (Travaux
d'Intérêt Public pour la Promotion de l'Entreprise et de l'Emploi - Public Works to Promote Small
Business and Employment): (a) preparation and dissemination of TIPPEE documents to enable the
replication of this type of project by public infrastructure agencies; (b) training of the entities in charge of
infrastructure works in TIPPEE procedures and guidelines; and (c) NGO training in TIPPEE procedures to
qualify them to prepare projects and monitor their social impacts.

Component B: Pilot Community-Based Infrastructure Schemes (Physical/Training/ Institution Building -
US$5.57 Million). This component was intended to test the TIPPEE approach in four under-serviced
neighborhoods (Libreville, Port-Gentil (2), and Franceville): (a) access and transit roads; (b) community
based development plans; and (c) neighborhood amenities (micro projects) such as footpaths, small bridges,
public lighting and latrines.

Component C: Project Monitoring and Evaluation (US$0.63 Million ­ Project Management).                     This
component included (a) Impact assessments, both socio-economic and environmental; (b) TIPPEE contract
monitoring; and (c) audits.

Component D: Support for a Longer-Term Program of Public Investments (PROTTIPPEE ­ US$0.63
Million). This component was to assist central and local governments in the replication of community
infrastructure upgrading schemes. It included: (a) public infrastructure expenditure reviews across sectors
annually; (b) public procurement reform; (c) a development strategy for the construction sector: (d)
preparation and feasibility studies for for a three-year public investment program to be implemented along
TIPPEE procedures (PIP-TIPPEE); and (e) engineering studies for PIP-TIPPEE (Phase I: 2001).

The components were appropriate for achieving the objectives. The project was intended to assist the
Government of Gabon in the designing, testing and adjusting of methodologies and approaches for
involving private local firms in small-scale infrastructure works and maximizing the impact of this work on
urban employment. It was to help (a) design and disseminate appropriate technologies and procurement
and contracting methods for labor-based works (TIPPEE); (b) test these procedures in poor neighborhoods;
(c) design and test evaluation (economic, social and environment impacts) and supervision methods; and (d)
develop a longer-term program for the sustainable development of local small and medium construction
enterprises was to be developed (PROTTTIPPEE - Programme Triennal de Travaux d'Intérêt Public pour
la Promotion de l'Entreprise et de l'Emploi - Three Year Program of Public Works to Promote Small
Business and Employment). It was expected that ultimately the improved and tested contract management
and implementation procedures developed under the project would be adopted by government ministries,
municipalities and autonomous public or private agencies. Legislation creating the legal and institutional
environment would be prepared and ready for approval, and a three-year multi-sector program of small
infrastructure projects would be identified.

3.4 Revised Components:
There was no restructuring of the project components. However, in the course of project implementation,
the first component (Capacity Strenghtending of Key Stakeholders) was modified to be implemented
together with the second component (Pilot Community-Based Infrastructure Schemes). This meant that the
preparation of the various technical documents detailing the methodology were left in draft until the pilots


                                                      - 3 -

(Component B) could materialize. The documents were then revised, on the basis of experience; they were
constantly adapted, through an iterative process, and tested in the latter parts of works that were carried
out under the Project. This was a practical decision because the methodologies developed under the first
component could be directly tested and improved during implementation. As a result, project costs are
combined under Component B (see Annex 2).

3.5 Quality at Entry:
The project was an innovative way to improve methodologies and approaches to facilitate the involvement
of private local firms in small-scale infrastructure works, while keeping execution responsibility within the
Government's structure. It aimed to create and strengthen contracting and supervision capacity at several
levels of government. Capacity would be built in government agencies (central and local) using the
TIPPEE method. Most of the sub-projects would be undertaken in a few low income urban neighborhoods,
but the project would also support the design of a subsequent TIPPEE program which would involve the
private sector in a broader variety of works. In this later program, central and local governments would
identify and implement projects in poorer neighborhoods and prepare a program of labor-based
infrastructure across various sectors (in particular in transport, education, and health).

The project was innovative and challenging, but it was to be managed by an experienced project unit
(PAPSUT) that had managed a previous urban and transport project. The project involved beneficiaries in
project selection and used affordable standards and local construction enterprises and engineering services,
both key ingredients of success in other similar projects. There was to be no restructuring of neighborhoods
and no resettlement, using an adaptable methodology focusing on improving (i) accessibility to
neighborhoods and (ii) access to basic services.

The project benefited from a number of awards at entry including an award for Excellence in Quality at
Entry and Outstanding Performance in the Development and an award for the Delivery of Adaptable
Lending Instruments. The Project's concept, objectives and approach were largely given high ratings. There
was a feeling that this type of poverty alleviation project was important for an oil exporting economy, that
the objectives were clear, and that the Project used neighboring country experience. The pilot was well
designed to lead to a larger follow-on project. Technical and economic aspects were rated high, including
the small size of the pilot designed to lead to larger, more sustainable municipal development. It was noted
that environmental impacts would be few and there were specific measures and provisions for community
consultation to minimize adverse impacts. Poverty and social aspects were rated high with good social
design and involvement of beneficiaries. The need for better identification of different gender needs was
mentioned, but the assessment noted that the poorest neighborhoods had been chosen.              The use of
management systems from a previous transport TA project and straightforward documentation was deemed
appropriate. Cost recovery measures were deemed appropriate, the institutional capacity was considered
sufficient, and the project was considered ready for implementation.

Quality at entry is rated satisfactory.

4. Achievement of Objective and Outputs

4.1 Outcome/achievement of objective:
The project achieved the objectives of a LIL. As expected, new methodologies have been designed and
tested and government and private sector personnel have been trained in their use. Management methods
and procedures have been designed and tested in poor urban areas for carrying out small-scale
infrastructure works. Transparent procurement methods are being used. The potential role of SMEs in
carrying out these types of works has been demonstrated. Information on the methods and procedures has



                                                      - 4 -

been disseminated to local construction industries and employment in SMEs is growing. Public entities are
increasingly able to contract works to the private sector which is rapidly gaining experience and providing
increased employment. The physical achievements of the project, limited to the improvement of the four
urban areas initially identified, are fully satisfactory.

Achievement of objectives is rated satisfactory.

4.2 Outputs by components:

Component A: Capacity Strengthening of Key Stakeholders

At the beginning of the project, NGOs were identified to assist in preparing neighborhood development
plans in Port Gentil which were to be replicated in other neighborhoods. Catalogues of TIPPEE projects
and reference documents for contracts and procurement were prepared. Informal training was carried out
for small and medium contractors in collaboration with municipalities. For micro community projects, 48
enterprises were trained in contract and project management. Municipal employees received training in
contract supervision. In 2002 the approach was modified. The component was to be implemented together
with the pilot operations under Component B.             The preparation of draft TIPPEE documents was
incorporated directly into the pilot operations and they were further tested in the preparation of a follow-on
project. This change by PAPSUT was creative and pragmatic, and it improved the process of learning by
doing, very much in the spirit of a LIL. Training was expanded for PAPSUT. Technical cells were
established in the municipalities, and municipal employees were trained in contract management. Extension
agents were trained in community participation, and small entrepreneurs and university graduates were
trained in project preparation (bid preparation, cost estimation, etc.). Degree training for young
entrepreneurs focused on management and marketing. Issues discussed in training sessions were tested in
project implementation. Manuals were prepared to reflect trainees' input and a monitoring and evaluation
system was established.
Upon Project completion, direct outputs are as follows: 7 new SMEs have been created out of the Project;
48 micro and small entreprises completed at least one microproject; 10 new or prospective entrepreneurs
have benefitted from extensive training in firm management and construction site management (formal and
on-the job training); over 20 have been trained in contract and project management ; about 25 municipal
employees have received extensive training in contract supervision and in technical and social engineering;
about 20 community participation specialists have been trained in surveying techniques; construction
workers have been trained in the use of topographic equipment, paving techniques, etc.; 4 engineers and 1
social development specialist have benefitted from direct on-the-job training while employed by the Project
Unit and are now considered as seasoned professionals.

Output under this component was satisfactory.

Component B: Pilot Community-Based Infrastructure Schemes

The first bids under this component were issued near the end of 2000 for transit road works for Port Gentil
(drainage and road paving). Road works in the Balise and the Quartier Sud neighborhoods were finished in
January 2003 (road works amount to about 4 km, against 2.7 planned; drainage to 5 km). Civil works in
Libreville and Franceville were launched in September 2002. Transit road works in Libreville amount to
2.2 km of road work against 1.5 planned; 1.56 for drainage. Pavement in Franceville amounts to 575
meters against 700 planned. PAPSUT helped the SMEs to implement works and to improve their
management.




                                                       - 5 -

Public awareness campaigns were undertaken for micro-projects. The micro-projects were prepared by
community development agents in consultation with communities, but by early 2003 progress was slow,
and additional agents had to be added. About 100 micro-projects were identified, substantially more than
had been planned originally, and more than 70 have been finished (again, many more than were originally
intended).    There were many different types: stairs, public lighting, walkways, drainage, community
latrines, etc. The component has provided a successful test of the TIPPEE approach, applied it to three
neighborhoods, and developed a model for sustainable community upgrading in Gabon.

Output under this component was delayed but was completed within the revised time-frame when the
project was extended. The delay in implementing the component was largely due to the lack of experience
in Gabon with community development.           However, the component was quite successful because the
communities became owners of the project. In effect, the micro-projects preceded the neighborhood
development plans, the latter being regularly adapted to the expressed priorities of the inhabitants.

The quality of works was reviewed in detail and certified by the Bank civil engineer, who participated in
every supervision mission. In the course of project implementation, the Bank recommended that a technical
audit be also implemented, with a view to facilitate preparation of the future project; PAPSUT drafted the
terms of reference but the audit could not be implemented, for lack of funds; it is recommended that special
provision for technical audits be part of the larger upcoming project.

Output under this component is rated satisfactory.

Component C: Project Monitoring and Evaluation

As implementation of the first components began, experts were identified to develop and assist in the setting
up of a socioeconomic monitoring system. Tools and techniques were developed for monitoring the
environmental aspects of neighborhood projects. Close monitoring of the project's social, economic and
environmental impacts was adopted, and environmental studies were carried out for Libreville and Port
Gentil. The guidelines were further developed after the second annual review, and environmental clauses
were included in works contracts. Performance and impact indicators were prepared for infrastructure and
performance indicators for the TIPPEE methodology for assessing social impacts. The results are being
used to improve community participation techniques, defined under Component A and tested under
Component B. The methodologies have been documented and disseminated in a manual, and municipal
representatives have been trained in their use and in conducting socioeconomic surveys.

Output under this component was satisfactory.

Component D: Support for a Longer-Term public Investment Program (PROTTIPPEE)

This was initially delayed and rated unsatisfactory well into the project period. A consultant was identified
to follow up on the Public Expenditure Revue of July 2001 to identify works that could be managed as
TIPPEE projects. PAPSUT commissioned a study to define a strategy for small and medium enterprise
development and to identify specific TIPPEE works for the future. Government involved PAPSUT in
discussions of the Code des Marchés Publics to use the TIPPEE experience in fostering development of
SMEs for public works. The preparation of a portfolio of projects suitable for a PIP-TIPPEE was added
to the component and discussed at the second annual review. PAPSUT has used the public expenditure
review to identify the types of public works appropriate for TIPPEE management. There is now a proposal
to use TIPPEE techniques in virtually all sectors of the economy, especially social sectors.

Although there were delays, output under this component is rated satisfactory and it forms a good basis for
the follow-on project that is under preparation.

4.5 Institutional development impact:
An existing Project Implementation Unit (PAPSUT) ­ had already shown its capacity to manage a TA
project (Programme d'Ajustement et de Planification des Secteurs Urbains et des Transports) mostly
involving sector studies in the transport and urban sectors and some pilot labor-based pavement of urban
roads in Libreville. Since PAPSUT had qualified staff with experience in Bank procurement, reporting
and other aspects of project management, it was selected to be the main implementing agency. Standard
bidding documents were developed near the beginning of the project to facilitate procurement and were
used in later phases. PAPSUT made improvements in project management and progress monitoring.
PAPSUT successfully expanded the types of intervention it undertook, and adapted to the requirements of
the project to include outreach to communities, entrepreneurs, municipalities and NGOs.

The project strengthened government and municipal capacity to identify and implement infrastructure
projects in poor neighborhoods. It institutionalized the TIPPEE methodology by involving, training and
providing experience to a variety of actors including government officials, municipal employees,
community organizers, private sector entrepreneurs and beneficiaries.

The project prepared an institutional framework for the sustained development of SMEs in construction
and other public works.        It defined a program of public expenditures open to SMEs and improved
procedures for contract processing and awarding. It identified the type of public works that could be
managed as TIPPEE activities and regulations for the Code des Marchés. The TIPPEE techniques
developed can now be used in many sectors, particularly the social sectors. A TIPPEE implementation
manual has been finalized in the context of the new Code des Marchés, defining training and dissemination
programs and preparing a first tranche of investments.

5. Major Factors Affecting Implementation and Outcome

5.1 Factors outside the control of government or implementing agency:
None.

5.2 Factors generally subject to government control:
Adequate counterpart funds were not transferred to the implementing agency at the beginning of the project
which delayed effectiveness. In 1999 a minimum (CFAF 300 million) was made available but a remaining
CFAF 300 million amount was not made available and contributed to delays. A further CFAF 50 million
was paid in 2000, leaving an unpaid balance of CFAF 250 million. A grant from the European Union of
CFAF 950 million helped the project to overcome these initial shortfalls and to get underway.
Nevertheless, counterpart funding remained an issued during implementation leading to slow disbursements
(only 22.5% by March 2003). Only near the end of the project was the counterpart funding shortfall
rectified.

5.3 Factors generally subject to implementing agency control:



                                                     - 7 -

The implementing agency generally worked in conformity with the project design and its actions generally
contributed to the successful implementation of the project. Some improvements in PAPSUT's accounting
system were necessary to improve the tracking of financial management and sources of funds. PAPSUT
improved its technical monitoring and its ability to help others act. It strengthened the quality of its own
staff and helped raise technical capacity in the municipalities. It developed the ability to supervise and
assist NGOs in local associations in developing community participation, which was essential to the
success of the project, and it improved its monitoring system. It took the lead in preparing the portfolio of
projects for PROTIPPEE (preparation of portfolio, elaboration of a Code des Marchés, financial guide,
training programs and technical studies. It successfully implemented the infrastructure schemes as planned
in the three cities; generated interest and set-up the necessary conditions for a successful identification and
implementation of the micro-projects (a more larger amount than expected), and completed the project with
a disbursement rate of 98.5%.

5.4 Costs and financing:
The original project cost estimate of US$7.39 million was exceeded in nominal terms (US$8.18 million),
because of exchange rate variation. Ninety-four percent of project cost was concentrated on component B,
Pilot community-based infrastructure. The Loan was disbursed at the end of the project.

6. Sustainability

6.1 Rationale for sustainability rating:
 Since this was a Learning Innovation Loan (LIL), the more important issue related to sustainability is
whether the methodologies developed under the project will be sustained and expanded. The project has
demonstrated that: (i) SMEs' can play a substantial role in the public works/construction industry, thanks
to efficient and transparent procurement rules and processing of invoices (payment typically under two
weeks); and (ii) that community participation can be mobilized and can play an important role. TIPPEE
methodologies have been developed, tested and improved. A large number of actors have gained experience
and training under the project (engineers and accountants in the project unit, municipal employees, private
contractors, extension agents, community development agents), who can be called upon to expand the
TIPPEE program.

The works will be maintained by the communities (for the micro-projects) and the municipalities (for the
larger infrastructure works). The latter have identified staff and earmarked funds to that effect. The quality
of maintenance will be assessed at the time of appraisal of the follow-on project.

The Government has requested a follow-on project to continue development of the TIPPEE approach to
expand it to other sectors and to align it with the PIP. This approach is also supported by the donor
community. Already, PROTOTIPPEE had been appraised jointly with an AFDB operation, currently
implemented by PAPSUT and to be completed soon. The main donors (EU, AFDB, AFD) have expressed
strong interest in the follow-on project.

6.2 Transition arrangement to regular operations:
Works have been transferred to the municipalities and the communities. Microproject works will be
maintained by the communities. Larger works will be maintained by the municipalities, which have
assigned staff and earmarked funds to that effect. Because of the nature of works (use of paving blocks),
maintenance needs will be reduced. Furthermore, the quality and sustainability of maintenance performed
thus far will be assessed during appraisal of the follow-on project and appropriate arrangements will be
included for continued satisfactory maintenance in the context of the new operation.

7. Bank and Borrower Performance

Bank
7.1 Lending:
The Bank used the LIL instrument to conceive an innovative project. It was able to use an existing
implementing agency and help it to expand its perspectives and capacities beyond the more traditional
projects that had previously been undertaken. Bank performance in preparation is rated highly satisfactory.

7.2 Supervision:
There were nine supervision missions, including three major annual reviews.             The Bank worked to
overcome problems, including a shortage of counterpart funding. Complexities were overcome, such as the
initial lack of capacity in the implementing agency, the lack of sufficient small scale entrepreneurs, the need
for more capable NGOs to work with communities, the need to introduce environmental assessments at the
community level, and the need to improve coordination between levels of government and those
implementing the project.

A Quality of Supervision Assessment was carried out by QAG in 2003. The overall quality of supervision
was judged to be satisfactory with regard to assessment of problems, focus on sustainability and good
advice and other follow-up actions. Supervision of fiduciary and safeguard aspects was also considered
satisfactory.    This included procurement, financial management and social aspects.             Adequacy of
supervision inputs and processes was considered satisfactory. The realism of project performance ratings
was rated marginal. After the QAG, the Bank team applied more scrutiny to these ratings; by then
however, project performance had much improved.

Bank supervision is rated satisfactory.

7.3 Overall Bank performance:
Given the innovative nature of the project that the Bank helped to create, the successful efforts of the Bank
to overcome obstacles in the early years of the project and the satisfactory conclusion of the project with
the possibility of developing a follow-on project with even greater impacts, overall Bank performance is
rated satisfactory.

Borrower
7.4 Preparation:
Before this project, there had been a substantial imbalance in public investment in Gabon. There was
excessive investment in high cost infrastructure projects and little investment by central and local
government to alleviate poverty.      In preparing this project the government showed a willingness to
experiment with new methodologies in order to address this imbalance and to confront urban poverty by
improving conditions in under serviced urban settlements. Government performance in preparation is rated
highly satisfactory.

7.5 Government implementation performance:
A lack of sufficient counterpart funding delayed the project and ultimately led to a 1½ year delay in
completion. Nonetheless, the Government did provide most of the required counterpart funding later in the
project and supported the innovative aspects of the project and efforts to prepare a more far reaching
follow-on project. On balance, Government performance is rated satisfactory.

7.6 Implementing Agency:
PAPSUT had to expand its expertise to implement this project which had many facets that went beyond the
more traditional infrastructure projects it had been implementing. It took time to develop procurement



                                                     - 9 -

processes and to train personnel to manage small scale urban works. It needed to develop the capacity to
work with municipalities, small entrepreneurs and NGOs and environmental consultants. In the end, it
largely succeeded. Implementing agency performance is rated satisfactory.

7.7 Overall Borrower performance:
Given the overall satisfactory outcome, despite the early and important delays due to lack of counterpart
funding, overall Borrower performance is rated satisfactory.

8. Lessons Learned

l A successful LIL requires an innovative design, should be adapted during implementation and should
    emphasize learning at all levels.     In this case the project involved public works agencies, local
    authorities, SMEs, environmental consultants, and community workers. The successful promotion of
    community participation and SMEs, which were largely absent previously, has laid the base for a
    larger project to expand the use of the TIPPEE methodology.

l Innovation is important.      The process of improving low-income settlements began by improving
    existing road alignments while avoiding resettlement. This was a key step to improving access to urban
    services and safety, transportation, ambulances, fire department, etc. Pavement was laid using locally
    produced concrete cement blocks, a labor-based technology easily managed by SMES. The new transit
    roads triggered subsequent community improvements. The drainoff from paved roads led to the
    construction of secondary drains by communities. Micro projects focusing mainly sanitation were then
    implemented in consultation with communities. For the first time in Gabon, municipalities were
    involved in road pavement projects, learning how to procure these types of works, and they became
    involved with community leaders in the design and implementation of micro-projects.

l Adaptability is important. The project was constantly adapted during implementation. The first two
    components were changed to be implemented jointly so that experience from the micro-projects could
    be fed into the design of the tools and techniques being developed for the TIPPEE methodology. Also
    during the project, it was decided to implement the micro-projects before preparing community
    development plans in order to ensure that the population would be involved in the planning process.

l A "learning by doing" process is important.         As the methodology was developed, manuals were
    prepared, and stakeholders were trained based on the pilot works. The project unit then used this early
    experience to train local government staff on the job, creating implementation units in the
    municipalities and involving them actively in project implementation.        The project also trained
    professionals to help communities choose works, to mobilize community contributions, and explain
    works procedures.     A methodology for community participation was also developed, tested and
    improved, and, although it was a time consuming process, it now provides the basis for future larger
    initiatives. A similar "learning by doing" process was used to extract lessons from early SME
    involvement and to design and provide training for young entrepreneurs in procurement and other
    management skills.

l It is important to consider replicability and maintenance from the beginning.         The project tested
    approaches to the to paving main access roads and transit roads with concrete cement blocks in
    selected neighborhoods without major restructuring. This turned out be socially and economically
    viable. Access was improved without upsetting the social structure of the area. The communities can
    maintain the streets at low cost, since concrete blocks can be easily replaced using labor-based
    methods.

l The issue of counterpart funding should be clearly addressed in the context of project preparation. The
    Borrower's share amounted to 50% for civil works; 25% for micro-projects; 25% for consultant costs;



                                                   - 10 -

    and 100% of all operating costs, or, in all, well above 50% of all project costs. It is recommended that
    these percentages be reduced in the follow-up project, as well as in other operations targeting poverty
    alleviation.

II /Description du Projet :

2.1      Description du projet lors de l'évaluation initiale.
Le projet était destiné à concevoir, tester et adapter des méthodes et des approches qui facilitent la participation des
entreprises privées locales à l'exécution de ces projets et qui maximisent leur impact sur l'emploi des couches
sociales défavorisées en milieu urbain. A cette fin, le projet avait été structuré en quatre composantes définies
ci-après :
-        Composante A : Renforcement des capacités locales de gestion des Travaux d'Intérêt Public pour la
Promotion de l'Entreprise et de l'Emploi (TIPPEE).
         Cette composante avait pour objet de concevoir et de mettre en place les outils permettant la réalisation,
         par tous les acteurs impliqués (maîtres d'ouvrages publics, ONG, bureaux d'études et entreprises), de
         travaux d'intérêt public pour la promotion de l'entreprise et de l'emploi. La composante comprenait la
         conception des outils, leur diffusion, la formation des différents acteurs à leur utilisation et la mise en
         place des équipements nécessaires ;
-        Composante B : Réalisation pilote des travaux TIPPEE de quartiers.
         Cette composante devait permettre de mettre au point les procédures de réalisation des travaux, en
         particulier la passation des marchés, et les méthodes de programmation et de réalisation des ouvrages
         d'aménagement de quartier (ouvrages structurant et micro-projets). La composante comprenait des
         travaux incluant des ouvrages de voirie, de drainage et d'équipements sociaux qui ont un caractère
         structurant pour les quartiers concernés et également des micro-projets de quartier qui s'articulent avec ces
         ouvrages structurants dans le cadre de plans d'aménagement de quartier cohérent. Elle comprenait
         également une assistance dans la préparation de ces plans d'aménagement intitulés « projets de quartier »
         ;
-        Composante C : Suivi-évaluation et gestion des TIPPEE.
         Elle devait permettre de mettre au point des outils de gestion intégrés efficaces, d'assurer un suivi efficace
         des opérations TIPPE au travers d'audits techniques et financiers et de prendre en compte les résultats des
         opérations pilotes dans la finalisation des outils de programmation et de réalisation qui auront été conçus
         au titre des deux composantes précédentes ;
-        Composante D : Préparation d'un Programme d'extension des TIPPEE (PROTTIPPEE)
         Elle devait permettre la généralisation de cette approche en matière de programmation et de réalisation
         des programmes d'infrastructures urbaines.

2.2      Modifications du Projet apportées lors de la Revue à mi-parcours

La gestion du Projet était organisée essentiellement autour de 3 organes : le COC, la CEP ou l'Unité de Projet et les
CTM.


4.1       Le COC
Le Comité Consultatif Interministériel d'Orientation et de Coordination (C.O.C.) du PROTOTIPPEE, créé par
l'Etat, avait pour mission essentielle d'harmoniser les différentes orientations des administrations ministérielles
pour les soumettre à la cellule d'exécution du projet (CEP), d'assurer le suivi de l'exécution et d'informer les
départements concernés de l'évolution du Projet.

Le COC était composé des représentants des départements ministériels suivant:
·         Ministère de la Planification, Président;
·         Ministère des Finances;
·         Ministère de l'Urbanisme;
·         Ministère de l'Equipement et de la Construction;
·         Ministère de l'Intérieur;
·         Ministère de la Santé Publique;
·         Ministère de l'Education Nationale;
·         Ministère du Travail et de l'Emploi.

Le secrétariat du COC est assuré par la CEP.

4.2       La Cellule d'exécution du Projet (PAPSUT).
La Cellule d'Exécution du Projet, entièrement à la charge de l'Etat avait pour mission essentielle d'assurer la
maîtrise d'ouvrage du projet pour le compte du Gouvernement. La Cellule d'Exécution du Projet (CEP) est la
Cellule de Coordination du PAPSUT. Pour la réalisation de sa mission, le PAPSUT a eu à poursuivre les objectifs
principaux suivants :
·         mettre en oeuvre des procédures simplifiées, transparentes et totalement contrôlables en matière de
passation de marchés publics, de prestations de services, de fournitures et de travaux urbains.
·         développer la création d'emplois, en particulier par le recours aux techniques de Travaux à Haute Intensité
de Main d'Oeuvre (THIMO) ;
·         attribuer, par appels à la concurrence, des marchés de prestations et en particulier de maîtrise d'oeuvre,
aux bureaux d'études sélectionnés par une procédure appropriée ;
·         attribuer, par appels à la concurrence, des marchés de travaux de préfabrications et de pose, par priorité,
aux petites et moyennes entreprises gabonaises du bâtiment et des travaux publics;
·         améliorer à travers l'exécution des travaux urbains générateurs d'emplois le savoir-faire de la main
d'oeuvre employée et la compétitivité des entreprises chargées de la réalisation des chantiers ;
·         améliorer les capacités de gestion des PME et des BE;
·         renforcer les capacités techniques des municipalités, notamment des CTM;
·         améliorer la gestion et l'entretien des ouvrages réalisés;
·         coordonner l'exécution des différentes composantes du PROTOTIPPEE et des différents travaux;




                                                         - 24 -

·        assurer une gestion efficace et transparente des opérations d'investissements urbains en particulier dans le
respect des coûts d'objectifs.

La Cellule était organisée de telle sorte qu'elle puisse assurer rationnellement les missions qui lui ont été confiées.
Son effectif de neuf (9) personnes, en ce qui concerne le personnel clé permanent, était composé normalement par :

         Un Coordinateur,
         Deux ingénieurs dont un ingénieur civil,
         Un chargé d'étude économiste pour la préparation du PIP TIPPEE,
         Un chargé d'étude socio-géographe pour les aspects relatifs aux projets de quartiers,
         Un comptable,
         Un aide comptable,
         Un agent administratif et logistique,
         Une secrétaire de direction.

Cette composition a été mise en place et même renforcée progressivement en fonction des besoins liés à
l'avancement et aux moyens.

4.3      Les Cellules Techniques Municipales.
Les Cellules Techniques Municipales (CTM) jouaient les rôles:
1. de représentant technique de la Municipalité dont chacune d'elle dépendait et d'assistant du Maître d'ouvrage
délégué (PAPSUT), dans le cas des projets d'infrastructure et de voiries,
2.       de conseiller technique de la Municipalité dont elle émanait
3.       et de conseiller des populations bénéficiaires, dans la gestion de micro-projets.

En tant que représentant de la Municipalité et assistant, la CTM fournissait un appui au PAPSUT en:
·        facilitant le travail des entreprises, des consultants et des ONG;
·        participant à l'élaboration des études techniques et la préparation des DAO;
·        participant à la supervision et à la réception des travaux ;
·        surveillant la gestion efficace et transparente des opérations des micro projets de sa municipalité.

En tant que conseiller de la Municipalité, la CTM fournissait un appui au PAPSUT en:
·        en informant régulièrement l'autorité municipale de l'évolution des projets exécutés sur son territoire;
·        en préparant et facilitant les contributions de la Municipalité aux succès des projets;

IX/ Impacts :

Les impacts du projet du projet sur ne sont pas très significatifs compte tenu de la dimension du projet (moins de 5
millions $US) et de son étendue limitée à quelques villes et administrations.

9.1.     Impacts sur l'environnement
Aucun impact négatif significatif sur l'environnement n'est actuellement connu. Quelques nuisances ont été
relevées et résorbées. Par contre les impacts positifs sont visibles. La transformation des quartiers est perceptible à
vue d'oeil. Les conditions de vie des populations se sont nettement améliorées, pour preuve l'engouement suscité
par les micro-projets sur la dernière année du projet tant au niveau des populations et que des autorités
municipales. La demande est forte et d'autres quartiers attendent une suite.

9.2.     Impacts sur la pauvreté et le chômage
Sur la pauvreté, l'impact se mesure surtout à travers la réduction des nuisances environnementales qui réduisent
les dépenses ruineuses de santé pour les familles. Sur le chômage, on ne pouvait pas s'attendre un impact
spectaculaire compte tenu du faible niveau d'investissement. Toutefois, la majeure partie des salaires distribués l'a
été auprès des jeunes des quartiers. On n'a pu noter que certains jeunes sont plus motivés que d'autres, ce qui a été
le cas de Port-Gentil où les jeunes ont compris le message des animateurs chargés de la sensibilisation et ont saisi
ce projet comme une opportunité pour apprendre un métier (coffreur, ferrailleur, et poseur de pavé). A Libreville,
ce sont plutôt les jeunes des travaux de préfabrications qui se sont montrés plus dynamiques et opportunistes. Ils
ont ainsi pu monnayer leur qualification auprès d'autres entreprises.

9.3.     Impacts sur la promotion des PME ;
Pour ce qui est particulièrement des micro-projets communautaires, au total 48 micro et petites entreprises (MPE),
dont 21 à Port Gentil, 14 à Libreville et 13 à Franceville, ont pu réaliser au moins un micro projet.

Les petites et moyennes entreprises (PME) au moins une vingtaine a pu être formée à la gestion des marchés

capacités sur le contrôle de la qualité des productions et les rendements.

Le projet a permis directement l'installation de trois fournisseurs de pavés et bordures dont deux ont été
adjudicataires de marchés de préfabrications dans le cadre de nos travaux, et indirectement d'un quatrième
(conseils en organisation et choix des matériels). Ceci augure d'une véritable compétition lors des futurs projets.
Tous ces fournisseurs sont des MPE et PME.

Le projet a convaincu (ce qui par ailleurs était son objectif) certains sceptiques et détracteurs sur la technique du
pavé et l'approche TIPPEE pour les PME, si bien que certaines municipalités, comme celle d'Oyem ont demandé
au PAPSUT d'assurer une formation et un encadrement de leur PME dans le cadre de l'exécution des travaux
d'aménagement de leur ville.

En outre, un mini programme d'incubation de PME a été réalisé dans le cadre du projet, dénommé Promotion et



                                                        - 32 -

Développement des PME (PRODEPE) en partenariat avec l'Office National de l'Emploi et le Fonds d'Insertion et
de Réinsertion financé par le patronat gabonais. De cette expérience, quatre (4) micro-entreprises de travaux et un
bureau d'études ont pu voir le jour et être adjudicataire de marchés mis en concurrence dans le cadre du projet et
ailleurs.

9.4.      Impacts sur le renforcement des capacités
Outre les entreprises qui ont pu bénéficier d'un renforcement de capacités, les administrations également, surtout
les municipalités à travers les CTM. Bon nombre d'agents n'avaient jamais utilisé un ordinateur, ou vu un GPS,
mené une enquête avec un questionnaire structuré, même pas contrôlé des travaux, ni analysé des offres encore
moins monté un petit DAO ou une consultation. Ces agents ont non seulement pu voir, toucher, mais mieux,
utiliser et pratiquer tous les outils, matériels mis à leur disposition par le projet. Certaines CTM telles que celles de
Port-Gentil qui est aujourd'hui la plus outillée, ont pu démontré à leur hiérarchie la nécessité de s'équiper de leur
propre matériel. La CTM de Port-Gentil dispose à ce jour, outre le matériel mis à disposition par le PAPSUT, de
son ordinateur portable avec imprimante, scanner et d'un GPS. Les agents ont appris à faire des rapports
régulièrement, à initier des réflexions et des petites enquêtes sur des questions techniques relevant de leur
responsabilité. Ils sont opérationnels à ce jour.

X/ Conclusions et Recommandations :

10.1.     La pérennisation des acquis et des investissements
Le PROTOTIPPEE a produit des résultats palpables tels que les réalisations physiques dans les quartiers, les
différents manuels et guides à l'usage des administrations et des PME. Mais il a également laissé des acquis en
terme de capacités et peut-être de changement de comportements, notamment au niveau de la prise de conscience.

Pour la pérennisation des investissements, les municipalités ont pris des engagements qui vont être traduits dans
leurs prochains budgets en terme de charges récurrentes pour entretenir les ouvrages réalisés. Une évaluation des
charges récurrentes leur a été fournie dans ce sens.

Quant à la pérennisation des acquis, notamment l'expertise développée au sein des CTM et des animateurs,
certaines municipalités qui ont compris l'intérêt de cellules telles que les CTM, les ont équipées, repositionné au
sein de leur administration et commencé à utiliser leur expertise pour d'autres projets avec d'autres bailleurs. Nous
pouvons donc être rassurés que l'expertise concentrée au sein des CTM ne disparaîtra pas. Quant aux animateurs et
autres formateurs, ils ont commencé pour certains à monnayer leur expertise dans d'autres projets en tant que
consultants.

La dynamique suscitée auprès des populations et des équipes municipales ne devrait pas restée longtemps sans
suite au risque de s'émousser. Il importe de maintenir ces dynamiques notamment en associant leurs acteurs à la
préparation d'un nouveau projet.

10.2.     Les leçons du Projet

Le projet nous a permis de tirer un certain nombre d'enseignements sur les différentes phases du projet.

10.2.1. LANCEMENT DU PROJET
1.        La mobilisation des fonds de contrepartie pour la mise en vigueur du prêt
La mobilisation des fonds de contrepartie a toujours posé problème. Il nous paraît indiqué d'associer aux
négociations du prêt un représentant du Trésor Public pour que les engagements pris soient au moins connus par
cette administration sinon discutés par elle même et non plus en son nom. Ainsi, il pourrait être envisagé de
relever le dépôt initial de mise en vigueur pour supporter les retards de trésorerie.
2.        La mobilisation des consultants




                                                           - 33 -

La difficulté de mobilisation des consultants a été en partie la cause des retards que le projet a connus. Il est
souhaitable les manifestations d'intérêt soient lancées le plus tôt possible, même en phase de préparation.
3.        Le décalage entre les documents utilisés par le Projet et les documents standard en vigueur à la
Banque
Cette question devra absolument être réglée au moment de la préparation et de la négociation du projet et les
conclusions retenues devront être reportées dans l'accord de prêt du projet afin d'éviter les contradictions entre la
cellule d'exécution du projet et le service de passation des marchés de la Banque.
4.        La maîtrise des procédures de la Banque
Il n'est pas souhaitable de démarrer un nouveau projet sans que le personnel clé n'ait été formé au préalable aux
procédures de la Banque que ce soit en passation de marchés ou en décaissement. Il importe de prévoir également
un recyclage ou un perfectionnement en cours de projet.

10.2.2. EXECUTION DU PROJET
5.        L'organigramme et les moyens de fonctionnement de la Cellule
L'efficacité du projet dépend beaucoup de son organisation et de ses moyens tant matériels qu'humains. Il n'est pas
souhaitable de continuer à travailler sans un organigramme clair. De même, il est nécessaire d'opérer une
clarification des rôles et compétences de chaque grande entité de l'organigramme. Cet organigramme devra être
consigné et transcrit dans le manuel de procédure de la cellule et celui d'exécution du projet.
§         Le système communication
Les nouvelles technologies de communication (internet et intranet) ne doivent plus être considérées comme un
luxe. Afin d'améliorer les communications avec la Banque et l'extérieur il est indispensable de disposer d'une
ligne ADSL haut débit et d'un site afin d'attribuer à chaque agent un nom de domaine. De même les
communications internes doivent être améliorées.
§         Le système de contrôle interne
L'absence d'un véritable contrôle interne a été ressentie tout au long du projet. Cette lacune devra être comblée. Le
système de contrôle interne ne devra pas seulement s'appuyer sur les hommes, mais d'abord sur les procédures.
§         Le système de classement et d'archivage
Il paraît utile d'avoir un système de classement performant, surtout lorsque plusieurs projets et plusieurs bailleurs
sont gérés simultanément. Un classement physique soutenu par un classement informatique permettant de
retrouver plus facilement un document devra être mis en place.
§         Le recrutement des cadres de haut niveau
Il est absolument nécessaire d'avoir des moyens humains adéquats aux différents postes clé. La nécessité de relever
le niveau de qualification de la partie comptable et financière est devenue une évidence.

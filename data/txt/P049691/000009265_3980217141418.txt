BACKGROUND
1.    The road network of Niger is a key factor for economic development of the country, The
Government of Niger has expressed the need for improved maintenance of the existing road
system. In this context, the World Bank is preparing an infrastructure rehabilitation project
which includes a proposal for regraveling of about twenty unpaved roads. (See list of roads in
Annex I and map in Annex 2).
2.    The environmental impact analysis was launched to study the extent of potential adverse
impact of proposed road rehabilitation work as well as identify any major site-specific impacts
which would require an additional comprehensive assessment and mitigation plan prior to the
project appraisal. (See terms of reference of the mission in Annex 3).
Environmental Issues
3.    The biotops of the project area include Sahelian pastoral grasslands, Sudano-Sahelian
plateau woodlands further south and small areas of Sudanian woodlands near the southern border
with Benin. The vegetation varies depending on soil types, rainfall patterns and land use'.
Rainfalls are often limited but intensive and may for a short time bring a surplus of water on the
ground with subsequent erosion risk during runoff, especially on slopes. The Sahelian zone is
traditionally used most for grazing, but with increasing population and settlement trends, more
and more land is being cultivated, often through extension onto marginal lands.
4.    The main environmental problem in Niger is land desertification. The natural ecosystems
have suffered severely over the last decades due to droughts and to the continuous expansion of
human activities in rural areas. It is estimated that the wildlife population has declined by 90%
over the last twenty years. Clearing of land for cultivation and the increasing need for and
collection of.fuelwood are factors contributing to the disappearance of protective vegetation in
large areas of the country. The subsequent soil erosion caused by wind and violent rainfalls is
now of major concern.

PROJECT DESCRIPTION
6.    The highway and transport sector component of the Infrastructure Rehabilitation Project
consists of:
a) provision of technical assistance to assist the Governnent in privatizing public works
equipment rental activities carried out by the Departmnent of Public Works
Equipment, making the National Road Laboratory autonomous, restructuring the
Ministry of Equipment and Infrastructure, preparing road maintenance strategies for
paved, unpaved and rural roads, implementing a sustainable mechanism for road
maintenance financing, and developing the use of small and medium enterprises in
road maintenance;
b) provision of technical assistance to the Department of Land Transport to improve
road safety and quality of passenger and freight transport services and to the
Department of Civil Aviation to commercialize airport management;
c) limited rehabilitation of public works equipment to ensure immediate availability of a
minimum of equipment for rental to small and medium enterprises;
d) provision of computers, software and other equipment to improve the capacity in the
Highway Department to monitor the road network condition and to program road
maintenance works;
e) execution of a regraveling programn of about 1,860 km of earth roads;
f) strengthening of the capacity of sector institutions to manage environmental concems;
and
g) provision of training services and seminars in conjunction with other project
activities.

7.    Works will consist in the regraveling of the road surface with lateritic materials, spot

rehabilitation or improvement of signaling and marking. The alignment will be kept unchanged,
except for minor adjustments. for example to create water holes.
POLICY LEGAL AND ADMINISTRATIVE FRAMEWORK
8.    During the 90th, the Govemment has taken several important initiatives towards the
development of a comprehensive legislation on environment and natural resources management.
But already before this time, concern for the natural resources was addressed in a number of legal
documents still in effect. For instance, the Forest Law provides a list of 15 tree species which
require special permission by the Government to cut or harvest. The hunting law regulates
protection of fauna and special nature and game reserves.
9.   In 1992, "Principes Directeurs d'une Politique de Developpement Rural pour le Niger"
was issued (see Annex 5) and in the following year "Principes d'Orientation du Code Rural" was
adopted by the National Assembly. These documents currently constitute the basis for
management of the natural resources of the country.
10.  Important recent undertakings in this field are the preparation of a "Plan National de
Lutte Contre la Desertification" and the work towards a "Plan d'Action des Forets
Tropicales". International Conventions signed by the Government include those on
International Important Wetlands, Desertification, Climate Change and the Biodiversity
Convention.
il.  In 1993, a national committee was created to follow up on the recommendations of
Agenda 21 and prepare a national environmental action plan. The work program was later
expanded and in January 1996, "Le Conseil National de l'Environnement pour un Development
Durable" (C?IEDD) was established with the mandate to help implement, to follow up and
evaluate "Le Plan National de lEnvironnement pour un Development Durable". It should also
coordinate governmental actions with respect to environmnental information and sensitization, as
well as follow up and evaluate the impact of development programs and projects affecting
natural resource management. CNEDD is linked directly to the office of the prime minister and
composed of the Secretary Generals of all ministries concerned by the environment. It is served
by an executive secretariat. Eight regional councils will be established throughout the country.
The plan will constitute the basis for a future comprehensive environmental legisiation.

Direction de I'Environnement
12.   According to decision N° 064/MHE/DAAF dated 03/09/92 defining its organization and
its responsibilities, "Direction de I'Environnement" (DE) under "Minister de l'Hydraulique et de
I'Environnement" is in charge of and includes services for, among others, reduction of land
degradation, fauna and flora, control of pollution and hazardous material and for environnentai
studies, training seminars and public environmental information. With respect to institutional
capacity, DE has currently only a few specialists to cover environmental impact of economic and
social development of the country including impact studies of projects, legislation and support to
other ministries.
Direction des Travaux Publics
13.   1996 by-laws defines the missions of "Direction des Travaux Publics" (DTP) under
"Minister de I'Equipement et des Infrastructures". It is in charge of the planning, organization
and implementation of all road sector works. Among others, it will initiate, undertake and
coordinate relevant studies for transport projects, including all types of roads from paved to dirt
roads. According to DTP, there is currently no regulation or directives with respect to
environmental impact studies under the road sector legal framework. DTP has no environmental
staff within its organization.

14.   Environmental impact analysis of development activities in Niger has not yet been
regulated by law. But this year the govemment has prepared a draft legislation on the
institutionalization of environmentai impact studies ("Avant projet - Ordonnance portant
Institutionnalisation des Etudes d'Impact sur l'Environnement"). The legislation is
expected to be signed before the end of the year. (See Annex 4.)
Environmental Impact
15.   Environmental considerations in connection with rehabilitation of unpaved roads in Niger
are mainly related to the composition of adjacent vegetation cover, the risk for wind and water
erosion, the hydrological conditions and to the existence of biodiversity of significant value.
Particular attention should be given to any removal of vegetation, disturbance of natural
ecosystems and soil conditions, to impacts on surface and ground water flows mcluding roadside
runoff conditions and arrangements for drainage and management of runoff water beyond the
immediate roadside, and to the location, extraction work and rehabilitation of quarries and
borrow areas.

discussed concerning the ecosystems through which the roads are passing, including topography.

17.  Inforrnation was also gathered with respect to the current state of the selected roads, the

Conclusions
19.  In general, with the exception of five roads for which specific recommendations are
provided below, adverse environmnental impact of the rehabilitation project is expected to be
limited due to a number of reasons.
*  The work only involves renewing the lateritic road surface using the existing roadbase.
*  No new alignments will be included.
 The improvement of the road conditions is not expected to change the traffic patterns to
any large extent and would therefore not entail significant increase of new developments
with subsequent secondary environmental impacts; and
-  The project roads are mOstly passing through areas of minor altitude differences. The
land is to a large extent used for cultivation and grazing. No natural habitats of
significant importance are registered near the selected roads.
20.   Hoawever, some of the roads involve shorter distances of sand encroachmnent. These will
require special considerations on sand stabilization. With appropriate planning of the project's
rehabilitation work including environmental mitigation measures for water management, soil
conservation and maintenance/replacement of vegetation cover, potential negative environmental
effects would be kept to a minimum.
Road 97, within Dosso Partial Reserve
21.   The southem part of road number 97, Gaya - Margou, is passing through the "Reserve
Partielle de Dosso". This reserve was created as a buffer zone for "W" National Park, which is
situated on the opposite side of the river Niger. During a few months of the year, when the water
level of the river is low, park animals occasionally cross over to feed in the reserve. A number of

protected tree species are also found in the area. A summarv of environmental aspects of the
Gaya-Birni n'Gaouré area is presented in Annex 6.
22.   Several vil1ages are located within the partial reserve of Dosso and existed already before
its creation. Large areas are under cultivation or used for pasture, particularly along the road.
Thus, the project does not involve impact on original natural habitats. However, a more detailed
environmental impact analysis should be carried out before the technical studies of project
execution are launched. Such an analysis should address any potential negative impact of the
project and provide detailed recommendations for mitigation. It should also identify
opportunities for the project to contribute to improving the environment for both wildlife and
human population.
Road 97 and 89, in the Dallol Bosso Area
23.   The northem part of the same road is following the valley Dallol Bosso. The valley is
continuing also north of Margou, and is there served by road number 89, Margou Ounditan. The
Dallol Bosso area is home of the last giraffes remaining in West Africa, and of considerable
interest to biodiversity conservation. It is presently unprotected. Most of the area is cultivated or
used as pastoral land, and the giraffes are co-existing with the local population and domestic
animais.
24.   The giraffes have become a recognized tourist attraction. The Government has so far
trained about twenty official guides from the nearby villages in an effort to draw upon wildlife
tourism as a source of local income, at the same time strengthening the awareness and protection
of wildlife. Thus, local traffic in this area is expected to grow, even if only slowly. One accident
between giraffe and truck has been reported.
25.   The road rehabilitation work is not expected to adversely affect or modify the conditions
for the giraffe population to any large extent, provided that adequate precautions are taken. In a
more detailed environmental impact analysis of the Dallol Bosso roads (97 and 89) measures
should be ideptified not only to minimize any potential negative consequences of the
rehabilitation work, but also to take the opportunities to improve the environmental conditions
and prepare for future traffic situation.
Rond 48 and 49, in the Keita Region
26.   The topography of the Keita region is characterized by hilly terrain with steep slopes.
Thus, the risk for soil erosion is particularly high in connection with the rehabilitation of roads
number 48 and 49, Madaoua - Keita - RN25 PK22. Special attention must be given and detailed
directives developed during the environmental study ofthese roads.

Road 86, Northwest of the River Niger
27.   Road number 86, Foneko - Yatakala. is located in the northern part of the region between
Niamey - Tillabery and the Mali - Burkina Faso border. This region of the country still contains
areas with only scattered settlements, particularly in the south. The whole region is now subject
to a larger environmental impact assessment triggered by requests from the mining industry to
the Government and the financing donor for the construction of access roads to future potential
mining sites. The proposed sites are located in the southem part, in areas not yet opened to
development. Even with the fact that road number 86 does not open up any new areas to
settlements and development, it would be important to follow the assessment work to benefit
from information that might be provided on the project related area.
SOCIAL IMPACT
28.   The unpaved roads included in the project are used by regional transports and local
populations. Current road conditions have not caused any limitation in the transportation of
goods and people. Regravelling of the surface is therefore not expected to influence traffic
intensity and encourage a significant increase in new developments.
29.   Improved road surface may invite increased speed of the vehicles and thereby an
increased safety threat to local people using the road as pedestrians or to non-motorized vehicles.
Appropriate measures should be taken to eliminate such safety risks particularly in connection
with villages and other settlemens.
30.   An additional safety concern is linked to the vehicles transporting goods and people
between villages. The project should ensure that there are adequate areas for loading/unloading
off the road during all seasons.
31.   Recomnmendations on local participation during project planning and implementation are
given below.

Guidelines on Environmental Aspects of Road Maintenance
33.   The document "Road Maintenance and the Environrnent" is prepared under the
SubSaharan Africa Transport Program, a World Bank/Economic Conimission for Africa
initiative. The paper gives environmental guidance for road maintenance activities. Of particular
relevance are the sections on maintenance of unpaved roads.
34.  It is recommended that the guidelines be adopted as a framework for integrated
environmental management in road maintenance in Niger and serve as a practical guidance to the
technical studies in the planning of the regravelling of each road proposed by the project.
Site-Specific Environmental Studies
35.   For each individual road to be regraveled, a detailed, site-specific enviromnental study
should be carried out at the planning stage and a set of mitigation measures should be prepared tO
be incorporated into the construction work plan (Cahier de charge des travaux). The studies
should be carried out by environmental specialists of DTP (re proposal for new environmental
unit of DTP - see below) as part of the technical pre-construction studies for each road.
Collaboration between environmental and road engineering staff is important.
36.   Each site-specific environmental study should be cleared by the ministry of environment
and other departments concerned in accordance with the new draft legislation on the
institutionalization of environmental impact studies. Draft temis of reference for the site-specific
studies were prepared according to the adopted guidelines (see Annex 7). The following issues
were emphasized:
a) Each site-specific enviromnental study should identify any sensitive areas with
respect to flora and fauna, which should be protected from major disturbances such as
removal of vegetation and borrow pit excavation. Recommendations on appropriate
management measures should be prepared, for example, to use borrow pit excavations
as,water holes for the fauna or for the agriculture.
b) The study should identify any areas of high risk for soil erosion. Depending on local
conditions of the road cnvironment regarding topography, fragility of the soils,water
infiltration capacity, extent of protective vegetation cover and use of the surrounding
land, recommendations should be provided on drainage and run off water
management, on soil conservation measures such as arrangements to prevent high
speed flow of run off water and on opportunities for improved protection of the
ground such as planting/replanting of trees.



10
c) The study should provide advice on species and methods for the establishment of
vegetation as soil and sand stabilization as well as for the replacement of damaged
vegetation cover.
d) The site specific environmental study should pay special attention to the location of
quarries and borrow pits. It should point out areas that, for environrnental reasons,
should not be exploited, but also recommend preferred locations for extraction of
materials. Collaboration with the project's road engineering experts is essential
e) The study should give recommendations on preferred locations for dumping of sand
in areas of sand encroachment.
f) The study should give recommendations on preferred locations of worksite facilities
and areas for storage of larger amounts of materials during the work period.
g) The study should ensure consultations with local people to protect and enhance local
living conditions.
Road 48 and 49, in the Keita Region
37.   The topography of the Keita region is characterized by hilly terrain with steep slopes.
The environmental study of the roads number 48 and 49 should pay extra attention to the high
risk for erosion and recommend on measures to minimize adverse environrental impact and, to
the benefit of both the road and the surrounding environment, restore currently degraded areas
influenced by the road. Close collaboration between the environmental and road engineering
staff of DTP is of great importance.

should analyze and take into account any recommendations which concem the area influenced bv
road 86.

Participation by Local People and Non-Governmental Organizations
42.   Unpaved roads are of particular importance to the local population. Provision for project
participation by local people and non-governmental organizations (NGOS) should be given.
During both the project planning phase with its technical and environmental studies for each
road, and during the implementation of the rehabilitation work, local people and NGOs should be
provided the opportunity to participate and provide opinions and recommendations to be taken
into account in the studies.
43.   Local people should be consulted, for example, to help finding the most appropriate
solutions for management of run off water near the settlements. The creation of semi-permanent
ponds after terminated use of quarries and borrow pits could provide an appreciated additional
source of water in areas of scarcity, thereby enhancing the local environmnent for both wildlife
and people.
44.   A first step towards participation was taken during this environmental impact study, when
a meeting was arranged with representatives of non-govermmental organizations (see Annex 10).
Among the present NGOs should be noted a federation of rural area groups with a total
membership of 2200.

Establishment Of An Environmental Unit Of DTP
47.   No environrnental specialization has earlier been represented within the organization of
the road authorities. However, with the creation of CNEDD as a coordinating body for
environmental management of economic development, directives were given requesting all
governmental institutions to assign focal points for the environment. With this new orientation
of the Government and emphasis on environnental issues, there is an urgent need for
strengthening the environrnental management capacity of the road sector authorities.
48.   The requirement for environrmental analysis studies of the individual roads of the
rehabilitation project is clear. Therefore, it is recommended that, as an early component of this
project, an environmental unit be established within DTP responsible for environrnental
management and impact assessment of road sector activities.
49.   The environmental unit should initially consist of two specialists covering:
a) ecology and natural resource management; and
b) hydro-geology/soil sciences/road engineering.
In addition, funds should be set aside for the procurement of social impact studies as
appropriate.
50.   The Terms of Reference of the unit would include the following:
a) develop procedures for integrating environmental aspects into road maintenance,
rehabilitation or construction works carried out by DTP;
b) promote cooperation between environrnental and road engineering expertise;
c) conduct environmental studies and environmental impact analysis of all road projects;
d) monitor the implementation of environmental analysis recommendations;
e) organize training of DTP staff on environmental management of road sector activities;
f) as a first assignment, conduct the site-specific environrental stucies under the road
rehabilitation project (PRI).
51.   Training should be provided to the new environmental staff members. It is recommended
that provision be given for study trips to one or a few African countries where the



14
roadienvironment institutional framework is successfully in place. Four staffweeks for each
person is recommended.
52.  In-service training should also be provided to the new environmental unit to help develop
work program, procedures and network. Three months of Technical Assistance would be
appropriate, divided in three periods covering the 2nd, 6th and 12th month after establishment.
53.   The DTP environnent unit should, in collaboration with the Environnent Department
(DE), and CNEDD plan and conduct training of DTP staff and of representatives from other
institutions concerned of environmental impact of road sector projects. Initial funding should be
provided by the road rehabilitation project and shared by CNEDD, DE and DTP to finance these
activities and operating costs of the environmental unit in DTP. Cost estimates for the
establishment of an environnental unit of DTP are presented in Annex 11.
ENVIRONMENTAL MONITORING
54.   The first part of the environmental monitoring of the project should include information
on the implementation of institutional recommendations. This includes the establishmnent of the
environmental unit of DTP adoption of its work program and training of its staff members. It
also includes the training seminars for DTP staff funded under this project.
55.   The second part of the environmental monitoring involves information on the physical.
biological and social environment at each local site before, during and after the rehabilitation
work. The purpose is to evaluate and ensure that required environmental mitigation measures are
properly implemented.
56.  In connection with each site-specific environmental study, the new envirorunental unit of
DTP should prepare a program for monitoring of the implementation of the measures
recommended in the different studies as well as monitoring of expected qualitative improvements
of the affected natural resources. The project should ensure appropriate finding.

I. INTRODUCTION
57.   Le Projet de Réhabilitation des Infrastructures a été préparé par le Gouvernement avec
l'objectif d'accroître l'efficacité de l'entretien routier. Il comprend une composante de soutien à
la réforme institutionnelle du secteur de l'entretien routier et une composante de travaux
d'entretien périodique d'une partie du réseau de routes en terre.
58.   Une analyse économique a permis d'identifier un réseau d'environ 1.800 km de routes
pour lequel un entretien périodique est justifié économiquement. Les travaux financés par l'IDA
seront effectués sur ce réseau en trois tranches de longueur à peu près identiques. L'étude
d'évaluation de l'impact sur l'environnement porte sur les routes suivantes qui constituent la
première tranche de travaux:
- Dogondoutchi-Dogonkiria: 73,5 km;
- Tabaram-Badaguichiri: 170,5 km;
- RNIE-Komaka: 55,5 km;
- Foneko-Yatakala: 92,6 kan;
- Gazaoua-Nigeria: 66,9 km;
- Magaria-Nigeria: 61,2 km;
soit au total 520 km.

indirects des travaux menés sur la route peuvent être ressentis sur les milieux naturel et humain.

projet. Une liste indicative d'impacts est donnée ci-après. La liste sera définie lors de la

Analyse des variantes
64.   Le consultant décrira les variantes envisagées pendant la préparation du projet et
identifiera si besoin est de nouvelles variantes. La variante d'abandon du projet figurera parmi
ces dernières. La notion de variante inclut la localisation du projet, ses caractéristiques
techniques, les techniques de construction et le phasage des travaux, et les procédures
d'exploitation de la route après achèvement du projet. Chaque variante sera évaluée en terme
d'impact, de coût et de faisabilité. L'analyse devra préciser les impacts irréversibles ou
inévitables et ceux qui peuvent être atténués. Les coûts devront inclure le coût estimé des
mesures de protection de l'environnement et être comparés aux bénéfice, y compris ceux
résultant de l'application de ces mesures.
Plan de limitation des impacts environnementaux
65.   Le consultant devra présenter les mesures d'atténuation nécessaires pour réduire à un
niveau acceptable les effets nuisibles ou les risques significatifs associés à la réalisation du
projet. Il devra préciser les actions, les ouvrages, les mesures de présentation et les correctifs qui
doivent être intégrés au projet et les coûts résultants. L'efficacité des mesures d'atténuation devra
être estimée. Ces mesures devront être inscrites ultérieurement dans les plans et devis de
construction et le cas échéant dans les normes d'exploitation.
66.   Le consultant évaluera sommairement la capacité des institutions locales et régionales qui
auront à mettre en oeuvre et suivre les mesures d'atténuation. Le consultant définira les besoins
de formation et ceux relatifs aux aspects institutionnels nécessaires pour mettre en oeuvre les
mesures d'atténuation. Les recommandations pourront s'étendre aux agences d'exécution, aux
arrangements intersectoriels, aux procédures de gestion, au personnel, au budget et au support
financier.
67.   Le consultant présentera les impacts résiduels du projet après la mise en oeuvre des
mesures d'atténuation. L'importance de ces impacts devra être évaluée. Le cas échéant, le
consultant devra envisagé les mesures visant à compenser ces impacts résiduels ou ceux n'ayant
pas pu être atténués. Le consultant devra préciser s'il existe des possibilités de réutilisation des
équipements ou des installations temporaires à des fins publiques ou communautaires. Le
consultant préparera un plan de gestion court et concis qui inclura le plan de travail, l'estimation
des budgets, le calendrier, le personnel et les besoins en formation et les autres appuis nécessaires
pour mettre en oeuvre les mesures d'atténuation.

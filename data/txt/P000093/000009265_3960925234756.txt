I   INTRODUCTION
1.01      The Goverument of Benin has requested the Association's asslt-
ance for a transportation project aimed at (a) the rehabilitation and main-
tenance of the country's port and highvay infrastructures; and (b) the
strengthening of the institutions involved in transport planning, road
maintenance, port management and cargo handling activities. The estimated
cost of tha Project is US$70.5 million equivalent. Of this, the Government
vill contribute US$4.8 million equivalent (about 7%). The remainder vill
be financed by IDA, US$19.5 million (28%) and by other donors, US$46.2
million  (65%).   The  Project  vill  continue  and  expand  the assistance
provided under the Cotonou Port Project (Credit No. 826), and the Third and
Fourth Highway Projects (Credits No. 746 and 1142).
1.02      The Project vill help  the Goverument  to  set up a coherent
strategy for the transportation sector, including a realistie package of
policy based measures, institutional development and sector investments and
maintenance based on agreed objectives and priorities. It will reinforce
the trend begun under previous projects towards increased attention to
maintenance of infrastructure and better utilization of assets.  It vill
also move the transportation sector towards increased liberalization and
deregulation,  leading to higher efficiency throughout the sector.   In
addition, it vill help channel co-financiers' funds and assistance towards
these worthwhile objectives.
II. THE TRANSPORT SECTOR
A. Background
2.01      Benin ls a relatively small country (112,600 km 23 on the West
African coast between Nigeria and Togo; the population is about 3.8 mil-
lion. Nith a GNP per capita of only US$267 equïvalent in 1985, it ranks as
a  least  developed  country.   The  economy  is  predoiainantly  rural;  an
estimated 73% of the labor force is involved in agriculture, producing
mainly subsistence, loy value root crops, and contributing just under 50%
of GDP. The tertiary sector increased rapidly until 1982, but stagnated in
the recent past, partly due to the economic problems in neighboring
Nigeria. The tertiary sector employa 20% of the labor force and accounts
for 34% of GDP. The transport sector i important, particularly due to
Benin's transit role for neighboring landlocked countries.   It provides
about 18% of formal employment and accounts for about 7% of GDP.

B. The Transport System
2.02      The transport system is import-export orlented and fairly vell
developed.  Ite backbone is the Benin route to the North of the country and
to Niger, consistîng of the port of Cotonou, the railvay and the road from
Cotonou  to  Parakou,  and  the  Parakou-Malanville  road.   Other  routes,
notably the coastal road, eastward to Porto-Novo (the second city of Benin)
and Lagos (Nigeria), and westvard to Lomé (Togo), radiate from Cotonou (Map
IBRD 19472R).
2.03      The port of Cotonou bas traditionally been the principal entry
point for goods to and from landlocked Niger (about 297,000 tons in 1986 or
70% of Niger's international traffic) and, to a lesser extent, Mali
(18,000 tous) and Burkina (5,500 tons). In 1986, about 30% (350,000 tons)
of Cotonou port traffic was transit cargo. Total port cargo throughput,
vhich vas about 1.5 million tons in 1979, fell to 0.8 million tons in 1983,
reflecting a decline in Nigeria's transit traffic. Traffic picked up to
1.2 million tons in 1985 and remained at this level in 1986. Annex 2-1
provides data on port traffic statistics. Competition from Lomé for traffic
to Niger and Burkina is increasing. Due to its cost advantage, Cotonou has
kept its share of high volume-low value traffic, but the better quality of
service offered at Lomé attracts high value commodities to that port.
2.04      Cotonou port has 1,250 m of general cargo berths, a bulk cargo
jetty and a little-used fishing quay; total annual port capacity la about 2
million tons, of which 1 million for general cargo. Under the previous
Port Project, port operations have been improved and are nov reasonably
efficient. Annex 2-2 provides details on port facilities and Annex 2-3 on
port productivity figures. Although Cotonou port has enough capacity to
handle expected traffic for the nineties, several structures have deteri-
orated due to rust, wave action and insufficient maintenance and need to be
rehabilitated or they will hamper future port operations.  The Project
vould include a Port Rehabilitation Program to avoid further deterioration
of these structures (paras. 3.03 to 3.08).
2.05      The road network totals aboùt 7,500 km, of which 1,000 km are
paved, 4,000 km gravel and improved feeder roads, and the balance un-    2
improved earth roads and tracks.  The density of the network (0.064km/Im )
is fairly low by regional standards, but suffices for present needs. In
order to adapt maintenance standards to traffie load, roads are classified
as part of the priority network (over 50 v.p.d.) or the low-traffie network
(under 50 v.p.d.), vith lover maintenance standards. The Project essen-
tially deals vith periodic maintenance and rehabilitation of the priority
network.

2.07      The motor vehicle fleet (excluding two-wheelers) is estimated at
approximately 25,000 vehicles of which 15,000 are private passenger cars.
Its annual growth has been 6 to 7% over the last eight years. Fleet growth
is expected to continue at the same rate.
2.08      The railway is operated by the Organisation Commune Bénin-Niger
(OCBN), a bi-national autonomous agency created by the Benin and Niger Gev-
eruments.  It manages and operates the railway up to Parakou and then moni-
tors the transhipments te and from Niger.  Most of the Benin route traffic
to Niger uses the 440 km south-north line between Cotonou and Parakou as
does the bulk of national long distance traffic. There are two little-used
coastal lines: the 107 km eastern link between Cotonou, Porto-Novo and
Pobé; and the 33 km weitern extension connecting Sègbohoué to Cotonou.
Overinvestments, stagnating traffic and late tariff adjustments caused OCBN
to accumulate financial losses since 1975. OCBN entered in March 1986 into
an agreeraent with CCCE aiming at financial rehabilitation and deferred

maintenance of tracks and rolling stock. This program is reasonable. With

C. Sector Organization
2.09      General.   The transport sector  is the responsibility of the
Ministry of Equipment and Transport (MET) which is in charge of public
works, housing, urban planning and transport. Port management and cargo
handling operations are the respousibility of two autonomous public
agencies under the tutelage of MET; MET is directly responsible for road
activities through its Directorate of Roads and Bridges; the railway is
operated by OCBN, also under the tutelage of MET. Cotonou has the nation's
sole international airport, served by Air Afrique, UTA, Camair, Nigeria
Airways and Aeroflot among others. The domestic carrier, Transport Aériens
du Bénin, is under liquidation.
2.10      The Port.  The port of Cotonou is run by the Port Autonome de
Cotonou (PAC), an autonomous public agency (Statutes in the Project File,
No 2-1). PAC is responsible for pilotage and berthing of vessels and for
providing and maintaining port facilities, i.e., the berths, sheds, fen-
cing, floodlights and services. PAC vas reorganized in November 1982 under
the Cotonou Port Project. The present organization is generally satisfac-
tory  (Annex 2-5),  and  no major  changes  are  required.   Port  staffing
increased by 25% between 1982 and 1985, peaking at about 700. Whereas this
has nov receded to 660 due to attrition, the present level is still not
justified. A 5% annual reduction in total staff number is included in
PAC's Action Plan for the 1988-1992 period (Annex 2-6) which vas agreed with
PAC's management during negotiations.
2.11      Management of the port has improved over the past five years.
The overall quality of managerial staff is good by regional standards.
ilovever, the efficiency of PAC's workshop needs to be improved (para. 3.08)

E. Sector Policy
2.31      During the 1976-1980 period, the Governient's policy was to in-
crease direct or indirect control over transport activities, but the
expected  improvements  failed  to  materialize.   Neighboring  countries
expressed their dissatisfaction by increasingly using alternative inter-
national transit routes. Since 1980, the Government has embarked upon a
slow deregulation process, permitting independent operators to carry out
some cargo handling operations, softening OCBN's monopoly on transport
between Cotonou and Parakou (in particular for food aid to Niger) and sup-
pressing the public monopoly on freight forwarding.
2.32      The Government is building up a Freight Bureau as a public agency
to regulate freight allocation for trucking. The objective is to ensure
that a fair price is paid for transport services, and to develop a data
bank which would help improve the management of the subsector, seeking in
particular reduction of empty hauls.  Given its recent creation, it is
still uncertain whether or not the freight bureau could actually meet this
objective, and a close monitoring of its activity vill be needed under the
Project because of the obvious risk that the bureau may cause administra-
tion costs without improving efficiency. Improvements in efficiency in
the sector, particularly on the Benin route, are expected from: (a) the
recent admission of the private sector to the market for freight forvard-
ing; and (b) reorganization of OBEMAP under the Project, including
financial restructuring accompanied by staff reductions.  At negotiations,
the Government agreed to evaluate the Freight Bureau'8 freight allocation
efficiency by December 1988 to decide whatever further reform is needed.

A. ORGANISATION, GESTION ET PROGRAMMATION DE L'ENTRETIEN ROUTIER
2.        Au regard des difficultés rencontrées actuellement dans le cadre
de l'entretien routier, le Gouvernement a accepté de réorganiser les
structures de l'entretien routier selon les orientations suivantes:
(a)   placer sous la responsabilité de la Direction des Routes et
Ouvrages d'Art tous les services intervenant dans l'entre-
tien routier (services centraux de planification, de
programmation et contr8le, services territoriaux chargés de

(b)   réorganiser le service du matériel pour qu'il soit iimédia-

destiné à l'entretien des routes, au point de vue de sa
gestion et de son exploitation et à terme, propriétaire de
ce parc. Ce service fonctionnera comme prestataire de
services aux unités chargées de l'exécution des travaux
selon une base tarifaire lui permettant à terme de recou-
vrer ses coûts d'exploitation et de renouveler son maté-
riel;
(c)   organiser les services territoriaux chargés de l'exécution
de l'entretien courant en petites unités géographiques
responsables d'une section de réseai déterminée;

II. PLAN D'ACTION POUR L'ORGANISATION D'UN BURE&U DE GESTION ROUTIERE
Objet
16.      LE bjet du plan d'action est:
(a)   de mettre en place un Bureau de Gestion Routière;
<b)   d'assurer une centralisation des données techniques sur
l'état et l'entretien du réseau routier; et
(c)   de planifier au niveau national et régional les opérations
d'entretien de routine et périodique pour permettre au
Service de l'Entretien Routier de programmer leur
réalisation.
Moyens
17.       L'Arrêté No. 0021/MET/DGM du 11 août 1985 a crée au sein de la
Direction des Routes et Ouvrages d'Art un Bureau de Gestion Routière qui
devient progressivement opérationnel. Ce Bureau de Gestion Routière, placé
sous l'autorité du Directeur des Routes, a pour objet de gérer l'ensemble
des données ayant trait au réseau routier, notamment:
(a)   données techniques (état, dimension, constitution, etc.);
(b)   données sur le trafic;
(c)   historique (date de construction, date des différents
travaux d'entretien périodique ou de réhabilitation,
évolution des caractéristiques`.
18.       Le processus de collecte systématique et de traitement des
données sera effectif avant le 31 octobre 1987.
19.       L'exploitation de ces données sera le point de départ d'une
programation rationnelle des travaux d'entretien, de réhabilitation, de
renforcement et de reconstruction. Le premier programme d'entretien,
réhabilitation et renforcement sera disponible avant le 31 mars 1988.

PLAN D'ACTION POUR LA DEP
Objectifs
L'objet du Plan d'Action ept de permettre à la D4rection des
Etudes et de la Programmation de remplit prioritairement les fonctions
suivantes:
(a) la supervision financière des organismes autonomeà
(statistique, préparation et exécution des budgets);
(b)   la coordination des investissements dans le secteur des
transports dans le cadre de la recherche de son intégration
optimale dans la politique de développement national;
(c)   l'harmonisation des conditions de concurrence inter-modes.
La prise en charge de ces fonctions se fera progressivement, au fur et à
mesure du renforcement technique des cadres de la DEP qui sera poursuivi
dans le cadre du projet. L'objectif fiscal étant d'amener la DEP à jouer
un r8le de plus en plus actif dans l'élaboration de plans d'investissement
et d'amener des équipes d'économistes dispersées au sein du secteur en vue
d'établir un Plan National de Transport.

sera évalué en fin d'année sur la base de ses performances par rapport à
ses objectifs. Cette évaluation servira de base pour l'attribution d'une
prime répartie de façon homogène entre les agents classés de 1 à 5 selon

Highway Maintenance and Engineering Project (Credit 215-DA)
1.        This first highvay project had three components: (a) a 4-year
highway maintenance program including the reorganization of the Directorate
of Public Works and training of personnel; (b) detailed engineering and
preparation of bidding documents for the reconstruction of about 318km of
highways; and (c) support to the Directorate of Land Transport. A credit
for US$3.5 million vas approved on September 9, 1970, and fully disbursed
at the closing date of December 31, 1974. The project was successful and
the economic rate of return (ERR) calculated after completion vas found to
exceed 42%, compared to the 30% appraisal forecast.
Benin Second Righway Project (Credit 415-DA)
2.        The main objective vas to rehabilitate the Parakou-Malanville
road that is part of the Benin route between the sea coast and landlocked
Niger and to continue the maintenance program. Credit 415-DA vas approved
for US$11.8 million on April 4, 1973 and on March 10, 1976 for an addition-
al US$9.0 million. Disbursement vas 87.5% of total credit at the comple-
tion date of May 15, 1977. The remainder of US$2.6 million vas cancelled.
The objectives vere not totally achieved: the design vidth of the road has
proven to be insufficient for the higher than expected traffie vhich devel-
oped and institution building made little progress. The weighted average
ERR at reevaluation vas 16% compared to the appraisal estimate of 13%.
Third Highway Project (Credit 746-BEN)
3.        The project vas essentially a follow-on of the Second Righway
Project. Main components included: (a) rehabilitation and resurfacing of
575km of bituminous and laterlte roads; (b) strengthening of the ongoing
routine maintenance program; (c) technical assistance to the Ministries of
Public Works and Transport. Credit 746-BEN vas approved on November 8,
1977 for US$10 million and closed on June 30, 1983. On its construction
component, the project yielded a re-estimated ERR of 122% but overall, the
project vas moderately successful. The slow government administration, a
limited absorptive capacity for technical assistance and the poor perform-
ance of a contractor vere constraining factors.

program for rehabilitation of paved and gravel roads; (b) improve the orga-

Bridges; tc) train local staff to maintenance tasks; (d) institute axle-
load control and road safety programs.  Credit 1142-BEN was approved on

Introduction 
The underlying Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) of the emergency works, as 
identified under this project, should be read as a provisional EIA as in this stage of the 
project no (detailed) design information of the emergency works is yet available. This 
provisional EIA however can be used as a starting point or framework for the EIA that needs 
to be carried out and approved by the Liberian environmental authorities before 
commencement of the construction works.  In the remainder of the document the underlying 
provisional EIA will be referred to as “pEIA” (provisional EIA).  
 
Background of the study 
This pEIA was prepared for the Proposed Emergency Works in the framework of the 
“Detailed Assessment of Emergency and Longer Term Actions Required to Re-Establish 
Efficient Port Operations in the Port of Monrovia”. 
 
The proposed emergency works are reviewed and the potential impacts on the environment 
are identified. Mitigation measures required to avoid or to limit negative environmental 
impacts of the emergency works are identified.  
 
This report presents the results of the pEIA of the proposed emergency works. 
 
Purpose of the pEIA 
The purpose of the pEIA is to identify the major issues and impacts associated with the 
emergency works and to identify any measures required to limit any such impacts to 
acceptable levels. The pEIA covers any significant issue of environmental and social 
concern.  
 
At the administrative level, the pEIA furthermore serves various purposes: 
 
·
To allow policy makers to appreciate the extent and significance of any potential impacts 
associated with the proposed works, and take these into consideration into project 
execution considerations; 
·
To provide basic information to the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) of Liberia in 
accordance with legally defined procedures; 
·
To provide clear instructions to works supervisors and contractors with regard to any 
measures that need to be implemented in order to limit any potential negative impacts to 
acceptable levels.

Scope of the pEIA 
Normally, in an pEIA the scope of work is detailed through an environmental scoping 
exercise. During this scoping exercise, the environmental impacts to be reviewed are 
identified and defined. However, since the detailing of the emergency works will be done in a 
later phase by the respective contractors, an accurate scoping exercise (mainly related to 
dredging works) could not be carried out yet. Nevertheless, based on existing information, it 
was possible to provide an pEIA which gives at least the framework for the contractors of the 
emergency works to build upon and will give them guidance in developing their projects, 
taking into account the most relevant environmental concerns.  
 
The scope of this pEIA may be summarized as follows: 
 
·
General assessment of legislative, institutional and policy frameworks for 
environmental and social assessment, management, and reporting applicable to the 
project; 
·
Execution of a reconnaissance-level survey of the locations of the proposed works 
in order to identify any environmental issues that need specific attention, and to 
specify, as required, mitigation measures at specific locations to avoid or minimize 
adverse environmental impacts; 
·
Review of general baseline environmental conditions in the project areas on the basis 
of literature review, expert judgement, and reconnaissance level field visits; 
·
On the basis of proposed emergency works and the reconnaissance level site visits, 
review of the and assessment of their potential negative environmental and social 
impacts;  
·
Formulate generic recommendations to mitigate the adverse environmental and social 
impacts that could arise during construction, operation and decommissioning; 
·
Recommend what should be done for more detailed environmental and social analyses 
for specific subcomponents, as necessary; 
·
Preparation of an Environmental Management Plan providing general direction on pre-
construction, construction, and operation and maintenance measures and practices to 
avoid or minimize adverse environmental effects that can be readily incorporated into 
bid documents  
 
A
public consultation program concerning the emergency works was not conducted due to 
the early stage of the program. In a later phase,the Port Authorities should inform project 
stakeholders about the works to be undertaken, and provide interested parties with the 
opportunity to comment on these work as being developed.

Contracting and implementation arrangements 
It is anticipated that the proposed works will be tendered in individual packages. The 
packages will be tendered internationally, since works may be considered too large for local 
contractors. Moreover, there is no local capacity for some of the works. Implementation on 
the ground will, however, be through local sub (contractors).  
 
Furthermore, it is considered that a supervising engineer will be appointed to guide and 
control all the works. Furthermore, under the general guidance of the NPA, project engineers 
will be assigned to supervise the works on a more day-to-day basis: to ensure that works are 
executed according to technical specifications, schedule and budget, and taking into 
consideration any health, safety, environmental and social (HSES) management measures 
as defined in this pEIA.

General overview 
A
general baseline environmental description of the area is presented in the following

The Port is located in

extends up to 65 km inland with a maximum altitude of 50 m. The area is composed of gently

Reconnaissance level site visit 
A
reconnaissance level site visit has been undertaken to the Port area and any issues of 
specific ecological or socio-economic concern have been noted. As a general summary of 
the analysis it may be concluded that: 
·
The immediate surrounding of the Port is a built-up area with residential and 
industrial use. 
 
·
The locations of the proposed works are mainly of an industrial nature, at some places 
mixed with administrative or housing activities. Land of any natural value does not exist 
in the Port area.
 The area is not known for any flora and fauna of particular national or 
international importance.
 
·
At the north side of the Port living quarters are present and some (recreational and 
fishing) use of the beach is noticed. Scrap is reworked locally. Here is a potential for 
interference with social consequences. However, the emergency works will take place in 
relative small areas not adjacent to places used by local inhabitants.  
·
It is concluded that there is a potential for interference with utility infrastructure (power 
lines, sewerage and drainage system, water pipes, and roads). Soil erosion/dust should 
be considered in dry season and soil wash-away in the wet season. 
·
Surface water sensitivity is not in general an issue as it concerns the Port area itself. 
However, it can be the case during the actual dredging activities (turbidity) and it will 
probably be an issue during disposal activities outside the harbour (land and water).  
·
Groundwater levels are generally around one meter below surface, and below the 
(fire fighting) pipeline depth 
·
At this stage of the programme no information is available on the detailed 
characteristics of the dredge spoil (physical and chemical parameters), dredging 
method and transportation of dredge spoil and the location of the disposal area of the 
dredge spoil. The dredging method should be as such that the rate of resuspension 
is as low as possible. The location of the disposal area should preferably be located 
close to the Port and big enough to store the whole quantity of spoil. Besides that, it 
should be avoided that material flows back into sea (if stored on land) or that material 
becomes resuspended (disposal at sea).

Potential Environmental and Social Impacts 
This section presents an analysis of the potential positive and negative impacts of the 
proposed emergency works. In evaluating the potential impacts of the works, the checklists 
as provided in the Environmental Assessment Sourcebook of the World Bank (Technical 
Paper Number 140, Volume II: Sectoral Guidelines, 1991) were used.  The following sections 
list only the main impacts identified, specifying these as much as possible according to the 
various (infrastructural) developments anticipated. 
 
Anticipated positive impacts 
The emergency works are proposed with the aim to improve port operations and services. 
Currently, the poor quality of operations and services imposes risks to the environment and 
to the health of the Port personnel and in general to the population around the Port. In a 
broader sense it hampers socio-economic development. Improved port operations would 
diminish on the short term environmental threats and reduce health hazards and will 
improve, on the long run, socio-economic development. 
 
In general, therefore, the proposed emergency works would have a substantial positive 
environmental and social impact in that it will improve the conditions in the Port.  
Furthermore, through improving this, this development will also have secondary benefits in 
stimulating social economic development. There is a further opportunity of maximizing 
positive impacts of the development, which is through the involvement of local population in 
unskilled (and where possible skilled) labour involved in the works. The contractor of the 
emergency works should be encouraged to do so.

Analysis of project alternatives 
Identification of alternatives 
Apart from the no-project alternative, two levels of detail in project alternatives can be 
distinguished: 
 
·
Emplacement of major facilities involving considerable land-take. This involves basically 
the dredge spoil dump site, which result in permanent land occupation and health and 
safety issues. 
·
Detailed design of infrastructure, which may entail mainly temporary impacts in terms of 
disturbance and health and safety issues.  
 
The no-project alternative 
Obviously, the potential negative impacts of the works needs to be set out against the 
benefits. The regular way to do such is by comparing the project with a no-project alternative.  
The no-project alternative leaves the port with among others: 
 
·
A
surplus of dredge spoil, 
·
A
tugboat and oil jetty with a lack of maintenance, and 
·
Some unsafe areas/situations in the harbour. 
 
The above state of affairs form treats for the environment and human beings in and around 
the harbour and are therefore not favourable. 
 
The emergency works will have negative environmental aspects, but as argued in Section 0 
the project
’s positive impacts largely surpass its potential negative (short term) implications, 
in that its objective is to improve port operations and services or in a broader sense to 
improve socio-economic development. Improved port operations would diminish on the short 
term environmental threats and reduce health hazards and will improve, on the long run, 
socio-economic development. 
 
In general, therefore, the proposed emergency works would have a substantial positive 
environmental and social impact in that it will: 
 
·
Improve the conditions in the Port.   
·
Stimulate social-economic development in a broader sense.

Environmental management Plan 
The principal purpose of the Environmental Management Plan (EMP) is to present a set of 
mitigation, monitoring, and institutional measures to be taken during planning and design, 
construction, operation and maintenance, and decommissioning of the proposed emergency 
works. The objective of these measures is to eliminate adverse environmental and social 
impacts, offset them, or reduce them to acceptable levels. The plan includes practical actions 
needed to implement these measures, which may largely be readily incorporated into: (a) 
construction specifications and drawings for inclusion in tender documents; and (b) operation 
and maintenance manuals. The Environmental Management Plan (EMP) presents the 
following topics: 
 
·
Summary of impacts
:
The predicted adverse environmental and social impacts for which 
mitigation is required are grouped and summarised. 
·
Mitigation measures
:
Feasible and cost effective measures to reduce potentially 
significant adverse environmental and social impacts to acceptable levels are defined. 
·
Monitoring measures
:
Activities to monitor the effectiveness of the defined mitigation 
measures are defined. These monitoring activities will allow for any additional remedial 
measures to be undertaken if mitigation measures are inadequate or the impacts have 
been underestimated within the pEIA, in particular where the results are not in 
compliance with the obtained permits, national standards and World Bank Group 
requirements and guidelines. 
·
Institutional measures
:
Responsibilities for mitigation and monitoring are clearly defined 
as well as arrangements for co-ordination between the various actors responsible for 
mitigation. Furthermore, training and capacity building requirements are presented.   
·
Implementation schedule
:
The timing, frequency, and duration of mitigation measures 
could not be specified in an implementation schedule since any details of the design are 
available. 
·
Costs estimates and sources of funds
:
For initial investment and recurring expenses for 
implementing of measures contained in the EMP, cost estimates are presented, in order 
to allow for integration into the total project costs.

·

Institutional arrangements 
Stakeholders
For a successful implementation of pEIA recommendations and the EMP, the roles and 
responsibilities of all stakeholders should be well defined and clear to all parties. 
Responsibilities for mitigation and monitoring are clearly defined below as well as 
arrangements for co-ordination between the various actors responsible for mitigation and 
monitoring. The institutions are at different levels comprising: 
1.  International institutions - World Bank as the donor support agency. 
2.  National institutions for planning and policy issues - Ministry of Lands, Mines and 
Energy, Ministry of Transport, Ministry of Planning and Economic Affairs. 
3.  National institutions for regulation and implementation of policies - Environmental 
Protection Agency (EPA), National Port Authorities (NPA),  
4.  Others - Private sector (contractor, consultants), NGOs. 
 
Institutional capacity and training of NPA
It has been identified whether there is sufficient capacity in terms of numbers of employees 
to carry out the EMP and it seems that the NPA does not have sufficient capacity in terms of 
number of environmental staff.  
 
Environmental and social awareness raising is an important element in ensuring effective 
implementation of environmental and social management measures. Training needs of 
relevant NPA staff (to be assigned) and local consultants, who will be involved in the 
emergency works, and more specifically in the execution of the EMP, should be identified. 
The following type of knowledge should be available at NPA: 
 
·
General knowledge on environmental and social management for selected NPA staff 
involved in the emergency works; 
·
Specific HSES knowledge for staff who will undertake regular monitoring of the 
Contractors of the works to ensure compliance with the EMP. 
 
It is recommended that the different training components are organised by a third party who 
is fully acquainted with international environmental management practices and relevant WB 
and EPA guidelines.

Conclusion 
This pEIA identified the major issues and impacts associated with the emergency works 
and proposed any measures (mitigation, monitoring, institutional arrangements) required 
to limit any such impacts. 
 
If these measures are included in the construction specifications and contract for the 
works and if they are properly implemented, then it is expected that all negative impacts 
associated with the emergency works will be minor or As Low As Reasonable Practicable 
(ALARP).

SECTION I - SECTORAL BACKGROUND
t Population and Urbanization
1.1   Mali's population was estimated at 8.5 million in 1990 and is growing at a rate of about
2.5 percent p.a, fuelled by a high fertility rate. Mali's iabor force represents about 32 percent of
the total population. In 1987, 85 percent of the labor force worked in the agricultural sector, 13
percent in services and only two percent in industry.
1.2   The urban population is currently growing at about five percent p.a., i.e., twice as fast
as the country's population. The ratio of urban to total population has increased from 13 percent
in 1965, to 17 percent in 1976, and 19 percent in 1989. Rapid urban population growth is partly
explained by significant rural-urban migration following a severe drought in 1983-85 and, more
generally, the search for better, yet elusive, living conditions in the city. The fact that internal
migrants are generally younger than those who stay behind has somewhat contributed to the
lowering of the average age in urban areas.
1.3   Bamako, the capital city, has experienced a very rapid population growth (almost eight
percent p.a.): its population more than doubled from 400,000 in 1980 to 850,000 in 1990. The
1988 employment survey conducted by the Office No4onai de la Main d'Oeuvre indicated that 5.5
percent of Bamako's population had lived in the city for less than one year. It is estimated that
some 20,000 persons come to Bamako every year in search of employment, a number that far
exceeds the creation of new job opportunities in the city. On the basis of data provided by the
1976 census and the 1988 employment survey, it is estimated that unemployment in Bamako has
doubled from eight percent in 1976 to 16 percent in 1988. In the latter year, unemployment was
greater among women (22 percent) than men (14 percent).
B. Urban Labor Markets and Emplovment
1.4   Serious imbalances exist between labor demand and supply in urban areas:
(a)   the public sector, which used to provide a form of guaranteed employment for
graduates, is being downsized in the context of the adjustment program;
(b)   the formal private sector remains fairly capital-intensive; and
(c)    many of the newcomers entering the urban labor market are unskilled laborers
lacking the training and education for productive employment.
1.5   The urban labor force is currently estimated at 500,000, out of which 100,000 or 20
percent are unemployed. In the midst of these difficulties, the Informal sector constitutes a kind
of safety valve, providing employment to about 280,000 people; this represents about 55 percent
of the urban labor force, and 70 percent of those employed in urban areas. The informal sector
has created jobs, distributed income, transferred technology and satisfied the basic needs of a



-2-
large and growing part of the population. The average age in the informal sector tends to be
lower than in the formal sector, reflecting E higher incidence of apprenticeship and other jobs
geared to the young.
1.6    One of the most n.-teresting characteristics of Bamako's labor market is that even the
formal sector relies heav.ly on temporary empioyment, with a significant number of daily,
seasonal and piece-work jobs. The 1988 employment survey indicated that 35 percent of those
employed have temporary jobs whose duration, in 47 percent of the cases, was less than one
month. Data gathered in 1987 on the hiring practices of private (formal) sector firms in Bamako
confirms this finding: employers indicated they hired almost 16,000 temporary workers for about
370,000 person-days of work, i.e., an average of about 23 days per job. In other words,
temporary employment was equivalent to about 1300 full-time permanent positions, compared to
only 625 persons hired full time by pri;ate (formal) sector firms in the same year. In brief,
temporary employment plays an important role in Bamako's labor market.
1.7    Overall economic difficulties, coupled witlh a rapidly growing urban population, have
placed significant strains on the ability of central and local governments to provide and maintain
publi- infrastructure and services in Mali's cities. In Bamako, for instance, heavy investments in
the -. S 'e system are not well maintained, solid waste collection is vastly inadequate, and urban
l'0o   ic,;s and sidewalks are in a serious state of disrepair. A shortage of public funds has
prev el   le rehabilitation and maintenance of public infrastructure. As a result, there has been
a deterio.4tion in economic productivity and in the quality of life which, in turn, discourages
private investm. ist and employment expansion.
1.8   The adjustmeat prof-am being carried out by the Government has had some inevitable
and highly visible she .-te  , :onsequences on the level of employment, at least in the formal
sector. Firstly, some 36 public enterprises (parastatals) have been rehabilitated, privatized or
liquidated, leading to a net reduction of some 3,700 in the number of employed persons from
1988 to 1992 and further divestiture is necessary. Secondly, as of end-1991, a departure program
had resulted in the voluntary resignation of some 1,000 civil servants, and in the departure of
some 2,500 other government employees who did not pass the civil service exam administered to
them. Finally, the restructuring of the agriculture sector is expected to result in a loss of about
2,000 positions in the Operations de Developpement Rural.
1.9   The Government has paid special attention to the situation of unemployed jeunes
diplOmds, a term encompassing workers having recently finished technical training, technicians
and university graduates. There are about 7,500jeunes dipl6m6s in Bamako alone, with 3,400
new ones being added every year; the market, however, can absorb only 30 to 50 percent of
them. While the public sector hired 1,900 diplOm&s in 1983, only 600 were hired in 1987, and a
iaximum of 250 per annum in more recent years. To reduce unemployment among dipfints,
the Government has launched a number of small employment-creation programs with support
from various donors, including Canada, Germany, the FED, the UNDP, the ILO and local
institutions such as the Banque Nationale de Developpement Agricole (BNDA). It has not been
possible to ascertain the effectiveness of these programs because they are in the early stages of
implementation.

C. The Construction Industry: Performance and Constraints
1.10   A survey of the construction industry was undertaken during project preparation to better
understand its strengths and weaknesses. The survey focused on its constraints, particularly as
regards public sector contracting and contract administration, labor regulatory framework, and
credit availability. The survey aimed to obtain a description of construction firms, including:
(a)    overall financial position (fixed capital, working capital, short- and long-term
debt, taxes owed, and amounts owed by central or local governments);
(b)    managerial aspects (qualifications of management and workers, proportion of cost
overruns and implementation delays in recent contracts, overall orientation of the
enterprise in connection with public works); and
(c)    employment by category (indefinite contract, fixed term contract, daily laborers)
and any hiring/firing practices worth noting.
1.11   The annual volume of cons ruction activities in Mali's formal sector is about
CFAz 7 billion, representing about CFAF 3 billion for building construction and about
CFAF 4 billion for public works. On the demand side, the public sector is predominant, but the
Government's procurement procedures and contract administration practices have traditionally
prevented it from having an effective role vis-a-vis the construction industry. On the supply side,
the construction industry is almost totally dominated by the private sector. There are 60
registered construction firms: the largest ones are able to implement projects over
CFAF 750 million while the smaller ones are able to implement subprojects worth less than
CFAF 50 million. Most firms focus their activities on small- and medium-sized building
construction, drainage works, and road repair. Their financial constraints and lack of heavy
equipment prevent them from executing more sizable public works such as road paving.
1.12   The annual volume of construction in the informal sector is difficult to ascertain, though it
must be quite significant since the bulk of housing in Mali is built and maintained "informally."
The formal and infor-nal sectors do not operate in isolation from each other. On the contrary,
formal firms routinely subcontract part of their work to small enterprises (many of which are
informal) and to tOcherons operating as short-term labor hiring mechanisms. There is a
Federation of Artisans regrouping 80 tacherons in the building and construction trades. They are
distributed throughout the six communes of Bamako. Their skills extend over more than 17
different areas: from daily helpers and masons, to brick makers and framers, to plumbers and
carpenters. Tdcherons are allowed by Government regulations to bid for projects below
CFAF 12.5 million. The Federation of Artisans itself is able to bid for works up to
CFAF 25 million, and has already executed a number of construction and building rehabilitation
works, including the renovation of the Swiss Cooperation office and the rehabilitation of the
Ministry of Labor, which was damaged during the events of March 1991.
D. IDA's Previous Role in the Sector - Lessons Learned
1.13 IDA-supported adjustmnent and investment operations have encouraged the development of
the private sector and, through it, the creation of economically efficient employment. As an
illustration, civil servants taking advantage of the voluntary departure program financed under the
Structural Adjustment Program have received severance payments that could be put to productive



-4 -
use. The reinsertlon program financed under the Public Enterprise Sector Adjustment Project
(Cr. 1937-MUL) has helped some 1,500 civil servants and workers of privatized or liquidated
parastatals create their own small enterprises in food processing, agriculture, transport and
services. Under the Fifth Highway Project (Cr. 1629-MLI), agreement was reached with the
Government that increasingly larger portions of the routine road maintenance program would be
contracted out to the private sector; if adequately dimensioned, relevant contracts could promote
the development of local construction firms. Through the many projects it supports, IDA has
advocated the need to increase the transparency of procurement procedures, and to make them
"friendlier' to small- and medium-sized local firms. Government must recognize that public
demand for works could and should be used as a wdol to attain other socially important goals such
as employment creation and the development of the local construction industry.
1.14   Four projects similar to the one proposed in this report are currently being implemented
in Senegal, Niger, Burkina Faso and Benin. The Public Works and Employment Project in
Senegal (Cr. 2075-SE) has been under implementation for over two years, and the experience
derived from it has facilitated the preparation of more recent projects. Senegal's AGETIP is
currently implementing a total of 110 labor-intensive subprojects whose total value is about CFAF
2.3 billion. It keeps an active (and growing) roster of 100 consulting firms and 400 contractors.
Among the major indicators of AGETIP's satisfactory performance are the following: it has
completed 53 projects; it routinely pays amounts due to contractors in about ten days (which has
already resulted in lower contract unit prices); it has created 433,000 person-days of employment;
and, as an indication of the average labor-intensity of technologies chosen, about 37 percent of
total amounts paid to contractors go .o salaries and wages.
1.15   Lessons Learned from the Senegal AGETIP. Preliminary reflections on the design of the
Senegal Public Works and Employment Project suggested improvements in the following areas:
(a)   In the appraisal of sub-projects, it was suggested that for social projects,
numerical indicators such as population, number of current and potential users of
social infrastructures, tax collection efficiency, etc., be used to rank subprojects.
(b)   In procurement, on the evaluation of the contractor's bids, it was suggested that a
post-qualification bid be introduced to take into account the remaining capacity of
the contractors in contract award as well as provita explanations for bids that are
15 percent lower than the reference price and to have an additional guarantee.
These changes have been reflected in the Agency's Procedures Manual;
(c)    Regarding the disbursement of counterpart funding, experience has shown that
Government has been slow in disbursing its counterpart funding. To address this
issue a strong covenant linking the review of subproject batches to the
disbursement of such funding has been included in the Development Credit
Agreement;
(d)    The training for contractors component should have been designed earlier to
enable participating contractors to have immediate access to training. For this
project, training for contractors will be available upon credit effectiveness;
(e)    A program to increase grassroots participation should have been promoted. This
program has been included in the design of this project; and

E. Rationale for Bank Group Involvement
1.16   Since 1988, the Government of Mali has implemented a sustained adjustment program that
is beginning to bear fruit. Severe imbalances in public finance have been brought under control,
external and internal payment arrears have been cleared, inflation has be, n keplt at a low level,
and real GDP has on average grown over three percent per annum. At *   sne time, incentives
for private sector growth have been improved through easing of restrL   . r gulations,
liberalization, tax and trade reform. A restructuring of public expenditure Is now underway with
the aim of reducing the share of civil service wages in public spending (still over 50 percent of
recurrent expenditures) and thus free funds to provide crucial public services, including public
works and maintenance of basic infrastructure. Despite the progress achieved, persisting
imbalances in the distribution of public expenditure, together with the constraints on the total level
of government spending, imply that GOM has not been able to undertake many urgently needed
public works. The adj !stment program has also not yet resulted in sufficient growth of the
private sector to absorb the large number of unemployed people partially resulting from the
closing of inviable public enterprises and the downsizing of the civil service under the adjustment
program.
1.17   Partly because of the adjustment program, the Government has requested that IDA finance
a program of essential, small-scale public works aimea at rehabilitating and maintaining urban
infrastructure and public facilities. The central government, the District of Bamako and its six
communes have a long wish list of public works that cannot be undertaken, within the existing
constraints on public expenditure. The execution of these works would have a positive long-term
impact on urban productivity and economic growth; and the use of labor-intensive technologies
would contribute in the short term to increased employment levels (particularly in the
construction-related trades). Finally, the project is expected to provide many new opportunities
for the local private sector and, as a result, will support effectively the reforms being carried out
at the macro level.
1.18  IDA is well positioned to provide the assistance sought by the Govermnent because it has
a wealth of knowledge about Mali's urban sector, and has firs;-hand experience with similar
projects in other Sahelian cou F as (Senegal, Niger, Burkina Faso) and in Benin. The project
would be a suitable complement to the Second Urban Project (Cr. 1677-MLI), currently under
implementation.

2.34   While the prequalification criteria do not discriminate against foreign or large firms using
modem equipment, these are not likely to be interested by the type and size of most subprojects
to be executed. Firms interested in submitting bids for labor-intensive public works are likely to
be small- and medium-sized enterprises with a low level of sophistication regarding administrative
procedures and financial management. To encourage the participation of such contractors, the
Procedures Manual contains simplified procurement procedures that do not require the preparati^n
of complex proposals and/or detailed price calculations. The highlights of the standard, simplified
procurement procedures (Annex 4, Section IV-2) are outlined below:
(a)    by means of local advertising, eligible contractors are invited to submit bids for a
given subproject;
(b)    bidding documents contain a draft contract written in simple language which,
among other things, describes the scope and nature of the public works, and

provides an indicative "reference price" with some straightforward explanations
about unit prices and quantities used to compute it;
(c)    bids are open in public session to which the representatives of bidders are invited;
(d)    bids are evaluated by a committee composed of the Agency's Technical Director
and Financial Director and any experts that may be required; the committee's
recommendation is presented to the Agency's Director General for decision; and
(e)   the contract is awarded to the lowest responsive bidder, taking into account any
additional criteria (such as timeliness or labor intensity) spelled out in the bidding
documents; however the bidder may be asked to provide explanations if:
(i)   the overall price is less than 20 percent of the re.'erence price; or
(ii)   the total price is less than 15 percent of the reference price and if the
individual unit prices are less than 25 percent of those used to calculate
the reference price;
In all cases, the bid evaluation committee justifies in writing to the Director
General, in reference to explicit criteria listed in the bidding documents, any

2.39   Since the project seeks to encourage the participation of small- and medium-scale enterprises,
it is crucial that steps be taken to ensure that they will be paid promptly for the work they execute.
Tbis will go a long way towards preventing contractors from going bankrupt. The Agency will
follow payment procedures stipulated in the Procedures Manual. Contractors executing works for
the Agency will be given initial advances in exchange for a guarantee covering 100 percent of the
amount of the advance (which in no case will exceed 20 percent of the contract amount). Payments
due to contractors and consulting firms will be paid by the Agency in cashable form within 30 days
of invoice receipt. The average payment delay will be a key evaluation criterion of the performance
of the Agency. The legal status of the Agency will allow its Director General to order payments,
by signirg checks and transfer orders, without being submitted to numerous clearances and without
being obliged to surrender actual execution of payments to the Treasury. It is worth pointing out that
promptness in effecting payments to contractors has an additional positive effect: lower bid prices.
As an illustration, Senegal's AGETIP has reported that faster payment procedures during the first two
years of operation have already resulted in a 15 percent reduction in unit bid prices.

2.49   The economic justification of some of the subprojects to be executed as part of the proposed
public works program cannot be easily expressed in quantitative terms. In the case of other
subprojects, quantitative information is available but cannot be easily translated into an economic rate
of return. This does not mean that the subprojects are not economically feasible. To gain a better

prepared for four typical subprojects (Annex 10): (a) garbage collection with animal-drawn carts in

and (d) rehabilitation and maintenance of minor roads in Bamako.

2.50   Some of the quantifiable benefits to be derived from the project depend on the specific type
of subproject. They include, among other things:
(a)    reduction in the cost of service delivery, as in the case of labor-intensive garbage
collection (using animal-drawn carts), which would otherwise be carried out using
more capital-intensive technologies (garbage collection trucks);
(b)    avoided coet of reconstruction, in the case of subprojects involving maintenance or
rehabilitation of socially required infrastructure (e.g., drainage network) and/or
buildings (hospitals) that would otherwise deteriorate to the point where full
replacement would be required; and
(c)    reduction in vehicle operating costs, in the case of urban road maintenance or
rehabilitation subprojects.
2.51   3enefits. The project will help central and local governments carry out a programn of urban
infrastructure rehabilitation, and maintenance, thus contributing to increased productivity of urban
areas. It will strengthen the managerial and technical skills of the private sector, especially in
existing small- and medium-sized construction and construction materials firms. It will create direct
and indirect employment as these firms hire laborers and technicians for subproject execution. It will
promote the local engineering and architectural consulting industry, which will be responsible for
technical studies and works supervision. It will demonstrate the possibility of efficient private sector
management in public works execution. It will demonstrate to Government departments the feasibility
of using labor-intensive technologies to execute civil works and physical services. Since the bulk of
project-related expenditures will cover local costs, this project is likely to have a greater multiplier
effect on the local economy than other projects. The project will also contribute to strengthening the
DGMP and to improving the public procurement system in general. The project is further justified
by indirectly securing other development and structural adjustment objectives to the extent that it
contributes to the creation and mairtenance of a favorable climate in which the Government may
continue pursuing its adjustment policies.
2.52   It is difficult to be precise about the volume of employment that will be created by the project
and the program. To obtain an estimate, we assume that:
(a)    on average, about 20 percent of subproject contract amounts will be paid out in
wages (including social security); and
(b)    actual average wages are about CFAF 1,071 per day (including social security); this
very likely overestimates payments received by workers in subcontracting informal
sector micro-enterprises.
On this basis, it is estimated that about 1,120,000 person-days of employment will be created by the
project over four years of project implementation. To the extent that actual average wages are lower
than indicated above, the amount of employment created would be greater.
L. Risks
2.53   There are three major risks associated with this project. The first major risk is that the
volume of employment created by the project may fall short of expectations or be insignificant when



-23 -
compared to the present level of urban unemployment. To reduce this risk, a strong emphasis on
labor-intensive technologies will be maintained throughout project implementation. However, it is
important to realize that Mali's overall unemployment problems cannot be solved by this project
alone, but it will play an important role in helping to improve the present situation. The second
major risk is that the Agency may misuse its considerable independence. This risk is reduced by the
selection of an adequate Director General whose appointment will have to be at all times acceptable
to IDA. In addition, the proposed intensity of supervision and frequent external audits will provide
periodic opportunities to deal with inadequate performance on the part of the Agency or with
Government interference. The third major risk is that local contractors and consulting firms may
lack the capacity required to implement the project on time. This risk is reduced by the fact that the
project has been dimensioned and spread over time to take into account the absorptive capacity of the
industry. In addition, technical assistance and training components included in the project are
expected to increase the capachty of local contractors and consulting firms. However, this risk will
have to be reassessed at the mid-term review.

Terms of Reference
Introdctio
1.    The Public Works and Capacity Building Project includes a program of information
dissemination and conscious raising among the population at large and the beneficiaries of
subprojects in particular. This program is seen as an important tool in (a) improving
communication between the grassroots and the local authorities; and (b) in increasing the
commitment of direct beneficiaries to maintain the civil works or continue the physical
services executed under the subprojects.
2.    The program will contain the following activities:
(a)    a series of sample surveys in selected neighborhoods in each commune of
Bamako before and mid-way through project implementation. These surveys
will help ascertain the priority needs of the local population and will help
identify subprojects;
@b) local meetings, before and after subproject implementation, with the leaders of
beneficiary neighborhoods and representatives of local groups. These
meetings will present the philosophy and modus operandi of AGETIPE and
the objectives of the subprojects and will encourage the support of the
population to maintain or continue the relevant works or services of the
subproject; and
(c)    a multi-media campaign throughout project implementation, which will
emphasize the importance of health and sanitation and the benefits to be
derived from responsible behavior and hygienic practices.
Outpu Reqired
3.    These activities will be outlined in an Action Plan to be prepared by a sociologist
under the supervision of the Director General of the Agency (AGETIPE-MALI). The
principal responsibilities of the sociologist are:
(a)   to organize and prepare the sample surveys. This includes the elaboration of
the sampling method, the identification of the sample; the design of the
questionnaire, the training of survey-takers, and the analysis of survey results;
(O)   to organize the local meetings. The expert will prepare the way for these
meetings by contacting the key leaders and representatives of the selected



- 137 -
ANNEX J-
Page 2 of 3
neighborhoods in order to gain their support. More specifically, he will
define the agendas, identify the speakers, prepare relevant documents to be
distributed, organize any sketches deemed necessary to promote grassroots
involvement, and prepare short minutes of the meetings. If necessary, he will
participate in and stimulate the discussions;
(c)   to elaborate the themes and messages for the media campaign. Given the
popularity of radio broadcasts, the expert will determine the optimal time to
reach the bulk of the population, and prepare concise and simple messages
which address and are sensitive to the concerns of the local population. He
will also make arrangements for periodic TV spots, which will illustrate the
works of the AGETIPE and other major events during project implementation.
He will prepare different notices, highlighting different themes on hygiene,

(d)   to prepare an implementation schedule for the above activities, in keeping with

(e)   to identify and recommend specialized firms, as necessary, to carry out the

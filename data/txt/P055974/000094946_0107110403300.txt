3. Assessment of Development Objective and Design, and of Quality at Entry
3.1 Original Objective:
The original objectives of the project were to:



*   help reduce the loss of human life and a deterioration of living standards that may result from the
floods and/or droughts caused by the 1997-98 El Ninlo event;
*   assist in reducing the loss of or damage to economic and social infrastructure and disruption of
economic activity that may result from this event;
*   assist in the rehabilitation and reconstruction of infrastructure facilities damaged by the El Niio floods;
and
*   help restore the agricultural productive capacity for the next sowing season in areas affected by
drought caused by El Nifio related phenomena.
The objectives of the project were stated clearly. They were aimed specifically to confront natural
disasters. The objectives were responsive to the circumstances the borrower expected based on limited
previous knowledge of the types of impact that could result from an El Nio event. The most recent El
Niuo event had been in 1982-83 that led to a reduction in GDP of 1.5-2.0 percent. Based on this previous
experience, heavy flooding was expected in the eastern lowlands, and higher areas were expected to suffer
from serious drought. The project was designed to support the overall CAS objectives of promoting
economic growth and poverty alleviation by minimizing the disruption caused by El Nifio to economic
activity and by regenerating economic activity in the drought prone areas that have the highest incidence
and degree of poverty in Bolivia.
When the project was appraised in early 1998, some of the expected flooding and most of the drought
conditions were already being observed. A national emergency had been decreed on September 22, 1997.
On October 27, 1997 the Minister of Defense established the Technical Operations Unit for Assistance and
Support (UTOAF) with responsibility to plan, coordinate and supervise the national response to the El
Ninio emergency. On October 25, 2000, the National Council for Disaster Response (CONARADE) was
created; it included the National Civil Defense Service (SENADECI) and a council of the concerned
ministers headed by the Minister of Defense. Although the project was appraised and approved several
months into the state of emergency, SDR2.0 million was provided in the Credit Agreement to finance
prevention works on a retroactive basis.
3.2 Revised Objective:
The project objectives were revised through formal amendments to the Development Credit Agreement as
follows:
*   An amendment in August 1998 reallocated US$6 million of the credit amount to assist in reducing the
loss or darnage to housing and social infrastructure, which resulted from an earthquake on May 22,
1998 in the towns of Aiquile, Mizque, and Totora and surrounding rural areas (near Cochabamba).
* In 1999 a second amendment was agreed upon to reallocate about US$2 million from the credit amount
mainly to reconstruct housing damaged by fires in the Departments of Santa Cruz and Beni.
These revisions of the objectives reflected not only the occurrence of new disasters, but also the availability
of resources in the credit due to the initial implementation delays of prevention and reconstruction works.
Both of the new disasters had affected populations with very high levels of poverty. As measured by
UTOAF the two areas affected by fire, Ascension de Guarayos and Canandoa had poverty levels of 93%
and 85% respectively. This new set of activities was also intended to test the capacity of UTOAF to
respond to disasters after the initial slow start.

3.3 Original Components:
Prevention Works (Intended to be undertaken in preparation for El Nifio -
US$3.0 million): (a) Flood Prevention works; (b) Drought Mitigation Works;
(c) Emergency Goods.
Reconstruction/Reaeneration Program (Specific composition to be determined after the nature of damage
became clear - US$18.8 million): (a) Rehabilitation and Reconstruction of Flood Damaged Public
Infrastructure Facilities; (b) Regeneration of Productive Capacity in the Drought Areas for the Next
Sowing Season (e.g., wells, small irrigation systems, earthen dams, seed procurement).
Institutional Strengthening and Public Information (US$4.0 million):  (a) Project Management and
Supervision; (b) Technical Assistance to Decentralized Implementing Entities in Sub-Project Identification
and Implementation; (c) Improving Medium Term Capacity for Monitoring Local Impact and Response to
the El Nifio Events; (d) Public Information Campaign.
Project Preparation Facility (US$0.5 million)
Unallocated (US$2.0 million)
The original components were structured to meet the objectives. A relatively small amount of funding was
allocated for prevention. The largest allocation of funds was made for reconstruction and regeneration,
which was the main focus of the project.
3.4 Revised Components:
In line with the revised objectives, two new components were added during the project period:
Earthquake Reconstruction: Reconstruction of houses, schools and public facilities to benefit about 750
rural families.
Housing Reconstruction: Reconstruction of houses damaged by fire for about 700 urban households in
Santa Cruz and Beni.
In addition, late in the project period, the government requested that US$250,000 be allocated to initiate
preparation of a long-term system for coordinating disaster risk reduction and mitigation activities.
3.5 Quality at Entry:
Because it was not possible to know the eventual impact of the El Nifio phenomenon, the objectives were
broadly defined and the components were structured to provide assistance in a flexible manner. However,
many of the works which had been intended to prevent damage could not be undertaken before the El Nino
phenomenon, which was already well underway when the project was appraised in March 1998. Credit
effectiveness was not until July of that year.  Therefore the inclusion of El Nifo prevention works in
anticipation of the 1997/98 El Nino event in this project proved to be inappropriate.
The need to establish a strong implementation unit was correctly identified. However, from the time a
national emergency was declared in August 1997 the staffing and attainment of sufficient capacity in
UTOAF progressed slowly (see section 4.5). The initial lack of capacity in UTOAF was expected to be
- 3-



compensated for by hiring a project management firm, but the difficulties and reluctance of government to
do so may have been underestimated. In addition, the capacity of the implementing agencies at the
departmental and municipal levels to prepare and implement reconstruction projects may have been
overestimated.
4. Achievement of Objective and Outputs
4.1 Outcome/achievement of objective:
* The objective of reducing the loss of human life and a deterioration of living standards was partially
met since only limited prevention works were carried out before the El Niio event.   However, a
significant set of "preventive" works was also carried out during the rehabilitation/reconstruction
phase, many of which have already proven to be beneficial in reducing the impact of annual flooding.
*   For the reasons stated above, the objective of reducing the loss of or damage to economic and social
infrastructure and disruption activity that may have resulted from the event was also partially met.
* The objective of assisting in the rehabilitation and reconstruction of infrastructure facilities damaged
by the El Nifo floods was partially achieved.
* The objective of helping to restore the agricultural capacity in areas affected by drought was achieved
in part.
*   The revised objectives of reconstructing and rehabilitating housing and social infrastructure damaged
by earthquakes and fires were successfully achieved.
4.2 Outputs by components:
Prevention Works and Emergencv Goods
Because the El Nifno event came earlier than expected, limited prevention works were carried out before
credit effectiveness. However, by the end of the project, US$5.9 million worth of "preventive" works had
been undertaken, of which US$2.7 million were financed by the IDA credit. Flood related works included
the irnprovement of 28 rural roads, 27 irrigation canals, 12 bridges, 21 river defenses and 11 retaining
walls. To mitigate drought impacts the project also financed 319 small reservoirs, 179 surface wells, 30
small water supply systems, 50 small irrigation systems, 59 water tanks and 48 dry latrines. The drought
mitigation and seed distribution (see below) were targeted to address the needs of rural populations in three
of Bolivia's poorest departments, Potosi, Oruro and Chuquisaca.
Reconstruction/Regeneration Program:
Procurement and Distribution of Seeds: Government designed the details of this component after
appraisal. It devised a program for the distribution of 6,500 tons of potato seeds in 12 key potato growing
regions in the drought affected regions, and US$2.6 million of the IDA credit was allocated to finance this
through the National Seed Council, PROSEMPA. The seeds component was appraised in detail in May
1998, and immediate funding was provided from the PPF and government bridge financing. Delays ensued
almost immediately due to bureaucratic complications, funding delays and lack of demand. These delays
were critical, given the need to sow the next crop. By August 1998 only 1,300 tons had been distributed.
By the end of 1998, only 2,100 tons had been distributed, and work under the component was ended by
early 1999 with less than half of the allocated amount spent.
Flood and Drought Reconstruction Components: Flood reconstruction included the repair of damaged
river defenses, bridges and roads. Drought reconstruction included the repair of irrigation and water
-4 -



supply systems, mainly in the highland regions. Delays on these components representing 60% of IDA
resources in the project became serious in early 1999 when only 20% had been approved by the Ministry of
Finance and payment processing was delaying implementation. In mid 1999 progress in meeting the
development objectives and in implementation were judged to be unsatisfactory. However, implementation
accelerated soon afterwards. Funds were reallocated to new components in March 2000 and most of the
remaining flood and drought reconstruction funds were used.
Earthquake Reconstruction:
This component was appraised in May 1998. The Social Fund (FIS) was made the implementing agency,
and an NGO was also involved in the community participation aspects. After initial difficulties,
implementation went well. By the end of the project, 980 rural houses (100% of the total estimated
damaged houses) had been constructed with IDA financing. In addition, 15 schools, 7 public buildings
and 2 hospitals were constructed, and two water systems were rehabilitated. Houses and public buildings
were reconstructed using earthquake resistant materials and all works are of good quality.
Housing Reconstruction:
This component was added in August 1999 after fires in Beni and Santa Cruz destroyed housing and it was
clear there would be a large shortfall in the drought component. The Social Fund also worked as
implementing agency for this component, and an NGO was involved to provide on-the-job construction
skill training. By the end of the project all 469 houses originally destroyed by fire had been reconstructed
in Ascension de Guarayos in the Department of Santa Cruz and 30 in San Ignacio de Moxos in Beni.
Houses were constructed with tile roofs that are less vulnerable to any future fires. A school and a public
building were also constructed in Ascension de Guarayos.
Strengthening of Executing Agencies
After some delay, consultants were provided to municipalities and departments to prepare sub-projects, to
prepare and evaluate procurement documents and to supervise works. This strengthened capacity at the
local level and improved sub-project implementation later in the project period. About US$400,000 were
used for this purpose.
Institutional Strengthening of UTOAF
This component was originally intended to support UTOAF with project preparation and implementation,
to finance a public information campaign and to improve the medium term capacity for monitoring and
responding to El Nifio events. Immediate progress was slow because of initial lack of capacity within
UTOAF and failure of UTOAF to hire a management firm as previously agreed. After a change of upper
management in 1999 the situation within UTOAF improved markedly (see section 4.5).
Long-term Disaster Prevention and Mitigation
US$250,000 was allocated from the IDA credit and US$50,000 was made available from UNDP for this
purpose. By mid-March 2001, US$210,000 had been spent from the IDA credit. UNDP financing was to
continue to support this activity after the March 30, 2001 closing date. UTOAF promoted the organization
and training of a national network (scientists, universities, meteorologists, local governments, etc.) to work
in disaster prevention and mitigation. Pilot programs were also undertaken to establish risk management
units in one city and one department. A web site has been established, the vulnerability of La Paz to
-5-



disasters has been assessed and training materials have been prepared.  However, the long-term
continuation of this work is uncertain (see section 4.5).
Despite early delays in prevention works, there was a significant improvement in management and
implementation as the progressed. On balance, the outcome is considered satisfactory.
4.3 Net Present Value/Economic rate of return:
Economic analysis per se was not undertaken for prevention and reconstruction works because of the
emergency nature of the project and the small scale of most of the investments. However, UTOAF carried
out thorough damage assessments and basic economic evaluation of sub-projects.
4.4 Financial rate of return:
Given the emergency nature and size of the sub-projects and the lack of sub-lending or other financial
criteria, financial rates of return were not calculated.
4.5 Institutional development impact:
From the beginning it was necessary to develop the operational capacity of UTOAF in order to implement
the project.  During the early stages of the project from October 1997 to July 1999 institutional
development was slow. Some staff and technical consultants were recruited under the PPF, but the
financial management system of UTOAF was developed slowly. A project management firm was supposed
to be hired, but bids were considered inadequate. In the end, because of the need to avoid further delay,
IDA agreed to UTOAF's recommendation to hire individual consultants. During the initial phase from
October 1997 to July 1999, implementation progress and project management were rated as unsatisfactory.
In mid 1999, inadequate management by UTOAF was noted as a major reason for delays in the drought
and flood reconstruction components that accounted for about 60% of the credit. At that time, the new
Minister of Defense changed UTOAF management. Management improved rapidly and capacity was
created within UTOAF to improve performance. The delays were one reason for the acceptance of a new
component in late 1999 for the reconstruction of housing damaged by fire, since there was urgency to
commit the project funds in order to finish works before the December 31, 2000 closing date.
Institutional development was not among the original objectives of the project. However, by the end of the
project significant progress had been made towards the development of a national capacity to respond to
natural disasters. UTOAF now has the capacity to assess damages and coordinate a response to natural
disasters. Procedures have been successfully implemented for assessing and prioritizing needs, preparing
projects, transferring funds and supervising execution. A law has been approved by Congress for disaster
management and is being implemented. In spite of this progress, there is uncertainty regarding the further
evolution of institutional capacity (see section 6.1).
5. Major Factors Affecting Implementation and Outcome
5.1 Factors outside the control of government or implementing agency:
There were social disturbances in September 2000, which resulted in delays in implementation and required
a three-month extension of the closing date.
5.2 Factors generally subject to government control:
- 6 -



Initially the government believed that the worst of the El Nifio phenomenon would be felt late in the rainy
season in April or May 1998, but it came earlier, and little contingency planning beyond the establishment
of UTOAF had been made. Funds were not included in the budget for that year which also contributed to
delays.
Most sub-projects were implemented in a decentralized manner at the local and departmental level where
capacity was weak and administrations and staffing changed frequently. UTOAF made efforts to acquaint
these levels of government with project procedures and to train personnel.  However, institutional
deficiencies, especially in Oruro and Potosi, continued to impede progress. Initial reluctance by the
Ministry of Defense to involve NGOs could also have impeded early implementation. There were also long
delays in contractor payments because of shortages of counterpart funds and cumbersome procedures not
appropriate for responding to an emergency situation.
5.3 Factors generally subject to implementing agency control:
At the beginning of the project, progress in implementation was severely constrained by deficiencies in the
management and staffing of UTOAF which could have been remedied by hiring a management consulting
firm as originally agreed. This delay was especially critical, given the rapid response required (see section
4.5).
5.4 Costs andfinancing:
The project consisted of prevention works, five types of reconstruction/regeneration works and three
operational categories with an initial cost estimate of US$28.3 million, US$25 million of which was
financed by the IDA credit (see Annex 2). Parallel financing from Japan, the EU and UNDP brought the
initial total cost of the entire program to US$35.91 million. By the end of the project the government had
increased its counterpart contribution from US$3.3 million to US$8.7 million, bringing the total cost of the
IDA financed project to US$33.7 million.
6. Sustainability
6.1 Rationale for sustainability rating:
Currently there is no clear provision for continued budgetary or institutional support to maintain
rehabilitated infrastructure. Inadequate maintenance has been a key issue in infrastructure projects in
Bolivia in the past and is likely to affect investments made under this project. This issue was recognized at
appraisal and it was assumed that it would be addressed through other projects in the sector. Sustainability
of public works under these circumstances is questionable.
Since the beneficiaries have a direct incentive and involvement in the maintenance of housing and social
infrastructure, the maintenance of these investments is more likely.
Building institutional capacity for disaster prevention and mitigation was not among the project's
development objectives, but good management by UTOAF during the latter part of the project produced a
positive institutional development impact. UTOAF is asking Government for an allocation to continue
operating for at least six months. Given Bolivia's vulnerability to natural disasters, it would be unfortunate
not to preserve at least some of this capacity for the future. However, current budget limitations and the
lack of a clear public policy for structuring and financing disaster management are likely to erode the
progress made under the project to develop the capacity to coordinate prevention and rehabilitation works.
- 7 -



Overall sustainability is unlikely.
6.2 Transition arrangement to regular operations:
Public works financed under the project have been transferred to the concerned ministries and local
governments. Housing units have become the property of their occupants and tenure has been regularized.
Schools financed in the earthquake area have been transferred to the relevant authorities and are
functioning. In one case, a large hospital in Totora has not yet received the equipment and staffing required
for its operation. At least a ten-month delay is foreseen before the complex can be operational. Public
works have become the responsibilities of ministries and local governments. It is possible that because of
budget constraints some of the works implemented under the project will not be maintained over time.
7. Bank and Borrower Performance
Bank
7.1 Lending:
There was one identification/preparation mission with two economists, an agricultural expert, an engineer
and a procurement specialist. Although the project was prepared quickly, it was designed with a similar
structure and components as in neighboring countries with lirnited consideration of the particular events
unfolding in Bolivia. In particular, the prevention phase came too late because the El Nifio event had
already occurred. Not withstanding the above, given the urgency and staffing constraints, lending is
considered marginally satisfactory.
7.2 Supervision:
Supervision by IDA was flexible and responsive to changing environment and institutional needs. There
were seven supervision missions during the two year implementation period. The initial mission had six
members including economists, an agriculture specialist, an engineer and a financial specialist. Frequency
and quality of supervision is rated satisfactory.
7.3 Overall Bank performance:
Given the rapid response, flexible design in dealing with an event which had unpredictable impacts, and the
accommodation of new emergency needs into the project when they were apparent, overall IDA
performance is rated as satisfactory.
Borrower
7.4 Preparation:
Government recognized a pending disaster and declared a national emergency on September 22 1997. It
created UTOAF and asked it to prepare an emergency plan and seek emergency assistance. Government
worked closely with IDA during preparation.  Government approvals and the need to establish sound
financial systems in UTOAF delayed initial progress. However, on balance borrower performance during
preparation is rated satisfactory.
7.5 Government implementation performance:
There were initial delays in approval by Congress and in signing the subsidiary agreement between the
-8 -



Ministry of Hacienda and the Ministry of National Defense.  During the early stages, the government
provided only US$0.4 million of the US$2.4 million it had promised in bridging funds which especially
affected the timely implementation of the seeds component. In the later years govemrnent support for the
project improved. Government supported improvements in the management of UTOAF which was critical
to the project's relative success during this latter period. On balance, therefore, government implementation
performance is rated satisfactory.
7.6 Implementing Agency:
Despite initial delays and poor performance at the beginning of the project (see section 4.5), sufficient
capacity was built within UTOAF to enable it to successfully implement the project.
Implementation was carried out in a decentralized manner through departmental and municipal
governments. At these levels institutional capacity was limited, especially at the municipal level,
throughout the project period. These constraints were addressed during the later years of the project
through the use of project preparation and supervision consultants and NGOs.
Overall implementing agency performance is rated satisfactory.
7.7 Overall Borrower performance:
Although there were problems with project management and delays at the beginning of the project, these
were largely overcome later in the project period. Overall borrower performance is therefore rated
satisfactory.
8. Lessons Learned
e   It is important for government to maintain a core capacity for prevention, disaster response and
mitigation. However, long term funding for this purpose is likely to be limited. Therefore, permanent
disaster management capacity should consist only of the highest priority skills (e.g. coordination and
management of multi-sector activities, risk assessment and public sector management) and fit within a
realistic and sustainable budget envelope. As natural disasters arise, specialized consultant services
can be contracted as needed.
* Prevention measures can have a substantial impact on the reduction of social and economic loss.
Countries with a history of El Nifio type phenomena should undertake contingency planning and
preventive measures, including routine maintenance. Many of these types of investments should be
incorporated into normal sector investment programs during non-emergency periods.
* Natural disasters and their impacts vary widely in nature from country to country and cannot be
addressed adequately with standardized approaches. Each requires an individualized design and
supervision (staffing and budget) tailored to the types of components included.
* In countries with decentralized government structures such as Bolivia, it is necessary to strike a
balance between the responsibilities of local government and the need for centralized disaster
forecasting, financing and coordination. Flexibility is required at the implementation level to reflect
differences in the capacities and resources of local and regional governments.
-9-



*   -Specialized NGOs can play a positive role in the implementation of small-scale projects at the local
level. However, provision should be made for training NGOs in project implementation and
procurement under emergency donor financed projects.

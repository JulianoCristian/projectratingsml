1. Project Context, Development Objectives and Design

1.1 Context at Appraisal
The situation of Djibouti at appraisal was seriously compromised. First, the country was recovering from
the domestic armed conflict between the Government and the opposition party, the Front pour la
Restauration de l'Unité et de la Démocratie (FRUD). The war lasted four years, from 1991 to 1994, and
had devastating effects in the country's economy and infrastructure. Since the peace accord which ended
the civil strife, reconstruction and development had been under way but at a slow pace. The social
reintegration of the demobilized soldiers and their socio-economic reinsertion were difficult. Social
infrastructures, such as hospitals, dispensaries, schools and water points, had to be reconstructed so that
the displaced population could be resettled. Multiple development activities needed to be initiated in
support of primary health care, primary schools and income generation. Second, a sharp inflow of an
estimated 120,000 refugees from Somalia and Ethiopia, went to Djibouti once the peace accords between
the Government and the FRUD were signed. These refugees had placed an enormous strain on the meagre
resources available and on the already fragile system of service provision in Djibouti. Third, the French
military presence had decreased after the war, and this had a negative effect on Djibouti's service based
economy. Finally, foreign aid, which was crucial to support the country's balance of payments and to
finance development projects, had declined sharply. Direct budgetary transfers, which had been in the
order of $50-60 million per year, fell to $43 million in 1993, $24 million in 1994, and $27 million in 1995.

The economic and social conditions of the country were appalling. Although GDP per capita was
estimated at $780, this indicator was distorted by high costs of living, due to high levels of imports, and
the existence of a large and well paid group of civil servants. The incidence of poverty was estimated at
45%, with a large share of the poor (52%) living in Djibouti-city. Given that the population in the country
was about 640,000, the number of poor was thus estimated to be 288,000 and of these 150,000 living in
Djibouti-city. The unemployment rate was about 45% for the entire country and 60% for the poor
population Sanitary conditions were well below acceptable standards, with only 33% of Djiboutians
having access to potable water and using ineffective sanitation services.

Life expectancy at birth was very low at 49 years. The infant mortality rate, at 114 deaths per 1,000 live
births, was one of the highest among Middle Eastern and Sub-Saharan countries. Diarrhea and
malnutrition constituted the leading cause of death among children under 5 years of age. A high
prevalence of HIV and a large consumption of khat, a drug imported from Ethiopia, posed enormous
health, social, and economic problems. The illiteracy rate was at 57% for the entire population and 64%
for the poor. The post-conflict situation, the severe poverty level, the abysmal social indicators, and the
absence of natural resources, gave Djibouti a status of a fragile country at the time of project preparation.

The Government was in the process of creating the National Executing Agency for Public Works
(ADETIP), a delegated contract manager for the various ministries in Djibouti. The agency, which
combined the infrastructure procurement functions of an agency for public works (AGETIP1) with the
community participation approach of a social fund, was considered the ideal mechanism to speed up
infrastructure provision in a post-war situation.

Rationale for Bank assistance. The deterioration of Djibouti's economic and financial situation became
untenable by 1995 when the Government had exhausted its access to Djibouti's National Bank (BND)
and operations and development expenditures had been cut to levels below which the functioning of the
administration was seriously compromised. The Government then entered into discussions with the IMF
and the World Bank regarding a structural adjustment program. In 1996 the GoD signed a stand-by
arrangement with the IMF, with the objective of stabilizing the fiscal situation. In 1997 the Bank
developed a poverty assessment and signed a technical assistance agreement to support economic reform.
These activities, aimed at assessing the social situation of the country and to provide economic advice,
marked a reengagement with Djibouti that had been interrupted during the civil war.

Subsequently the Bank approved two projects: The Ex-Combatants Reintegration project and the Social
Development and Public Works project (PDSTP). Both projects addressed pressing needs of post-war
Djibouti. The PDSTP supported the construction of most needed infrastructure in the poorest
neighborhoods of Djibouti-city, the strengthening of community participation in those neighborhoods,
and the development of poverty monitoring tools, all very relevant activities at the time.

1.2 Original Project Development Objectives (PDO) and Key Indicators (as approved)
The project development objective is to enhance the living standards of the poor in the city of Djibouti by
increasing the quality and access to basic economic and social infrastructure and services and by
generating employment opportunities. The project is expected to achieve this objective through
approaches that foster labor-intensive techniques, community participation, and the development of small
local entrepreneurs in the construction sector.

The key performance indicators included in the main text of the PAD were:

    1) Percentage of subprojects included in community development plans in target zones, in relation to
    total number of subprojects financed by ADETIP (excluding cultural heritage subprojects).

    2) Percentage of rehabilitated or constructed infrastructure operational and routinely maintained.

    3) Number of person-month of work created.

    4) Improved capacity of private construction and engineering firms that have been awarded contracts,
    as measured by the progression in the volume of activities, number of permanent staff and
    investments in equipment.

In addition, the following indicators were included in Annex 1 of the PAD:

    5) Percentage of poor population benefiting from the project.

    6) Population access to, use of, and satisfaction with infrastructure constructed or rehabilitated.

    7) Number of community development plans prepared among the 19 target neighborhoods.

    8) Updating and dissemination of poverty statistics.

1.4 Main Beneficiaries
The direct beneficiaries were poor residents of Djibouti-city, living in target neighborhoods. The expected
benefits were categorized as: (i) social benefits deriving from improved access to infrastructure and
development of social capital; and (ii) economic benefits resulting from employment generation and
reduced transaction and opportunity costs associated with difficult access to services.

The following organizations were direct beneficiaries of institutional strengthening activities: the
executing agency, ADETIP, which needed to be created and made operational, and the National
Statistical Bureau (originally labeled DINAS and later on DISED), which was to be strengthened in its
capacity to gather and analyze poverty data.

Indirect beneficiaries included NGOs participating in community strengthening activities and small
infrastructure contractors which were the object of capacity building activities.

1.5 Original Components
The project included three components, social and economic infrastructure, support to community
development, and institutional capacity building.

The first component aimed at improving the physical environment in poor neighborhoods of Djibouti city.
The component was further divided in four subcomponents depending on the type of infrastructure to
rehabilitate or construct: streets and drainage, commercial infrastructure, social infrastructure, and public
buildings forming part of the city's cultural heritage. To maximize the impact of civil works on
employment generation and local private sector development, the design and construction of works was
supposed to be carried out by small firms using labor intensive techniques.

The second component was designed to complement activities under the first component and maximize
sustainability through increasing community participation in project implementation, and the provision of
selected social services to targeted communities. The component was divided in two subcomponents, one
to finance the preparation of community development plans (CDPs), and the other one to implement
projects proposed by communities under their CDPs and that fell into either one of the following
categories: basic social and economic infrastructure, management of infrastructure, and management and
organization of basic services.

The third component was designed to develop poverty monitoring competences and to strengthen
institutional capacity among the stakeholders to implement the project. The first subcomponent was
intended to finance national and local household surveys, the preparation of a poverty map for Djibouti
city, and institutional strengthening for poverty monitoring. The second subcomponent financed training
and equipment for ADETIP, DISED, NGOs, and other key Government officials involved in poverty
reduction programs.

1.6 Revised Components
The project components remained the same throughout project implementation.

1.7 Other significant changes
In mid 2003 the Bank approved an additional financing of SDR 3.7 million intended to increase the
coverage of the infrastructure investments from 11 to over 20 neighborhoods. The additional amount was
completely allocated to component 1, because it was the most advanced in implementation and
disbursements, whereas the social development component was still lagging behind. The rationale to
grant the additional financing was that the project ratings had been consistently satisfactory and ADETIP
seemed to be making significant progress in its consolidation. With the additional financing the total
credit was of SDR14.7 million.

2. Key Factors Affecting Implementation and Outcomes

2.1 Project Preparation, Design and Quality at Entry
The project was prepared in a 2-year period. The project was identified in June 1997, appraised in March
1999, and approved in May 1999. The project was prepared in the Human Development Unit of the
Africa Region. The credit amount was originally SDR 11.0 million.

Given that this project was one of two to be prepared after the civil war, it did not count on much existing
background documentation. The only current Bank document available was the 1997 Poverty Assessment
(PA). The PA developed a poverty reduction strategy, endorsed by the Government, and including the
following pillars: (i) raising growth and generating employment; (ii) increasing investment in human
capital; and (iii) developing targeted interventions for the poor. The project design incorporated elements
of all three pillars by addressing the needs in infrastructure of the very poor of Djibouti-city, where a
large percentage of the country's poor lived, including a social development component to increase
community participation in the provision of infrastructure.

The value added of Bank support derived from its experience with AGETIPs and social funds in the
Africa region, mechanisms that had proven to be efficient in the provision of infrastructure to the most
vulnerable groups, particularly in emergency and post conflict situations as it was the case in Djibouti.
The main lessons incorporated in project design were related to this experience. A Djiboutian delegation
visited projects in Senegal and Mauritania to understand the advantages and disadvantages of each
approach. The Government then decided to create an independent agency that would carry out
procurement and financial management activities associated with infrastructure provision, on behalf of
line ministries, like an AGETIP, with the strong community participation approach of a social fund. It
was decided that the social development component would be executed by ADETIP, in consultation with
the beneficiary communities. The government's participation in the study tour as well as the creation of
ADETIP, were clear proof of the government's commitment to the project.

The lessons learned with the AGETIP experience called for simple, flexible and transparent procurement
and disbursement methods in ADETIP. In that sense an operational manual was prepared and an
international firm was hired to develop a comprehensive information system. Regarding the risk of low
sustainability of infrastructure investments, usually associated with AGETIPs, the project design included,
for the first time in Djibouti, a strong community participation approach. It was expected that, by
increasing the involvement of the beneficiary communities in project selection and the financing of
complementary social development activities, there would be greater ownership of the project and
therefore increased care of the infrastructure. No mechanisms were established to ensure routine
maintenance from the corresponding line ministries and government agencies for which the infrastructure
was to be built. Finally, even though the PAD mentions that lessons from the implementation of an urban
development and an education project were incorporated, there is no specific discussion of these.

The project development objectives were clear and responded well to the needs of the country at the
moment, as well as to the current poverty assessment recommendations. Some of the targets to measure
the PDOs were, on the other hand, too ambitious, as discussed in section 3.1. The components were
straightforward, complementary of one another, and clearly linked to the development objectives. There
was clear commitment of the government to undertake the necessary steps to finalize ADETIP's creation.

Even though the project design was clear and practical, it failed to acknowledge the possible
complications deriving from: (i) the fact that ADETIP was a new agency where organizational structures,
procedures, and expertise needed to be developed from scratch; (ii) there was no experience in the
country with delegated contract management for the construction of public works, the approach proposed
for ADETIP, and therefore no clear indication that a certain level of demand for the agency's services was

going to exist; (iii) there was no experience in the country with community participatory approaches; and
(iv) there were practically no consultant firms to carry out preparatory studies, infrastructure designs,
infrastructure works, and supervision of works . In that sense, the overall moderate assessment of risk and
the 4-year project implementation period, envisaged in the PAD, were inappropriate.

In addition, the PAD did not include any analysis and discussion of usually required studies for
infrastructure projects, particularly the environmental and social assessments. On the environmental side
the project was categorized B, thus requiring an environmental assessment, but there was no
corresponding annex in the PAD. Therefore, there was no discussion of the institutional and legal
background for environmental impact evaluation in Djibouti, the capacity of ADETIP to undertake such
responsibility, and possible training necessary to build capacity in this area. Nevertheless, a simplified
guide for the preparation of environmental impact studies, including a classification of projects, a
methodology for impact evaluation, and draft terms of reference, was incorporated in the project
operational manual. This guide was an adaptation of the environmental guides of AGETIPs.

Regarding social aspects, the project design relied on the Poverty Assessment dated 1997, which shed
light on the profiles and priorities of the poor in the city but not on the specific profiles of the various
neighborhoods to intervene, existence of community organizations, possible problems that could be
encountered. A diagnostic of community participation in Djibouti as well as a study on NGOs are listed in
the PAD's annex that contains the list of documents in the project file, but no analysis of these documents
was included. The PAD also mentions that there had been pilot consultation workshops with local
residents, entrepreneurs, associations, and authorities in three pilot neighborhoods. Unfortunately the
analysis of this important pilot was not included in the PAD, apparently because it had not been fully
implemented at the time of appraisal. Likewise, there was no specific study to determine how the
employment generation aspect of the project was to be developed.

Quality at entry rating by QAG
QAG reviewed the project's quality at entry in 1999 and rated it marginal (3 rating). The main aspects
that were criticized by the panel were: (i) participatory pilot projects were not completed during
preparation and the community and institutional assessment was conducted after the project preparation
was concluded; (ii) inadequate arrangements for monitoring and evaluation of social impacts; (iii)
financial management arrangements, the project implementation plan and the first year program were not
ready by Board presentation; (iv) the PAD did not include sufficient information on institutional
arrangements; and (v) risks were underestimated. The QAG review concluded that the operation was too
large for Djibouti's technical capacity and size and that the existing pilot was not fully implemented and
thus did not provide insightful lessons that were much needed given the novelty of the participatory
approach. In addition the institutional framework was considered complex, with several ministries
intervening and a brand new agency selected to execute the project. The panel felt that the project was not
ready for implementation and that the expectations regarding its impact and time frame were unrealistic.

2.2 Implementation
The project was prepared by the Human Development Unit of the Africa Region. Following a request
from the Government to move its entire portfolio under the responsibility of the MENA Region, oversight
of the project was assigned to the Yemen country office, at the end of 1999, promptly after board
approval. The Yemen's resident representative was the task team leader (TTL) for two years from
December 1999 until around November 2001. During that time the project did not fall under the

In 2002 there was a period of transition with a new Yemen resident representative taking over the project

After the 2004 QAG supervision review, a new supervision team was put in place and its first mission

team, adequate budgets, more detailed PSRs/ISRs, and thorough comments from management. This more
detailed supervision resulted, for the first time, in the downgrading of project performance ratings, which
was adequately used to provide feedback to the government, discuss problems and look for solutions. The
most immediate government remedial actions had to do with procurement and financial management
procedures. The change of ADETIP's community participation expert, which had happened before the
QAG assessment took place, and the agreement of the Government to increase the allocation of the
community development component as proposed by the new TTL, were clear signs of the Government's
renewed commitment to improving this important aspect of the project. These changes allowed significant
improvement of project implementation. The poverty monitoring and analysis subcomponent continued to
experience problems. The Bank team decided to adopt a hands-on approach and contribute the time of an
international consultant who worked with counterparts in DISED and produced the poverty map.

The project ratings improved steadily in its last years of implementation. The last ISR, corresponding to a
May 2007 mission has satisfactory ratings for the PDO, implementation progress, counterpart funding,
monitoring and evaluation, and the three components. Moreover, the support to community development
component was rated highly satisfactory. Issues related to financial management and procurement
(discussed in 2.4.) explain moderately satisfactory ratings in these aspects, in the last ISR, but without
affecting the completion of activities. This outcome is the result of perseverance of both the client's
implementing team and the Bank's supervision team.

By and large the project was well implemented as evidenced by the completion of all the tasks, the
achievement of most of the project indicator targets, the positive ISR ratings (please refer to data sheet for
more details on indicators and ratings), and the consolidation of ADETIP as a public works and social
development agency. All the completed infrastructure works under the PDSTP have been officially
handed over to the corresponding ministries and the disbursement rate reached 99% of the total amount
(as of January 30, 2008).

The main problem of project implementation was its pace. The project took approximately two additional
years to complete with respect to the date that was envisaged at the time of approval of the supplemental

2.3 Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) Design, Implementation and Utilization
A total of 8 project indicators were identified in Annex 1 of the PAD. These are presented in the data

2.4 Safeguard and Fiduciary Compliance
According to the PAD, the project only triggered the environmental assessment safeguard. From
December 2002 onwards, the cultural property safeguard is also listed as triggered. The reason for this
change is related to the start of works to rehabilitate public buildings forming part of the city's cultural
heritage. In addition, a plan to safeguard the city's cultural assets was developed through the institutional
capacity component. Compliance with these safeguards has been consistently rated as satisfactory in
ISRs, but no explanation has been included in these reports. However, the aide-memoires contain some
comments regarding the cultural heritage activities.

The fiduciary aspects of the project, financial management and procurement, were the two aspects most
intensely supervised in the project, with specialists in these fields participating in almost all supervision

conciliation of the project's accounting information and the use of project funds by categories, the
systematic control of receipts, the management of the special account, the improvement of the quality of
the audit, the review of the interim financial reporting, the examination of statements of expenses, and the
preparation of periodic disbursement plans. The fact that some specific issues were raised repeatedly in
various aide memoires speaks to the remaining weaknesses in ADETIP's financial management capacity.

In terms of procurement, the aide memoires report minor problems identified either by ex-post reviews or
technical audits whose recommendations were subject to follow up by Bank's supervision missions. As in

2.5 Post-completion Operation/Next Phase
There are two issues to analyze regarding the sustainability of PDSTP. First is ADETIP's transformation
into a new agency following the Government's new poverty reduction strategy, and second are the new
operations under preparation.

Under the Government's new strategy for poverty reduction (Cadre Stratégique de Réduction de la
Pauvreté), adopted in May 2004, a new agency is to be created, combining ADETIP and the Social Fund
(SF). The SF, which has been financed in part by the African Development Bank (AfDB), has supported
social development activities in poor areas of Djibouti, but using a different modality of intervention than
ADETIP's, involving micro-credit. The objective of the fusion is to strengthen social development
activities in the country, avoid overlaps, and encourage micro-credit. After major discussions, some of
them with the various donors working in Djibouti, the Government decided to go ahead with the fusion.
The law creating the new agency was approved in parliament on November 27, 2007. The decree to start
making operational the new agency, the Djiboutian Agency for Social Development (ADDS), was
approved in January 2008. The Government has expressed its interest to maintain ADETIP's basic
structure, functions, and procedures intact. The selection of the former director of ADETIP as the interim
director of ADDS, through presidential decree, is a reassuring step in this direction.

A follow-up project is under preparation, with expected Board date in April 2008. The project would
improve the infrastructure of a large neighborhood in Djibouti-city, Quartier 7, one of the poorest, most
populous, and least served by public services. Community participation would be a central element from
the beginning of the project, with consultations on the needs of its residents and possible solutions,
continuing into project execution with oversight of works and community organization for the proper use
and maintenance of the new facilities. The works envisaged would include paving of roads, construction
of drainages, recovery of a large park, construction of a community center and a sports field., The project
would take a different approach than PDSTP in that it would concentrate its efforts in one geographic area,
becoming more of a comprehensive urban upgrading project. The government has consciously adopted
this approach of targeted interventions in specific geographic areas in order to increase the overall impact
and visibility of the works and to leverage the strengthening of social capital. The proposed $3.0 million
grant would be part of a larger program, estimated at $46 million, to be co-financed by AFD, the African
Development Bank (AfDB), the Islamic Development Bank (IsDB), and the Government itself.

3. Assessment of Outcomes

3.1 Relevance of Objectives, Design and Implementation
The overall project development objective was highly relevant to the country situation and to its
development priorities at the time of approval. It is highly relevant now, as well. The design was clear
with three components complementary of each other and in support of the PDO. The project design
proved relevant to the achievement of objectives. The participation of the target communities in the
selection of priority works, and in parallel the development of activities to increase social cohesiveness,
ensured ownership of the infrastructure improvements, fostered the strengthening of social capital in poor
neighborhoods, and encouraged the beneficiaries to take a more active role in the solution of their
problems.

Two of the targets set in the PAD were unrealistic. First, reaching 87% of the poor in Djibouti-ville was
an over optimistic target for a project with discrete interventions in a number of neighborhoods. In
addition there was no baseline to compare this target to. Second, the generation of employment

Djibouti                                             - 9 -                                            ICR

opportunities, in the scale proposed in the PAD was unrealistic for the number and type of investments to
be constructed during the project. Moreover, certain infrastructure works did not require a high intensity
of labor. It is also important to stress that there were practically no firms to work with at the time of
effectiveness. The few firms that existed had serious problems with basic activities such as understanding
contractual documents, respecting deadlines, operating and managing building sites, and ensuring the
quality of final products. Therefore at the beginning of project implementation ADETIP had to work hand
in hand with the various consulting firms for the studies and the construction firms for the public works,
to establish basic procedures and a work culture. It took some time before these firms could operate on
their own in the new business environment. Finally it was necessary to reconcile the approach of labor
intensive techniques with the need to ensure quality and timeliness in the construction of the largest
infrastructure facilities.

While project implementation suffered from innumerable delays, the project sequencing worked out
relatively well in the sense that some infrastructure works were built first, generating confidence in
ADETIP's ability to deliver concrete results, among the direct beneficiaries as well as within the client
ministries. In a second stage, when ADETIP was an already established and trusted agency it could focus
its attention to the community development component and match it with the provision of infrastructure.

3.2 Achievement of Project Development Objectives
The project development objective to enhance the living conditions of the poor in the city of Djibouti by
increasing the quality and access to basic economic and social infrastructure and services was achieved
through targeted interventions totaling 74 in 21 neighborhoods and covering the following investments:
construction and/ or rehabilitation of schools, health centers, community centers, public markets, sports
facilities, water standpipes, street works and drainages, as well as literacy campaigns, hygiene and solid
waste campaigns, training in handicrafts and other skills, and support to community organizations. All of
the neighborhoods targeted have poverty levels higher than 72%. These neighborhoods represent 89% of
all the poor neighborhoods in the city. It was not possible though, to quantify the exact number of poor
people benefiting from the project, as required for the PDO indicator. The 1997 and 2007 poverty data are
not comparable in terms of measurements, methodologies, and geographic area definitions.

The resulting infrastructure is in good condition, with 80% of the constructed or rehabilitated
infrastructure considered operational. The degree of satisfaction towards neighborhood infrastructure,
among the beneficiary population, increased by 87% as verified with focus groups in 6 of the
neighborhoods intervened. A total of 22 community development plans were developed, surpassing the
original target of 15. These plans were instrumental to motivate the beneficiary communities and to guide
the rationale of investments. Of the subprojects financed by ADETIP, 60% were included in the
community development plans. The poverty map was completed and is being used by various agencies of
the government. The additional financing approved in 2003 was used to increase the number of
investments and therefore it was very useful to achieve the project's outputs.

The project was also intended to enhance the living standards of the poor by generating employment
opportunities. A target of 17,500 person-months of work created was established in the PAD, but only
8,300 were achieved. The jobs created in target neighborhoods were of short term duration and did not
constitute a means to enhance the living standards of the poor as stated in the PDO. As it was discussed
previously the target for this indicator was unrealistic. In fact there was no documentation in the project
files as to how this target was estimated. To ADETIP's favor, the agency meticulously registered the jobs
created through each contract, not only for infrastructure works, but also for the community development
and institutional components. This careful measurement can now be used to estimate a more realistic
target, depending on the types of contracts, nature and duration of the works, and technology used. These
detailed standards have been used to calculate the new short term job generation targets for the new
project.

On the other hand, the project had a considerable impact in supporting the network of small consulting

3.3 Efficiency
No internal rate of return for the project was calculated at the outset. Instead, a methodology to calculate
the internal rate of return for different types of investments was included as an annex of the PAD. In the
end, individual rates of return for projects were not calculated, partly because of the competing demands
that ADETIP faced. The project coordinator agreed that this is an area that needs improvement in the new
project.

Even though there is no data to verify the economic feasibility of the works, it is expected that the overall
internal rate of return for the project is above the interest rate prevalent in Djibouti because: (i) most of
the infrastructure that was built or rehabilitated was small, used local materials, used a good portion of
non-skilled labor and therefore involved low costs: (ii) the social and economic benefits deriving from the
investments reach far beyond the targeted neighborhoods; and (iii) intangible benefits such as the increase
of self esteem and community dynamism are quite significant in this type of project.

3.4 Justification of Overall Outcome Rating
Rating: moderately satisfactory

In spite of all the constraints that the country faced at the beginning of project implementation, some of
which continue to be pressing, the project achieved important results, notably the rehabilitation of basic
infrastructure in the city, the mobilization of communities towards greater engagement in local
development, the injection of dynamism and professionalism in Djiboutian public works consulting firms,
the awareness raising among urban policy makers of the cultural heritage value of Djibouti-city's historic
center, and the creation and consolidation of ADETIP. Few shortcomings make the overall rate
moderately satisfactory. These are: (i) the lack of strong evidence regarding the number of poor residents
benefiting from the project (see 3.2.); and (ii) the lack of an economic analysis of the sub-projects and the
project in general, that could prove the efficiency of the operation (see 3.3.).

3.5 Overarching Themes, Other Outcomes and Impacts

(a) Poverty Impacts, Gender Aspects, and Social Development
The project did target its interventions in the poorest neighborhoods in the city. At the beginning of the
project the neighborhoods to intervene were selected using a poverty map prepared in 1997 by
independent consultants, on the basis of the 1996 household survey. Later on, when the new and much
improved poverty map was prepared, in 2007 it was used to verify that most of the project investments
took place in the poorest neighborhoods of the city: Ambouli, Boulaos, Quartier 3, Quartier 4, Quartier 5,
Quartier 7, Quartier 7 Bis, Stade, Balbala, Doraleh, PK-12. All of these neighborhoods have a poverty
rate fluctuating between 72 to 100%.

The poverty map comprises two atlases. One of Djibouti-city only, already published, combines
information from the national household survey (EDAM-IS2), the health survey (PAPFAM), and the
energy consumption survey (EDSF). The second atlas has information from EDAM-IS2 for the 5
prefectures of the country. The poverty map is a powerful tool for public policy in Djibouti that is already
being extensively used. It will also be instrumental in the preparation of future PRSPs and CASs

Gender was not identified as an area of project intervention at the outset. Nevertheless, many of the
community participation activities, such as the literacy campaigns, the hygiene and solid waste campaigns,
and the training in handicrafts, were specifically targeted to women. These sub-components were very
successful in empowering women in their neighborhoods. Participating women learned to read and write,
to hold a basic accounting, and to sew, all of which helped them increase their revenues, although in a
small scale. The literacy program benefited 7,165 people of which 73% were women. The program
allowed the beneficiary women to read their children's report cards, help them with homework, fill out
administrative and travel forms, and participate more effectively in community activities. The women
associations in the various neighborhoods were strengthened throughout the project, accounting for 10 out
of 18 beneficiary associations. The case of the vocational training in Balbala, a very large and deprived
neighborhood, and one considered relatively unsafe, is cited as one of the major gender achievements of
the project. In the center women learned sewing, cooking, computer literacy and hairdressing. More work
in these lines will be necessary to consolidate a gender approach in subsequent operations.

The project achieved a high degree of social development in the neighborhoods intervened. Although the
community development component was initially overlooked, the change of Bank supervision team in
2004, the increasing trust that ADETIP generated in the Government and the beneficiaries alike, and the
change of ADETIP's social development specialist, made it possible to satisfactorily implement this
component. The fact that the project design did not include specific activities to finance under this
component, but rather an ample menu of possibilities, all geared towards increasing community
participation, has permitted a great degree of flexibility in adjusting to the demands of the communities.

6. Lessons Learned
In terms of project design, the main lessons learned are:

In post-conflict situations it is important to prepare projects with realistic targets and timetables
In a country recovering from a severe social conflict and with low implementation capacity, like Djibouti,
the project targets and timetables must be realistic so as to make them reasonable to achieve. All the
associated risks must be flagged up front. It is also advisable to foresee an implementation in clear phases
that can achieve results at a reasonable pace.

Project preparation should be thorough and envisage a flexible project design
The social and environmental assessments, and institutional and economic analyses are important aspects
of project preparation that allow a good knowledge of the local conditions and a tailored approach. These
studies are most useful during implementation and can be used to establish the baselines for project
monitoring. A flexible project design is important in a post conflict situation, in order to adapt to the
development pace of the country, and in the case of PDSTP, the evolution of the executing agency.

In terms of project implementation, the main lessons are:

Community participation was an element of success for local infrastructure subprojects in the PDSTP
Community participation is fundamental to ensure project ownership, better care of the infrastructure, and
to motivate change within the beneficiary communities. Neighborhood improvement projects should be
developed after a thorough neighborhood development plan, including community participation, is
prepared and validated. In the case of the PDSTP the use of certain facilities and its care and maintenance
were below expectations at the beginning of the project when the social development component was not
yet operational. This situation was reversed once the communities were involved in project
implementation.

Close support and supervision is required for projects in countries with low implementation capacity

Djibouti                                             - 15 -                                              ICR
Social Development and Public Works project                                                      March 2008


A close supervision ensures that problems are identified early on and that solutions are put in place and
followed up. This means that technical assistance is made available as needed and that Bank supervision
is intense. Supervision budgets should correspond to the effort envisaged.

A trade-off between using a labor intensive approach and ensuring the quality and timeliness of
infrastructure works is required
Although labor intensive techniques are the recommended approach to build local infrastructure in very
poor countries like Djibouti, where unemployment is rampant, these should be used wisely in view of
other equally important goals such as timeliness and quality of construction. If job creation is indeed a
project objective, then it should be studied carefully during project preparation, using experts in this field
and setting realistic targets.

Capacity building should be a key activity of projects in Djibouti and accompanying TA for this purpose
should be provided.
Capacities remain very low in Djibouti and therefore project implementation requires considerable
support and technical assistance. In spite of procedural weaknesses in procurement, highlighted earlier in
this report, ADETIP managed to develop a strong procurement function. Capacity building in
procurement in the case of PDSTP is a good example of what can be achieved with close support and
follow-up. At the beginning of project implementation, international consultants were hired to establish
procurement procedures within ADETIP. In addition, unallocated funds were used to train key personnel
in procurement. During project implementation ADETIP appointed a technical auditor who did three
interval missions focusing on shortcomings in implementation. Key findings of technical audits were
subject of follow-up by ADETIP management and Bank missions which did improve the quality of
implementation. ADETIP, assisted by specialized consultants, carried out capacity building workshops
for local consulting firms and small and medium enterprises to raise awareness and improve response to
public demands. Results were successful having an impact on the overall capacity in this area all over
the country. ADETIP provided support to other implementing agencies in the country financed by IDA
and other donors. For instance, the technical manual of PDSTIP including all procurement procedures,
BDs, RFP, Contract Forms and Evaluation Forms were used for projects financed by other donors (AFD,
BID BAD) for the implementation of projects in Education and Health.

L'objectif du programme de développement social et des travaux d'intérêt public (PDSTIP) a été défini
suite à l'accentuation de la crise économique et financière connue depuis 1991 par l'économie
djiboutienne due aux facteurs suivants :
    -    L'afflux massif des réfugiés de Somalie et d'Ethiopie ;
    -    La guerre civile de 1991 à 1994 ayant opposé le gouvernement au front de la restauration de
         l'unité et de la démocratie (FRUD) ;
    -    La forte réduction de l'aide étrangère ;
    -    La forte incidence de la pauvreté.


A ces éléments s'ajoute la faiblesse des indicateurs sociaux (santé, accès à l'eau potable, mortalité
infantile, etc.).
Toutes ces raisons ont conduit la Banque Mondiale et le Gouvernement Djiboutien à définir le PDSTIP
autour d'un objectif global, visant à lutter contre la pauvreté en améliorant le niveau de vie des
populations pauvres dans la ville de Djibouti par une meilleure accessibilité aux équipements socio
collectifs et aux services sociaux. Pour ce faire, le PDSTIP a été défini autour de trois composantes2 :
    -    L'amélioration des infrastructures et des équipements socio collectifs ;
    -    L'appui au développement communautaire ;
    -    Le renforcement des capacités institutionnelles.


Composante 1 : Infrastructures économiques et sociales (contre-valeur de 15,8 millions de dollars).
Cette composante vise à améliorer l'environnement physique dans les quartiers pauvres de Djibouti ville ;
elle consiste à effectuer des petits travaux d'infrastructure de base destinés principalement à améliorer :
         -    les travaux de voirie et le drainage sur les réseaux secondaire et tertiaire ;
         -    la construction/réhabilitation d'équipements commerciaux (marchés, places publiques, etc.) ;

Afin de maximiser l'impact de ces travaux publics en termes de création d'emplois et de développement

oeuvre du projet et de fournir des services sociaux aux pauvres. Les ONG et associations disposant des

    plans de développement de quartier. Ces plans incluront : les investissements considérés comme

2) Des subventions pour des sous-projets communautaires (contre-valeur de 2,5 millions de dollars). Le
    projet financera des sous-projets proposés par les communautés soit directement, soit par
    l'intermédiaire d'associations nationales ou d'ONG internationales, dans les domaines suivants : a)

    d'en assurer la viabilité ; et c) gestion et organisation de la distribution en eau, hygiène,
    assainissement et services environnementaux (par exemple, nettoyage des rues et regroupement des
    ordures ménagères), y compris des campagnes d'information, éducation et communication (IEC) dans
    ces domaines.


Composante 3 : Renforcement des capacités institutionnelles (contre-valeur de 3,2 millions de dollars).
Cette composante vise à renforcer les capacités institutionnelles des divers intervenants participant à la
mise en oeuvre des deux autres composantes ou au suivi de la lutte contre la pauvreté. Elle comprendra les
deux sous-composantes suivantes :
a) Analyse et suivi de la pauvreté (contre-valeur de 0,5 millions de dollars) Le projet permettra à
    l'administration centrale d'assurer le suivi et l'analyse de la pauvreté en finançant une enquête
    nationale sur les conditions de vie des ménages (EDAM-BC) et d'utiliser les données de cette enquête
    pour la mise à jour, l'exécution et l'évaluation rétrospective des politiques et programmes de lutte
    contre la pauvreté. Cette sous-composante comprendra aussi la conception et l'actualisation d'une
    carte de la pauvreté à Djibouti ville, et des enquêtes/études ayant pour objet d'évaluer l'impact du
    projet sur la pauvreté.

1. CONTEXTE.
         1.1. Contexte général du pays.

Au début des années 1990, la République de Djibouti a connu une crise politique suscitée par une série de chocs
internes et externes, transformée par la suite en un grave déséquilibre économique et social. Sur le plan interne,
la traditionnelle stabilité politique a été mise à mal par des revendications politiques3 qui ont débouché sur un
conflit armé entre des unités rebelles et les forces gouvernementales. Du point de vue externe, le tarissement
progressif des aides budgétaires étrangères, notamment des pays membres de la Ligue Arabe, accentué par le
contexte de conflit interne et l'afflux des victimes des troubles des pays voisins, ont provoqué de profonds
déséquilibres macroéconomiques. Le déficit de la balance courante était estimé à 27% du PIB et le déficit
budgétaire a atteint son sommet avec 7 milliards DJF (9% du PIB) en 1994. L'Etat pris par l'engrenage des
difficultés budgétaires, n'étant plus en mesure d'honorer ses engagements vis-à-vis des créanciers tant intérieurs
qu'extérieurs. L'accumulation des arriérés a fini par entraîner l'économie dans un cercle vicieux où les
entreprises publiques et privées ont vu leurs activités se détériorer considérablement, situation illustrée par
ailleurs par l'augmentation du chômage. Dans un tel contexte, le Gouvernement djiboutien n'avait plus de choix
que de recourir aux programmes d'ajustement structurel appuyé par le Fonds Monétaire International (FMI) à
partir de 1996.

La vocation des différents programmes du FMI consistait dans un premier temps à stabiliser l'économie tout en
lui prodiguant les mesures nécessaires pour la restructurer et la rendre viable et dans un deuxième temps à
répondre de façon efficace à l'extrême dénuement des populations, en particulier dans les milieux ruraux où la
vulnérabilité et la misère sociale s'est accrue de façon dramatique. L'application de cette vision a débouché alors
sur la définition d'une stratégie globale qui consistait à améliorer l'accès des plus pauvres aux services sociaux
de base, de sorte à ce qu'avec les ressources publiques minimales mobilisées on obtienne le maximum de
retombées sociales et économiques. L'intervention stratégique et ciblée des pouvoirs publics devait contribuer à
l'expression et à l'émergence de l'initiative privée, seule voie possible pour asseoir un développement
économique viable du pays, en tablant in fine sur le développement humain et non sur l'accumulation tous
azimuts des équipements publics, afin de réduire substantiellement la dépendance des plus pauvres.

C'est pourquoi, au lieu d'avoir une implication frontale de l'Etat dans les activités économiques à proprement
parler, le programme structurel a organisé le retrait progressif mais systématique de l'Etat dans l'économie, pour
se concentrer sur la couverture des besoins essentiels dans les milieux pauvres pour en libérer le potentiel
économique.

         1.2. Situation avant le projet.

Le passage en revue de la situation antérieure au projet permet de recadrer les difficultés auxquelles le pays était
confronté et d'apprécier la pertinence des choix alors arrêtés pour y répondre. Les points saillants qui ont justifié
la mise en oeuvre du projet et tirés de l'étude du SDAU de Djibouti réalisée en 1998, concernent la situation
urbanistique de Djibouti-ville, les indicateurs socio-économiques de la population et la déperdition des
infrastructures publiques.

l'évolution des infrastructures publiques de Djibouti-ville n'a pas suivi l'évolution des besoins de la population.
Au lendemain de l'indépendance, la ville a connu une croissance rapide alors que les équipements publics de
l'espace urbain connaissaient un dépérissement. Au terme de la période allant de 1983 à 1998, la ville s'est
étendue de 1 000 hectares selon l'axe Nord-Sud et l'expansion projetée pour la décennie suivante était de même
étendue. De plus, la guerre interne de 1991 à 1994, même si la capitale n'en a pas été le théâtre direct, a eu entre
autres conséquences, la difficulté sinon l'abandon de l'entretien des équipements socio-collectifs de base par les
autorités publiques.

Concernant l'école publique, les indications chiffrées de l'étude du SDAU, donnaient un ratio de 0,09 classes
pour 1 000, au lieu de 0,17 nécessaire. Sans donner de chiffres, l'étude fait également état de l'insuffisance de
l'habitat social illustrée par le développement des bidonvilles et le même constat d'insuffisance est dressé pour
les équipements de santé. Les dysfonctionnements des équipements sociaux de base étaient caractérisés par le
manque d'assainissement et la dégradation des locaux des écoles et des dispensaires dans les quartiers de la
capitale.

                                  ii. Rappel de la situation socio-économique de la population :
Elément essentiel ayant suscité et précipité le projet, la situation socio-économique se résume à la pauvreté
généralisée des populations. Accusant les séquelles de la guerre intervenue entre 1991 et 1994, le contexte
économique national était particulièrement difficile avec un taux de croissance constamment négatif à partir de
1996 et il faut attendre 1998 pour voir le taux de croissance redevenir positif (+0,8% en 1998).

Le bilan dressé en 1996 par l'EDAM-IS4 de la DINAS, devenue la DISED depuis, et enrichie par la carte établie
par l'ADETIP en 1998 a montré que 60% des ménages djiboutiens n'avaient pas l'eau courante, que 66%
d'entre eux n'avaient pas un habitat décent et que 37% étaient pauvres. La distribution spatiale a par ailleurs
montré que la localisation de la pauvreté était au Sud, le Nord de la ville abritant les foyers dits aisés et les
classes moyennes se trouvant au centre.

2. DESCRIPTION DU PROJET.

          2.1. Conception et composantes du projet.
Guidée par la recherche du plus grand impact, la conception du projet a systématiquement obéi à une
considération : l'accès aux services et infrastructures socio-économiques décisives (déterminantes) pour garantir
le développement humain et réduire ainsi l'extrême dépendance des plus pauvres, voire permettre leur essor
économique. C'est ainsi que le projet s'est concentré d'une part sur la couverture des besoins essentiels et
d'autre part sur le renforcement des capacités tant institutionnelles que communautaires afin de mobiliser et
d'impliquer les bénéficiaires dans la conduite du projet de développement.

Selon les dispositions qui fixent les missions du PDSTIP on trouve la réhabilitation des infrastructures socio-
économiques, la contribution au recul de la pauvreté par l'utilisation des ressources dans des travaux à haute
intensité de main d'oeuvre et la réhabilitation d'édifices publics constituant le patrimoine culturel de la ville de
Djibouti.

Reproduisant la conception de la stratégie, le PDSTIP s'articule autour de 3 grandes composantes :
          La composante « Infrastructures économiques et sociales » : d'un montant de 10,8 millions USD, la
          vocation de ce volet consiste à réhabiliter ou à reconstruire les infrastructures de base de l'espace public
          dans les quartiers pauvres de la Capitale par le recours aux technologies à haute intensité de main
          d'oeuvre pourvoyeuses d'emplois.

          La composante « appui au développement communautaire » : destinée à compléter l'objectif de
          réhabilitation des structures socio-économiques par l'appropriation du projet par les populations
          bénéficiaires, cette composante a reçu une enveloppe de 2,6 millions USD. Ce programme a porté sur
          deux volets : (i) le programme de mobilisation sociale, financé à hauteur de 0,1 million USD, pour la
          conception des stratégies de mobilisation et d'implication de la société civile dans la définition et
          l'application des plans de développement des quartiers. Ce programme doit également permettre la
          pérennisation des investissements ; (ii) le financement des sous-projets communautaires pour une valeur
          de 2,5 millions USD où le projet apporte son concours financier aux sous-projets portés par les
          communautés et les associations et traitant des sujets suivants : infrastructures économiques et sociales,
          gestion des équipements construits et/ou réhabilités et gestion et organisation de la distribution en eau,
          hygiène, assainissement et services environnementaux. Toutes les initiatives communautaires destinées à
          consolider le projet et à pérenniser les réalisations sont encouragées et appuyées dans le cadre de ce
          volet.

          La composante « renforcement des capacités institutionnelles » : programmé à hauteur de 3,5 millions
          USD ce volet participe au financement de l'élaboration et du suivi des études entreprises par le
          Gouvernement dans le cadre de la lutte contre de la pauvreté. Il s'agit notamment de :
         (i)               l'analyse et le suivi de la pauvreté pour un montant de 0,5 million USD pour la mise à jour
                           des enquêtes EDAM I et II ainsi que la conception de la carte de la pauvreté à Djibouti-ville.

         2.2. Planification du projet.
Conformément à sa conception, la planification du projet a reproduit les axes d'intervention du PDSTIP en
définissant les modes de sélection des sous-projets pour chacune de ses composantes.

    a. la réhabilitation des infrastructures : afin de remettre à niveau les équipements publics de première
         nécessité, le PDSTIP a mené des consultations sectorielles où les représentants des principaux
         départements ministériels ont exprimé leurs attentes. Le projet a donc été principalement planifié en
         fonction des souhaits formulés par les Ministères de l'Education Nationale, de la Santé, de la Jeunesse,
         de l'Intérieur et de l'Habitat, de l'Urbanisme, de l'Environnement et de l'Aménagement du Territoire
         d'une part et de la population civile appuyée par les chefs d'arrondissement et de quartiers d'autre part.

    b. le ciblage de la pauvreté à Djibouti-ville : la planification des activités engagées dans le cadre de ce
         volet a été réalisée sur la base des données socio-économiques établies par l'enquête EDAM I que la
         DINAS avait élaborée en 1996. Cette étude de la DINAS, la première du genre combinant plusieurs
         critères socio-économiques, avait permis de dégager un indice composite sur le développement humain à
         Djibouti. Ce travail a permis d'obtenir une cartographie de la distribution de la pauvreté dans la Capitale.
         Trois niveaux d'appréciation de la pauvreté ont ainsi pu être évalués où l'incidence de la pauvreté était
         mesurée à 20%, 35% et 64% respectivement dans les quartiers aisés et les autres quartiers dits populaires.
         Une mise à jour des données socio-économiques a été entreprise en 2002 enfin et la même approche a
         été adoptée par l'ADETIP en 2007 pour intégrer les données les plus récentes dans la nouvelle carte de
         la pauvreté à Djibouti-ville. L'analyse rétrospective de la planification d'origine à la lumière des
         données recueillies en 2007 a permis de confirmer la justesse du ciblage établi en 1998 car aujourd'hui
         encore les quartiers jugés pauvres concentrent toujours un taux de pauvreté situé entre 72 et 100%5.

    c. la réhabilitation des édifices publics : face à la dégradation du patrimoine architectural historique de la
         capitale, l'ADETIP et la Direction de l'Urbanisme ont exprimé la nécessité de restaurer et de préserver
         l'environnement urbain. Cette initiative a été entérinée par la décision de la Banque Mondiale de
         consacrer 1,62 millions USD pour restaurer 3 édifices, soit 15% du financement du projet. Le caractère
         quelque peu complexe de la restauration des bâtiments historiques a généré des retards et des rajouts
         dans l'exécution des travaux conformément à la planification, néanmoins l'expérience acquise est jugée
         largement positive puisqu'elle a eu un effet d'entraînement des autres secteurs et suscité auprès des
         autorités nationales l'intérêt d'entreprendre de tels travaux.

         2.3. Mise en route et déroulement du projet.
La mise en route s'est déroulée dans un contexte d'absence de bureaux d'études à Djibouti. Les seuls bureaux
d'études en activité à cette époque étaient le bureau Madame Dubois, CHI CASABAN, qui ont quitté le pays par
la suite.

Les experts de la Banque Mondiale en mission avaient à cet effet préconisé qu'un bureau d'études international
couvre la période de lancement des activités. Mais cette dernière option a été finalement abandonnée pour se
contenter des réalisations de l'ADETIP qui a assuré tant bien que mal ce rôle en plus de sa mission de maître

Le milieu des entreprises était particulièrement sinistré principalement à cause de la défaillance de l'Etat qui

précision portant sur la nature des travaux à entreprendre ni sur la priorité des projets. Il a fallu que l'ADETIP
identifie chaque domaine d'intervention et monte les sous-projets susceptibles de trouver exécution sur le terrain.

                                                               ii. La priorisation des besoins
La mise en route du projet est également passée par la création effective de l'ADETIP de toutes pièces. Il a fallu
repérer et recruter un personnel restreint et performant, élaborer les manuels de procédures, doter l'agence d'un
Conseil d'Administration et d'une Assemblée Générale dans un laps de temps relativement limité. A peine
surmontée l'étape de la genèse et pour s'assurer que l'agence avait bel et bien pris corps, elle devait affronter et
réussir le projet pilote en réhabilitant le dispensaire de Hayableh. Ce test subi avec succès lui a permis de
bénéficier du premier financement de la Banque Mondiale.

La notion de maîtrise d'ouvrage délégué, qui était alors inconnue dans le pays s'est quelque peu heurtée à des
incompréhensions de la part de certains interlocuteurs de référence. En effet, il a fallu rassurer les différents
départements ministériels traditionnellement engagés dans le bâtiment pour justifier l'intervention de l'ADETIP
à leurs côtés, voire le plus souvent à leur place pour concourir aux mêmes objectifs qu'eux sans leur laisser
l'impression de les dessaisir de leur mission.

La localisation de la pauvreté à Djibouti-ville s'est également avérée délicate à opérer du fait de la généralisation
de la pauvreté, obligeant ainsi le projet à intervenir partout au risque de voir l'ADETIP submergée.

Eu égard à ces éléments, on peut affirmer que la mise en route du projet n'a pas bénéficié de conditions
préalables favorables à son succès, car elles n'étaient tout simplement pas réunies pour l'essentiel. Néanmoins,
son lancement a eu lieu et a abouti à des résultats satisfaisants, ce qui constitue à ce titre, un tour de force qui est
à l'honneur de l'équipe en place.

fait de se retrouver éloignée de la réalité du terrain. C'est pourquoi grâce à cette composante l'ADETIP a
multiplié les rencontres et tissé un réseau de contacts qui s'avèrent payants aujourd'hui. Un programme dit de

5. PERTINENCE DU PROJET : par rapport aux attentes des populations.

Les attentes des populations ont été identifiées auparavant à travers des consultations qui ont été organisées à la
veille de la mise en route du projet. A la lumière de l'enquête EDAM de 1998, qui donnait la localisation de la
pauvreté au sein de la capitale, 22 quartiers ont été retenus pour définir les « programmes de développement des
quartiers » (PDQ) avec la participation des populations. Une première série de PDQ9 élaborée en 2001 a porté
sur 11 quartiers et une deuxième série en 2003 avec le même nombre de quartiers.
La limitation du financement alors rendu disponible, a imposé que seuls 2 ou 3 besoins prioritaires aient été
retenus dans chacun des 21 quartiers concernés.

Le portefeuille de PDQ composé de 11 secteurs fait état de réalisations diverses :

Le PDSTIP a largement contribué à l'accès aux infrastructures de base et équipements sociaux sur l'ensemble
des quartiers cibles. Et aussi enregistrés un taux de création d'emplois élevés.

Si toutefois on constate parfois un léger décalage entre les réalisations et attentes exprimées par les populations ;
c'est du d'une part à la réalisation tardive du PDQ (soit 2 ans après le démarrage) et la limitation budgétaire
d'autre part qui a conduit au « saupoudrage » d'actions au lieu d'interventions intégrées. Il est également à noter
une autre cause non moindre de ce résultat, qui tient du fait que les associations de quartiers n'étaient pas
suffisamment structurées au départ, créant ainsi une altération de la communication entre les différentes parties
prenantes dans la définition et la conduite des projets. Cette première expérience de mobilisation sociale fut
néanmoins fort utile car elle a permis à l'ADETIP de recourir à l'Institut national d'administration publique
(INAP) par le biais d'une convention de financement afin de formaliser les rapports que l'agence doit
développer avec la société civile.

Bien qu'il soit difficile d'évaluer l'impact de façon précise, il importe néanmoins de rappeler que les effets
multiples du PDSTIP ont introduit une réelle amélioration des conditions de la population, tant au plan sanitaire
qu'au plan éducationnel ou en matière de salubrité publique et de ce fait on peut affirmer qu'une proportion
importante des populations pauvres a bénéficié des interventions du projet.

6. ADETIP

Lors de sa constitution en 1999, l'ADETIP était composée du Directeur Général, du Directeur Technique et
d'une Assistante de Direction. L'agence était rattachée au Ministère de l'Economie, des Finances et de la
Planification chargé de la Privatisation et au départ, elle ne disposait pas d'une comptabilité, cette dernière se
résumant à l'établissement des coûts et des états financiers sommaires. Cette situation a imposé le recrutement
d'un spécialiste qui a mis au point un système de comptabilité en l'an 2000 et ce n'est qu'en 2002 que la
comptabilité fut systématisée.

Ainsi au départ le projet a connu un démarrage difficile, d'autant que l'ADETIP avait engagé des études en régie,
ce qui ne lui permettait pas d'assurer avec ses faibles moyens une gestion efficiente du PDSTIP. Parmi les
composantes du PDSTIP le génie civil était prépondérant au départ et l'appui au développement communautaire
n'est devenu effectif qu'en 2001. Après le départ de la première responsable de la composante communautaire,
son remplacement n'a été effectué qu'en 2003, année du véritable redéploiement de l'appui communautaire. A
partir de 2001, l'ADETIP a décidé de confier en sous-traitance différentes tâches techniques, ce qui lui a permis
d'assurer sa véritable fonction de maître d'ouvrage délégué.

Outre l'étude et la mise en oeuvre du PDSTIP, l'ADETIP était, en tant que maître d'ouvrage délégué (MOD), en
charge du PRUSI du PK12 pour le compte de l'AFD, du PDSS, de la cellule SID et du Fonds Social de
Développement. Au départ, et malgré cette charge de travail, les effectifs n'ont pas beaucoup varié, car en 2003,
l'ADETIP comprenait 4 cadres, une assistante, un chauffeur et un gardien soit 7 personnes. Après la mise en
oeuvre de la composante appui au développement communautaire, l'équipe s'est étoffée et a été portée à 11
membres suite au recrutement d'un ingénieur, d'une secrétaire standardiste, d'une assistante pour la Direction
Technique, et de deux comptables. En 2006, 2 ingénieurs sont recrutés ce qui porte les effectifs de l'ADETIP à
13 membres avec un taux d'encadrement supérieur à 60%.

Au départ, cette évolution modeste des effectifs de l'ADETIP par comparaison avec la charge de travail, la
multiplicité des tâches et leurs implications variées n'a pas permis à l'ADETIP d'organiser une gestion planifiée
de ses ressources humaines. En effet, bien qu'ayant évolué, le renforcement des capacités se faisait sans plan
préétabli, et cela apparaît clairement au niveau de certains indices. Ainsi et à titre d'exemple, on peut citer
l'absence d'un plan de formation du personnel et d'un centre de documentation pour assurer une diffusion
régulière de l'information, classer et gérer les notes, les rapports techniques et tout document produit dans le
cadre du PDSTIP. Cette fonction, comme c'est souvent le cas pour des organismes débordés par les multiples
tâches, est assurée par les différentes directions, gérant ainsi une documentation quasi-personnelle.

Il est incontestable que le personnel de l'ADETIP est caractérisé par un haut niveau de compétence pour avoir
été en mesure de faire face aux demandes multiples, et également pour avoir engagé une composante
communautaire complexe en s'appuyant sur des ONG dont les capacités pour la plupart étaient relativement
limitées. En dépit de ces difficultés, l'ADETIP a acquis des capacités non négligeables qui auraient gagné à être
rationalisées par un "plan triennal de formation" ciblé en fonction de la spécificité, des profils et des exigences
du PDSTIP. La gestion de l'information et de la documentation aurait contribué à assurer une meilleure
circulation et distribution de l'information.

Le rattachement fin 2002 de l'ADETIP à la primature, a marqué une nouvelle phase pour l'ADETIP dans la
mesure où aux rapports sectoriels se sont substitués des rapports intersectoriels. Ce changement a renforcé les
prérogatives de l'ADETIP et a garanti un meilleur acheminement des décisions relatives au PDSTIP. En dépit de
l'urgence qui a présidé à l'engagement et à la mise en oeuvre du PDSTIP, le décalage entre la réalisation des
projets d'infrastructures et le lancement de la composante "appui communautaire" n'a pas été préjudiciable au
renforcement des capacités de l'ADETIP.

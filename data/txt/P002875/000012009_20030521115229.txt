3.  Assessment of Development Objective and Design, and of Quality at Entry
3.1
Original Objective:
Page  6
The Project objectives were to help the country : (i) open the economy to facilitate private sector 
development, accelerate the economic recovery and firmly establish economic growth; (ii) reduce 
the fiscal deficit by rationalizing (redirecting) public expenditures; (iii) prioritize rehabilitation and 
maintenance (over extension) of the country’s infrastructure; and (iv) reduce poverty by providing 
basic public infrastructure and services and creating employment.   
The objectives were clearly described and are consistent with the Country Assistance Strategy
 
(CAS) and the IDA-supported Economic Recovery and Adjustment Credit (ERAC). A political
 
crisis in the early 1990s had brought about a serious economic crisis from which the country was 
just beginning to recover as this project was being prepared.  GDP had fallen by 23% from 1992 
to 1993.  The CAS of 1995 sought to help Togo correct economic imbalances and facilitate
 
growth with equity.  It identified degraded physical infrastructure as a constraint to development 
and it placed importance,
 
inter alia,
 on rehabilitating Togo’s transport infrastructure, improving
 
transport sector management and service provision and assuring transport sector stability.  
Rehabilitation and sustainable management of the road sector was given a first priority within the 
transport sector in order to revitalize the economy, boost agricultural production, create jobs and 
improve accessibility to services, especially in rural areas.
The IDA-financed ERAC supported Togo’s economic recovery during the period from 1996 to 
1998.  The objectives of ERAC included: (i) diversified agricultural production and exportation;
 
(ii) regeneration of manufacturing through development of the private sector and small and
 
medium enterprises; (ii) revival of the private sector; (iv) creation of an enabling environment for 
Lome to become a regional trade and financial center; and (e) reducing state expenditure and
 
increasing resource mobilization.  
When the crisis appeared to be easing in 1994, it was clear that the poor state of the transport
 
sector, particularly road transport, was a critical constraint to economic recovery.  A National
 
Committee for Transport Strategy had been set up in 1992.  By 1994 it was agreed that a
 
National Transport Sector Investment Strategy would be developed in collaboration with IDA
 
and other donors, to be financed using US$850,000 from existing IDA credits.  Funds from
 
existing credits were also used to begin restructuring the status of force account workers in the
 
roads sector and restructuring the ownership of maintenance equipment.  Early work on the
 
transport study in 1995 proceeded slowly because of consultant procurement problems.  
However, by late 1995 the study was underway focusing on: (i) road maintenance; (ii)
 
restructuring of the Directorate of Public Works Equipment (DMTP) to make it more
 
autonomous and to improve the use of existing road maintenance equipment; and (iii) community 
participation in rural road maintenance. It also focused on the creation of an autonomous Road
 
Maintenance Fund (FER) to mobilize resources for continuous maintenance financing.  This
 
strategy provided the basis for the Road Transport Project and for interventions by other donors 
in other transport sub-sectors.  
The first two objectives of the project indicated that the project was expected to contribute
 
directly to the broader economic program of the CAS and the ERAC, especially through the
 
development of small and medium enterprises (contractors), helping Lome to become a regional
 
trade center and increasing resource mobilization (through road user charges for the purpose of
 
- 2 -
Page  7
road maintenance).  The Road Transport Project was also expected to contribute to the objectives 
indirectly by encouraging road maintenance by the private sector and rural communities.  The
 
second two objectives called more directly for improvements in the road sector because they
 
emphasized infrastructure maintenance and poverty alleviation as it relates to road improvements.  
Because the political and economic situation of Togo was improving when the project was
 
appraised and because the project focused only on one mode of transport, roads, it cannot be
 
considered too risky or too complicated within the context that existed in Togo at that time.  The 
project did involve the reorganization of existing road maintenance agencies, partial privatization 
of maintenance functions and changes in the financing of road maintenance, but these changes
 
were agreed to as part of the strategy and confirmed as part of a Government Policy Letter.  They 
were not too complex or risky, particularly in comparison to similar changes being achieved in
 
several other African countries during the same period.  The creation of an independent Road
 
Maintenance Fund also greatly reduced the risks to the project from economic uncertainty.  When 
political and economic problems reemerged during the late 1990s this source of funding enabled 
road maintenance activities to continue while most other donor funded activities stagnated. 
IDA had been associated with the transport sector in Togo since 1968 and had led donor
 
assistance in the sector.  Five IDA-assisted projects had dealt with national roads, one with feeder 
roads, and one with both. Despite achievements, inadequate road maintenance remained a serious 
issue.  The European Development Fund and IDA were the major active donors in the sector and 
in assisting the road contracting industry.  Others included the German Cooperation (KFW) in
 
ports, the French Development Fund (CFD) in rural roads and airport infrastructure, and the
 
African Development Bank (ADB) in roads.

3.3
Original Components:
The components, as listed in the SAR of August 1997, were: (i) Road Repair and Maintenance
 
(US$ 48.6 million) to reduce transport costs by restoring and maintaining the national road
 
network, improving the competitiveness of the Togolese transit corridor and improving the
 
accessibility of rural areas. This was a first tranche priority program (1997-2000) together with
 
technical studies and supervision for paved road rehabilitation and maintenance, including
 
rehabilitation of the 105 km.  North-South axis (with large inputs from other donors),
 
rehabilitation and periodic maintenance (regravelling) of unpaved roads, and rehabilitation of
 
bridges and culverts, construction of toll booths and consultant services.  (ii) Institutional
 
Strengthening (US$ 3.6 million) to improve the economic efficiency of the transport sector and
 
increase public sector effectiveness and management capacity, including enhancing the
 
effectiveness of transport sector operators and small and medium enterprises (PMUs) and
 
ensuring maintenance through a Road Maintenance Fund.  This component focused support on
 
the General Directorate of Public Works (DGTP) and the General Directorate of Transport
 
(DGT).  There was also support to the Road Maintenance Fund (FER);  (iii) Road Safety
 
Improvement (US$ 1.0 million) to reduce the costs associated with the development of road
 
- 3 -
Page  8
traffic in Togo by treating dangerous stretches and intersections, improving transport conditions
 
and safety, and establishing the necessary framework and institutions, including a Center for
 
Automotive Technical Control (CCTA), for effective road safety management and supervision;
 
(iv) Feeder Road Management (US$ 1.6 million) for: (a) improving access to agricultural areas
 
and services; (b) improving the planning and management of the rural road networks by
 
rationalizing investments in rural feeder roads and standardizing the norms of their maintenance;
 
and (c) mobilizing local community participation in rural road maintenance and management in a
 
pilot operation to test community participation.  (v) Technical Assistance for all components: 56 
person-months of technical assistance for the institutional strengthening component, 11
 
person-months for the road safety component and 73 person-months (of which 58 local) for the
 
rural road rehabilitation and management component. All of the technical assistance was to
 
consist of studies and short-term assistance.
The broader priority investment program for trunk roads was to be financed largely by other
 
donors, including the Kuwait Fund, the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC) 
Fund, the French Agency for Cooperation (FAC), French Development Agency (FAD), the
 
Western African Development Bank (BOAD) and European Development Fund (FED), although 
not all could confirm their participation at the time of appraisal.  Given the fluid economic
 
situation at the time, it was expected that donor availability could change during the project
 
period. 
The General Directorate of Public Works (DGTP), under the Ministry of Works, was the lead
 
agency of the project. DGTP was responsible for road planning, inspection and contracting.
 
Institutional development activities were assigned to the General Directorate of Transport (DGT), 
under the Ministry of Transport, and under it to the Directorate of Road Transport (DTR). The
 
DTR was to be supported by and would have oversight of the Center for Technical Control of
 
Automobiles (CTTA).  A key element of the project was the creation of a Road Maintenance
 
Fund (FER), which would be financed from a dedicated fuel surcharge and tolls.
The aim of the pilot rural roads component was to integrate the efforts of the Directorate of Rural 
Roads (DPR), which was previously under the Ministry of Rural Development and Tourism, and 
bring it under the DGTP which would then have the responsibility for all rural roads, not only the 
roads traditionally under the control of DPR.  This would form a partnership between state
 
agencies and local communities for road maintenance.  All of this was discussed in depth with the 
concerned parties before the project and an implementation manual for rural road management
 
was agreed upon.
 
The components were planned over a number of years before the project was initiated.  They 
were related to achieving the objectives.  Economic recovery and private sector development 
required improved trunk and rural road systems. To do so it was essential to prioritize 
rehabilitation and maintenance, which would lead to more cost effective road management.  The 
rural road maintenance component was particularly consistent with the objective of poverty 
reduction.

3.5
Quality at Entry:
Project preparation was preceded by the preparation of a detailed strategy for the sector using a
 
participatory process involving the government, transport agency officials and representatives of
 
IDA and other donors.  The process included identification of issues and priorities, discussion
 
with public and private stakeholders, including local professional organizations, and a three-day
 
national seminar in November 1995. This led to the establishment of two fundamental sector
 
priorities: First, to rehabilitate road infrastructure and establish an effective routine and periodic
 
maintenance system, with road maintenance having a priority over any other consideration.  The
 
poor condition of the paved network posed an especially high cost to the economy.   Second, the 
strategy sought to modernize the institutional environment to make the best use of the public and 
private sectors. Government presence would be strengthened where essential (e.g. law
 
enforcement, road safety).  However, many regulations would be reduced or repealed (e.g. road 
transport tariffs, allocation of sea freight between carriers).  Economic rents obtained by public
 
sector companies would be eliminated.  Government capacity would be redeployed towards its
 
main priorities of planning, monitoring and supervision.  Government would give up
 
force-account works and production activities (e.g. road works, equipment maintenance). The
 
Government also aimed to standardize the management of rural roads with the participation of
 
rural communities in maintenance.
The following recommendations were made to achieve the above priorities:   (i) secure regular
 
financing for road maintenance by establishing an autonomous Road Fund directly financed from
 
the sub-sector; mobilize financing for the already identified priority rehabilitation; (ii) employ the
 
private sector for road maintenance works, port equipment maintenance, and container handling;
 
(iii) review and update the private law of transport; (iv) reinforce administrative, human and
 
financial capacity of key government departments; and (v) transform the public equipment pool
 
into a commercial company leasing its equipment.
The strategy also covered other sub-sectors such as ports and rail and air transport.  These modes 
were to be assisted by other donors.  The strategy was confirmed by the government in a Letter of 
Sector Policy dated May 29, 1996, signed by the Prime Minister.
A Project Implementation Manual was prepared which covered procurement arrangements,
 
standard bidding documents, key performance indicators, reporting formants, selection criteria for 
roads and works, economic justification methodology and environmental guidelines.   
Quality at entry is rated satisfactory.
4.  Achievement of Objective and Outputs
4.1  
Outcome/achievement of objective:
From early in the project period, as noted by the first supervision mission, there were delays by
 
the government in providing adequate counterpart funding. This problem continued throughout
 
the project period.  Ultimately due to the accumulation of arrears to IDA, there were three
 
- 5 -
Page  10
suspensions during the project (January 1, 1999, November 14, 2000 and January 1, 2002) which 
constrained progress. However, at a time when other IDA-supported projects stopped entirely,
 
this project was able to continue at a limited pace and achieve many of its objectives.  In spite of
 
periodic counterpart funding constraints and the suspensions, the project disbursed about 68% of
 
the credit amount.  In addition, the Road Fund proved to be successful.  It was able to collect
 
most of its projected revenue which was mostly used to finance road rehabilitation.  Because of
 
funding constraints, with IDA concurrence Road Fund resources were also used as counterpart
 
funds for some of the periodic maintenance program and to cover part of the overhead of DGTP. 
This enabled the project to achieve more than would otherwise have been possible.
(i) The first objective was macroeconomic in nature. This roads project has contributed to private 
sector development by opening road maintenance to private entrepreneurs. Although fewer road 
works were implemented than planned, the road improvements that were accomplished are
 
contributing to economic recovery.  (ii) The project contributed to the achievement of the
 
objective of rationalizing public expenditure by supporting the development of the Road
 
Maintenance Fund (FER) which was able to finance road maintenance and some administrative
 
costs in the sector without drawing on the public budget.  Fuel levies were collected directly from 
oil companies and placed in bank accounts managed by FER, which insulated FER from the
 
government financial crisis.  (About US$30 million were collected by FER in this way, almost
 
equal to the US$ 34 million actually disbursed under the IDA credit. (iii) The project also
 
contributed to the redirection of resources from new construction to rehabilitation and
 
maintenance.  This was largely due to the development of FER which focused almost entirely on 
maintenance.  The government followed the maintenance strategy; no new road construction
 
projects were undertaken.  (iv) The project helped to reduce poverty by providing improved roads 
and creating employment.  Fifty to one hundred small and medium enterprises (PMEs) were
 
employed, creating 1000 to 1500 person years of employment. Before the project most road
 
maintenance had been done by force account. The rural roads maintenance component developed 
three pilot projects which benefit the rural poor.     
The last two supervision missions rated achievement of development objectives as unsatisfactory, 
mainly because the project was in suspension and progress on several components was halted or 
delayed.  At the end of the project, in spite of the suspension, the successful development of FER 
and capacity built in DGTP had enabled some of the development objectives to be attained:
 
rationalizing of public expenditures because funds to support road maintenance are directly linked 
to collection of road user charges through the FER; poverty alleviation because the improvement 
of the road network, particularly in rural areas where the poor live, have increased jobs
 
opportunities and access to basic services.   Attainment of development objectives is therefore
 
rated satisfactory

B. 
Institutional Strengthening
 
The main transport institutions, especially DGTP and DGT, received training in procurement,
 
contract management, road maintenance, etc.  Some officials received training in planning and
 
project control in Côte d’Ivoire in 2000.  A first tranche of office equipment and vehicles was
 
procured by DGTP.  DGTP had intended to put in place a data base to be used to plan
 
investments and program road maintenance, but this has not yet been done.
A final report on the computerization of road transport documents in DGT has been prepared, but 
due to suspension, action to implement the recommendations of the study has not been
 
undertaken.  A consultant has been selected to assist DGT in installing a transport sector data
 
base, but this was not possible due to suspension.   Efforts to establish training for local
 
contractors and engineering firms were delayed, but courses for road works contractors and
 
consulting firms were held in January 2001 and February 2002. TORs were prepared for
 
consultants to assist the unit in DGT in charge of analyzing accidents, but due to suspension the
 
services were not procured. 
The Road Maintenance Fund was established with its own board.  Early in the project period the 
FER began to achieve collections near its target, and it maintained administrative expenses near
 
the targeted 2% of revenues.  By 1997 the FER was collecting almost 100% of projected user
 
fees.  By early 1999 counterpart funds for the project were insufficient, and it was agreed that
 
FER would provide part of the counterpart contributions for road rehabilitation. Two percent of
 
FER’s revenue was also passed to DGTP to cover part of the agency’s overheads, but this could 
not be used to cover civil service salaries.  The successful development of FER was important
 
since it enabled a large amount of road maintenance to be executed as planned in spite of the
 
government’s serious fiscal problems and the suspensions.
C. 
Road Safety Improvement
This component, managed by DGT with support from DGTP, developed slowly. The Office of
 
Road Safety (OSR) was established by decree, but due to budgetary constraints it is not yet
 
operational. The CCTA was expected to contribute to financing of OSR’s operating costs.  By the 
Mid-Term Review the bids had been requested for the construction and equipping of the CCTA
 
headquarters, and the building has now been completed.  However, it is not yet equipped and
 
CCTA is not yet operational.  CCTA requested bids for technical equipment, but did not receive 
acceptable proposals.  Further tendering has not taken place due to the suspension.  Proposals for 
office furnishings for CCTA have not yet been requested by DGT.  
D. 
Feeder Road Management
Annual programs have been prepared by the Rural Road Directorate (DPR) and financed by the
 
- 8 -
Page  13
FER.  These were intended to establish a partnership between government agencies and rural
 
communities in order to establish a more efficient and cost effective road maintenance program.
Rehabilitation and Elimination of Critical Points
: The program, financed by IDA and implemented 
by DPR, covered about 250 km and costing FCFA 728 million, has been largely implemented.  
However, about 60 km were not fully completed due to the lack of capacity of some of the
 
contractors.  
Recurrent Maintenance
:  Each year beginning in 1997 recurrent maintenance programs were
 
undertaken for rural roads financed by the FER.  The program was prepared by the Directorate of 
Rural Roads (DPR) following the priorities adopted by the CNCPR and the Council of Roads.
 
The program for 1998 involved 97.5 km for CFAF 58 million. In 1999 it involved 181 km for
 
CFAF 271 million.  The program for 2000 involved CFAF 580 million.  Small and medium
 
enterprises were selected for the work and supervised by DPR.  Manual routine maintenance was 
successfully contracted out to individual contractors. 
Pilot Community Road Maintenance Program
: Beneficiaries were trained to take over the
 
maintenance of roads that were subject to this recurrent maintenance program.  Rural road
 
commissions were established under Village Development Committees (CVD) or under Village
 
Road Committees (CPV) where there was no CVD. The program involved local populations and 
road users, including transporters, drivers, merchants, NGOs and religious organizations.  These
 
were involved in disseminating information, sensitizing rural communities, organizing and training 
communities and managing pilot programs.  A rural road maintenance manual was prepared.  The 
program was implemented in three zones where communities were involved in the program and
 
much maintenance has been undertaken. By 2002 the new system had brought about maintenance 
of about 500 km. of rural roads, 90% of the target.  
Consultants were recruited near the end of the project period to evaluate this first phase of the
 
rural roads program under the supervision of DPR. The study showed that the participatory
 
approach had empowered communities to manage infrastructure.  Communities had become
 
involved and the rural road management system had been perfected.  A system of programming
 
community maintenance had been established, although it was still weak.  A system was set up to 
mobilize financing from road users, but it was not functioning well due to the limited resources in 
the rural communities.  The issues of making the strategy sustainable and extending it, how to
 
extend the sensitization process, and resource mobilization from users need to be considered
 
further.  These projects required large inputs from NGOs (up to 70% of cost).  It would be
 
important to explore ways of reducing this cost in order to make the approach more easily
 
replicable.
4.3  
Net Present Value/Economic rate of return:
Before appraisal optimal maintenance strategies were designed for paved and non-paved roads
 
which would minimize the present value of road maintenance costs.  Internal rates of return
 
(IRRs) were calculated for each road segment, using vehicle operating cost reduction as the
 
benefit and the HDM model.  The IRR for the paved road maintenance to be financed by IDA
 
under the project was calculated to be 460%.   There was an effort to optimize resource
 
- 9 -
Page  14
allocation for non-paved road segments financed by IDA in order to maximize the number of
 
usable segments.  IRRs for 51 segments varied from -2% to 340%.  The calculation of IRRs for 
rural roads was not considered feasible, but proxy measures were used for selection such as
 
population density and amount of agricultural production.
At the end of the project the DGTP was not able to undertake ex-post analysis to confirm the
 
economic costs and benefits of the project.  Because of the suspension, DGTP had not been able 
to hire  consultants to build the necessary road data base.  Therefore, it was not possible to
 
calculate IRRs for the improved roads.  Also, without consultant assistance it was not possible
 
even to collect data and calculate IRRs for a few sample road segments.  However, to provide an 
indication of the substantial benefit of the project, DGTP prepared lists of traffic counts for the
 
improved road segments, before and after the project.  Traffic levels were significantly higher on 
every road segment, despite the worsening economic conditions in the country and actual costs
 
are close to appraisal estimates, therefore suggesting a higher IRR than forecast.  Data from these 
traffic counts are presented in Annex 3.
4.4  
Financial rate of return:
Financial rates of return are not applicable to this type of project.
4.5  
Institutional development impact:
Substantial institutional development took place during the project in spite of the negative
 
economic and financial environment. The most important institutional development was the
 
development of the Road Fund (FER).  With its own assured sources of funding, it was able to 
finance the road maintenance program and contribute to the overhead costs of the project (see
 
Section 4.2 (B).
DGTP and DPR were able to develop as capable technical agencies in charge of the roads and
 
transport sectors.  DGTP hired about 15 technical experts (engineers, etc.) to assist in project
 
supervision.  They participated in strategy formulation and policy dialogue, and they developed
 
skills in planning, procurement and maintenance supervision.  When government funds were not 
available to pay salaries and overheads, it was agreed that the Road Maintenance Fund would
 
cover  the cost of  these 15 experts. The capacity built and maintained in this way continues to be 
available to manage road maintenance and transport.
DGT was intended to be the lead agency for the planning and policy formulation of road
 
transport, but it developed slowly.  There were budget constraints and changes in management.  
The Office of Road Safety (OSR), which had been newly established at the beginning of the
 
project, was to receive support, and  CCTA was to be created for vehicle inspection.  However, 
these agencies are not yet operational (see Section 4.2 C).   The Credit Agreement called for the 
creation of an Equipment Leasing Company (SLM) to make road maintenance equipment
 
available to private contractors.  It was created in 1998. However, it did not receive an expected 
FCFA 300 million from Government in initial capital.  Moreover, the ownership of the equipment 
has not been transferred by government to SLM.  Both factors constrain SLM’s ability to
 
- 10 -
Page  15
maintain the equipment and assure its availability.  SLM’s problems are further compounded by
 
the present economic situation.  Its equipment could fall into disrepair.  Given these problems,
 
divestiture should be considered.    
The development of the FER and of improved capacity in the DGTP were important
 
accomplishments of the project. The present process by which the FER is funded has the support 
of government and can be expected to continue. The strong team built within DGTP continues to 
work effectively.  The slow development of agencies in the road  transport sector and of the SLM 
has been less positive, but these components were less critical to the overall success of the
 
project.  On balance, institutional development is rated substantial.

Factors generally subject to government control:
The economic situation of the country deteriorated considerably during the project period.

6.  Sustainability
6.1
Rationale for sustainability rating:
The capacity built within FER and DGTP will contribute to the sustainability of the works
 
financed under project (see section 4.B). The FER has been funded for six years. It continues to 
collect fuel levies which will be used to continue maintaining the roads.  While the situation of the 
government budget continues to be difficult, it is likely that government will continue to support 
most of the overhead expenses of the road network maintenance agencies for the foreseeable
 
future to enable them to continue to manage road maintenance.  
During the project about 70% of the funds from the FER were used to pay for routine
 
maintenance.  This was sufficient to pay for 100% of routine maintenance cost, which is the
 
primary mission of FER.  Due to the condition of the network and the economic condition of the 
country whereby the government could not pay counterpart funding, funds  from FER were also 
used as a contribution  for periodic maintenance (20% of resources) and rural road maintenance
 
(6% of resources).  An  allocation was also made to cover FER's and part of DGTP's overheads 
(2% each of  resources).  The limited allocation (2% of the road fund resources)  to DGTP was a 
pragmatic solution, within FER's legal mandate, to help DGTP to manage the the road
 
maintenance program during this period of economic disruption.
The construction of a road data base and the transport sector data base is unlikely to continue
 
without further outside assistance.  The future of the OSR, CCTA and the SLM is also less clear, 
but these agencies are less critical for ensuring sustained road infrastructure management.   
Since 1998 program implementation and annual maintenance program have been financed by the
 
FER.  Because of the sound capacity built in FER and the DGTP, sustainability is rated likely. 
6.2
Transition arrangement to regular operations:
Roads which were improved under the project continue to be maintained by DGTP and DPR with 
funding from FER.

8. 
Lessons Learned
·
In projects aimed at broad sector reform, careful analysis, strategy formulation and
 
consensus building among government, the private sector and donors prior to projects are
 
important to achieve meaningful change.  This is especially important in projects involving major 
policy and institutional reforms.
·
As in several other African countries, a road fund financed from user fees is a very
 
important way to ensure the availability of funds for maintenance.  In this case, even when the
 
government lacked budgetary resources and government agencies lacked funds, the Road Fund
 
continued to be funded.  
·
Mobilizing local counterpart funding in rural areas for road maintenance is difficult.  The
 
overhead costs for community mobilization (fees to NGOs) are high and are likely to make
 
replicability difficult.  Additional means of financing rural road maintenance should be considered.
·
When a Road Fund is introduced, the responsibility of each agency should be spelled out 
clearly in the Manual of Procedures to eliminate the possibility of disagreement.  The Road Fund 
provided funds to DGTP which executed the works.  During the project conflicts arose between
 
the agencies concerning how the funds should be managed and which agency should contract the 
work.  The problem was resolved during the project by clarifying the role of each agency.  
·
 The small and medium contractors (PMEs) had difficulties developing under the new
 
maintenance system. It would have been useful to develop a system of assistance for PMEs prior 
- 14 -
Page  19
to or during the project.  In this case, other donors such as the UNDP could have financed such 
assistance during and after the project. It is recommended to accompany the privatization of road 
maintenance activities by a widespread training program for PMEs to quickly build up the
 
necessary capacity in the construction industry.

Avant- propos
du Ministre de l’Équipement
Le présent Rapport de Clôture sur le Projet de Transports Routiers (PTR) fait le bilan de l’exécution des 
objectifs physiques et de la mise en place d’institutions d’appui au développement du secteur des
 
transports routiers favorables à la croissance de l’économie et au recul de la pauvreté au Togo. 
Si le présent Rapport insiste sur l’importance du contexte historique, c’est pour situer, à un stade donné de 
notre développement, la direction historique prise permettant de fixer tout un chacun sur les évènements et 
les actes posés dans le temps, et leur impact. 
Si le présent Rapport insiste sur l’importance du contexte historique, c’est pour situer, à un stade donné de 
notre développement, la direction historique prise permettant de fixer tout un chacun sur les évènements et 
les actes posés dans le temps, et leur impact. 
L’un des objectifs du Projet reste la lutte contre la pauvreté, et les résultats atteints dans l’exécution du
 
PTR n’ont pas démérité, car le projet a joué un rôle de premier plan dans la vie des plus défavorisés et a 
porté son influence dans l’économie du marché. Les activités engendrées ont aussi contribué
 
considérablement à améliorer le niveau de vie des populations.  
Le Togo a réussi, grâce à des réformes inspirées de la logique du marché et du pragmatisme, à mettre en 
place un mécanisme stable de financement de l’entretien routier pour améliorer l’efficacité du secteur des 
transports routiers. Le bon fonctionnement du FER a incité les « petits » opérateurs à entreprendre des 
activités d’entretien routier à une échelle graduelle, en développant leurs capacités et leurs ressources,
 
devant des opportunités qui leurs sont offertes d’augmenter leur revenu. A travers les performances du
 
Fond d’Entretien Routier, le Rapport traite de l’égalité des chances et d’insertion de tous, et tout
 
particulièrement de la démarginalisation de ces « petits ». En effet, avant la mise en place du FER, on ne 
comptait pas plus de trois PME au Togo. Aujourd’hui, elles sont plus de trois cents, grâce à cette nouvelle 
institution qu’est le FER. La totalité des prestations d’études et de contrôle se faisait exclusivement par les 
consultants étrangers, ce qui n’est plus le cas aujourd’hui.
En contribuant à la mise en place d’institutions structurées et efficaces à l’instar du Fonds d’Entretien
 
Routier du Togo, la Banque a ainsi soutenu une vraie stratégie de lutte pour le combat de la pauvreté et de 
la corruption, du fait que cette institution gouvernementale comporte des mécanismes efficaces pour
 
contrôler le comportement des dirigeants, pour ne pas gâcher les ressources fournies par les parties
 
prenantes. Cette initiative correctement conçue a fait progresser le pays. Nous accordons aux
 
mécanismes institutionnels du FER toute la place qu’ils méritent dans le processus de développement du 
secteur des transports, dans le concept d’une interdépendance des Institutions avec les dimensions
 
humaines, physiques et macroéconomiques du développement,
Le Rapport admet que l’Emprunteur a eu des manquements, notamment, ceux liés aux retards de
 
- 27 -
Page  32
paiement de ses échéances financières dus aux contraintes financières isolées de l’heure, mais, de l’autre 
côté du Bailleur, il souligne ce qui semble être des attitudes non favorables à une réussite parfaite du
 
Projet.  
Le Rapport débouche sur une note de satisfaction quant aux résultats atteints qui sont conformes à la
 
déclaration de politique générale du gouvernement, de mai 1996, qui était la manifestation de sa volonté et 
de son engagement à mener toutes les réformes nécessaires à l’amélioration de l’efficacité du secteur des 
transports, afin d’assurer des bases solides à notre économie nationale, dans le cadre du programme de 
relance de l’économie du pays. Au cours de la période d’exécution du Projet, la collaboration de nos
 
partenaires en développement pour soutenir son succès à travers leur participation au financement du
 
programme du PTR, a été cohérente et substantielle.
 
Fermement, le Rapport
 
dégage un héritage positif certain pour un développement durable. Ces grands
 
enseignements, ainsi que l’analyse détaillée et les nombreuses réflexions avancées tout au long du
 
Rapport, aideront la Banque et les Responsables de l’Emprunteur à soutenir, dans le présent et le futur, les 
institutions du secteur des transports routiers mises en place, qui assurent une croissance stable
 
bénéfique pour tous et, par là, améliorer le niveau de service du réseau routier dans l’intérêt des usagers 
de la route.
 
Nous ne pouvons clôturer le Projet de Transports Routiers du Togo, initié à partir de 1995, sans remercier 
le premier Chef de Projet de la Banque (M. Hedi Larbi)  qui en a assuré un pilotage dynamique ; nous
 
tenons à lui exprimer notre profonde gratitude, pour son assistance empreinte de professionnalisme pour 
la mise en place du PTR, avec comme soubassement,  la somme d’études stratégiques et d’expériences 
concrètes de la Banque et de l’Emprunteur. Il est généralement dit que tout ce qui est bien préparé ne peut 
échouer complètement. Nous remercions également tous les experts de la Banque Mondiale et de
 
l’Emprunteur qui ont contribué à la réalisation des objectifs atteints du Projet de Transports Routiers.
Enfin, il faut que les Gestionnaires des activités de maintenance des infrastructures routières maintiennent 
le cap d’une gestion saine et transparente des crédits alloués, afin que les Institutions d’appui au
 
management des infrastructures routières continuent à jouer leur rôle de structure de garantie pour les
 
bailleurs de Fonds et pour nos partenaires au développement, qui accompagnent notre pays dans sa
 
politique d’entretien de la route. La relance de l’économie nationale est aussi à ce prix, car, la route du
 
développement  passe par le développement de la route.

RESUME DU RAPPORT DE CLOTURE
Introduction
. Le présent document résume d’une manière la plus simple, le Rapport de Clôture du  Projet 
de Transports Routiers (PTR) dont les objectifs ont été consacrés : (a) à la réhabilitation des routes
 
nationales, (b) au renforcement institutionnel, (c)  à la sécurité routière, et (d) à la réhabilitation des pistes, 
pour lequel un crédit IDA 2989-TO d’un montant de 50 millions USD (équivalant à 30 milliards de FCFA) a 
été approuvé le 14 octobre 1997 et est entré en vigueur le 12 janvier 1998. Le Rapport vise à établir un 
bilan final de la réalisation des objectifs de développement du projet et, à partir de ce bilan, proposer les 
mesures appropriées que l’Emprunteur juge nécessaires pour recadrer la structure et le plan de mise en 
œuvre de futurs projets du genre sur financement de la Banque.
Les performances IDA du passé
.
 
Dans le passé, l’IDA a apporté son appui au secteur des transports
 
depuis 1968, et a pris la tête de l’assistance des bailleurs de fonds actifs dans ce domaine. L’IDA a financé 
8 projets de transports, pour un montant de 183 millions de dollars (soit environ 110 milliards de FCFA) ; 
aux sept autres projets achevés il y a longtemps de cela, le présent projet PTR, en cours de clôture est le 
huitième. L’IDA a soutenu le domaine routier en tant qu’infrastructure de transport la plus importante, mais 
a aussi soutenu le transport ferroviaire. Des 8 projets de l’IDA dans le secteur routier, 5 portaient sur les 
routes nationales, 1 sur les pistes rurales, 1 sur les deux types de réseau, et l’actuel, en cours de clôture, 
sur les deux types de réseau y compris les ouvrages et le volet institutionnel. Pris ensemble, les projets 
IDA constituent un effort prolongé très significatif qui a obtenu des résultats substantiels, malgré certaines 
insatisfactions. 
Le Projet PTR, une véritable révolution
. Le Projet PTR a été une véritable révolution dans la mise en 
place des réformes solides préalables à sa mise en vigueur. La mise en place d'un fonds d'entretien
 
routier, 2ème génération, faisait partie des conditions d'existence du projet, voire l'une des premières
 
conditionnalités. Le programme discuté lors de la préparation du projet a servi de base du programme
 
d’investissement prioritaire quinquennal. Ce programme, préparé après une large consultation de toutes 
les parties prenantes, a obtenu l’appui des Bailleurs de Fonds auxquels il a été présenté en 1997. Un des 
succès du projet PTR tient dans le fait qu’il a un caractère de « projet glissant », qui est un mécanisme de 
gestion souple inappliqué pour les projets précédents. Un projet glissant  jouit d’un programme qui permet 
la mobilité ou le glissement de la destination des crédits alloués par les bailleurs de fonds à l’intérieur d’un 
programme prioritaire adopté sur une période déterminée de sa réalisation. Toutes les mesures
 
préconisées aux différentes étapes pour la gestion du secteur ont été prises dans la grande majorité par 
les ministères concernés.
Les performances physiques du PTR.
 
Les projets financés sous la houlette du Projet de Transports
 
Routiers sont de trois natures, à savoir : i) les projets financés par le prêt IDA proprement dit (Cr 2989-TO), 
ii) les projets financés par les autres bailleurs de fonds pour la réhabilitation des routes bitumées (BAD,
 
Fonds Kowétien, Fonds OPEP), et  iii) les projets d’entretien courant sur financement local FER. La mise et 
le maintien en place des crédits de l’IDA pour financer le PTR étaient conditionnés par l’existence d’un
 
Fonds d’Entretien Routier efficace pour l’entretien courant et sa bonne gestion. Les résultats physiques
 
- 29 -
Page  34
globaux de maintenance des infrastructures routières, tous financements confondus, sont :
·
Réhabilitation de routes revêtues de 390 km environ ;
·
Grosses réparations de plus de 200 km de routes revêtues ;
·
Mise à niveau de 1200 km de routes en terre nationales ;
·
Réhabilitation de plusieurs ouvrages critiques sur les routes et pistes ;
·
Elimination des points critiques et rechargement du réseau de 1000 km environ de pistes rurales ;
·
Entretien courant de l’ensemble du réseau routier national, des pistes rurales classées et des 
ouvrages critiques.
Le FER, une réalité
. Malgré les efforts de l’IDA et des autres bailleurs de fonds, le problème persistant a 
été, dans le temps, celui de l’organisation et du financement de l’entretien routier. Ce qui est chose faite 
aujourd’hui par la mise en place d’un Fonds d’Entretien Routier autonome, nouvelle génération dont
 
l’organisation et le fonctionnement ont été jusqu’à présent satisfaisants aux dires des audits commandités 
annuellement par les bailleurs de fonds. En effet, six (6) ans après la mise en place du PTR et du FER, le 
Togo est entrain de réussir son pari sur la mise en place d’un Fonds d’Entretien Routier autonome (FER), 
nouvelle génération, au lendemain du sortir de la crise socio-économique des années 90. Pour pérenniser 
l’acquis du FER, des mesures et dispositions d’accompagnement solides, réalistes et durables sont prises 
pour consolider la volonté politique du gouvernement de promouvoir le secteur privé dans le processus de 
privatisation de l’exécution des tâches d’entretien routier, jadis réalisées par les services techniques de
 
l’administration sous leur auto-contrôle, afin : i) d’assurer un niveau de service acceptable aux usagers de 
la route et ; ii) de conserver les acquis physiques du patrimoine routier grâce aux efforts des bailleurs de 
fonds pour leur réhabilitation/reconstruction.
Au niveau des ratios d’investissements financiers
. Le PTR, malgré les péripéties qu’il a connues, a
 
atteint des performances louables qui sont de 94 % pour le montant total engagé et de 67,8 % de
 
décaissements au niveau de l’IDA. Toutefois, il est à noter que le montant des décomptes des travaux
 
effectivement réalisés avant la date de clôture du PTR, soit le 30 septembre 2002 et non encore payés 
s’élève à 730 901 798 F CFA dont 687 972 174 F CFA sous financement IDA. Le complément est pris en 
compte par les crédits du Fonds d’Entretien Routier du Togo, gestions 2002 et 2003. La prise en compte 
de ces derniers travaux ferait ainsi passer le taux de décaissement, sur les ressources IDA, à 70 %, soit 
un montant global d’environ 23, 0 milliards de F CFA. Sur la même période, la contre partie du
 
Gouvernement est estimée à 1,0 milliard de FCFA, assurée par le FER qui totalise 28,0 milliards de FCFA 
de ressources mobilisées pour soutenir l’action des bailleurs de fonds pour l’entretien routier.
Au niveau du renforcement institutionnel
. 
a)
En ce qui concerne les performances institutionnelles,  nous résumons que le PTR a été une 
motivation et une occasion bien saisie
 pour : i) former les cadres gestionnaires et ingénieurs pour le
 
management du PTR, afin de relever leur niveau de compétence en matière de procédures de passations 
des marchés, pour mieux répondre au souci de bonne gouvernance et de gestion  transparente et
 
équitable des crédits mis à notre disposition, telles que recommandées par les dirigeants politiques aux 
maîtres d’ouvrage délégués, pour reprendre l’écho des bailleurs de fonds exigeant de l’Emprunteur la
 
bonne utilisation des crédits alloués, ii) établir, entre les acteurs impliqués dans les tâches d’entretien
 
routier, - qui sont les cadres gestionnaires et ingénieurs de l’administration, les entrepreneurs, les
 
fournisseurs, les prestataires de services et les consultants privés, - des règles de jeu claires, connues,
 
comprises et acceptées de tous, car, ces règles définissent les procédures de passation des marchés
 
- 30 -
Page  35
publics et de gestion de l’exécution des contrats d’entretien routier, iii) au niveau de la DGTP, réorganiser, 
renforcer en nombre et en qualité les effectifs des cadres pour la gestion technique et financière des
 
activités de maintenance des infrastructures routières, bien que le besoin se fasse sentir encore, iv)
 
garantir aux opérateurs économiques (entreprises, fournisseurs et consultants) l’égalité d’accès aux
 
marchés publics dans un esprit de compétitivité et de concurrence  loyales. 
b)
La création de la Commission Interne d’Attribution des Marchés (CIAM) au sein du ministère 
chargé des T.P
., et pilotée par la DGTP, a été renforcée par la mise en place, dans chaque Direction
 
Régionale, d’une Commission Interne Régionale d’Attribution des Marchés (CIRAM), pour mieux
 
rapprocher les managers des opérateurs économiques, et permettre une consommation rapide et efficace 
des crédits, dans le respect des procédures de passation des marchés.
c)
Avant la mise en place du FER
, on ne comptait pas plus d’une trentaine de PME au Togo dans le 
secteur BTP pour l’entretien routier. Aujourd’hui, elles sont plus de trois cents, grâce à cette nouvelle
 
institution qu’est le FER. Les consultants locaux dépassent une trentaine. 
d)
Un acquis irréversible reste la performance atteinte dans l’exécution des travaux de
 
cantonnage
 (désherbage) sur les routes, qui sont exécutés maintenant selon le « Single Man Contractor 
», ou contrat individuel, sous le contrôle des Directions régionales et financés intégralement par le FER. Le 
taux de couverture du réseau de routes et pistes nationales est de 100 %, pour un rendement moyen
 
d’exécution de 97 % qui jadis était  presque nul. Au total, plus de 800 agents des Travaux Publics ont été 
licenciés et transformés en cantonniers sous contrats individuels. 
e)
La nouvelle Société de Location de Matériel (SLM) 
est mise en place depuis 1997, mais, à la 
date de clôture du projet, elle est restée toujours sans moyens adéquats de soutien, et le capital de 300 
millions de FCFA annoncé au départ n’est toujours pas mis en place.
f)
En ce qui concerne particulièrement la Banque de Données Routières (BDR),
 il faut noter que 
cette structure qui devrait être mise en place dans le cadre du PTR au niveau de la DGTP, comme outil de 
management du réseau et de planification des investissements, n’a pu l’être. Ce n’est pas grave, surtout, si 
la Banque mondiale avait concédé que les experts de la BAD maintiennent ce volet dans leur plan de
 
financement lors de l’évaluation de leur projet en 2001. Le principe du caractère glissant du Projet de
 
Transports Routiers selon sa conception initiale a été complètement ignoré par la Banque en se cachant 
sous le voile de double emploi alors qu’on y était pas encore. Ce mauvais agencement a fait perdre à la 
DGTP cette éventuelle opportunité pour la mise en place d’une Banque de Données Routières (BDR), au 
cas échéant, par la BAD.  
g)
Recentrage des activités de la DGTP.
 Les réformes institutionnelles survenues avec le PTR ont 
permis aussi, au niveau des Travaux Publics, la refonte profonde du système de gestion de l’entretien
 
routier avec la création d’un Fonds d’Entretien Routier, avec un Conseil des Routes, dont on a déjà  parlé, 
et la restructuration de la Direction Générale des Travaux Publics (DGTP), par une recentralisation de ses 
activités. La DGTP a également pu, grâce à sa restructuration, acquérir et capitaliser des compétences en 
matière de maîtrise d’ouvrage et de gestion financière et technique des activités d’entretien du réseau
 
routier, tout en bénéficiant de l’appui des crédits du FER pour son fonctionnement minimum, puisque la
 
DGTP ne reçoit plus de crédits de fonctionnement en provenance du trésor public (budget de l’Etat) depuis 
le démarrage du Projet. Cette capitalisation institutionnelle lui a permis d’élaborer régulièrement et de

Page  36
mettre en œuvre un programme d’entretien routier bien suivi et encadré, financé sur les ressources du
 
FER, et également d’assurer la mise en œuvre des composantes du PTR liées à la maintenance des
 
routes. 
h)
Au niveau des Transports
, les réformes prévues n’ont pu être totalement conduites à terme,
 
malgré les efforts qui ont été mobilisés pour leur mise en œuvre. Au-delà des textes qui sont déjà pris sur 
la réglementation du secteur, le PTR devait assurer la mise en place d’une Banque de Données pour le 
secteur des transports et également l’informatisation des documents de transport. Les conditions de
 
clôture du Projet n’ont pu permettre le financement des équipements et de l’assistance identifiés, pour
 
rendre opérationnels l’OSR et le CCTA dont les bâtiments physiques sont terminés.
i)
Bonne gouvernance et lutte contre la corruption
.
 
Les médias libres d’information, qui sont, bon

Facteurs de risques liés aux agences d’exécution et de supervision
. Le retard dans l’exécution du
 
PTR est un facteur de risque maîtrisable, en bonne partie aussi, par les 2 partenaires en développement, à 
savoir, l’agence d’exécution (DGTP) et l’équipe de supervision de la Banque, pour ce qui est de leur
 
ressort. Après la revue à mi-parcours du projet en septembre 2000, la minimisation des risques de retard 
dans l’exécution du projet n’a pas été de mise, au sein des 2 équipes d’encadrement du projet,  équipe 
togolaise chargée du management du projet, et équipe Banque mondiale, chargée de la supervision du
 
projet ; à défaut d’une continuité du projet dans une confiance renouvelée, ceci devait se faire par une
 
harmonisation des points de vue sur les procédures de « traitement et de présentation des dossiers entre 
le Bailleur et l’Emprunteur » pour permettre de mieux avancer les affaires dans un climat de confiance

Mesures d’amélioration pour les futurs projets
. Pour les futurs projets du genre sur financement de la 
Banque, les améliorations essentielles doivent être portées sur les points qui ont été développés dans le 
rapport, que ce soit au niveau de l’Emprunteur ou de celui du Bailleur. Ces mesures concernent
 
essentiellement :
-
L’amélioration des performances financières et administratives,
-
Le maintien d’une gestion efficace des institutions de l’Emprunteur,
-
Le maintien des bonnes capacités managériales de l’Emprunteur,
-
Un bon suivi et encadrement des PME et BME nationaux par l’Emprunteur,
-
Une meilleure implication des PME dans les grands travaux routiers,  avec l’appui du bailleur,
-
Une culture des bonnes relations de partenariat par tous les experts de la Banque.
-
Une revue du nouveau CCAG pour une meilleure adaptation aux réalités
CONCLUSION 
En conclusion générale, le Projet de Transports Routiers initié au Togo en 1997 a été un nouveau départ, 
une renaissance, car il a inauguré une nouvelle ère dans le domaine  de réformes courageuses qui ont
 
permis la mise en place d’institutions structurées et solides pour garantir la pérennité du développement
 
durable du secteur des transports routiers. Les résultats atteints dans l’ensemble sont satisfaisants. 
Le grand capital est la restructuration et la refonte du système d’entretien routier suivi de la création d’un 
Fonds d’Entretien Routier, 2ème génération, grâce auxquelles les agences d’exécution du PTR ont pu
 
acquérir et capitaliser des compétences en matière de maîtrise d’ouvrage et de bonne gouvernance. Les 
audits, pour leur bienfait, seront toujours maintenus pour le contrôle de gestion des activités des
 
institutions  permanentes créées au niveau des agences nationales d’appui au secteur des transports
 
routiers (DGTP, DFER. DGT, SLM, ect..)

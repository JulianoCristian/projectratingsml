I. DISBURSEMENT PROFILE

                   1. PROJECT CONTEXT, DEVELOPMENT OBJECTIVES AND DESIGN

1.1 Context at Appraisal

After becoming independent, in 1961, Madagascar witnessed a long period of economic decline
due to poor governance and protectionist and inward looking policies. This decline resulted in a
25% reduction in per capita income. In the late 1980s and 1990s, two liberalization episodes
improved economic performance. After the second episode, the country experienced a period of
growth powered by the dynamisms of the industries that benefited from preferential trade access.
Between 1997 and 2001, GDP grew at about 4% per year, while inflation was kept under control.
Despite this economic growth, by the time the project was designed in 1999 income per capita
was US$ 235, one of the lowest in Sub-Saharan Africa.


The Second Health Sector Support Project (CRESAN II) built on the results and lessons learned
from the Health Sector Improvement Project (CRESAN I). The project was designed to support,
in collaboration with other donors, the implementation of the Government's Health Policy
adopted in 1996 and the National Health Development Plan (Plan Directeur 1997- 2000). As
stated in the PAD, in 1998 a preliminary financial gap for the implementation of this Plan was
estimated and IDA and AfDB, as donors of last resort, agreed to finance the activities for which
no other funding could be identified. The designed project covered most of the strategic priorities
as set by the National Health Policy and Plan but it focused mainly on increasing coverage of
basic health care services, and in the fight against malaria. Finally, the project followed the CAS
1997 objectives of developing human capital through basic health care, and strengthening of
public sector to improve its ability to deliver quality services.

1.2 Original Project Development Objectives (PDO) and Key Indicators

The project's overall development objective was to contribute to the improvement of the health
status of the population through more accessible and better quality health services. Specific
development objectives were to: (i) improve quality of, and access to primary health care services
with a focus on rural areas; (ii) support priority health programs with emphasis on endemic
infectious diseases, reproductive health (including family planning, sexually transmitted diseases
and HIV/AIDS) and nutrition; and (iii) strengthen sector management and administrative capacity
within the Ministry of Health, and at provincial and district levels, to enable successful
decentralization and sector reform. While the project's main focus was on primary health services
and malaria reduction (65% of the proceeds of the credit), the project also contributed to
reproductive health and nutrition activities and to the strengthening of health sector capacity. By
supporting the implementation of the health sector's Policy and Development Plan, the project
also contributed to the ongoing health sector reform process.

The key performance indicators (KPI) of the project as stated in the PAD were: (i) increased
accessibility to health facilities (PHC facilities within a 5Km radius); (ii) increased outpatient
health facility utilization rates; (iii) increased district hospital utilization rates; (iv) increased
contraceptive prevalence rates; (v) increased child immunization rates; (vi) reduced hospital
fatality rate in malnourished children hospitalized for nutrition rehabilitation; and (vii) maintain
HIV prevalence rate in pregnant women. The last KPI did not appear in the CA but was kept in
all monitoring documents.

1.3 Revised PDO and Key Indicators, and reasons/justification

Even though the project was restructured in 2003 and additional financing was approved in 2005
its PDO was not changed nor were the key development indicators.

1.4 Main Beneficiaries

As stated in the PAD, the project was intended to benefit the entire population; however, "its
thrusts and the areas selected for financial support favor the poor, the rural population, women,
and children".     In the Credit Agreement, though, this distinction between urban and rural
population did not appear.

1.5 Original Components

Component 1: Development of District Health Services: This component was a critical one to
ensure reaching the DO of increasing coverage of quality health services. The objective as it
appeared in the PAD was to help develop primary health care and first referral level services in
districts, to improve geographical accessibility to services and to foster the quality of care. This
component was meant to finance civil works for health centers and district hospitals based on a
Health Infrastructure Development Plan, a condition for effectiveness of this project. This
component would also fund the procurement of drugs and provision of appropriate logistical
support to sustain the functioning of the newly constructed or re-habilitated facilities.        In
accordance with the PAD, this component was also meant to support the decentralized health
administrations by constructing and equipping them (DIRDS and SSD); however, this did not
appear in the credit agreement. To ensure that the decentralized health administrations were also
supported, when the project was restructured in 2003, the wording of the CA changed to ensure
that the acquisition of drugs and provision of logistical support benefited not only the health
facilities but the "efficient functioning of health districts".

Component 2: Infectious Disease Reduction: This component aimed at reducing the spread of
the major communicable diseases in particular malaria, plague, schistosomiasis, and tuberculosis.
In the case of malaria, the objective in the highlands was to maintain the reduction in malaria
transmission which has been achieved with support from CRESAN I. In the lowlands, the
objective was to support the Government strategy aiming at reducing morbidity and mortality
associated with malaria without compromising the building up of immunity by inappropriate
interventions. In the case of Tuberculosis, the aim of this component was to improve access to
diagnosis, prevention, and treatment, reduce drop-out rates, monitor the effectiveness and
efficiency of TB drugs, and decentralize and strengthen supervision. For plague, the objectives
were to improve case detection and management and the prevention and control of outbreaks.
Finally, for schistosomiasis, the objective was to contain the epidemic. This component would
finance drugs, insecticides, ITNs, development of appropriate technical means designed to
improve prevention and control of plague outbreaks, carrying out screening campaigns and
acquisition of drugs and laboratory equipment necessary to contain the spread of schistosomiasis.
In the credit agreement the objective appears slightly different as this component was supposed to
develop an infectious disease reduction program (IDRP).

Component 3: Family Planning and Reproductive Health (including STD/HIV/AIDS):
According to the PAD, this component was aimed at improving access and quality to
reproductive health services and to sexually transmitted disease services including HIV/AIDS. In
the Credit Agreement the objective of this component is stated differently. According to the CA,
the objective was to develop and carry out a national reproductive health program designed to:


                                                    2

(a) promote safe motherhood and improve case management of high risk pregnancies; (b)
increase access to family planning services including the provision of modern contraceptives; and
(c) carrying out of appropriate information education and communication campaign design to
increase awareness on adolescent sexuality, STI, including HIV/AIDS. This difference between
the PAD and the CA was mainly a difference in wording; the PAD described this component in
terms of its objectives while the CA described it in terms of activities.

The project would finance the provision of logistical support including drugs, laboratory
equipment and diagnostic tools required to enhance the delivery of adequate family planning and
reproductive health services.

Component 4: Child Nutrition Services: According to the PAD, this component aimed at
developing the Government's capacity to deal with severely malnourished children referred to its
nationwide health facilities. This was to be achieved by strengthening the referral system's
capacity to provide nutrition rehabilitation with properly equipped and trained staff at referral
centers provisioned with the necessary specialized food rations. The project would finance
training of health district staff, refurbishing of health centers to handle severely malnourished
children, providing specialized rations, and assuring adequate supervision and program
monitoring.

Preventive community-based nutrition activities were financed through another Bank project, the
second community nutrition project. CRESAN II only financed therapeutic care for severely
malnourished children.


Component 5: Institutional Strengthening: The objective of this component was to support
the development of technical and administrative capacities of MINSANPFPS with an emphasis at
provincial and district levels.

1.6 Revised Components

The project became effective in August 2000. By then the country had been affected by three
major cyclones that destroyed most of the health infrastructure in two provinces. It was then
decided that the first component would only rehabilitate and re-construct damaged facilities
instead of constructing new ones. The Infrastructure Development Plan (IDP) was then waived
as conditionality for the effectiveness of the project.

The credit agreement was amended several times (see table in annex 2), the first time to respond
to the 2001-2002 political crisis. It was also amended in 2005 to give supplemental financing and
in 2006 to extend the projects closing date to December 31st 2007. Additionally, the project
proceeds were re-allocated many times. All these changes were approved by management (the
Country Director and/or the VP in cases where his approval was needed).

At the end of the 2001-2002 political and socio-economic crisis the new Government presented to
the international community its reconstruction strategy. To better respond to this strategy, and to
solve some issues that had originally slowed disbursements, the CA was first amended in early
2003. This amendment changed the project's first component to ensure that the procurement of
drugs and the logistical support would help not just the functioning of facilities but the
functioning of the entire health district. This change allowed direct support to the district health
services (SSD) as well as provincial/regional services. This amendment also transferred all
construction of basic health centers and district buildings to the Community Development Fund;



                                                  3

however, the Fund did not agree to continue the work started by CRESAN II so in the end all
reconstruction work was financed by the project.

This amendment also changed the second component to ensure that the screening campaigns,
drugs, and laboratory equipment were not limited to fight schistosomiasis (as the original CA
seemed to imply) but all major infectious diseases.

The third component was also restructured in 2003 to transfer all STI/HIV/AIDS preventive
activities to the World Bank financed Multisectoral STI/HIV/AIDS project. This amendment
also extended SALAMA's list of drugs and supplies.

Finally, this amendment changed the disbursement percentage of some categories of expenditure
and increased the amount of the credit allocated to institutional capacity. After this restructuring,
disbursement increased significantly all through the rest of the project life.


In 2005 a supplemental credit of US$ 18 million was approved. As most of the proceeds of the
original credit were used to respond to external shocks such as cyclones and a political crisis, the
Government requested this additional financing to ensure the achievement of the PDO.

1.7 Other significant changes

The project financed activities to promote and improve dental care; particularly, the project
financed the construction, rehabilitation, and equipment of dental services and the fluoridation
and ionization of salt. However, support to dental services was not included in the PAD, the
credit agreement, or any of its amendments.


                    2. KEY FACTORS AFFECTING IMPLEMENTATION AND OUTCOMES

2.1 Project Preparation, Design and Quality at Entry

This project improved upon the design of CRESAN I by taking many of the lessons learned from
that project (as listed in the ICR) into its design and implementation. In contrast to CRESAN I,
this project supported a comprehensive policy framework and a sector strategy, it had a list of
agreed performance indicators, and its design and implementation had much better coordination
with donors and other stakeholders.


Although health status depends on many factors often outside the health sector, the PDO focused
on the health services that are more effective in treating and in some cases preventing the major
causes of mortality and morbidity in the country: major infectious diseases, reproductive health
and nutrition. The development objectives were also very responsive to the country's context and
they proved rather flexible which allowed the project to respond to difficult circumstances:
cyclones and political crisis. Nevertheless, to reach the PDO the design of the project only
focused in geographical barriers to access health services and did not take into consideration
financial barriers which are more important. However, at the time the project was appraised, WB
operations only focused on health care supply rather than demand issues. The follow-up WB-
financed support program in the country, Madagascar Sustainable Health System Development
Project, addresses these issues and includes the piloting of insurance schemes.




                                                  4

2.2 Implementation

a) Factors outside the control of government or implementing agency: During the
implementation of the project there were major cyclones in the country; in 2000 alone 3 major
cyclones destroyed a large share of the health infrastructure in at least two provinces. There was
also a major cyclone in 2003. In addition, the electoral crisis of 2001 and 2002 brought to a halt
Government activities for more than six months.          The crisis was produced by a contested
presidential election in December 2001. During the crisis the country had virtually two different
Governments and the highlands were isolated from the costal areas by a blockade and the
destruction of several bridges. As consequence of the crisis, GDP decreased by about 15%, and
due to the insecurity many health centers lost personnel and utilization of health services declined.
After gaining international recognition, the new government presented to the international
community its reconstruction strategy which included among other things the temporal
elimination of user fees for health services. The World Bank endorsed this program. The Interim
CAS in October 2002 expressed the Government's and Bank's intention to restructure CRESAN
II to better respond to the effects of the crisis and also to solve a few issues that had originally
slowed the disbursement of funds, as even before the crisis the project had a slow start.

b) Factors subject to Government control: The failure to deposit counterpart funds and to
develop an Infrastructure Development Plan led to postponing the effectiveness date for this
project. The IDP was later eliminated as a condition although the government was supposed to
do this to ensure a more rational development of the delivery system. This IDP was never done;
although a health map to facilitate planning and development of the health infrastructure system
was financed by the project.        The failure to deposit counterpart funds also slowed the
implementation of activities at different points in time. Additionally, the many changes in the
organization of the MINSANPFPS, especially after the 2001-2002 crisis, affected the
implementation of the project, especially the activities financed through the capacity building
component. Finally, at the very beginning of the project one of the problems that led to slow
disbursement was the insufficient number of staff in the implementation agency to cope with the
management of two large projects: IDA and AfDB. This problem did not last long though.


c) Implementing Agency Control: One of the factors that contributed to many of the
project's successes was the managerial capacity and flexibility of the implementation agency,
particularly after the crisis.

2.3 Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) Design, Implementation and Utilization

The original M&E as it appeared in the PAD included a group of relevant key performance
indicators. However the entire M&E framework was complex and included indicators that could
not measure actual outcomes from the project. There were more than 120 indicators of which
more than 60 were indicators of achievement of planned activities. Due to the complexity of the
original M&E design, early in the project, in its first supervision mission, a new set of indicators
was agreed. The number of indicators was greatly reduced and only indicators for health status
and indicators to monitor the progress of each component of the project were kept. 24 indicators
were kept to measure most of the components of the project.              Only for the institutional
strengthening component the indicators were not specified but left as general indicators of
physical and financial execution. Unfortunately, no target was clearly established for many of
these indicators.    Reliable information on most of these indicators was available, though.
CRESAN I helped build capacity in the Ministry information system and this helped considerably
in the data collection for the M&E framework of the second project.


                                                  5

Throughout the life of the project some high level outcome indicators were monitored such as
infant, maternal mortality, and HIV prevalence. However, these high level indicators are not as
useful to monitor progress towards the PDO, as the impact of project activities on these indicators
is difficult to measure in such a short period of time and as they also depend on many other
factors outside the control of the project.    This is especially the case for child survival which
depends on many factors outside the health system. It is important to note, however, that by the
time this project was designed and during most of its implementation, these were common
indicators used in similar projects in the Bank. This has recently changed given the limitations
associated with these indicators.


Finally, CRESAN II changed considerably during its implementation but the indicators were
never changed to reflect this. For instance, early on it was decided that the project would only
rehabilitate facilities. However, the first three KPIs, aimed at measuring geographical access to
health services, were kept while there were no indicators to show any progress in the quality of
services offered. Similarly, another KPI was HIV/AIDS prevalence rate among pregnant women.
This indicator was never changed even though very early on in the project all activities related to
HIV/AIDS were transferred to another WB financed project.

2.4 Safeguard and Fiduciary Compliance

In general, the project design and implementation complied with all safeguards and fiduciary
policies and requirements. All through out the project there were no major issues related to
procurement or financial management with exception of the Government's failure to deposit
counterpart funds in time. Indeed, the exceptional performance in financial management of the
project's PIU made possible a change in the Bank's disbursement for the new project, SHSDP. In
the new project disbursements will be done in advance based on the estimates provided in the
Financial Management Report. Before, payments were made contingent on the presentation of a
statement of expenditure.

In its environmental assessment, the project was rated C in the PAD. This was partly because the
project was supposed to use environmentally friendly insecticides (pyretroids) in limited areas for
indoor spraying. However, during the first years of implementation the insecticide procured was
DDT. There should have been then, according to Bank's operational policy, an assessment of
"the country's regulatory framework and institutions to promote and support safe, effective, and
environmentally sound pest management", at a minimum it should have been noted whether the
country had restrictions on the distribution and use of DDT as the Bank cannot procure it if these
restrictions are not in place. The Bank's supervision team had originally opposed the procurement
of DDT with the credit. However, the Government insisted on using it as WHO had given poor
countries a 10 year moratorium (starting 1998) to continue its use. In addition, by the time the
project became effective, DDT was the least expensive alternative, and the Government had at
least 6 months supply of it. Eventually the Bank team accepted the procurement of DDT on the
condition that a study would be conducted on possible alternatives so that other insecticides could
be used earlier than what was established by the Moratorium. The procurement of DDT was
always done through WHO and under their supervision and many measures were taken to ensure
an environmentally sound use of this insecticide (e.g. personnel were trained; storage of DDT
followed rigorous measures; the cleaning of equipment was done far from households, rivers, or
wells, etc.). By 2004 the project started to procure alternatives to DDT and by 2005 it stopped all
DDT procurement.




                                                  6

In contrast to the original credit, the supplemental was classified B and all the procedures related
to a category B project were followed. The Integrated Safeguard Data Sheet created for the
supplemental listed all possible safeguard issues and their management. By then the Government
had stopped using DDT for insecticide campaigns and a medical waste management plan had
already been prepared for the HIV/AIDS project.

2.5 Post-completion Operation/Next Phase

The financing of some of the activities of CRESAN II will be continued by the follow-up WB
financed project, Madagascar Sustainable Health System Development Project (SHSDP). This
project will continue the support to the districts health systems; in particular, it will continue
financing the district PTAs (annual work programs) and the salaries of the regional "gestionnaire
comptables" or accounting managers. This support will be progressively decreased under the
assumption that the national budget will eventually finance them completely. At the moment,
SHSDP provides 75% of the salaries of the accounting managers. The new project will also
finance some of the activities related to the fight against infectious diseases as financier of last
resort. Other activities will be financed by other sources, for instance, malaria activities will be
fully financed by both the Global Fund and the Presidential Malaria Initiative that is now funding
indoor spraying in the highlands. Finally, SHSDP will also finance activities related to maternal
and reproductive health.

All activities related to the treatment of severely malnourished children have been taken over by
the ONN (Officine National de Nutrition) which is now funding them.

There is evidence that the capacity built at the deconcentrated levels of MINSANPFPS will be
sustained. For instance, the project created capacity to develop district development plans and
annual work plans (PTAs). The districts are and will continue preparing these PTAs as they are
now the basis of the program budgets and of budget discussions.

Concerning the functioning of some of the equipment and means of transportation (ambulances,
motorbikes, QUADS, etc.) procured by CRESAN II; there is evidence of lack of maintenance as
not enough resources are set aside for this, limiting the long term use of this equipment.


                            3.     ASSESSMENT OF OUTCOMES

3.1 Relevance of Objectives, Design and Implementation

The objectives of the project are still relevant today. Improving access and quality of health
services, controlling malaria, reducing maternal and child mortality, controlling the spread of
HIV/AIDS, and implementing an effective family planning strategy are some of the commitments
of the Madagascar Action Plan (MAP) 2007-2012 (a second generation poverty reduction
strategy). The Bank's CAS 2007-2011 aims at contributing to these goals by helping to improve
access and quality of services. The original project aimed at the same goals. However, in the
case of access to services, the project only tried to improve geographical access; even though,
financial access was and remains the largest barrier for service utilization in the country. Data
from different EPM1 surveys (e.g. 2002, 2004, and 2005) suggest that the main reason for non-

utilization of services has been financial barriers, even more important than geographical access
to services. In 2004 and 2005 among those ill that did not use a health service (excluding those
who did not use a service because the illness was not serious) more than 50% of them indicated
that financial problems were the main reasons for no consultation while distance was the main
reason for 20% of respondents.         In 2002 when user fees were temporarily eliminated, the
percentages were 38% and 27% respectively.

The design of the project emphasized support to different vertical programs such as malaria,
bilharzias, plague, and others. However, little was done to ensure a more integrated support to
the health system. In the implementation of the project, though, this integration was promoted in
different ways: (i) support to an integrated packages of services included in the PTAs; (ii) the
promotion of social marketing of a package of consumables from different programs (e.g. anti-
malarials, contraceptives, ITNs, etc.); and (iii) the support to the installation of integrated
surveillance posts (e.g. malaria, arbovirosis, others).


Finally, the large support that the project gave to the CRENIs (intensive nutritional recovery
centers) was justified given the urgent situation generated by the 2001-2002 political crisis.
Currently, the Government is trying to emphasize prevention and ambulatory treatment to avoid
the need for hospitalization. This is a more cost-effective approach, but could not have responded
in time to the urgent needs created by the crisis.

3.2 Achievement of Project Development Objectives

The main development objective of the project, to improve population health through more access
and better quality of health services, was achieved as access to some key services increased (e.g.
family planning, treatment of severely malnourished children, childhood immunizations, malaria
preventive and curative care, mass treatment for bilharzias and filariosis, etc.) or was maintained
thanks to the rehabilitation and equipment of facilities damaged by cyclones. There are also
indications that quality of particular services increased such as IMCI, treatment of plague cases,
and care of severely malnourished children.

As discussed before, the project changed considerably but the KPIs did not change accordingly.
As a result, some of the KPIs became irrelevant to measure the impact of the project. This was
particularly the case of the KPIs intended to measure the effects of increased geographical access
to health facilities: (i) increased accessibility to health facilities; (ii) increased outpatient health
utilization rates; and (iii) increased hospital utilization rates. The project increased access to
highly effective health care services, but many of those services were offered through outreach
activities such as household indoor spraying, immunization, mass treatment of bilharzias and
filariosis, or through social marketing such as distribution of ITNs, anti-malarials, and
contraceptives. As many of these services were offered outside health facilities and no new
health facilities were built, the KPIs related to general utilization rates cannot correctly measure
the impact of the project. Finally, the KPI that measured prevalence of HIV also became
irrelevant as all the activities aimed at controlling HIV/AIDS were transferred to another project.

The KPIs that remained relevant to measure the impact of the project achieved their goals:
           The contraceptive prevalence rate reached 24% surpassing the target of 17%.
           Child immunization rates largely increased. There is no recent survey data on this
           indicator (the NDHS 2003/2004 shows a 61% full immunization rates), but
           administrative data on DPT3 coverage of 95% in 2006 makes it highly possible that the
           target of 71% full immunization rate was achieved.



                                                    8

           The fatality rate among children hospitalized for malnutrition decreased to 6.5%,
           surpassing the original target of 10%.
           Utilization of health facilities did not increase, but, thanks to the project rehabilitation
           and equipment of facilities damaged by cyclones, utilization was maintained.

Other indicators monitored through the project either achieved their targets or progressed
significantly towards them (see data sheet), such as hospital fatality rate for severe malaria,
malaria incidence, number of cured TB cases, antenatal coverage rate, percentage of skilled
attended deliveries, proportion of children severely malnourished leaving the CRENIs cured, etc.

In addition, the high level indicators followed through the project cycle, maternal, child, and
infant mortality decreased. However, the causal links between these health outcomes to project's
activities are not easy to elucidate as they depend on many factors and not just access to quality
health services (e.g. income, education, sanitation, household behavior, etc.). It is also difficult to
see any change in these indicators in a short time period. Nevertheless, the project financed
activities that were and continue to be relevant and effective in improving health outcomes in
Madagascar as will be analyzed in detailed in the next paragraphs.

The first component of the project received 54% of the entire credit. One of the original
objectives of this component was to increase quality and geographical access to services.
However, due to damage caused by the cyclones it was decided that no new facilities would be
built and that resources available would be used to rehabilitate damaged ones. The Bank was not
the only agency that financed this reconstruction and rehabilitation; however, the facilities
rehabilitated by CRESAN II were those of more difficult access. About 45% of this component
was used in reconstruction and equipment of these facilities.

Given the change in this component, we could analyze progress in achieving its objective by
evaluating whether access to quality services was maintained. In this context, the objective was
achieved as a marked decreased in geographical access to functional services was avoided. The
project did not collect information on access to functional facilities, but it collected information
on utilization of basic health services. In this regard, with the exception of 2003, outpatient care
utilization rates changed only slightly (43%-49%).            In 2003 utilization rates increased
considerably (69%) but this was the result of a temporary policy to eliminate user fees for these
services, this policy was in effect from late 2002 to the end of 2003.

finance maintenance and other functioning costs, such as outreach activities, and allowed SSDs to
finance part of their recurrent expenditures, including supervision activities. In 2005 when the
government started program budgets, these funds became particularly significant for the
functioning of the districts, as there were significant delays and blockages in the disbursement of
the national budget.

This component also helped the installation of regional health bureaus. At the start of the project,

The second component aimed at controlling infectious diseases. This component originally
received about 27% of the credit, most of it to fight against malaria. However, in the final
allocation this component only received 12% of the credit. As new sources of funds became
available to finance some of the activities in this component, resources were reallocated to other
activities that were not receiving support. For instance, since 2004 the Global Fund has financed
most malaria preventive and treatment activities in the coastal zones. After 2005, most TB
activities have also been financed by the Global Fund.

Nevertheless, in the highlands the project continued to finance activities aimed at preventing a
major outbreak of malaria and consolidating the gains of CRESAN I, mainly through financing
the indoor insecticide spraying campaigns. Indeed no major outbreak has been detected in the
highlands since 1987 and the incidence of suspected malaria among children under five was kept
below 10% during all years of the project. The actual target was to achieve a 4.5% of morbidity
due to suspected malaria among children under five. However, a survey of the Pasteur Institute
of Madagascar (IPM) showed that only 15.7% of children 0-5 years with suspected malaria
actually have the disease. This would result in less than 2% of children with malaria in the
highlands. In the highlands the project also supported the establishments of weekly surveillance
posts under the technical assistance of the Pasteur Institute of Madagascar.

In the coastal areas the project financed the initial procurement of long lasting ITNs and the initial
massive distribution of PaluStop, a treatment kit for the effective home management of un-
complicated malaria among children under five. This distribution was made both through social
marketing, with the active participation of the NGO PSI, and through health facilities. All these
activities were implemented before the Global Fund resources became available in 2004. In
general, the malaria program in the coastal areas has only recently started to produce major



                                                       11

improvements, mainly due to large distribution of ITNs in the last two years. CRESAN II
contributed to the initial establishment of all these activities.

The project supported the TB program until the beginning of 2005 when Global Fund resources
became available. The project financed the training of health and lab personnel, the procurement
of drugs, reactive material, and other diagnostic supplies, supervision, and IEC campaigns. There
is evidence of improvements in the TB program in the years when the project supported its
activities.  For instance, in 2000 78% of expected ss+ (sputum-smear-positive) cases were
diagnosed; in 2005 84% of them were. In addition, the cure rate increased from 70% in 2000 to
75% in 2006. These improvements were maintained; in 2006 89% of expected ss+ cases were
diagnosed and the cure rate reached 78%. The international targets to combat TB are 70%
detection rate and 85% cure rate. In Madagascar, the first target was surpassed, although not the
second.

Concerning plague control, there are more clear indications of progress. The project was the only
external source of financing for these activities. Although there was an important increase in the
incidence of plague in 2003 and 2004, the number of new cases decreased significantly in 2005
and 2006; in addition, the lethality rate decreased considerably from 20% in 2001 to 7.8% in 2006.
Other indicators showing the improvements in the plague program are the higher rate of case
confirmation and the lower percentage of pulmonary plague cases. To support this program,
CRESAN II financed surveillance and many activities aimed at preventing new cases (e.g. IEC
activities, construction of 2607 traps and nets, procurement of insecticides in all affected districts,
training of 800 community health agents in the endemic districts, etc.). In addition, the project
also supported treatment through the distribution of technical information, drugs, and reagents.
Finally, the project also financed the development and distribution of rapid diagnostic tests. It is
likely that the large decrease in cases reported in 2005 and 2006 reflects not only the effect of
many of the activities listed above but also a better diagnostic of suspected cases.

As in the case of plague, CRESAN II was the only source of external financing for the bilharzias
and filariasis program.        The project financed the training of personnel, supervision,
implementation, procurement of drugs, and the production and distribution of protocols/manuals.
The second program was able to achieve a reduction of the parasitical load due to filariasis
infection of 25% in districts where mass treatment had been applied. In the case of Bilharzia,
since the project started, 369 new villages have been diagnosed as hyper endemic and 79% of
them have received mass treatment. The project also supported some activities in the fight against
arbovirose, rabies, and others.

Before all STI activities were transferred to the MAP project, CRESAN II financed the
procurement of drugs and supplies for the fight against STI, IEC campaigns to generalize the
syndromic approach to STI treatment, and syphilis tests. There is no information on the impact of
these activities.

financed through its third component.          During the project's life cycle, total fertility rate

urban areas). These surveys also found an increase in the national contraceptive prevalence rate

purpose (to transport patients), only a few of them are being used for outreach activities.
Similarly, even though all ambulances are used after almost 4 years, there are some issues with
their maintenance. The administration of these ambulances was subcontracted to NGOs. The
project only paid for their functioning in the first year; after that the NGOs managed them
through a cost-recovery system. But many NGOs have stopped managing the ambulances as the
fees they are allowed to charge (hospitals set these fees) are not enough to allow their functioning.
At the moment, some of the ambulances are being run by hospitals while another solution is
found on how to ensure their functioning.

Although the improvement in transportation and communication is likely to have improved
utilization of maternal services, financial barriers might have still limited the access to those that
could not afford the ambulance service. Hospitals tried to limit this problem by setting a low fee
for this service, but this fee proved to be insufficient to finance the NGOs that were managing
them.

This component also financed an extension of the list of generic drugs distributed by SALAMA,
the central procurement drug agency. This extension included drugs and supplies needed for
surgery. CRESAN II financed the initial stock of SALAMA and also the initial stock for
hospitals. This allowed lowering the price patients had to pay for these drugs and supplies.

Finally, there is evidence on quality improvements of some of the services supported by this
component. For instance, the EEEFS surveys collected information on whether the five key
pieces of information included in the IMCI protocols were collected from patients under five
years of age. The proportion of patients for which all the information was collected increased
significantly between the two surveys (see tables below). The project financed the training of 635

The nutrition activities financed under the fourth component also resulted in a large
improvement in the therapeutic care of hospitalized malnourished children.            Originally, the
project aimed at supporting a small number of CRENIs (Intensive Nutritional Recovery Centers).
However, during the political crisis, the nutrition situation in the country worsened significantly
and as a response the project started to finance the treatment provided by all CRENIs whose
management was subcontracted to different NGOs. The Tana CRENI, managed by MSF-France,
became a center for excellence. In addition, the project rehabilitated 15 centers and provided
furniture, nutritional inputs, and materials to 36 of them. As a result of all these activities the
quality of the services provided in these centers improved. Through out the project the fatality
rate of severely malnourished children hospitalized in CRENIs decreased (from 26% in 2001 to
6% in 2006) and at the same time the percentage of children leaving the CRENIs cured increased
(63% in 2001 to 87% in 2006). These results surpassed the original targets set by the project.
Part of the success was due to the project ensuring not only the supply of these services but also
eliminating any financial barrier to access as it financed all these services to ensure that they were
provided free of charge. Finally, as a good practice that started with the support of the project,
the government introduced an innovative public-private partnership with NGOs that has been
replicated in other contexts. For instance, the recently created CRENAMs, which are centers for
the ambulatory care of children with moderate malnutrition, are managed by CBOs. The project
later on also supported similar public-private initiatives as was the case of contracting with NGOs
for the management of the 39 ambulances and the social marketing distribution of ITNs, PaluStop,
contraceptives, and others.

The final component of the project aimed at strengthening the capacity of the Ministry of Health
and especially at its deconcentrated levels. However, most activities aimed at strengthening the
managerial capacities of districts and regions were financed through the first component of the
credit.    This component financed, nevertheless, the salaries of the regional "gestionaire
comtables". Their presence helped the lower levels of the Ministry to increase their budget
execution capacity. This component also paid for BLU data equipment and their installation in
districts to allow better communication inside the different levels of the ministry. Unfortunately,
only 20 out of 104 BLU data are still used for transferring data, although they are still used for
their radio functions. This problem was partly due to the initial procurement of inadequate
accompanying material, lack of service, problems with the antennas, etc. A local firm was
competitively selected to install and maintain the equipment which was originally procured
through the UN system, but the local firm did not improve the situation.

The rest of this component supported the central level of the Ministry. However, most of the
support given at this level was punctual and only included financial support and not necessarily
technical support. Indeed, there did not seem to be a comprehensive strategy or a clear goal
behind this support as was the case with the support given to the districts.

Nevertheless, this component financed activities that improved coordination and information
sharing. For instance, this component financed the interconnectivity of all departments at the
central level and a streamlined access to Internet facilitating coordination and information sharing.
This component also provided the support that CRESAN I gave to the information system
ensuring the collection and analysis of reliable data from all the health system; data that allows
better planning at all levels. The Ministry of Health produces annual statistical yearbooks; some
of the information used in this evaluation comes from those yearbooks.



                                                  15

The project also financed the development of several policy and planning documents, including
the Human Resources Development Plan and the Hospital Policy. More importantly, the project
gave financial support to the preparation of the National Health Policy 2003-2006 and the Sector
Development Plan. It also financed the health map as well as the software to update the map and
software to manage human resources in the ministry. All these tools are meant to improve the
planning capacity of the ministry.

This component also financed the organization of many workshops and meetings that had the
purpose of improving the coordination inside the ministry.         After the 2001-2002 crisis the
Ministry experienced many changes, not only in terms of personnel but also organizational.
These changes caused some disruption at the time and some coordination and harmonization
challenges inside the ministry. This component financed many activities aimed at overcoming
these issues.


Finally, this component financed an audit of the Ministry and some rehabilitation work, including
a fully equipped meeting room.

3.3 Efficiency

Government's intervention and World Bank support to the activities financed by CRESAN II
were justified as they were aimed at lessening market failures such as the presence of externalities
or public goods (see annex 3). Equity concerns also justify the project's activities as they were
aimed at lessening the inequalities in access and utilization of health services. In addition, the
majority of the activities financed were also the most cost-effective to prevent or treat the major
causes of mortality and morbidity in the country. Some of these activities generated benefits that
went beyond an improvement in health status by also improving the efficiency in the use of
public resources. Nevertheless, a few activities financed by the project do not have a clear
justification for intervention such as the support to dentist cabinets. These activities represented,
though, a very small share of the credit.      Finally, the share of the resources that went to
administrative purposes was relatively high. But most of these expenditures were one-time
expenditures as was the case of the cost of installing the regional health bureaus or the
interconnectivity of the MINSANPFPS.

3.4 Justification of Overall Outcome Rating

Rating: Satisfactory
The development objective of improving the health status of the population through more
accessible and better quality services was achieved as access to some key services increased (e.g.
family planning, treatment to severely malnourished children, malaria preventive and curative
care, childhood immunizations, mass treatment for bilharzias and filariosis, etc.) or was
maintained thanks to the rehabilitation and equipment of facilities damaged by the cyclones. The
quality of particular services increased such as IMCI, treatment of plague cases, and the care of
severely malnourished children. The key development indicators that remained relevant through
out the lifetime of the project, such as contraceptive prevalence rate, child immunization, and
reduced hospital mortality for severely malnourished children achieved their targets. Other
indicators followed through the project also achieved their goals or progressed significantly
towards them. In addition, the project was very successful in responding to the needs created by
external shocks such as major cyclones and a political crisis. There were nevertheless some
shortcomings.     Even though there are economic or equity justifications for Government's



                                                16

intervention in most of the activities financed by the project, the project also financed a few
activities that were not part of the credit agreement and whose justification was not clear as was
the case of the construction and rehabilitation of dental cabinets. The Government had requested
support for these activities as dental problems represent a relatively important cause of
consultation at CSB. In 2004 dental problems were the fifth major cause of consultations,
representing 3% of the total. Finally, the last component financed activities that were important
to facilitate coordination and harmonization inside the ministry of health; however, many of these
activities were one-off and did not seem to follow a coherent approach to strengthening capacity
at the central level as was done with districts and regions.


                    4. ASSESSMENT OF RISK TO DEVELOPMENT OUTCOME

Rating: Moderate
At the moment, many of the activities financed by the project will also be financed by the WB
follow up project (SHSDP). But there is a slight risk that they will not continue once this
financing stops. For instance, given the low public expenditure on health in the country (about
US$ 7 per capita), it is not clear whether enough resources will be allocated to non-salary
recurrent expenditures that ensure the smooth functioning of health facilities or to salary payment
of the accounting managers, who are currently mainly financed by SHSDP. In addition, there is
evidence that not enough resources are currently allocated to the maintenance of the equipment
procured by the project. This will limit their use in the long term.

Finally, the country is often affected by natural disasters, particularly cyclones.           In the
reconstruction of health facilities, the project made sure that the new structures utilized anti-
cyclone designs and materials. However, this is not the case in the rest of the health facilities that
received some support (e.g. equipment) during the project.


                    5. ASSESSMENT OF BANK AND BORROWER PERFORMANCE

5.1 Bank Performance

(a) Bank Performance in Ensuring Quality at Entry
(i.e., performance through lending phase)
Rating: Moderately Satisfactory
In general, the project's development objectives and its design remain relevant to the country's
health needs. The project design built on the results and lessons learnt from CRESAN I,
eliminating many of the challenges faced by the first project. However, some aspects of the
project design leave some room for improvements. First, when identifying the main causes of
low utilization of health services, the project emphasized geographical access and no provision
was made to improve financial access. However, at the time when the project was designed, WB
projects focused on health care supply and not on demand issues. The new project, SHSDP, will
look at measures to reduce these barriers to access. Second, the monitoring and evaluation
framework, as appeared in the PAD, was complicated and needed to be simplified early on during
the project's implementation. Third, the project design did not ensure an integrated support to the
fight against infectious diseases. Finally, regarding environmental aspects, at the start of the
project's implementation it became apparent that the Government wanted to continue the use of
DDT and not pyrethroids for indoor spraying. During appraisal, this should have been identified
and a detailed assessment of the Government's capacity to used this insecticide in an
environmentally safely manner should have been done.



                                                  17

(b) Quality of Supervision
(including of fiduciary and safeguards policies)
Rating: Moderately Satisfactory
One of the factors that ensured the achievement of the PDOs, despite the political crisis and the
cyclones, was the flexibility of the Bank team to respond quickly and adapt to different
circumstances. For instance, as a response to the cyclones, it was decided that the resources from
the credit would be used to rehabilitate the damaged facilities. In response to the 2001-2002
crisis, the project was restructured to ensure a better response to the new context and the demands
of the new Government. Indeed, after this restructuring, the disbursement of the credit proceeds
increased significantly. Also as new sources of funds became available, the project funds were
reallocated to ensure that these funds supported underfunded activities. Finally, the World Bank
team ensured a relatively smooth transition from the support of this credit to the follow up project
SHSDP. The team worked very closely with the MINSANPFPS as well as other donors and
NGOs, in particular with USAID, UNICEF, and PSI. This close coordination also ensured a
good use of available resources, avoiding any duplication of efforts. Similarly, the Bank team
also provided technical advice as well as practical ideas that helped in the achievement of the
PDOs. The introduction of a long duration contraceptive of easy insertion, the promotion of
social marketing of different consumables, and the promotion of different public/private
partnerships are examples of this. Not all the ideas worked, though, as was the case of the pilot
that introduced the QUADS 6x6.

Although the project's TTL changed a few times, for a few years the project had the same TTL
based in Antananarivo. The presence of the same TTL in the country helped ensure the close
coordination as well as the rapid response of the team to unpredictable situations.

Despite all of these positive aspects of the WB team supervision, there were other aspects that
could have been improved. This is particularly the case of the M&E framework which was not
changed despite all changes in the project; making the monitoring of the project's progress
difficult. Also, although the support to the construction and rehabilitation of dentist cabinets was
a response to Government's request these activities were not part of the original credit agreement
and their cost-effectiveness and their priority level in terms of the country's burden of disease are
not very high. Finally, more efforts were needed to ensure that enough recurrent cost would be
set aside for the maintenance and general functioning of the equipment financed by the project.

(c) Justification of Rating for Overall Bank Performance
Rating: Moderately Satisfactory

The World Bank team provided the support and assistance needed to achieve PDOs that remain
relevant to the country's health needs. The Bank supervision team was highly proactive and
responsive to the needs of the country, but the M&E framework was never formally changed to
correctly assess and follow the progress of the project. In addition, there were some aspects of
the project's design that could have been improved, such as a the M&E framework, a non-
integrated approach to support the health sector, and issues related to the environmental
assessment of the project.

5.2 Borrower Performance

(a) Government Performance
Rating: Moderately Satisfactory

The failure to deposit counterpart funds delayed the implementation of the project at several
points in time. Even at the closure of the project, part of the counterpart funds was still

(b) Implementing Agency or Agencies Performance
Rating: Satisfactory
One of the main factors that allowed the success of this project was no doubt the hard work,

agency worked very closely with both the Bank team and the Ministry of Health, as it is part of it.
The agency was quick in identifying problems in the implementation of the project and in trying
to solve them. The team was composed of technical people with all the needed expertise (e.g.
procurement, financial management, M&E, health technical aspects, etc.) to ensure a relatively
smooth implementation of the project. The work of the agency started with some delays, mainly
due to an insufficient number of staff. But this issue was solved very early on.

(c) Justification of Rating for Overall Borrower Performance
Rating: Satisfactory
The failure to deposit counterpart funds delayed the implementation of the project at several
points in time. However, one of the main reasons behind the successful implementation of

considered Satisfactory.


                               6. LESSONS LEARNED

The project was able to reach its goals despite all obstacles thanks to the flexibility used by the
WB and Government's team in its implementation. This flexibility helped to respond to the
cyclones and to evolving needs. This flexibility also allowed quick reallocations of resources
from activities funded by other sources to others that were not.

The decentralization of the activities financed by the project, and particularly the technical
assistance offered to the deconcentrated levels of the Ministry also allowed a smoother
implementation of activities and the achievement of the PDO. However, technical assistance to
the central level is also needed to ensure a sustainable development of the health system.

A strong coordination with other partners under the leadership of the Government is important for
success. This was shown in the success in family planning services. All donors have signed a
MOU with the Ministry for a coordinated approach in family planning activities. Each year the
ministry prepares a calendar of activities showing all financial gaps. This is then discussed with
all donors. This experience is now replicated in the entire health sector.

It is important to ensure that there are enough resources available for the functioning of
investment goods. CRESAN II financed the procurement of medical equipment and vehicles (e.g.




                                                  19

ambulances, QUADS, motorbikes, etc.). However, there is evidence of insufficient resources
allocated to maintain them.

To increase service utilization it is important to make sure that all causes of non-utilization are
dealt with. This project only aimed at improving geographical access by trying to build new
facilities. However, the main cause of non-utilization of health services has always been their
cost.

Public-private partnerships in the health sector can be a useful tool to provide quality health
services; however, enough resources need to be provided to ensure that the cost of this provision
is covered.

As the project changes, it is important to revise the monitoring and evaluation framework to
ensure that the project's progress is monitored correctly. CRESAN II changed significantly
across the years but the M&E framework did not.

                        ANNEX 3. ECONOMIC AND FINANCIAL ANALYSIS

(including assumptions in the analysis)
Justification for Government intervention
Government intervention in some of the activities financed by CRESAN II is justified as they were
aimed at lessening market failures such as the presence of externalities or public goods. In
addition, CRESAN II also financed activities aimed at lessening the inequalities in access and
utilization of health services.   Finally, some of these activities generated benefits that went
beyond an improvement on health status by also improving the efficiency in the use of public
resources.

One of the common justifications for government intervention on markets is the presence of
externalities, where economic agents can impose a cost or benefit to others without paying or
charging for it. Without government intervention too much of the negative effect or too little of
the benefit would be produced.

Infectious diseases, particularly malaria, are the major causes of mortality and morbidity in the
country, especially among children under five. When preventing or treating an infectious disease
individuals do not necessarily take into account the effect of their action (or lack thereof) on
others. Without government intervention the level of preventive and curative efforts will be
lower than optimal. CRESAN II financed activities aimed at preventing and treating many of
these diseases. The entire second component, representing 12% of the credit finance activities
aimed precisely at the control of malaria, plague, bilharzias, filariasis, and others. Similarly, part
of 3rd component financed activities aimed at controlling the spread of sexually transmitted
diseases. Finally, the support given to CSB in the first component has also helped in the
prevention and treatment of many infectious diseases, especially childhood diseases (e.g. malaria,
diarrhea, vaccine preventable diseases, etc.).

In addition, some of the interventions used to prevent infectious diseases can be characterized as
public goods.      Nobody can be excluded from benefiting from a public good and a person
benefiting from it does not decrease the potential benefit to others. These characteristics render
almost impossible the private provision of these goods. For instance, vector control such as
indoor spraying to control malaria is a public good; CRESAN II financed these activities.

Another example of a public good is the collection and dissemination of public information for
the control of diseases and the supportive laboratories to monitor safe drugs, food, and water.
These activities were also financed by the project, especially in its second component.

Similarly, some information campaigns for activities without a marketable product associated
with it will make private provision very difficult, justifying government intervention.            For
instance, all the campaigns to sensitize families on behavioral change financed by CRESAN II
thought its support to the district health system and also as part of the control of infectious
diseases fit into that category.

There are also equity considerations that justify government intervention in some of the activities
financed by CRESAN II. There are large income inequalities in the utilization of health services
in the country. Partly this is due to lower physical access to health services in rural isolated areas,
and partly due to financial and cultural barriers to access services.




                                                  23

The financing of basic health services can be justified on equity concerns. This is especially the
case in rural areas, as these services tend to be used more by the poor than the rich who tend to
visit private services or higher level public facilities. CRESAN II financed the rehabilitation and
equipment of the most remote basic health services damaged by the cyclones.

As the private sector is almost not present in rural areas, improving the services provided by
public service facilities does not risk the displacement of the private sector in these areas.

Also, most of the activities financed by CRESAN II were meant to support progress towards
achieving MDGs 4 and 5, those of reducing child and maternal mortality. These goals were
endorsed by the Malagasy Government as well as the international community justifying both
Government's and World Bank continued support to these activities.

There were, nevertheless, some activities were the justification for government's intervention is
not as clear. That was for example the case of the support given to dental care. About 10.5% of
the entire first component went to different activities related to dental care (e.g. rehabilitation,
construction, and equipment of dental cabinets, salt fluorination, etc.). Dental affections are not
infectious and they are not a major cause of mortality in the country. Although they are the fifth
major cause of consultation in CSBs; they only represented 3.4% of all those consultations in
2004. Equity concerns might justify this intervention, but there are other interventions with
higher priority level than this. All this said, the fluorination of salt is a highly cost-effective
preventive measure. However, the same thing cannot be said about the support to dentistry
cabinets.

Other benefits of the project
Improving the allocative efficiency of public expenditure on health is not on itself a justification
of Government intervention in the sector, but it constitutes a benefit from the project above the
benefits to the population health status. The project further improved the allocative efficiency of
public expenditure as it financed preventive care, public health activities, and in the case of
hospital care it will only finance first referral hospitals for activities related to maternal health.
More specifically, the focus of CRESAN II is on the most cost-effective interventions to prevent
and treat the illnesses that represent the major burden of disease in Madagascar by focusing on
health interventions that can be provided by the households and communities themselves and for
those that can be provided at primary health care facilities. This was reflected in the project's
emphasis on information campaigns, on strengthening of community participation on health care,
and on improving the quality of the services provided by basic health centers. All this said there is
room for improvement.

Some of the activities supported by CRESAN II improve coordination among donors, as was the
support to the PTAs.        By improving coordination, CRESAN II also improved allocative
efficiency by diminishing transaction costs and thus diminishing administrative costs.

Even if the project was technically sound and cost effective their results would have not been
attained if the Government did not have the capacity to implement it.              Without lessening
bottlenecks in the management of resources the health system would have not been able to reap
all the benefits of the resources it received. A large share of the project was indeed used to
finance many activities aimed at strengthening the health system, in particular, strengthening the
capacity of the district levels to manage primary health care services.




                                                  24

Nevertheless, a very large share of the project went not just to capacity building activities but to
administrative costs. For instance, in the first component as much as 18% of the resources were
used to finance the functioning of the regional health services.        However, many of these
expenditures were one-time expenditures needed to make this newly created services work.

                                                            1.            CONTEXTE DU PROJET

              La mise en oeuvre du Projet CRESAN 2 se situe dans un contexte marqué par (a) l'engagement du Gouvernement dans un processus de
réduction de la pauvreté ; (b) la décentralisation des compétences au niveau périphérique, et (c) l'exigence d'améliorer l'efficacité et la qualité des
services rendus à la population bénéficiaires. Finalisé en septembre 2002, le DSRP a pour objectif « de réduire le taux de pauvreté en l'espace de 10 ans,
soit passer du taux actuel de 70% à 35% en 2013, et ce, à travers la promotion d'un développement rapide et durable « En matière de santé, le DSRP fixe
les objectifs globaux pour le secteur : (a) promouvoir la santé de la mère et de l'enfant ; (b) intensifier la lutte contre la malnutrition ; et (c) renforcer la
lutte contre les maladies transmissibles et non transmissibles. Le DSRP établit également les indicateurs précis d'impact et de résultats attendus des
services de santé avant fin 2005.

              Par ailleurs, le DSRP entend lier les résultats attendus des services de santé à la normalisation du financement du secteur et à la prise de
décision décentralisée jusqu'au niveau du district sanitaire. Des initiatives ont effectuées pour : (a) mettre en place les outils de programmation (tels que
les comptes nationaux de santé, le budget programme) ; (b) améliorer la répartition des allocations budgétaires ; (c) reprendre la participation financière
des usagers (PFU) mais sous sa nouvelle dénomination FANOME ; et (d) renforcer la gestion des ressources (humaines, financières et matérielles) du
secteur.

                                                          2.             HISTORIQUE DU PROJET

              Entre 1992 ­ 1999, l'IDA a soutenu le développement du secteur dans le cadre de la mise en oeuvre du Projet CRESAN 1 qui avait pour
objectifs : (a) la lutte contre les maladies transmissibles (paludisme, tuberculose, pets et IST/SIDA), (b) l'amélioration de la santé maternelle et infantile ;
(c) l'appui à la centrale d'achat des médicaments essentiels ; (d) l'appui au développement des districts de santé, au recouvrement des coûts et à la gestion
communautaire ; et (e) le renforcement institutionnel du Ministère. Le Projet CRESAN 2 continue à soutenir ces objectifs à travers les cinq composantes
du Projet qui sont : (a) appui au développement des services sanitaires de district ; (b) la prévention et la lutte contre les maladies transmissibles ; (c)
l'amélioration de la santé de la reproduction et de la planification familiale (y compris la lutte contre les IST/SIDA) ; (d) l'amélioration de la nutrition; et
(e) le renforcement des capacités institutionnelles du Ministère. Ces objectifs s'intègrent parfaitement avec ceux du DSRP et du Business Plan du
MINSAN.

              L'évaluation préalable du CRESAN 2 s'est déroulée en juin-juillet 1999, et l'Accord de crédit pour un montant total de USD 40 millions a
été signée le 03 février 2000 par le Gouvernement et l'IDA. Il est entré en vigueur en août 2000 et a démarré effectivement un mois plus tard. L'Accord
de crédit fixe la date de clôture du Projet au 31 décembre 2006.

                                           3.           RESUME DE L'EXECUTION DU PROJET CRESAN-2

              Conformément au PAD du Projet, l'objectif de développement global du Projet est de contribuer à l'amélioration de l'état de santé de la
population en créant des services de santé plus accessibles et de meilleure qualité. Les objectifs de développement spécifiques sont :

-      améliorer la qualité des services primaires, l'accent étant mis sur les zones rurales,
-      soutenir les programmes de santé prioritaires en mettant l'accent sur les maladies infectieuses, la santé génésique (y compris le planning familial,
       les maladies transmissibles sexuellement et le VIH/SIDA) et la nutrition ; et
-      renforcer la gestion sectorielle et les capacités administratives à l'intérieur du Ministère de la santé et au niveau des provinces et districts pour
       permettre le succès de la décentralisation et de la réforme sectorielle. Tandis que l'accent majeur du Projet porte sur les services médicaux
       primaires et la réduction du paludisme et aux activités de nutrition, ainsi qu'au renforcement des capacités du secteur sanitaire. En soutenant la
       mise en oeuvre du plan de politique et Développement du secteur de la santé, le Projet contribue également au processus de réforme en cours dans
       le secteur de la santé.

              L'appui aux districts maintient les activités identifiées initialement au niveau des DIRDS et des SSD/EMAD. Cependant, les activités de
développement institutionnel de la DDDS ont été modifiées, notamment pour relancer et renforcer le processus de planification au niveau des Districts
(les PDD) et pour appuyer les audits des districts.

              Prévu par l'accord de crédit, le PDIS a été mis en veilleuse après la décision de réhabiliter les CSB détruits par les cyclones en 2000. Cette
décision a réorientée les activités du Projet qui priorise la réhabilitation des CSB par rapport au objectifs initiaux (extension des couvertures sanitaires).

              Compte tenu du faible taux de décaissement dû essentiellement au retard dans l'exécution des activités prévues et de la crise politique 2002
qui a paralysé le pays durant le premier semestre 2002, une nouvelle reprogrammation des activités du Projet a touché essentiellement : (i) la refonte de
l'organigramme du Ministère, (ii) de la réduction de la durée d'implémentation du PTA initial 2002 de 12 à 4 mois et (iii) de la volonté de la nouvelle
administration d'impliquer plus largement les ONG afin d'accroître la capacité d'absorption du secteur.

              Depuis septembre 2002, le Projet a rapidement établi un rythme de décaissements élevé. A la clôture, le taux de décaissement du projet est
de 99.20 %.

              L'exécution du Projet CRESAN 2 est marqué aussi par une série de réallocation du 10 janvier 2003, du 03 juin 2004, du 11 février 2005 et
l'obtention d'un crédit supplémentaire le 13 septembre 2005 et une réallocation le 06 juillet 2007.

              Nonobstant quelques retards, les activités du Projet ont pu démarrer normalement comme prévu.

Composante 1 : Développement des Services de Santé de District

              L'amélioration de la qualité de soin

               L'opérationnalisation des objectifs du projet CRESAN-2, à travers le MINSANPFPS, passe au niveau décentralisé par les Districts Sanitaires,
point stratégique de cette politique. Les CSB et les CHD forment pour cela le niveau opérationnel du projet dont l'amélioration de l'offre de soins de
qualité est l'une des principales activités stipulées dans le business plan du MINSANPFPS. La réalisation des activités par le CRESAN-2 comme (i)
l'équipement du CHR d'Antsiranana, (ii)l'élaboration des normes et standards des CHD, (iii) l'évaluation de la fonctionnalité des CHD/CHR, (iv) l'octroi
de consommables médicaux et d'équipement et mobilier pour CSB ainsi que (v) l'études pour l'approvisionnement en eau potable des CSB, ont
largement contribué à la confiance, et à l'utilisation de la population des services offerts par les centres de soins primaires et les services hospitaliers de
référence de premier recours dans les District Sanitaires. Même si le taux d'utilisation des centres de santé de base n'a pas évolué à cause de la
réorientation du projet, au milieu de son parcours, vers la réhabilitation des centres détruites par le cyclone, grâce à la formation du personnel, la
fourniture de médicaments et le développement du système de recouvrement des coûts, le taux d'accès de la population aux infrastructures sanitaires
(CSB moins de 5km) a augmenté de 57% (base line 2000) à 60% en 2004 (carte sanitaire).

               Le programme de réhabilitation des CSB, suite aux dégâts cycloniques, a permis de reprendre rapidement les services de santé de base. 242
infrastructures sanitaires, dont 9 Bureaux de Santé de Districts (BSD) et 3 Accueil Triage Urgence (ATU) des CHD ainsi que 153 centres de Santé de
base (CSB) ont été achevés en 2004. La programmation des fonds du Crédit supplémentaire renforçait l'appui d'urgence pour l'amélioration de la qualité
de service des maternités des CHD1/CHD2 et CHRR afin d'assurer une meilleure prise en charge et de contribuer à améliorer les taux de fréquentation de
ces services par les femmes enceintes. De 33,5% en 2001(annuaire des statistiques sanitaires) à 40,8% en 2006 (service des statistiques sanitaires). Le
taux moyen d'utilisation des lits d'hôpitaux de District a vu une augmentation significative de 7,3%.

               Développement de Services spécialisés : Santé bucco- dentaire
               L'incidence élevée de la carie dentaire, conséquence de la raréfaction en fluor des eaux de surface et de consommation courante, constitue un
problème de santé publique au niveau des Districts en terme de santé bucco-dentaire. Face à cette situation, le MINSANPFPS, avec l'appui du projet, a
opté pour la fluoration du sel alimentaire (Antsiranana et Morondava) et à l'amélioration de la prestation de services de santé bucco-dentaire. 46
nouveaux cabinets dentaires ont été crées et 115 dotés d'équipement moderne.


               Décentralisation du système de santé

               Si auparavant, certains partenaires comme le FNUAP, l'Union Européenne, la Coopération Française, la GTZ et l'USAID/JSI ont organisé
leur soutien financier et matériel selon les activités retenues par les PDD et les PTA dans à peu près la moitié des 111 districts sanitaires du pays, depuis
sa mise en oeuvre, le projet CRESAN-2 a assuré le financement du PTA des SSD sur l'ensemble du territoire selon une démarche de planification
ascendante à partir des CSB jusqu'au DRSPFPS en passant par les Services de District Sanitaire.
               Au début, le projet CRESAN s'est penché sur le renforcement de la capacité de planification et de gestion des Services de Santé de
District avec le concours de deux ONG internationaux en tant que facilitateur et encadreur. Un manuel de procédure a été élaboré pour guider l'octroi des
financements. En 2004, des constats ont été fait sur la lenteur d'exécution et que seulement le quart des districts a pu justifié plus de 90% de dépenses et
13% des SSD n'ont pas engagé de dépense. Le mesures prises par le projet à savoir : (i) l'appui au niveau régional (directions et hôpitaux) ;(2)
l'augmentation du nombre de gestionnaire comptables suivant la superficie et le nombre de régions province ;(iii) l'harmonisation du financement des
districts ; et (iv) la simplification des procédures d'approvisionnement financier a porté ses fruits car le décaissement se faisait de plus en plus rapide par
la suite.
               Par ailleurs, l'appui du projet à touché d'autres volets comprenant : (i) la dotation en matériel informatique de 88 SSD, (ii) la dotation en
véhicules 4x4 pour 34 SSD, (iii) l'élargissement de la gamme en médicaments et consommables pour les CHU/CHR/CHD, (iv) l'appui au
fonctionnement des 22 DRSPF, 22CHRR et 11SSD, (v) la dotation en extracteur d'oxygène (19 ) et ECG des CHD2/CHRR (26).

               Pour parfaire le rôle des DRSPF en terme d'appui stratégique et de coordination, le projet les a équipé de matériels informatiques (27
ordinateurs), de mobiliers de bureau et de matériels de reproduction (48).

Composante 2. Lutte contre les Maladies Transmissibles

               Lutte contre le Paludisme

               Le paludisme constitue l'une des maladies les plus meurtrière à Madagascar. Le projet a pour objectif de maintenir la réduction de la
transmission obtenue par CRESAN -1 et réduire la morbidité et la mortalité tout en préservant la prémunition. La part du projet CRESAN 2 dans le
financement de la lutte contre ce problème de santé publique vise en même temps le renforcement des mesures de prévention, l'amélioration de la prise
en charge, la surveillance épidémiologique et l'appui au fonctionnement des centres de prestation de service.

               Les mesures entreprises pour la prévention se traduisent principalement par (i) le soutien et la mise en oeuvre de la campagne d'aspersion
intra domiciliaire (CAID) avec l'achat de 60 tonnes de DDT, (ii) l'impression de 10 000 unités de guide et carte conseil pour la prise en charge à domicile
et l'achat de 500 000 moustiquaires imprégnées.

               Pour la prise en charge, le projet a contribué (i) à l'impression de 6 500 guides de prise en charge, (ii) à la formation de 141 agents de santé
et laborantins, (iii) la dotation de 36 000 tests rapides et la mise à disposition communautaire de chloroquine. La collaboration avec l'Institut Pasteur de
Madagascar a permis de mettre en place un réseau de chimiosensibilité. La dotation en équipement des centres de santé, l'appui sur la maintenance et la
supervision a résolu plusieurs problèmes dans la mise en oeuvre.

               Lutte contre la Tuberculose
               L'apport du projet dans ce programme se situe à deux niveaux : (i) l'amélioration de la prise en charge, par la dotation de 215 laboratoires
en petits matériels et réactifs, la formation de 180         agents de santé et laborantins, la diffusion de 800 manuels techniques et par la dotation
d'antituberculeux ainsi que (ii) l'appui au fonctionnement et à la supervision.

               Lutte contre la Peste
               L'appui du projet CRESAN 2 vise particulièrement à améliorer la détection et la prise en charge des cas ainsi que la prévention et le
contrôle des épidémies. La lutte contre la Peste est plus complexe car elle nécessite une action plus coordonnée avec d'autres secteurs. Néanmoins, le
nombre de cas déclarés par an a diminué de 1 334 cas en 2000(rapport service Peste) à 308 cas en 2006 (rapport service Peste). Parmi les cas traité on
note une diminution du taux de létalité de 20% en 2000 (rapport service Peste) à 7,8% en 2005 (rapport service Peste).


               Lutte contre la bilharziose
               L'expérience positive de la décentralisation des mesures de prévention, notamment sur les traitements de masse au praziquantel de la
bilharziose intestinale et urinaire ainsi que les activités de communication (dépliants, sensibilisation, diffusion de message), associée à amélioration de la
prise en charge par la dotation de médicament, ont amenée au contrôle de ce problème de santé publique.

               Lutte contre les maladies émergentes et ré-émergentes

             La volonté du MINSANPFPS dans la lutte contre la filariose a reçu l'appui du projet CRESAN-2 dans le cadre du développement de

             Les épidémies de grippe et de conjonctivite sont vite maîtrisées par l'achat de médicament par le projet.

Composante 3 : Soutenir le programme de Santé de la Reproduction, PF et IST/SIDA.

              Plusieurs intervenants participent au financement et à la réalisation de ces programmes. Pour la maternité sans risque, l'appui du projet
assurait essentiellement l'amélioration de la référence/ contre référence par la dotation en matériel de transport (voitures et vedette, faisant fonction
d'ambulance et de service) des CHD2 et CHR assurant les interventions chirurgicales d'urgences (OC). Les CSB sont, par contre, doté de matériel de
communication (BLU). Pour cela le projet a contribué à l'installation de radios BLU à alimentation solaire dans tous les CHRR et CHD2 (39) et dans
329 CSB2.

             Le programme de Santé Reproductive a obtenu un renforcement conséquent sur les équipements, formation des prestataires PF / SRA /
PCIME, et les outils de gestion, ainsi que les produits contraceptifs nécessaires au bon fonctionnement des centres. Le projet à mis une attention
particulière à l'introduction de 15 000 unités d'Implanon® en tant que méthode de longue durée, levier de la prévalence contraceptive, et a contribué à
l'approvisionnement des autres méthodes de courte durée (Néo shampoon, Lo féménal, Dépo Provera).

             Le leadership de la lutte contre les IST/ VIH / SIDA est assuré par le CNLS avec le projet Multisectoriel pour la prévention du SIDA(PMPS).
Le projet CRESAN-2 a largement contribué dans la partie préventive par (i) la formation de 3700 agents prestataires sur la PEC des IST classiques selon
l'approche syndromique, (ii) le renforcement de la sécurité transfusionnelle, (iii) la dotation de 55 500 test RPR (iv) et l'éducation de la population par les
activités d'IEC.

Composante 4 : Lutte contre la malnutrition

             La contribution du projet était très conséquente dans la lutte contre la malnutrition. A cause du contexte économique et social, une
réorientation de l'appui a été initiée après la crise de 2002 pour faire face à la recrudescence de la malnutrition. La stratégie étant de collaborer en
partenariat avec des ONG afin d'assurer le maximum d'appui en terme de couverture spatiale et de disponibilité en terme de temps. Il s'agit d'une
première expérience de collaboration de sous-traitance avec une vingtaine d'ONG nationales et internationales. Le résultat étant très positif car le taux de
mortalité des enfants de 0 à 5 ans sévèrement malnutris à chuter de 26% en 2001 (rapports CRENI) à 6,8% en 2006 (service nutrition), alors que le
nombre d'admissions a augmenté. Le processus d'appui du projet a commencé par la réhabilitation de 15 centres CRENI, à la dotation en matériel et
mobilier, puis à l'approvisionnement en intrant alimentaire et médicament de 36 centres CRENI. Les ONG ont été recruté selon leurs expériences et leurs
disponibilités. La qualité de soin est assurée par l'utilisation du guide de prise en charge, la formation des agents des CRENI et par l'évaluation de
l'impact des activités permettant ainsi de recevoir des feed back pour améliorer les services offerts. La proportion des enfants sévèrement malnutris
sortants des CRENI guéris est très encourageant avec une augmentation de 62,8% en 2001 à 87,0% en 2006 (service nutrition).

Composante 5 : Renforcement Institutionnel

             L'appui du projet dans le cadre du renforcement institutionnel englobe plusieurs aspects dont le but est d'améliorer l'organisation et le
fonctionnement interne du MINSANPFPC par le biais de la mise en réseau intranet, la diffusion de l'annuaire statistique, la dotation en équipements
bureautique, le développement du logiciel GRH, la décentralisation du système, et la rénovation de la salle de conférence. Par ailleurs, quelques éléments
spécifiques comme l'élaboration des Codes de Déontologies de Infirmiers, l'enquête sur les normes de qualité des soins et l'appui à la réalisation de
l'EDS 2003 ont pu être réalisé. Une somme d'environ 5,9millions USD a été budgétisée pour renforcer aussi bien d'une manière directe ou indirecte la
capacité institutionnelle.

                                                           8.            PERENNITE DU PROJET.

              Bien que la pérennisation du projet peut être conditionné par le maintient ou non de l'orientation stratégique éventuelle ou contextuelle, la
large contribution du projet CRESAN-2 au renforcement du système de santé mérite une continuation soutenue pour maximiser les résultats et impacts
notamment sur le renforcement institutionnel et la lutte contre les maladies transmissibles dont l'éradication ne peut se faire que sur plusieurs années
successives.

En ce qui concerne la maintenance des investissements, une étude du mécanisme de financement interne et externe à la formation sanitaire doit être
entreprise pour assurer la continuation des activités. Pour les centres de santé de base, la subvention de la commune par l'Etat et le PADS peut être une
source non négligeable de financement. Pour les Hôpitaux, à part le budget de l'Etat, une partie des recettes FANOME et le PADS sans parler des
partenariats locaux, peuvent contribuer à l'entretien des bâtiments. Le cadre de contractualisation ainsi que les procédures doivent suivre la politique
Nationale de maintenance.

Les risques épidémiques des maladies transmissibles sont souvent en relation avec la situation environnementale et les conditions climatiques. La
survenue des aléas climatiques peut provoquer une situation d'urgence. Le Service des Urgences et des Catastrophes du MINSANPFPS doté d'un budget
conséquent peut y faire face.

La lutte contre le paludisme venait de prendre un nouveau souffle à Madagascar grâce au financement venant du PMI et du Fond Mondial. Le
financement de la lutte antipaludique est garanti jusqu'à 2011.

Le financement de la lutte contre la tuberculose a été assuré par l'Etat et le Projet (environ 3% du coût total du projet) jusqu'en 2004. Par la suite, le
Fonds Mondial et la GDF ont pris la relève.

A ce jour, seul le projet CRESAN-2 finance les activités de lutte contre la bilharziose. Ainsi, le PDSSP continuera à appuyer le Service concerné (prise
en charge et riposte contre les épidémies).

La planification familiale a pris une place importante dans le développement économique et sanitaire du pays. Ce volet est actuellement financé par
plusieurs bailleurs notamment l'USAID et le FNUAP. Une harmonisation stratégique et une consolidation des plans au niveau d'une plate forme semble
nécessaire car le système vit à double vitesse (gratuit au niveau secteur public et payant au niveau secteur privé). La redynamisation des méthodes de
longues durées est incontournable pour « booster » la prévalence contraceptive et pour stabiliser les acquis.
              Pour la lutte contre les IST / VIH / SIDA, le leadership se trouve actuellement au niveau du CNLS. Récemment une convention de
partenariat vient d'être signée entre le Ministre de la Santé, de la planification familiale et de la protection sociale et le Secrétaire Exécutif du CNLS pour
clarifier les rôles et responsabilité de chaque institution et pour faciliter la réponse au niveau du secteur Santé.

D'une manière globale la pérennisation des acquis passera par les points suivant : (i) le développement des capacités administratives et de gestion
financière du MINSANPFPS ; (ii) la décentralisation effective des processus décisionnels au niveau de districts avec l'implication de la communauté et
les individus ; (iii) le renforçant les capacités du secteur de la santé sur l'évaluation périodique de la qualité et l'efficacité des soins primaires et des
activités de lutte contre les maladies transmissibles ; en garantissant que les interventions de soins de santé primaires et de santé publique aient
suffisamment de ressources pour avoir un impact positif sur la mortalité et la morbidité ; en développant des mécanismes de partage des coûts et en
impliquant l'utilisation des services dans la gestion des fonds ; en impliquant les communautés soit directement soit indirectement par le biais de corps
élus, dans les interventions de soins de santé ; en comptant sur les ONG pour faire usage du secteur privé.

              Pour le développement des capacités administratives et gestion financière du MINSANPF, notre recommandation s'oriente sur (i) l'appui
technique et financier de la DEP et de la DAF dans le renforcement de la gestion budgétaire, (ii) Au niveau de districts sanitaires, sur le maintien des
structures d'appui par la continuité des assistances des GCR aux Districts sanitaires et aux DRSPF.

                                           9.          PRINCIPAUX ENSEIGNEMENTS TIRES DU PROJET


             Cette partie analyse les points forts et points faibles afin de mesurer chacune des composantes du Projet au niveau financier, institutionnel et
technique.

             Trois éléments principaux sont à retenir de la performance du Projet :
       1. La collaboration technique et financière des différents intervenants dans le cadre du programme ; la politique du Gouvernement et le Business
Plan du Ministère démontrent l'engagement des autorités en matière de développement sanitaire et établissent un cadre de travail cohérent pour le
interventions et les intervenants. Ceci a permis au Projet d'avancer très rapidement dans les décaissements depuis la fin de la crise. En plus, même si les
mesures formelles de coordination envisagées au départ (les revues biannuelles de suivi de l'exécution et la revue annuelle d'évaluation de
programmation du CRESAN) n'ont pas vu le jour, la collaboration informelle et le soutien des différents partenaires ont joué un rôle très important au
cours et après la crise.

       2. La flexibilité du Projet qui lui a permis de répondre aux besoins du moment et de prendre des initiatives avec l'appui de la Banque grâce à une
interprétation de l'Accord de Crédit de manière à pouvoir répondre aux besoins du pays, notamment en fonction : (i) des catastrophes naturelles
(orientation des dépenses de génie civil vers les zones sinistrées) et de évènements politiques (par la suspension du financement de la contre partie du
Projet par le Gouvernement, la subvention des dépenses de fonctionnement du Ministère pendant la crise) nécessitant des actions ponctuelles
d'envergure ; (ii) des autres sources de financement en plus (telles que le MAP, le FID, le Global Funds) ; (iii) des initiatives exigeant des moyens très
importants (maternité sans risque, l'appui aux districts, la mise en place des cabinets dentaires, l'achat des moustiquaires imprégnées, le renforcement des
CRENI

             3. La capacité d'absorption résultant de l'expérience de l'équipe du Projet et             des modalités de passation des marchés arrêtées;
l'accroissement du rythme de décaissement après la fin de la crise était dû en grande partie :
aux capacités managériales de l'équipe expérimentée du BCP, au choix des méthodes de passation des marchés (surtout en passant par le système des
Nations Unis pour les fournitures) et aux contrats avec le secteur privé pour la prestation des services.

             Par ailleurs quelques facteurs de risques ont été observés, la non disponibilité des fonds de contre partie à temps et l'insuffisance de
capacité managériale de certains services du ministère de la santé et du planning Familial constitue des contraintes à moyen et à long terme pour les
activités du Projet.

             Au niveau de la programmation budgétaire du Projet, on a observé des écarts significatifs entre le montant prévisionnel des marchés et les
montants réels.

                                                     10.           LES APPRECIATIONS SUR L'IDA


             Au cours du deuxième phase du Projet d'Appui au Secteur Santé, des tables rondes avec les bailleurs de fonds dont la Banque mondiale ont
été effectuées régulièrement et un forum pour discuter des progrès dans la réalisation de la politique de secteur et de la contribution apportée par les
bailleurs de fonds à cette politique afin d'obtenir un consensus sur les modifications des orientations, sur les affectations du budget et autres thèmes du
genre durant ces six années. Une fois au moins par an les progrès dans l'exécution du Projet envisagé sont discuté soit à l'occasion de la mission de
supervision de la Banque soit lors du lancement des plans de santé opérationnels annuels du secteur. Ces discussions ont servi à un ajustement précis du
Projet aux changements pouvant survenir dans le secteur à une coordination, au niveau technique, avec le Gouvernement et les bailleurs de fonds.

             La Banque mondiale a joué un rôle primordial dans le dialogue de politique générale avec le Gouvernement sur la réforme du secteur de la
santé. Grâce à l'assistance du Projet de santé en cours, la banque a joué un rôle instrumental en aidant le MINSANPFPS à concevoir un cadre de
politique large, à effectuer des réformes sectorielles clés et à renforcer les capacités institutionnelles. Par ailleurs, la Banque a joué un rôle primordial
dans la coordination des Bailleurs de fonds en veillant à ce que les programmes annuels de travail du Ministère de la Santé soient discutés et acceptés
avec la participation de tous les Bailleurs de Fonds et à ce que des rencontres régulières aient lieu entre le MINSANPFSP et les Bailleurs de fonds et entre
les Bailleurs de fonds eux-mêmes. Le soutien de la Banque durant ces six années consiste à assurer que la politique du secteur de la santé est efficacement
mise en oeuvre.

             Le soutien financier apporté par la Banque au MINSANPFSP a renforcé ses capacités de planification et de gestion du secteur, assurait au
MINSANPFSP des examens de programme annuels en étroite collaboration avec le Bailleurs de fonds et afin de continuer le soutien au processus de
décentralisation. Le montant accordé par la Banque Mondiale au Projet CRESAN -2 est estimé à 29.000.000 DTS pour le Crédit initial et 12 300 000
DTS pour le crédit supplémentaire.

             En termes de gestion financière et de portefeuille du Projet, des améliorations significatives ont été effectuées :
       -     le délai de 15 jours pour l'approbation du rapport d'évaluation technique,
       -     la restauration des délais habituels de 3 semaines pour le paiement direct et le réapprovisionnement de fonds,
       -     l'introduction du Formulaire 384-P, 384 C, et les Contrats dans le système de la Banque mondiale,
       -     la mise en place du Site Client Connection afin de suivre en temps réel l'évolution des traitements de dossiers.


             La flexibilité du projet pour faire face à des situations prépondérantes et urgentes était parmi les points amenant le projet à atteindre les
objectifs définis. L'avis et la décision rapide du Task Manager vis-à-vis des changements sont très importants et sa présence sur le territoire Malgache est
plus que nécessaire pour un constat de visu et pour faciliter d'avantage la réponse aux problèmes.

Pour conclure, le plan d'action national, «MAP», et le Plan de Développement du Secteur Santé (PDSS) constituent le cadre dans lequel s'insère le
développement de l'approche sectorielle élargie, regroupant tout le soutien financier et technique octroyé au secteur santé au sein d'un cadre commun de
planification et de gestion s'articulant autour d'un programme des dépenses gouvernementales. Dans ce contexte, le Gouvernement sollicite et compte sur
la continuité dans une approche plus élargie de l'appui financier et technique de l'IDA à travers la première phase du Projet de Développement du
Système de Santé Pérenne (ou PDSSP).

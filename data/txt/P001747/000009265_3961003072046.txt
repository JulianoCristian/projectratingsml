1.07         For puiposes of this Project and in line with the definition adopted during
Government's divestiture progiam, the Malian private sector is defined as lncluding all those
enterprises (in an> area of economic activity), whose ownership is at least 80% held by non-
government interests. It therefore excludes the Government-owned PEs and those mixed companles
(SEM) in which the Government's share-holding exceeds 20%. As a group, over the past decade the
growth in the numbers of private enterprises has out-paced that of the public and mixed enterprise
sectors. An industrial sector survey undertaken in 1989 by the Direction Nationale des InduM*s
(DNI) revealed that whie privately-owned enterprises made up 45% of all industrial firms in 1981,
by 1988 that number had grown to 64%. The increase in the proportion of private industrial firms
was a reflection of the de facto failure and closure of several PE factories during the 1980 (in coment,
agricultural equipment, brick manufacturing, etc.) even before the recent formal PE privatizations and
liquidations. Ihis trend is expected to continue, as Gowernment expands its PE restructuring
program. Despite past biases, Mali's private sector enterprises account for the largest share of past
growth in employment and output. They employ over 90% of Mali's working popu lation and produce
a similar proportion of the country's output.
1.08         Mali's private enterprises run the gamut of the business spectrum, from small
backyard farms, family-run bakeries, two-person metal forges, restaurants, fruit packaging
cooperatives, medium-sized vegetable canneries, to large factories milling flour, weaving textiles,
producing batteries, or mining gold. Other than farming and trading, the key sub-sectors are food
processing, beverages, tobacco, construction, transport, and mining. Mali's modest industrial sector
(accounting for about 5% of GDP in 1990) is largely based on processing agricultural products
(paper, wood-working, textile, leather and clothing related goeds). Services comprising warehousing,
garages, banking and insurance make up the balance. Private enterprises in Mali are geographically
concentrated in the Bamako district, which houses close to three-quarters of them.
1.09         Private enterprises are essentially of small or medium size. They are estimated to
account for: (i) ail businesses of under ten workers; (à) 90% of businesses with ten to 25 workers;
(ii) about 90% of the companies with 26 to 100 workers and (iv) 40% of ail 101-500 workers
businesses. shere are no private enterprises with more than 500 employees. Many of Mali's private
enterprises are thought to be informal, in that they operate with no formal accounting systems, are
not registered, or do not pay any taxes.  ;urvey undertaken in 1990 by the Caub du Sahd indicated
that about 1500 small firms and an iru;v <rablo number of such informal enterprises are an important
source of training for craft worket. P id technicians in Bamako, Segou and Sikasso, where they
operate in wood and metal prod';tion, car and cycle repair, electrician work, and building and
construction.
1.10         Recent sector studies undertaken by consultants in preparation for dths Project (para.

2.01         The principal objective of the Project is to help foster the development of private
sector enterprises, so that they can lead the growth of Mali's economy. It aims at puting into place
the mechanisms and measures to back up the Government's strategy of breaking from past reliance
on the public sector. The Project proposes to achieve this by:
(a)    completing implementation of reCent improvements to the regulatory
environment;
(b)    assisting a private business support strucware (APEP) to coordinate a program
of institutional support to private non-financial enterprises. It is expected that
the intervention of APEP would foster a process of private sector institutions
assisting private sector enterprises in a dynamic and liberal market, and result
in improving private business management, removing technical constraints
facing private businesses, and restoring the banks' confidence in private
business's ability to conceive and prepare bankable projects. The catalytic
role that APEP is expected to play in the Project should increase businesses'
awareness of the value of these services once local firms providing such
services have been enabled to develop recognized capabilities, and thus hk ,'
in developing a self-sufficient consulting and training market locally. By the
end of the Project, this market should be in a position to maintain its growth
though possibly at a slower pace;
(c)   improving the functioning of economic chambers, BEP and departments of
the administration responsible for administering regulations affecting private
enterprises. CCIM in particular should by the end of the Project be able to
respond better to the common needs of private enterprises, thus
supplementing the individual services whose provision would be coordinated
by APEP, and BEP should become an effective Government agency for
implementing divestiture programs; and
(d)   inducing institutional and financial strengthening of the banking sector and the
preparation of a coherent financial sector strategy. In particular the Project
sets the stage for deepening Mali's financial system through: (a) the proposed
staff training to enable banks to better assess the risk of lending to private
enterprises, (b) the assistance to banks to bring up to internaticnal standards
their management systems, credit and financial policies and accounting and
auditing practices and (c) the studies to explore the possibilities for
establishing private locally based grassroots financial (urban mutual banking)
institutions and the modalities for financing housing in Mali. If successful,
the mutualist credit institutions could complement the short-term lending of
commercial banks, in particular lending to micro and small enterprises and
provide a link between the formal banking system and local communities.

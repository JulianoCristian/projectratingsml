Project Description:           The project will assist the Government in rehabilitating and main-
taining essential transport infrastructure and in improving sectoral
efficiency, including the strengthening of existing institutions as
well  as  the adoption  of sound  policy and  regulatory measures.
Subject to annual reviews and updatings,  the project covers the
Government's  1988-1990  total  sectoral  expenditure  program.   The
program places particular emphasis on road maintenance, including
increased funding and institutional improvements, selective road
rehabilitation of national and feeder roads, and upgrading of a
limited number of road sections. The project also includes compo-
nents for coastal protection, road safety improvements, and an
action plan to reduce railway deficits. Additionally, the project
will finance technical assistance to improve transport planning, for
manpower development and training and for the preparation of a set
of policy actions including a study of road user charges to improve
road pricing policy.
Project Benefits and Risks:   The project is designed to reverse the downward trend in efficiency
of transport infrastructure and services which, if unattended, would
increasingly deter economic activity, particularly agricultural
development and the important transit trade.  It will thus benefit
both the urban and rural populations of Togo, as well as the econo-
mies  of  neighboring  land-locked  countries.   Rehabilitation  and
maintenance of roads and other infrastructure have very high econom-
ic returns leading to substantial savings of foreign exchange funds,
as do improvements in road safety.  The coastal protection component
would avoid a potentially calamitous interruption of phosphate
exports vital to the economy, and of transport along the coastal
road; loss of these services would result in serious reductions in
export  earnings  and  would  require  costly  reconstruction.   This
component would also minimize involuntary resttlement at Aneho.
The main project risks are that the Government may not be able to
fully carry through the institutional and policy aspects of the
project, to resolve the main constraints in mobilizing adequate

T. TNTRODIICTION
A. Background
1.01      The Government of Togo has requested the Association's assistance
in financing a three-year slice of its 1986-1990 transport sector program.
The proposed project would cover the last three vears 1988-1990 of this
program and comprise rehabilitation, periodic and routine maintenance and
selective upgrading of the existing transport infrastructure, combined with
a set of measures to improve the institutional framework and the overall
efficiencv of the sector.   The proposed project builds on the recently
completed Feeder Roads Project (Cr. 810-TO) and the shortly to be completed
Fourth Highway Project (Cr. 1139-TO) as well as the Second Structural
Adjustment Credit (SAL) (Cr. 1599-TO).
1.02      The  project  would  help  the  Government  implement  a  coherent
strategy for the transport sector covering a realistic package of policv
measures, institutional developments and sector expenditures based on
agreed obiectives and annual reviews.  IDA assistance to the project would
help to (a) reorient the Government's public expenditure priorities from
expansion and upgrading of the transport infrastructure to proper mainte-
nance and rehabilitation; (b) reinforce the rational use of resources in
the composition and implementation of the program through increased use of
economic criteria; and (c) chz.nnel and direct support of the international
community to  the  development  of the  sector.   The  project  would  also
strengthen preliminary actions already taken by Togo under SAL II, i.e.,
increasing railway tariffs and budget allocations for road maintenance and
closing an uneconomic railway line, and would respond to a Government
request that the Association assume a leading coordinating role in the
transport sector.
B. The Economy
1.03      Togo, a countrv with a land area of 57,000 kMm2, a population of
2.9 million and a per capita GNP of US$250 (1984), is located on the Gulf
of Guinea between Ghana to the west, Benin to the east and Burkina to the
north (Map IBRD 20133). About two thirds of the population is concentrated
in the south.  Except for a long mountain range, which runs in the center
of the country along the border with Ghana and in the north along the
border with Benin, the terrain is flat and presents no major obstacles to
transport.
1.04      Togo's  economy  is  open  and  rests  mainlv  on  three  pillars:
agriculture,  phosphate  mining,  and  commerce.   The  agricultural  sector
accounts for almost one third of GDP and employs about 80% of the labor
force.  The sector provides a satisfactory level of food self-sufficiency
and accounts for about 30% of Togo's foreign exchange earnings, principally
through cocoa,  coffee and cotton exports,    Mining,  mainly phosphate,
accounts for about 10% of GDP and 45% of exports.  Commerce,  including



transit trade, and tourism account for about one-fourth of GDP, transport
for about 7% and construction for about 47.
1.05      Economic growth and policies have undergone several changes dur-
ing the last decade and a half.  Following a period of prudent economic
policies and rapid economic growth (7X p.8.), the Government, taking advan-
tage of the rapid increase in phosphate prices in 1974, undertook expan-
sionary fiscal and monetary policies and an ambitious public investment
program which, however,  did not contribute to long-term   onomic growth.
T.his, aggravated by deteriorating terms of trade, resuLted in serious
imbalances and deficits and a declining GDP between 1980 and 1983.  In
1982, the Government adopted a two-pronged approach of financial stabiliza-
tion and economic restructuring assisted by four IMF stand-by arrangements
and two IDA-supported structural adjustment programs. The present phase of
these programs   aims at reversing the economic decline and laying the
foundation for sustainable growth over the medium term, while continuing to
improve the balance of payments and the public finance.  The openness of
the Togolese economy and the competitive nature of the transit trade,
particularly south-north where the Togo corridor competes with several
other routes, make an efficient low cost transport svstem an essential part
of the Government's development program.
II. THE TRANSPORT SECTOR
A. Transport System and Sub-sectors
2.01      The transport sector in Togo serves the national transport needs
and is also an important transit link for land-locked Niger, Burkina and
Mali (almost exclusively northbound). The southern coastal road also serves
as the main transit link between Ghana to the west, and Benin and Nigeria
to the east.  The transport system encompasses all traditional modes of
transport except river.   It consists of a relatively extensive  (about

Road Safety
2.14      As  in many other African  countries,  road safety  is a major

Coastal Erosion
2.15      Coastal erosion is occurring all along the coast of Togo, east of
Lomt- and to some extent on the coast of Benin.  The prime source of the
problem is the littoral sediment transport which occurs eastward along the
coast of the Gulf of Benin.  This phenomenon is affected by man-made works
which interrupt the littoral drift, particularlv at the port of LomA (and
also at the port of Cotonou in Ber.in), where two moles stretch out from the
sand beach.  Sediments accumulate along the west mole, which acts as a
complete barrier to the littoral drift. As a result, sand supply downdrift
is prevented, which causes the coastline east of the port to recede on
average, 5 to 15 meters per year.  Of particular concern is the erosion
occurring at Kp4m4, the phosphate export wharf, and near the site of the
historic town of Aneho, 30 kilometers east of LomA. To prevent this, the
proposed project would (a) include a coastal protection program aiming at,
in the short and medium term, protecting the valuable assets at Kp6m6 and
Aneho; and (b) finance studies to identify measures to address the longer
term problems.
Cost Recovery
2.16      Cost recovery is a problem in some sub-sectors but not in others.
In the road sub-sector fuel prices exceed border prices and cannot easily
be increased further at present due to lower prices in the neighboring
countries of Benin, Ghana and particularly, Nigeria.  Road user revenues
exceed recent road expenditures (maintenance and construction) and would
also exceed the greatly increased level of road maintenance expenditures
foreseen under the proposed project  (Table 2-1).  There is, however, an
imbalance between the taxatio'  on light and heavy vehicles in the sense
that road user revenues from heavy vehicles do not cover road costs attrib-
utable to this type of vehicle.   This is particularly true  for heavy
foreign vehicles which generally do not pay any road user taxes in Togo as
they often import large quantities of fuel in auxiliary tanks; fuel in
excess of 200 liters is supposed to be taxed but this provision is largely
not enforced.  However, in light of the importance of the transit trade to
Togo's economy and the competitive situation between the Togo corridor and
alternative routes for the land-locked countries, there is a need for care-
ful study before substantial new road user taxes on heavy vehicles are
introduced in Togo.  A study of road user taxes for heavy vehicles in the
Togo corridor, taking into account the situation in the competing corri-
dors, as well as the existing problem of axle loads, is therefore a high
priority item in DGT's work program and will be funded under the PPF
(para 3.17).
2.17      Cost recovery is also a major problem in the railway sub-sector
as already discussed in para 2.13 above. It is not a problem in the port

SOTONAM)  have  cost  recovery  problems.   However,  the  trucking  company

C. Sector Strategy and Policy Framework
2.19      The Government's sector policy as preserted in its policv state-

J. Environmental Implications
3.42      The only project component which has environmental implications
is the coastal protection works.  The main purpose of the coastal protec-
tion program is to control shoreline erosion at the two sites of Kpeme and
An6ho, thus preventing valuable infrastructure to be lost. However, it is
widely recognized that installations protecting limited stretches of coast-
line such as the proposed groin systems may provoke erosion problems down-
drift of the protected area, in this case to the east of Aneho.  The pro-
tection system (nine groins at Agbodrafo-Kp6me and five groins at Aneho,
one of which would be a T-shaped groin) is designed in such a way that
downdrift effects would be minimized. Moreover, the set of groins would be



- 21 -
built in two phases. The first phase would aim at urgently protecting the
most endangered areas.  Model studies are underway to optimize the design
of the second phase and minimize the long term effects.
3.43      Nonetheless, the construction of the grolns of the second phase
at Aneho still mav entail erosion of the beach barrier which now closes the
lagoon further to the east of An6ho.  While a semi-permanent break would
help reduce the risk of annual flooding of Aneho during periods of heavv
rainfall, a permanent break may result in ecological changes in the lagoon.
To prevent this, a barrage could be built across the lagoon to prevent the
ingress of the saline wedge and yet enable the excess waters of the lagoon
to discharge when necessary. The feasibility studv of the barrage is being
financed bv FAC.  A provision is included in the program to cover the
possible cost of the barrage, estimated at about US$3 million equivalent.
3.44      The general erosion process which is moving eastward will finally
reach the coast of Benin.  At present, accretion is occurring along the
adjacent coast of Benin as a result of the erosion in Togo.  Without the
project, the erosion might affect the first sensitive target, Grand Popo,
which is located 27 km East of An6ho, in about 20 years.  The project is
likely to speed up the process by no more than two to three years. Conse-
quently, protective  structures along the Coast of Benin should not be
required as a result of the project. The Beninese Government is currently
monitoring the phenomenon along the coast of Benin with a view to imple-
menting a preventive policy. Moreover, periodic meetings are held on this
matter between Togo and Benin.
3.45      In summary, the environmental impact of the coastal protection
works will be confined to the vicinity of the structures and will be mainly
positive by protecting valuable assets.   A possible adverse effect may
affect the marine conditions at the outlet of the lagoon and further east.
However, this effect is limited in comparison with the general erosion
process occurring along the coast of the Gulf of Benin, and it will be
minimized through the use of appropriate design features determined through
the information obtained from the monitoring (para 3.42).

Transport System and Sub-sectors
Ports and Coastal Erosion
1.        The port infrastructure comprises a deepwater port in Lom4 and
a phosphate wharf at Kpeme.  The Port of Lomi  is the focal point of the
national transport system and the terminal of the Togo route to the land
locked countries of Niger (109,000 tons in 1986) and Burkina (86,300
tons in 1986) and Mali (22,500 tons).   It is also a transit port for
Nigeria and other  destinations  (43,000  tons).   In  1986,  about  20%
(244,000 tons) of Lome port general cargo traffic (1191,000 tons) was
transit cargo.  This was down from 327,000 tons (37%) in 1985.  Total
port cargo throughput, which was about 2.7 million tons in 1980, fell to
1.3 million tons in 1984, reflecting a decline in crude oil transship-
ments for Nigeria and in clinker exports.  Traffic picked up to 1.6
million tora in 1986, a 6% increase over 1985 figures. Table 1 provides
data on port traffic statistics.   The port capacity is by and large
sufficient to meet future general cargo traffic for the next ten years.
Port operations are carried out by the port administration and compare
reasonably well with other African ports; a problem of aging cargo
handling equipment and shortage of spare parts is being handled with
German assistance.
2.        The wharf of Kpeme is a vital facility for Togo.  It handles
phosphate exports (2.7 million tons in 1985) which account for about
40-50% of the country's exports. The on-shore foundations of the wharf
are threatened by coastal erosion.  In July 1985, a rip-rap revetment
was urgently provided to protect the most endengered part of the wharf.
Such a protection can only be temporary because erosion of adjacent
areas will continue to threaten the wharf and the factory itself.
Clearly, the destruction of the wharf would cut the country's economic
lifeline. To prevent this, new protective measures are included in the
proposed project (para. 3.16 and Annex 3-1, para. 7-9).
3.        Coastal Erosion is occurring not only at Kp6mg but all along
the coast of Togo, east of Lome and to some extent on the coast of
Benin. The prime source of the problem is the littoral sediment trans-
port which occurs eastward along the coast of the Gulf of Benin at
various  rates  depending  on  the  orientation of  the coastline.   The
littoral transport is particularly heavy along Togo and Benin where its
rate is one of the highest in the world (between 1 and 1.5 million cubic
meters per year). This phenomenon is affected by man-made works which
interrupt the littoral drift. An example of this is the construction in
1968 of the port of Lom6, featuring two moles which stretch out from the
saud beach.  These moles are effectlve in reducing wave activity within
the port, but the west mole acts as a complete barrier to the littoral
drift.    As  sediments  accumulate  along  the west mole,  sand supply
downdrift is prevented, which causes the coastline east of the port to
recede (in average 5 to 15 meters per year). In some areas, the erosion
has been v'rtually halted by a sandstone reef named "beach rock", but in

Railways
4.        Unless major new phosphate on other bulk traffic sources are
discovered the Togolese Railways has no clear long-term prospect. It is
therefore faced with decisions regarding a gradual phasing out of
operations while minimizing economic and financial losses.   The main
question concerns the speed of this phasing out and the prospect of
preserving part of the network for the medium-term.
5.        The railway network consists of three main branches extending
from Lom6 to Aneho (47 km), Kpalime (119 km), and Blitta (276 km) with a
spur off the Blitta line to the CIMAO plant at Tabligbo (50 km). Except
for the CIMAO spur, the railway lines are generally paralleled by paved
roads. The Lome-Aneho line is in such an advanced state of deteriora-
tion that operating safety has been gravely threatened and as a result
daily services have been cut from 4 in 1981, to 1 by mid-1986. The line
was closed in April 1987 with the understanding that the llne would
exceptionally continue to be used only for transport of rocks for the
coastal protection works. The Lom6-Kpalime line is also run down, but
not to the same extent. Its track is estimated to have a safe residual
life of five to six years at the end of which period services would have
to be cut back,  preparatory  to eventual  closure of the  line.   The
Lom6-Blitta line is in a much better state, although track repairs are
needed. The branch line to Tabligbo was built to serve CIMAO, a large
export oriented cement complex which started to operate in 1982; CIMAO
was, however, closed two years later with no immediate prospect for its
reopening due to high production costs and depressed market conditions.
The average age of CFT locomotives is about 25 years and that of its
rolling stock about 40 years, excluding those of CIMAO which are not
available to CFT for general use.  Traffic which has been declining in
recent years, totalled some 1.5 million passengers and only 151,000 tons
in 1985 with average dlstances of 45 and 62 km respectively, and 1.1
million passengers and about 142,000 tons in 1986 (Table 2).  It repre-
sents only some five to six percent of interurban passenger traffic and
less than two percent of goods traffic.

7.        Air transport in Togo is focussed on international traffic,
with the international airport at Lome handling some 350,000 passengers

Five secondary airfields play a minor role and the international

Roads
8.        Road Network:   The Togolese road network consists of about
7,500 km of roads of which 1,580 km are paved and 128 km are engineered
gravel roads. The remaining network totals about 1,200 km of ordinary
gravel/earth roads, 3,050 km of rural feeder roads and 1,500 km of
unclassified tracks (Table 3).  The last administrative classification
of this network was made by a Presidential Decree in 1968. At that time
the national network consisted of 15 national roads, totaling about
1,700 km, and an undefined length of other roads.  This classification
will now be updated to take into consideration the important extension
and uipgrading works carried out by the Togolese Public Works Department
(DTP)  with  the  financial  assistance  of many  external  donors.   The
density of the network (0.13 km per square km) compares favorably with
those in neighboring countries and is sufficient to serve the needs of
agricultural, commercial and social activities in the country. A 1985
evaluation of the paved network classified 40% as good, 22% as medium,
and 38% as bad; while for the gravel roads the shares were 20%, 10%, and
70% respectively.

in Togo is a major problem.  Estimates from studies in countries with
similar road networks and traffic patterns indicate that the economic
loss through road traffic accidents in Togo would be somewhere between
CFAF 2.5 billion and CFAF 7.5 billion a year, covering material damage,
production loss, administrative costs and hospitalization expenditures,
but not assigning economic value to human life or suffering.   These
losses are in the same order of magnitude as the total yearlv resource
use in the proposed program for road infrastructure. A decrease in the
accident volume during the early eighties can be explained by the
general economic difficulties and a subsequent stagnation in vehicle
ownership and road traffic.  There is at present no systematic data
collection in the area of road safety.  The figures that are published
are  of  limited  reliability,  which makes  it difficult  to draw  firm
conclusions on the development in number of accidents and in accident
risk.  Health risk due to road traffic accidents in Togo is however of
the same magnitude as in European countries with more reliable statis-
tics and with 40 times as many motor vehicles.  Unless Togo is facing
maior economic difficulties,  the countrv will experience a growth in
vehicle ownership, which is presently extremelv low when compared to
other African countries, and thereby a continued rise in accidents from
the bottom level in 1984.  Efforts to improve road safety are todav weak
and uncoordinated.  Clearlv, improvements are urgently called for and
would yield verv substantial benefits.

SECTOR MANAGEMENT AND INSTITUTIONAL FRAMEWORK
Transport Planning
1.        Due  to  the  recent  critical  economic  situation  in  Togo,
short-term considerations have taken lead over medium-term planning with
most planning being centralized at MPM.  Starting with SAL II greater
emphasis is being given to medium-term planning with strengthened
overall planning capabilities  at MPM.   MPM  is thus responsible  for
overall planning and sector priorities while the technical ministries
are  responsible  for  sectoral  management  and  studies  of  project
preparation and execution. Studies, construction and maintenance of the
national and district road network are the responsibilities of the
Public Works Department (DTP) of MEMPT (para. 13 below), while feeder
roads and tracks are the responsibility of the National Feeder Roads
Service (SNPR), previously in the Ministry of Rural Works but recently
integrated in DTP, as well as the Ministry of the Interior through its
District offices.   SOTOCO and other agricultural agencies under the
control of the Ministry of Rural Development (MDR) are also involved in
the construction and maintenance of feeder roads.  MCT is responsible
for the conception, application and control of all measures to promote
transport, including the supervision of the Togolese Railways and other
transport enterprises.
2.        MCT was  reorganized  in  1980  to  improve  overall  transport
planning  and  coordination  through  the  establishment  of  a  General
Directorate  of Transpert  (DGT)  with  responsibilities  for:   (i) the
implementation of the Government's transport policy; (ii) development of
transport plans and control of their implementation; (iii) studies and
control of tariffs; (iv) coordination of the transport directorates,
agencies and companies; and (v) documentation and studies as well as
professional  training  in  the  transportation  field.   DGT  includes  a
planning and coordination division and a general administration unit as
well as modal directorates for Road Transport (DTR), Maritime Affairs,
Civil Aviation (DAC) and Meteorology.
3.        The Fourth Highway Project provided technical assistance to
DGT  and  its  planning  and  coordination  service  through  on-the-job
training and a number of sector and subsector studies as indicated under
Section II of the Project File.  MCT has recognized that the progress
made under the Fourth Highway Project was insufficient to make DGT fully
self-sufficient and that there is a danger that the momentum will be
lost.   It  has  therefore  requested  the  Association's  assistance  in
strengthening DGT's planning and studies capabilities through the
provision of further technical assistance and training both on-the-job
and through outside seminars or courses. At the same time MCT will take
steps to recruit at least one more economist for DGT and to clarify the
internal relationship between DGT and the modal directorates.

Transport Enterprises
4.        The port of Lom6 is run by the Lome Port Authority (PAL), an
autonomous  public  agency.   The  Board  of  Directors  is  composed  of
representatives from ministries, publlc users and neighboring countries.
PAL is responslble for the extension, improvement and rehabilitation
works as well as for the operation, maintenance and pollce of the harbor
and management of the real estate surrounding it.  PAL Is carrying out
both on-board and shore handling operations. Its policies, particularly
tariff  policies,   are  subject  to  Government  approval.   However,
Government does not interfere in operational matters, where autonomy is
real.   The quality of management  is good and will be sustained and
further improved through new programs of technical assistance from
bilateral donors.
5.        Financial accounting at PAL corresponds  to normal business
practices  and  generates  reliable  information.   Data  processing  is
contracted  out  which  generally  results  in  timely  accounts.   Cost
accounting is the main area of financial management whare progress is
needed and PAL has recently hired an expert for a 2-year duration with
financing routinely provided by KfW.
6.        Port profitability has improved over the last 3 years, the
rate of return on fixed assets in operation rising from 1.4% in 1983 to
2.6% In 1985 (see Table 1). Tariffs were increased in June 1986 after
eleven years of stability which, according to the 1986 preliminary
results, brought the working ratio down to 0.55 from 0.64 a year before.
A substantial part of the income came from the interest earned on
deposits: as a sole shareholder the Government may claim part or total
of the yearly income but largely abstained to do it leaving PAL with an
accumulated cash of CFAF 4.5 billion as of March 1987.
7.        PAL's spending is tightly controlled by the Government; any
expenditure in excess of CFAF 5 m and CFAF 10 m must respectively get
prior authorization from the Minister and the President of the Republic.
This severe constraint is not speclfic to PAL and calls for a general
overhaul of the legislation in Togo and not for actlon at the sector
level.
8.        Financing  of  investments  is  the  responsibility  of  the
Government for port infrastructure, building and capital dredging. All
other investments are paid for by PAL which also bears all maintenance
and other operating costs.
9.        The  Togolese  Railway:   Management  and  operations  of  the
railway in Togo is entrusted to CFT (Chemins de Fer du Togo), a state
agency with very limited autonomy in practice. Financially, CFT works
as a "Budget Annexe" and all payments are centrally controlled prior to
their liquidation at Treasury. With technical assistance received under
the Fourth Highway Project, complementary commercial and cost accounting
systems has been rejuvenated but then deteriorated rapidly after
technical assistance  left  in  1984.   Draft statements  for  1985 were
prepared in early 1986 and found not reliable. One reason is that the
information collected by the accounts department is not exhaustive, as a
result of its extra-legal status and of the lack of interest shown by

the higher managements for its activity. Cost accounting faces the same
difficulties with in addition inherent weaknesses due to the deficient
supervision of staff assigned to it.   Financial management  is weak
overall and despite the controls exerted at the Ministry of Economy and
Finance on the budget preparation and execution, there is relatively
little accountability of CFT's management for the resources it uses.
Other serious deficiencies are found on the operational side and with

10.       Although no accurate assesasment is possible because the only
reliable data are those kept on a cash basis and on a budget type which
does not allow to relate revenues and expenditures to any given year,
the financial position of the railway has clearly worsened, nearing the
point of no return wlth a working ratio of 2.2 in 1986.  The tariff
increases agreed under SAL II were unable to buffer the influence of
declining traffic and the CFT revenues, as shown in Table 2, fell 16% In
1986 from the previous year. The passenger traffic which generates more
than 50% of the total revenue was 23X lower and the cement traffic fell
33%  to  a  low  26,000  tons.   There  is    little  margin  to  further
adjustments which would not immediately result in more traffic diversion
to road transport.  The financial imbalances are partly due to a the
closure of the CIMAO operations; it also has to do wlth the contlnuation
of non-economic services, a labor force (about 1060 staff) in excess of
needs and an equipment both obsolete and insufficient in number.
11.       The  Government  critically  needs  to  redefine  its  policy
regarding CFT for the running of which about CFAF 600 m must be injected
each year as a supplement to the insufficient operating revenues. The
path to a very unlikely financial recovery would call for drastic
measures to contain costs, such as eliminating passenger traffic,
discontinuing non-viable services, redeploying the still usable rolling
stock and motive power, and laying off a large quantity of staff. The
two branch lines LomA-Aneho and Lome-Kpalime are generally paralleled by
paved roads and are prima facie candidates for an early closure; the
Lome-Aneho line was closed in April 1987, except for temporary rock
transport (Annex 2-1, para. 5). The future of the railway would rely on
a hypothetical reclaiming of the lost cement traffic, the continuation
of the port shuttle, the only profitable branch, and the attraction of
about 70,000 tons of textile traffic associated to an ongoing
development  in the north.   Consultant services are proposed  to be
financed under a Project Preparation Facility to refine the assessment
of the financial condition of the railway, forecast potential traffic
over the next 15 years, and measure the net earning capacity of CFT
under a set of realistic scenarios.   Additional consultant services
would be hired to prepare a detailed action plan for rehabilitation of
the railway, if deemed possible, which would then be executed under the
proposed Program. On the other hand a phasing out option would call for
measures designed to smooth out the process and minimize its social
cost, such as retraining of staff for which a line of credit could be
made available under the proposed credit.

Road Administration
13.       MEPT,  through its Public Works Directorate  (DTP),  has the
overall responsibility for managing about 2,500 km of national and
district roads; these are likely to become national roads under the
expected reclassification of the road network.
14.        DTP has no financial autonomy:  comiitments are proposed to
the approval of the Ministry of Economy and Finance for spending under
the general budget, and of the Ministry of Planning for investments.
The same authorizations are needed for payments which are executed by
the Treasurer through a lengthy process. This system is safe but little
control is exerted on cost effectiveness In road works. DTP has three
technical divisions in charge respectively of the road network, official
buildings and public works equipment. The responsibilities of the Road
Division (AR) include planning, design and/or control of, and execution
of, maintenance and construction works by force account. AR also has
the administrative control of LNBTP, which is an autonomous institution
under control of MEPT. It has an important road construction equipment
fleet operated by the road construction and maintenance section.  The
Equipment Division (APM), with five regional workshops, is responsible
for procurement of road construction and maintenance equipment, vehicles
and spare parts, maintenance, repairs and periodic inspection of that
equipment and cost accounting. Due to a lack of specialized staff and
insufficient funding, the APM's equipment availability is relatively
low.
15.       Routine  maintenance  works  of  the  national  network  are
implemented by five regional subdivisions each one being divided in
local sectors. The regional subdivisions are responsible for the more
specialized works such as surface patching of paved roads, mechanical
reshaping of gravel roads, maintenance of official buildings and houses
belonging to the state, assistance and/or control of works carried out
by other MEPT directorates while the local sectors are responsible for
manual routine maintenance.  For road maintenance questions, the chief
of subdivisions report directly to the DTP which is responsible for cost
accounting and control of implementation.
16.       The administration and development of feeder roads is still
ill-defined and shared between several Government agencies without clear
strategy   for   planning,   construction   and   maintenance.    During
negotiations of the now completed feeder roads project (Cr. 810 TO) the
Government agreed to establish a specialized service within MAR to
monitor the project and, progressively monitor the economic, social and

Human Resources
17.       DTP has a total staff of about 1620 of which about 700 are
regularly employed on road works  (Table 3).   Only 24  (1.5%) have a
university degree and 18 (1.1%) a technical degree. On the other hand
about 950 mechanics and laborers are illiterate.
18.       These figures illustrate the poor staffing situation in DTP.
The training programs developed under the Fourth Highway Project have
got mixed results for the reason that most of trained people were below
an acceptable level to benefit from the programs.  This situation was
aggravated by the temporary freeze on new recruitment and the retirement
in 1985 of staff over 55 years of age or with 30 years service in the
public sector.  The recruitment freeze is only now being selectively
lifted.

Introduction

notamment la concurrence accentu6e des transports routiers.   Les re-

operations de la CIMAO.
2.        Dans le contexte de la planification du secteur des transports
par 1'Etat, tous les modes et toutes les institutions du secteur font
l'objet d'un examen visant A determiner leurs resultats actuels, les
carences de la politique generale et les insuffisances institutionnelles
ainsi que les r6formes A mettre en oeuvre pour assurer la rehabilitation

ressort d'enquetes prAliminaires que la degradation de l'infrastructure

choses jette A juste titre de graves doutes quant A l'adoption des
correctifs usuels, tels que nouveaux investissements, restructuration de
la gestion, etc.  Le systeme financier des CFT manque considArablement
de clarte.   Parall*lement,  on espere d'une  fason tres vague que se

Past and Ongoing IDA Projects in the Transport Sector in Togo
1.        The Association has  so far contributed  only  to  the highway
subsector.  Assistance totalling US$48.2 million has involved four highway
projects and one feeder road project.   The Bank Group's projects were
implemented with reasonable success except in road maintenance and training
which suffered from shortcomings in maintenance operations and a shortage
and turn-over of trained personnel and the feeder roads project which
suffered from difficulties with local funding and staffing.
2.        The First Highway Project  (Cr.  131-TO, US$3.5 million,  1968)
consisted of a four-year road maintenance program and preinvestment studies
of about 450 km of secondarv roads.  The project was completed in November
1973, about a year later than originally scheduled. Equipment purchase and
personnel training for the maintenance program were executed, however,
setting up a new maintenance organization proved more difficult than
expected, and at project completion only half of the expected yearly output
had been achieved.   The Project  Performance  Audit Report  (1977)  also
concluded that the quality of PWD staff and availability of counterparts
were overestimated, and part of the road maintenance equipment was diverted
to other tasks.   In spite of these shortcomings,  the project remained
economically justified.
3.        The Second Highway Project  (Cr. 450-TO, US$8.7 million,  1973)
consisted of a rehabilitation of the paved Blitta-Sokode road (78 km),
construction of the Sokode-Tchamba-Kambole (75 km) and Agou-Notes roads
(51 km) to gravel standards; a survey of about 2,000 km of unpaved roads;
preinvestment studies for reconstruction of the Aneho-Tabligbo road
(45 km); and technical assistance to the Road Transport Service (DTR) in
MTC. As a result of worldwide inflation, the bids for road construction
were 30% higher than appraisal estimates, and construction of the
Agou-Notse road was postponed and later included in the Third Highway
Project.  Improvement of the Tchamba-Kambole road (40 km) by force account
was deleted and carried out by PWD under its own budget. This project was
completed in June 1979, about 30 months later than estimated at appraisal.
The delay was due to late implementation of technical assistance to DTR and
late installation of laboratory equipment.  The Project Completion Report
(1979) concluded that the objectives of the project were basically met.
Each project component was economically justified, but, the technical
assistance component failed to achieve expected objectives (Project Comple-
tion Report, 1979). The borrower's performance was good, except in insti-
tution building which was to be remedied under the Third Highway Project.
4.        The Third Highway Project  (Cr.  693-TO,  US$10 million,  1977)
consisted of reconstruction of the Aneho-Tabligbo (45 km) and the
Agou-Notse (51 km) roads; purchase of road maintenance equipment; provision
of technical assistance and fellowships to PWD for road maintenance and
strengthening of the road planning and design unit, and to MCT for overall

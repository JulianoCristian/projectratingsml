        I.Introduction

 1.     This Project Paper seeks the approval o f the Executive Directors to provide, under OP
 13.20, an additional loan in the amount o f US$5.0 million to the Government o f Panama, (Loan
7032-PAN). The Republic o f Panama (RoP) has agreed to supplement the loan with an
additional US$2 million in counterpart funding.

2.        The proposed additional loan would finance the costs associated with the further
scaling-up o f activities in order to enhance the impact o f this well-performing project. N o
changes are proposedto the design o f the project. The Additional Financing (AF) would fund the
expansion o f all activities related to the three components o f school quality, early childhood
education, and institutional strengthening, as well as the continuation o fproject coordination.

3.      The largest part o f the additional financing would go toward funding the expansion of
non-formal early childhood interventions, increasing the number o f beneficiaries by 3,125
children in the non-formal preschool program (CEFACEI) and 900 children in the home-based
early childhood education program. In the school quality component, the distance education
program (telebhsica) for grades 7, 8, and 9 would be scaled up to include 20 new centers for 7th
grade, which, together with support to previously established centers in all grades, would serve a
total o f 6,972 students in isolated rural areas. In this area, the AF would also finance the
development o f materials and teacher training for multigrade and bilingual (Spanish and
indigenous) education as well as supervision and evaluation o f infrastructure projects.
Continuation o f the scholarship program would be ensured through fundingprovided directly by
the IFARHU-Institute      for Training and Enhancement o f Human Resources (Instituto para la
Formacidn y Aprovechamiento de Recursos Humanos). Finally, in the area o f institutional
strengthening, MEDUCA would continue to build its human resource database, expand the
management information system to include new modules, and strengthen processes o f
decentralization and modernization o f MEDUCA. It would also begin a process o f
systematization o f all school information into a single management database.

4.      The project development objectives would remain unchanged. Thus the AF would
continue to support the project's key development objectives, including: (i)improving the
quality and efficiency o f basic education; (ii)improving equity o f access to preschool and basic
education; (iii)expanding coverage o f preschool and early childhood education; and (iv)
strengthening the planning, supervision and policy-making capacity o f MEDUCA.

Specifically, the project has carried out the following activities:

8.      Component 1: Improvement o f the Oualitv o f Basic Education. The project has fully
carried out the scholarship subcomponent, awarding 34,893 scholarships (as compared to a goal
o f 24,000) totaling over US$l 1.0 million. It has distributed over 260,000 sets o f materials to
multigrade students, distributed bilingual language materials to all first- and second-grade
students in the three target indigenous areas o f Kuna, Ngobe and Ember6 (a total o f 25,040 sets
o f materials), and provided training to all first-grade indigenous teachers. It has also established
80 distance education centers (compared to its goal o f 30), which have served a total o f 7,701
students over the course o f the project. Within the infrastructure subcomponent, 227 educational
infrastructures have been completed, and 89 o f the 316 proposed infiastructures are still in the
process o f construction. It should be noted that while the initial plan was to finalize these
activities before the closing date o f the project, progress has been delayed in the last four months
by the remoteness o f some o f the schools, the recent floods that affected communities in Col6n
and Cocle where construction had to be postponed, and unforeseen delays within the Social
Investment Fund, which i s the dedicated implementing agency responsible for the infrastructure
component. Although these events were beyond the immediate control o f the Ministry o f
Education, the Department o f School Infrastructure o f the Ministry o f Education has adopted
measures to ensure the completion o f all activities by October 30, 2007. Additional financing
will provide for outside supervision to ensure that all constructions are completed within the
necessary requirements, especially those inthe most isolated areas.

9.      Component 2: Expansion o f Preschool Education. Through this component, the project
has established 868 non-formal preschools (CEFACEIs) and 554 home-based early education
programs in rural, indigenous and marginalized urban areas, serving a total o f 112,018 children
and surpassing its goal by more than 40 percent. The success o f this component is evidenced by
the scope o f its coverage and evidence o f its quality, as illustrated in two evaluations o f this
component. Since project inception, enrollment o f 4- and 5-year-olds has risen substantially,
increasing from 38 percent in 2001 to 60 percent in 2006-an achievement to which the existing
project has made a significant contribution. About 32 percent o f all children enrolled in
preschool programs attend CEFACEIs, up from 7 percent in 2001. Furthermore, the most recent
results o f an evaluation o f both early childhood modalities show that in addition to expanding
coverage o f preschool and early childhood education, such coverage has been expanded with
quality, and has contributed to children's success in the first and second grades as evidenced by
test scores on Spanish language and Mathematics.

10.     Component 3: Institutional Strenahenina. The project has supported the creation and
implementation o f a computerized human resources information system (SIAREH) for the
management and administration o f teachers at the national level. The system has increased the
efficiency o f teacher hiring, transfers, and payments, reducing the number o f official teacher
complaints from 35 percent to 1 percent o f all cases. It has also facilitated other types o f human
resource transactions via Internet, such as the generation o f letters and certificates, vacation
requests, maternity and study leave requests, and incorporation into the retirement program, and
would eventually house a digital documentation center. The system has been developed
simultaneously with the creation o f the MEDUCA website which would act as a portal for
SIAREH.

 12.     Justification for Additional Financing: The additional resources are needed for the
Government to scale up the activities already inprogress. Despite gains innet enrollment rates in
both pre-primary and secondary education over the past seven years, coverage in both areas
remains deficient, especially in extremely poor and isolated areas. The majority o f underserved
students reside inindigenous, rural, or marginalized urban areas-the same populations that would
benefit from this additional financing. Moreover, the proposed activities would contribute to the
national goals o f universalizing educational coverage o f 5-year-olds by the year 2009 and
increasing coverage o f 3- and 4-year-olds. Furthermore, while the project has made headway in
developing and distributingmaterials for multigrade schools, such schools make up 74 percent o f
the total in the country. As such, the project would need to continue these efforts in order to
eventually meet the needs o f all multigrade and indigenous schools.

13.      Inaddition to scalingup project impact and effectiveness, the additional financing would
have the added benefit o f serving as bridge until a new project, currently in the pipeline for
Board approval in the second semester of 2008, i s prepared. The Government does not want to
delay the continuation and scaling-up o f program activities in areas o f the country where need i s
greatest and where the program has produced positive results. The additional financing would
provide a foundation for defining the strategic direction o f the new project and would prepare for
the transition away from the Project Coordination Unit and into reliance on MEDUCA's own
structures and capacities. Finally, there is a strong rationale for expanding some key elements o f
the project, especially given the need to supplement the supply o f non-formal preschool centers
to meet demands created by the Red de Oportunidades CCT program in poor, underserved, and
isolated areas.

        IV. Consistencywith ISN

27.     The proposed activities are aligned with the current Interim Strategy Note for Panama,
which was approved in August 30, 2005. Inparticular, the expanded activities would contribute
to the ISN objectives o f (i)reducing poverty and improving income distribution, and (ii)
developing o f human capital. The project aims to correct inequities in social spending by
targeting investments in early and basic education and ensuring that educational programs reach
the most destitute groups o f Panamanian society many o f whom live in conditions o f relative
isolation and neglect in indigenous, rural and marginal urban communities. The project would
continue to support expansion o f early childhood, preschool education, and pre-secondary
distance education (telebcisica) services to children o f poor families who do not have access to
early educational experiences and who often have the greatest difficulties in entering, remaining
in and completing basic education. By supporting innovative educational models such as non-
formal early childhood interventions and distance education that involve the family and the
community, the project would increase awareness o f child development needs and foster the
creation o f an enriched community and home environment for child growth and education. By
supporting institutional development, the project would assist the government to strengthen its
capacity to replicate project activities inother areas and implement its decentralization strategy.

        V. Appraisalof Scaled-up ProjectActivities

28.     The AF would fund activities that are ongoing and as such the appraisal o f the scaled-up

29.     Economic and Financial: Results indicate that overall, the project i s economically viable.
According to the original economic analysis carried out in the P A D for the second basic
education project, it i s viable even when we consider the full costs o f the project (including
capacity building, institutional strengthening, and the opportunity cost to households with
children), and the economic impact o f the program is expected to increase given the expansion in
the number o f children participating. Furthermore, since the initial analysis was carried out, new
evidence has come to light about the benefits o f early childhood education in Latin America,
especially among disadvantaged children. Children who attend preschool have better learning
outcomes in primary school and accumulate more years o f education on average than children
who do not attend preschool. Along these lines, the 2005 impact evaluation o f the early

3. Assessment of Development Objective and Design, and of Quality at Entry

3.1 Original Objective:
The objective of the Urban Infrastructure Services Development Project (UISDP) ­ approved in 1998 - was "to
contribute to the expansion and solidifying of the credit market for public autonomous and private providers of
local public services by enhancing FINDETER's capacity to offer long-term financing for urban infrastructure
investments." This UISDP program objective bears an important relation to two other programs involving local
urban finance and FINDETER.

FINDETER was founded in 1990 to develop the municipal and urban-services credit market. This new institution
inherited FFDU, (the Financial Fund for Urban Development, which had made municipal loans in the 1980s), and
this previous organization's mandate to support decentralization under the decentralization law of 1986.

First, the UISDP program followed on a previous World Bank-sponsored project (PDT 3336-CO) implemented
from 1991 to 1996 with FINDETER as the executing agency. This first project sought to "assist Government in the
implementation of its Municipal Development Program" and further decentralization to local government. During
much of the 1990s, FINDETER channeled sizeable amounts of central government grant funding to local
governments. These grants came packaged with credit to write-down the costs of "social" infrastructure and
sometimes exceeded the amount of FINDETER credit, thus confusing FINDETER's role and detracting from a
hard-credit organizational culture. Relative to this previous World Bank-project, the UISDP program sought to
strengthen credit markets for local development and the capacity of FINDETER to function as a market-rate lender
rather than as a grant-making institution. The 1996-97 period during which the project was designed appeared
particularly propitious for expansion of credit for local development. Many financial institutions (IFIs) had started
lending for urban services, a municipal bond market was developing, and the prospects for FINDETER providing
funding for these IFI´s to increase urban-service lending and to lengthen the tenor of loans necessary for social
infrastructure ­ the key value-added of FINDETER funding in the Colombian market context - seemed good.

Second, a companion IDB program ­ prepared parallel to and approved in 1998 along with the UISDP ­ aimed "to
(i) solidify the sub sovereign credit market by enhancing FINDETER´s capacity to finance municipal investments;
(ii) strengthen the financial and management capacity of municipalities to get better access to commercial
borrowing and improve the provision of municipal infrastructure services; and (iii) help FINDETER become an
efficient second-tier financial institution". Relative to this companion IDB program that funded FINDETER to
extend credit for local government, the World-Bank UISDP program supported the institution to provide
market-rate funding for autonomous public-service agencies (i.e. mainly water and sanitation companies) and the
private sector.   This restriction of funding under the UISDP program to autonomous service companies and the
private -sector reinforced a Colombian government policy for shifting urban-service provision ­ particularly for
water and sanitation ­ from the central administrations of municipalities to public, private, and mixed companies.
In this regard, Law 142 of July 11, 1994 required: (a) that service companies adopt business accounting and
management practices, including self-financing of capital investments; (b) set deadlines for conversion of urban
service providers into public, mixed- or private-sector enterprises; and (c) established a commission at the national
level (the "Superservicios") to regulate each service.

A third comparison distinguishes the UISDP program and FINDETER, generally.                      Typical "municipal
development funds" (or "MDF's) lend directly to local governments and take the form of central government
agencies or windows, within ministries. In contrast, FINDETER operates as an autonomous second-tier finance
institution that rediscounts urban service and municipal loans made by qualified first-tier lenders, mostly
commercial banks. These first-tier lenders take 100% of the credit risk, while FINDETER loans are backed by
both the balance sheet of the first-tier lender and the guarantees (transfers and project revenue) that secure the loan
of the IFI to the end borrower. This more market-oriented structure has noted advantages: a) the private sector
rather than Government takes the credit risk on the infrastructure loan, thus avoiding the moral hazard of most
MDFs and better securing MDF loans; and (b) one of the key goals of MDFs ­ stimulating private credit ­ is
achieved directly rather than through a demonstration effect that often fails to work well. However, in turn, this
structure requires that first-tier financial institutions participate by "intermediating" FINDETER funding to
service-providers, and adds an extra cost ­ the margin of the first-tier lender.

The subprojects financed under the UISDP program (henceforth, "the project") consisted of investments to develop,
improve and/or to expand services to the eligible project entities in sectors under the responsibility of local
governments such as: water supply and sewerage, urban transport, environmental protection, solid waste
management, bus terminals, river wharves, market places, parks and green areas, abattoirs, local
telecommunications, education, health and gas distribution and other eligible sectors. The program directly
supported the CAS goal of improving infrastructure services by establishing a financial mechanism not only for
public autonomous agencies but also for private providers of urban public services. It also furthered the CAS goals
of: (i) enhancing public-sector responsiveness and efficiency; (ii) ensuring sustainable development by supporting
reforms mandated under Colombia's Public Services Law (Law 142 of July 11, 1994) to modernize service
provision; and (iii) facilitating access to long-term capital for sustainable economic growth.

Risks to the project. The project PAD identified seven potential risks: (i) deterioration of economic conditions
such as a substantial fall in real GDP and/or household incomes that could adversely affect the feasibility of
market-rate loans for urban public services (in 1997 before the loan, the Colombian economy began to experience
deterioration of GOC finances in the context of a persistent real appreciation of the Colombian peso, continued
inflationary pressures, and sluggish economic performance and higher unemployment, which lead the GOC to
adopt restrictive fiscal monetary measures designed to reverse these trends and restore macroeconomic
equilibrium); (ii) Lack of continued government commitment to decentralization and private-sector participation
for the provision of urban public services; iii) political interference that might compromise the institutional and
operational capacity of FINDETER; iv) lack of interest as well as the financial and technical capacity on the part of
service providers to determine demand, formulate appropriate subprojects and seek FINDETER's financing; v)
lack of interest as well as financial and institutional capacity on the part of the private sector to participate in the
provision of urban public services; vi) deterioration of the regulatory framework and technical support for greater
autonomy and private-sector participation in the provision of urban public services; and vii) counterpart funding
shortages. The project was rated "M" (Modest Risk), and some of the risks were identified through evaluations and
addressed during project design.

Assessment of project objectives and risks. The objective was clear, appropriate and important for the country and
for the CAS. In particular, FINDETER has had a crucial role in providing long-term financing (8 to 12 years,
compared to tenors of 2 to 5 years typical of other sources) for urban infrastructure investments to public
autonomous and private providers. This finance helped to correct credit market imperfections that obstructed local
infrastructure service development, particularly for sub-projects with high social value.

In retrospect, the project design also assessed the major risks appropriately, and allowed for the changes necessary
to finish implementation satisfactorily. In particular, the national macroeconomic crisis of 2000 to 2002 led to a
sharp reduction in demand for FINDETER-funded loans, both because end-users lacked capacity to assume new
credits and because first-tier lenders had excess liquidity and no need for FINDETER rediscounting. These factors
led to a reduction in the size of the project in 2002. FINDETER lending increased rapidly over the last two years
of the project with the gradual recovery of the economy and of end-users in 2003-4. The emphasis on strengthening
the financial capacity of FINDETER in the project design also helped foster a substantial cut in FINDETER's
operating costs, a strong diversification of FINDETER's products, streamlining of its procedures, and a focus on
marketing ­ which also proved crucial elements in the recovery of the institution in 2003-4 from the 2000-2002
crisis. (The detailed description is in section E).

rediscounting of sub loans to eligible public service providers by first-tier financial institutions (IFI´s). These

Assessment. The design of the component generally reflected the project objectives (with one exception: a lack of
connection to capital markets ­ see below), and allowed the flexibility necessary for normal execution of the project
in adverse circumstances. Even during the macroeconomic/municipal fiscal crisis of 2000 to 2002, the project
continued to function, albeit at much reduced disbursement levels. While some first-tier lenders went bankrupt
because of problems with urban-service loans, FINDETER's financial solidity remained unaffected. As detailed
under Section 4 below, the project component performed satisfactorily in meeting the objectives as measured by
these indicators.

The one substantial drawback in the design involved the lack of indicators, technical assistance, or           other
mechanisms to connect the funding of FINDETER and its end borrowers (local governments and urban- service
companies) to capital markets. Such a connection would have, in principle, contributed importantly to improving
the terms (such as the tenor and interest rate) of urban-service credit. In retrospect, however, promoting such a
connection     might    arguably     have   been    premature    ­     given    the   damage    caused     by    the
financial/municipal/macro-economic crisis of 2000 to 2002.

3.4 Revised Components:
This single component continued throughout the life of the project. However the severity of the economic, financial
and local government crisis of 2000 to 2002 forced changes in the overall project (and FINDETER itself),
including: (i) revisions to key performance indicators (annex 1); (ii) loan amount (US $26 million of the loan was
cancelled in 2002) principally as a consequence of a reduction in demand from end-borrowers and from IFI´s,
which had excess liquidity during this period; (iii) changes to the organic law of the financial system (following

the project scope that allowed lending to private sector), which expanded the eligibility for borrowing from the
institution and the diversification of FINDETER's products (new products and sectors:         FINDETER in 2003
(Resolución 086) established differential rediscount rates geared to the loan period ( from DTF+1,75 for 5 years,to
DTF+3 for 12 years). Loans for the private sector became eligible for FINDETER finance (before this project,
FINDETER could not lend to the private sector), as did new sectors including gas, energy, telecommunications,
transport, housing, tourism and environment. FINDETER has also begun lease financing, thereby supporting
Colombian tax law that favors this mechanism. In addition, FINDETER has introduced a greatly streamlined
form of loan approval and underwriting mainly for its private-sector loans called "automatic rediscounting."); and
(iv) extension of the loan closing date from June 30, 2003 to June 30, 2004.

In addition to these modifications to the project, FINDETER also reformed its administration and staffing. In its
first round of reforms, the organization gradually reduced the number of staff from 359 persons to 297 in 1997.
Second, in 2003, FINDETER´s cut personnel staff to 150 persons. In effect, these changes to the project and
internal reforms supported FINDETER in overcoming the crisis of 2000 to 2002, and established a new mode of
operation adapted to current realities. This new mode of operation is characterized by low operating costs and
margins, and a strong focus on marketing and innovation - i.e. promoting new products, sectors and financial
intermediaries.

3.5 Quality at Entry:
The quality at entry was Satisfactory. The project supported the country CAS (aimed to improve infrastructure
services by establishing a financial mechanism not just for public autonomous, but for private providers of urban
public services) and proved well conceptualized and formulated ­ arguably with the exception of understating the
importance of connecting FINDETER and urban-service funding to capital markets. The project objective also
reinforced the priorities of GOC, including the implementation of Law 142, private-sector participation in public
infrastructure, and the deepening of financial markets. Review of the list of projects financed (under "outputs by
components" below) shows that the project mainly benefited low-income and poor households. The project design
took into account a realistic assessment of the possible risks during project implementation and incorporated the
flexibility necessary to continue operating even in difficult circumstances without losing sight of the end results.
Bank field visits to 40% of the 60 subprojects financed by the project showed a high quality of execution and
consistency with Bank policies, and with environmental and social analyses in FINDETER´S operational manual.
There were no specific environmental or resettlement safeguards for this project.

4. Achievement of Objective and Outputs

4.1 Outcome/achievement of objective:
The achievement of the project's objectives also proved satisfactory as measured by various metrics, including the
performance indicators (annex 1).

Some background on project exection is necessary as a preface prior to evaluation of the objectives (listed in
Table 4):

Since the approval of this operation in 1998, FINDETER has broadly gone through three periods, 1998-1999;
2000-2002, and 2003-2004.

First Phase    During the first phase (1998-1999), FINDETER reached project indicators and loan disbursement
schedules, with financial strength. Funding was principally based in capital and external loans. In this period
FINDETER worked with high administrative costs and with a very low flexibility to respond to market changes
(FINDETER products remained almost the same from 1997 to 2003).

Second Phase With the 2000-2002 economic, financial and municipal crisis (described in Section 5 of this
document), loan demand dropped drastically, affecting key performance indicators and loan disbursements. This
decline in demand provoked excess liquidity in the market that influenced banks to lend directly to municipalities
rather than intermediate FINDETER credit.          The inflexibility in FINDETER's interest rate and lack of
diversification (at the time) hampered the recovery of the organization during this period. At this time,



                                                        - 6 -

Government considered fusing various second-tier financial institutions, including FINDETER.            Although a
number of second-tier finance institutions were fused, FINDETER has remained independent.

Third Phase Since 2003, as part of its strategy to overcome the crisis and the new GOC development program,
FINDETER concentrated its efforts in 45 key areas:

    ·   Product diversification. The principal changes in products have involved: (i) gearing the interest rate to
    the term of loan in order, to be more competitive (this also reduced FINDETER's financial margin and
    stimulated a reduction in average loan terms); and (ii) the introduction of automatic rediscounting, which
    permitted a much streamlined method of making loans (although this new product is not totally automatic; it
    represents a big change in timing and procedures for loan approvals in FINDETER, because the sub borrower
    does all the process through the IFI, and FINDETER is not involved in the identification, approval or
    intermediation stages). At the start of the project in 1998, traditional loan discounting accounted for 100% of
    loans. In 2003 automatic rediscount represented more than 60% of total approvals.

    ·   Sector diversification. FINDETER added new sectors as a result of: (i) the national development plan
    law (law 812) that included tourism in the eligible sectors to be financed by FINDETER; (ii) Law 795 that
    incorporated "environmental projects" as an eligible sector; (iii) a government decree that incorporated VIS
    micro finance as a sector to be served by FINDETER; and (iv) a marketing focus on other important sectors as
    transport, energy, gas, and telecommunications.

    ·   Sub-borrower diversification. Historically, FINDETER´s portfolio (except this loan) was concentrated in
    lending to the central administrations of municipalities. Law 795 enabled FINDETER to lend to: (i) mixed or
    private companies; and (ii) autonomous service providers. These            modifications have greatly changed
    FINDETER´s portfolio and allowed real growth in response to the crisis (private sub borrower's share of total
    loans went from 7% in 1997, to 51% in 2003).

    ·   Diversification in types of IFIs. FINDETER traditionally has operated through commercial banks. Law
    795 permitted rediscounting of loans originated by leasing companies, which accounted for 21% of
    FINDETER´s loan portfolio (around 35 IFI´s) in 2004. Also 4 financial corporations and 2 CCFs (Cajas de
    Compensación Familiar) are now channelling FINDETER resources.

    ·   Institutional Reform. FINDETER reduced dramatically its administrative costs (from 6.7% of total
    assets in 1997 to 1.3% in 2004). This cut in administrative costs has proved crucial to compensate for
    reductions in FINDETER's financial margins, as a consequence of lower inflation, exchange-rate changes, a
    lower DTF rate (FINDETER lost significant sums hedging its foreign exchange risk in 2002-2003. In 2002
    the devaluation affected margins, and in 2003, a special coverage was made, but there was no devaluation.
    Also with low inflation and low DTF rate inColombia, the financial sector margins have been reduced
    drastically), and the gearing of FINDETER interest rates to loan tenors. The internal reform also involved a
    new marketing strategy focused more on loans disbursements.

These measures and the gradual reactivation of the Colombian economy permitted a rapid growth (33,2%) in
FINDETER lending in 2003 and, again, in 2004. This increase in the loan portfolio changed the financial structure
of FINDETER funding. Traditionally FINDETER has funded its loans by long term multilateral projects and
invested capital (its capital). FINDETER funding in 2003 began to depend crucially on short-term liabilities
(96% of 3 years or less) in the form of CDT´s ­ provoking an increase in interest-rate and liquidity risk.
FINDETER growth is now supported by short-term local debt, and the organization's interest rate risk is linked to
the DTF rate, instead of the external exchange rate (when growth was funded by multilateral loans).

l   FINDETER continues to gear its programs and lending to the priorities of national government. In turn,
    Government has given FINDETER a key role in an urgent priority ­ the expansion of housing micro credit for
    low-income families. This evolution and new opportunities for FINDETER ­ including the revitalization of
    demand for its credit, new sectors, new products, and a key role in housing microfinance ­ require a new

Another important success involves IFI intermediation of FINDETER funding. The PAD stipulated that no more

funding.

FINDETER has also made a great effort to become more efficient by: (i) reducing administrative costs between
1997 and 2003 from 6.7% to 1.3% of total assets; and (ii) creating new financial products (automatic rediscount)
that demand less participation of the institution in project identification, approval and intermediation.

In summary, FINDETER started the UISDP project as a grant-making foundation as much as a financial
institution, and lacked the culture and many of the systems of a market-rate lender. The institution is now
established as a second-tier bank. This outcome is due in no small part to the orientation and support of this
project. However, the strategic vision study notes that further strengthening of FINDETER's financial expertise
and systems remains essential to sustainable credit for local development.

4.3 Net Present Value/Economic rate of return:
In the PAD, a cost-benefit analysis was carried out from a sample consisting of five investment proposals for a total
cost of US $21.8 million (about 17% of total project cost). The analysis yielded a weighted (by investment cost)
average return of 31.8%.

Individually, all investment proposals were evaluated either by cost-benefit analysis or cost effectiveness. Overall it
was established that the minimum economic rate of return to guarantee the economic feasibility of the project was
12%, and that subprojects financed by FINDETER should meet this benchmark.

FINDETER is carrying out an ex-post evaluation on 10% of the projects (6 projects). Preliminary results show that
individually the sub-projects were in compliance with the operational manual and had an economic rate of return
above 12%. The final and more detailed results of this ex-post evaluation will serve as input into the preparation
of a sequel program. However, FINDETER continues to finance very similar projects to those of the prior
Bank-sponsored program as anticipated in the PAD. In addition, FINDETER's financial spread has decreased
substantially, thus offering more competitive rates. Hence, prima facie, a similar (conservatively) or higher (most
likely) rate of return (31.8% under the old program) is expected.

5. Major Factors Affecting Implementation and Outcome

5.1 Factors outside the control of government or implementing agency:
The economic/financial crisis of 2000-2002 constitutes the principal factor that affected project implementation.
The economic crisis came largely from increases in public expenditures and the worsening of the internal war.
Also, Colombia's vulnerability to the regional economic situation proved important, especially the uneven
performance of the US and Venezuelan economies during this period, the most important trading partners of
Colombia. The Gross Domestic product declined 4.2% in 1999 and the public sector deficit moved from 0.3% in
1998 to 4.1% in 1999. With no growth in municipal incomes, the situation resulted in large structural deficits at
the national, department, and municipal levels.

By June 2002, sub-national governments (municipalities and departments) faced a critical situation that had direct
implications for public-service providers, most of them public or public-private companies, which constituted the
major source of demand for project subloans made by IFI´s. In contrast, public expenditures doubled from 1990 to
2000, and GOC transferred an important share of these resources to municipalities under its decentralization
program. This situation largely explains the reduction of the demand for FINDETER loans during the period.
Parallel to this economic and municipal crisis, a financial sector crisis (linked not only to municipal finance but
also to private and housing debt) affected IFI´s. Many IFI´s experienced serious problems in loan collection, other
risks and excess liquidity (the expired lending indicator went from 5,1% in 1995, to 14.9% in 2000. The reliability
ratio went from 13.4% in 1995 to 10.%9 in 1999). As a result, commercial banks were unable to continue lending
to subnational governments ­ even good customers - with problems in payment capacity. IFI´s also tightened terms
on lending by reducing tenors and asking for additional guarantees.

An external assessment of the project at the time recommended loan cancellation as the best option for both GOC
and the Bank. Fortunately, strong Bank and FINDETER commitment to make the necessary changes resulted in
the decision to continue with the project.

5.2 Factors generally subject to government control:
The GOC focused on the stabilization of IFIs and local governments to confront this crisis:

To attack financial sector problems, GOC instituted the financial reform law (law 795 of 2003, adjusts legal
framework of the financial system organic statute), introduced more regulatory requirements and provisions,
created a capitalization line-of-credit in Fogafin (Fondo de Garantías Financieras), passed a new housing law (law
546 of 1999), and took possession of and liquidated some IFI´s. Of particular importance, the financial reform law
established that a loan had to be made at an interest rate that reflects the full costs of the financial institution,
including invested capital and funding. This modification constituted the legal framework for the interest-rate
segmentation made subsequently by FINDETER.

Three laws addressed the local government crisis. The law 617/2000 established the obligatory use of current
incomes to finance current expenses, investment and provisions, and created limits on administrative costs. Law
358 prohibited incurring local-government debt in excess of payment capacity; Law 550 (Law of economic
intervention) permitted the restructuring of debt and was used by at least 42 local governments. In the short term,
these measures reduced the demand for FINDETER sub-projects lending. Three years of these measures, however,
have resulted in positive financial results for municipalities and departments; 3 of every 4 municipalities have
improved their fiscal performance as evaluated by DNP. The current outlook is for continued measured growth in
demand for municipal credit, and large infrastructure investment in sectors such as transport, gas, energy and
telecommunication are expected under the TLC (free trade agreement).

6. Sustainability

6.1 Rationale for sustainability rating:
The project sustainability is rated likely due to: (i) the commitment of GOC to FINDETER; (ii) FINDETER's
financial strength (good financial indicators, high level of equity of FINDETER and low loan arrears because of
securing the balance sheets of regulated financial institutions as well as the underlying guarantees to the
sub-borrowers); (iii) current and potenial access to local capital markets; and (iv) strong management and
institutional capacity.

FINDETER continues to have good financial standing, reflected by its "AAA" rating for five consecutive years
given by Duff&Phelps. FINDETER has at present a good level of equity, and implemented a good coverage
(Forwards non-delivery) for the exchange rate fluctuation risk. Although recently the lending growth has been
strongly financed by local debt, the invested capital still finances 45% of the lending portfolio. The PAD contained
one benchmark relevant to FINDETER's efficiency as a second-tier finance institution ­ a target return on average
invested capital of at least 5% per annum. With an important growth in FINDETER lending, and despite the
margin reduction (caused by a low DTF and the rediscount rate segmentation), the indicator was 4.99% for 2003.
The structure of second-tier finance of FINDETER has protected the entity well against the risks of first-tier
lending.

Nonetheless, with recent growth financed funded mainly by short-term CDTs, FINDETER has increased its



                                                         - 15 -

interest-rate, funding and liquidity risk, while its marginal financial margin has shrunk to minimal levels.
Meanwhile, the fundamental developmental challenge of increasing the term-to-maturity of urban-service credit
through connecting with capital markets and of lending to the sectors critical to the Government's development
plan - particularly water and sanitation ­ remain.       These issues must be addressed for FINDETER to have
sustainable growth and substantial development impact in the coming years.

Although FINDETER has tapped local markets, this source ­ alone ­ is insufficient to fund the organization's
growth. If FINDETER continues to grow at an accelerated rated with local short term funding, projections indicate
much greater risks of all types that would likely generate substantial increases in funding costs and a rapid
decline in sustainability. In addition, local capital markets are growing, but still are limited in size and relatively
short- term. Hence, although FINDETER must fund its program partly on local capital markets, it is difficult to
supply the long-term capital for continued growth in FINDETER lending from this source alone in the short term
(the external strategic planning consultant has identified other options of local funding including: (i) bonds; and
(ii) securitization of FINDETER's portfolio, in order to get long ­term funding in the local market). For these
reasons, the strategic vision study concludes that FINDETER needs long term funding from a combination of
internal and external sources (the external strategic planning consultant has recommended (i) multilateral loans
and (ii) accessing the external financial market ( when country conditions are favorable)) in order to have a
sustainable operation that meets its principal client's needs. FINDETER has projected a basic financial scenario
for the next 10 years, where lending growth is tied to inflation, the funding rate is between 3% and 4%,
devaluation is 9% , and forward devaluation is between 7.57% and 8.61%. This basic scenario will require around
US $150 million of external funding (in addition to FINDETER's domestic funding) between 2005 and 2007.

Thus, a new project would have crucial value not only in continuing present activities but also in meeting
developmental challenges. In this regard, the institution enjoys the continued strong backing of GOC. Although
with the crisis some second-tier banks were merged, the GOC decided to maintain and support only 3 second tier
banks; FINDETER, FINAGRO and BANCOLDEX. The GOC has given FINDETER a very important role in the
new sectors described in the national development plan, and also in the national VIS strategy.

In this context, the strategic vision study provides a framework for the organization and FINDETER's
developmental value-added over the next five years.

6.2 Transition arrangement to regular operations:
FINDETER is planning to request a new Bank operation to implement the recommendations of the Strategic
Vision study. The principal recommendations of the strategic vision study are to: (i) continue to target territorial
entities, help small municipalities get long-term funding, and assist large qualified municipalities to use new
source (particularly bonds); (ii) concentrate on FINDETER's traditional sectors with opportunities for growth (TLC
Free Trade Agreement) including electricity, gas, telecommunications, water and transport, without leaving the
new ones; (iii) move cautiously into VIS microfinance; (iv) continue administrative improvements to allow the thin
margin of a well-functioning second-tier bank (administrative cost of 0.5% to 1% of total assets, Marulanda
Consultores); (v) unify operative and approval procedures, improving automatic rediscounting; and (vi) access
other local and external funding mechanisms such as bonds, securitization of its portfolio, and the external market.

A PPIAF grant from the Bank is currently funding a logical sequel to the strategic vision study - the preparation of
a funding plan for FINDETER. This plan will specify the mix of and steps for accessing the internal and external
funding sources best suited to finance FINDETER's growth within the framework created by the strategic vision
study.

8. Lessons Learned

A series of lessons have emerged from the above evaluation, the strategic vision study, and this ICR:

FINDETER's role as a second tier development bank continues to be crucial. FINDETER continues to be a
development bank of fundamental importance in developing the municipal and urban services market. For a new
operation, the territorial entities (local governments and autonomous service providers) will be the sector objective
for FINDETER. FINDETER can help small municipalities get long term funding and large qualified
municipalities to use new sources and connect to capital markets. FINDETER's position as a second-tier finance
institution has proved key in protecting the institution from credit risk and bankruptcy (the fate of some first-tier
lenders as a result of the crisis).

Key challenge: extending term-to-maturity of urban-service finance and of FINDETER funding. At the time
the project was formulated (1996-97), municipal finance in Colombia was expanding rapidly, and longer terms
with funding from local financial markets seemed imminent. FINDETER has financed long-term credits by
joining funding from multi-lateral financial institutions with its capital. The crisis, however, resulted in an abrupt
reduction in the amount and term of credit for urban service provision that could be funded in domestic financial
markets, even by FINDETER, with its government backing. As the organization recovered and grew rapidly after
the crisis in 2003 and 2004, FINDETER has increasingly funded itself with short-term liabilities (certificates of
deposit mainly with 1-3 year terms), which has heightened the financial risks faced by the organization in its
efforts to lend long. Thus, influencing markets to achieve longer terms remains a fundamentally unfinished
developmental objective. Accomplishing this goal requires connecting urban infrastructure finance ­ in particular,
that of FINDETER - to domestic capital markets.

Importance of private-sector participation. Although private participation was mainly concentrated in 2 or 3
sectors in this project, its role has proved important in accomplishing the main outcomes of the project, and crucial
in increasing loan volume in response to the crisis. While FINDETER lent overwhelmingly to the central
administrations of municipalities before this operation, the organization now makes over half of its loans to
private-sector end borrowers, including autonomous service companies. FINDETER's new products and new
sectors are, in large measure, geared to the private sector. Private participation will continue to be critical in the
sustainability and impact of a new operation.

Sectors to finance and role within the Government's development priorities. FINDETER has played an
important role in traditional sectors as water and sanitation, education and transport. Although new sectors have
been incorporated, it is probable that demand will concentrate in electricity, gas, telecommunications, water and
transport. FINDETER continues to have a critical role in water and sanitation, in particular. Recent studies by
DNP (DNP, 2004) show that relatively large government investment in water and sanitation has resulted in
disappointing increases in coverage. Thus, continuing FINDETER's traditional emphasis in the water and
sanitation sector continues to have key developmental value. Government has given FINDETER a key function in
housing microfinance of low-income households. However, although creating this market holds great
developmental value and ­ experience has shown elsewhere ­ can occur at scale on a market basis, this role also
involves political risk in the Colombian context (e.g. the interest-rate cap for "housing" loans) that only decisions
by Government can resolve. Hence, FINDETER should advance with care in this sector pending these decisions.



                                                          - 18 -

Automatic rediscounting. Although automatic rediscounting has proved to be an excellent lending instrument,
FINDETER should improve this mechanism, partly by learning from other second-floor banks' automatic
rediscounting. In particular, there is a need for a strong commitment to and more effective procedures for ex-post
evaluation of projects and for monitoring and enhancing the role of intermediary financial institutions. A reformed
automatic rediscounting mechanism deserves a central operational role in a new Bank-financed operation, and it is
probable that traditional rediscounting will slowly disappear (as recommended by the strategic vision study).

Flexibility, innovation and continuous learning. The project has demonstrated the need for flexibility, financial
innovation and continuous learning under the complex framework of the economy. Although project preparation
during 1996-97 and the project emphasized many changes that subsequently proved crucial, FINDETER adopted
most of these modifications only in 2002-3 in order to survive the crisis. Hence, a new operation could usefully
support FINDETER to strengthen and institutionalize its capacity as a "learning organization" able to lead as well
as adapt to political, economic, and financial variables affecting the funding of urban infrastructure. In particular,
such flexibility and innovation requires enhancing FINDETER's financial expertise through training programs (as
recommended by the strategic vision study).

Active Bank involvement in the project. Close Bank involvement as part of the FINDETER team clearly made
important contributions to the organization and the operation during the eventful period of the project (1997 to mid
2004). Meeting the challenges noted above for a subsequent project requires a similar collaboration, as evidenced
by the strategic vision study and the PPIAF funding plan.

1. INTRODUCCION


El objetivo establecido en el Préstamo 4345-CO llamado Proyecto de Desarrollo de Servicios de Infraestructura
Urbana era contribuir con la expansión y consolidación del mercado de crédito para proveedores autónomos
públicos y privados de servicios públicos locales, fortaleciendo la capacidad de FINDETER para ofrecer
financiación de largo plazo para inversiones en infraestructura urbana.

Este objetivo fue complementado por los del Segundo Proyecto de Desarrollo Municipal, financiado por el BID, el
cual tendía a: (i) ampliar y consolidar el mercado de crédito subsoberano fortaleciendo la capacidad de FINDETER
para financiar inversiones municipales; (ii) fortalecer la capacidad financiera y de gestión de los municipios, con el
fin de que tengan mayor acceso a los empréstitos comerciales y mejorar la prestación de los servicios de
infraestructura municipal; y (iii) ayudar a FINDETER a convertirse en una institución financiera eficiente de
segundo nivel.

El componente único del proyecto apoyó la financiación y ejecución de subproyectos a través del sistema de
redescuento de subpréstamos de FINDETER, ofrecido por las entidades financieras (IFIs) a proveedores elegibles
de servicios públicos. Dichos subproyectos consistieron en inversiones para rehabilitar, mejorar o expandir la
provisión de servicios públicos urbanos por parte de entidades autónomas públicas y compañías privadas en
sectores elegibles bajo la responsabilidad del gobierno local, como lo fue el sector educativo para éste proyecto
específicamente a pesar de ser elegibles otros sectores como el de transportes urbano, plazas de mercado,
telecomunicaciones, salud, entre otros.

Se destaca que la formulación y presentación de los proyectos evaluados y financiados por FINDETER cumplieron
con los lineamientos del Préstamo y algunos de los proyectos financiados contaron con el concepto favorable y
expreso del Banco Mundial.

El contrato de préstamo 4345-CO fue firmado entre FINDETER S.A. y el Banco Mundial el 31 de Julio de 1998, la
fecha de cierre del crédito fue el 30 de junio de 2004. Este documento corresponde al informe de cierre del
préstamo citado anteriormente.

2. OBJETIVOS, CONCEPCIÓN, DISEÑO Y DIMENSIONAMIENTO DEL PROYECTO


Los objetivos del Proyecto fueron cumplidos, teniendo en cuenta que de manera conjunta con el BID se buscó el
mismo enfoque en las inversiones, uno dirigido más a beneficiarios de crédito privados y el otro a entidades
territoriales entre otros; igualmente se buscaba fortalecer la gestión de las entidades municipales y ayudar a
FINDETER a convertirse en una institución financiera eficiente de segundo nivel.

La concepción del programa buscó contribuir a la expansión y consolidación del mercado de crédito para
proveedores autónomos públicos y privados de servicios públicos locales, y éste aparte se ve reflejado en los
indicadores de resultados claves del proyecto y en la misma carta suplementaria del Banco en la que la
participación de compañías de servicios desde 1998 llegó a ser del 25% en número de operaciones y en monto el
45% sobre el total aprobado en FINDETER.

Sin embargo en el momento que el proyecto fue preparado entre FINDETER y Banco (dos años, desde 1996 hasta
su firma 1998), el proceso de descentralización en Colombia señaló que los principales problemas a los que se
enfrentaban las administraciones territoriales eran de índole económica y de gestión en la prestación de servicios
públicos; posteriormente a la firma del Préstamo con el Banco Mundial nace la Ley 617 del 2000, "Por la cual se
reforma parcialmente la Ley 134 de 1994, el Decreto Extraordinario 1222 de 1986, se adiciona la Ley Orgánica de
Presupuesto, el Decreto 1421 de 1993, se dictan otras normas tendientes a fortalecer la descentralización, y se
dictan otras normas para la racionalización del gasto público nacional", donde se presenta como una de las
políticas prioritarias del Gobierno el fortalecimiento de las finanzas territoriales mediante Programas             de
Saneamiento Fiscal, se pretende darle viabilidad financiera a las entidades territoriales y garantía de la Nación a
los empréstitos que se contraten para este fin, siempre y cuando se comprometan en la ejecución de medidas de
ajuste fiscal y reestructuración institucional orientadas a garantizar su viabilidad financiera y mejorar su eficiencia
administrativa.

Lo anterior condujo a que la participación de empresas prestadoras de servicios públicos para el financiamiento en
inversiones de infraestructura bajara o en el evento de que las mismas hubiesen sido financiadas con recursos del
Préstamo, los desembolsos no se ejecutaban, por la aplicación de la misma Ley 617/00, lo que llevó a la no
intermediación financiera del crédito. Esto no quiere decir que el dimensionamiento hubiese sido inadecuado, fue
simplemente una situación coyuntural que llevó a que el mismo Préstamo se hubiese redimensionado y se cancelara

5.5.1    Incidencias de la devaluación y la DTF en los estados financieros

La devaluación es una variable que históricamente ha incidido en los estados financieros de FINDETER. La
Entidad en el 2003 a través de mecanismos de coberturas (Forwards Non Delivery), estabilizó el comportamiento
de esta variable, logrando una devaluación promedio para el año del 7.81%, sin importar las volatilidades del
mercado.

El 2003 presentó uno de los niveles históricos más bajos de la DTF. La tasa DTF efectiva anual promedio fue de
7,79%, frente a 8.83% durante el 2002 y debido a que nuestra cartera está principalmente referenciada a esta
variable, los ingresos por cartera pasaron de $91.872 millones en el 2002 a $86.010 millones en el 2003, a pesar
del aumento en el saldo de la cartera.




                                                         - 36 -

FINDETER es especialmente sensible a las variaciones de la DTF, por su bajo nivel de apalancamiento en relación
con el monto de su cartera de créditos, de tal forma que una variación de la DTF, afecta fundamentalmente los
ingresos por cartera, sin tener mayor impacto sobre los egresos originados en los pasivos.

5.5.2    Operaciones de cobertura

La Superintendencia Bancaria remitió a la Presidencia de FINDETER una comunicación en la cual analizaba y
mostraba que históricamente la Financiera había tenido pérdidas por diferencia en cambio, y requería que se
adoptaran mecanismos de cobertura.

La Junta Directiva acogió como política estar cubiertos mediante forwards NDF, dando estricto cumplimiento a lo
establecido en la Circular Externa 014 de 1998 expedida por la Superintendencia Bancaria y al concepto de la
comunicación antes mencionada.

Como resultado de la política de cobertura adoptada por FINDETER, se logró reducir el valor en riesgo (VAR) de
la entidad en un 65%, al pasar de $8.800 millones en junio de 2003 a $3.058 millones en diciembre del mismo año
cuando se logró una cobertura del 100%.

Los forwards NDF, por efectos de la valoración, generaron un gasto para FINDETER de $20.663 millones en el
año de 2003, mientras que la diferencia en cambio del pasivo y las cuentas corrientes en el exterior, generaron un
gasto de $1.616 millones. Lo anterior quiere decir que por efecto del cambio del precio del dólar frente al peso, la
Entidad tuvo un gasto neto de $22.279 millones, lo que equivale a una devaluación promedio del 7.81%.

Es importante resaltar que cuando se usan derivados como herramienta de cobertura y no como instrumentos
especulativos, lo importante es evaluar la disminución del riesgo y no los resultados, porque así como en el 2003 se
presentó una reevaluación en términos de tasa de cambio de reexpresión de los estados financieros (-0.27%),
también pudo haber ocurrido una devaluación como la de 2002 del 22% que generó un gasto de $37.292 millones.
La bondad del mecanismo está en que FINDETER eliminó la volatilidad en el estado de resultados por efecto del
precio del dólar.

6.1 Beneficiarios de Crédito

Como conclusión general del SPDT, se puede resaltar que FINDETER logró apoyar la participación de
beneficiarios de carácter privado al desarrollo de las políticas del gobierno a través del presente Préstamo.

A pesar de la problemática de las entidades territoriales, que se resumía así: (i) Déficit fiscal creciente originado
por el incremento inverso de los gastos de funcionamiento e inversión frente a la dinámica registrada por los
ingresos propios. Como consecuencia la mayoría de los departamentos y municipios del país presentan un ahorro
corriente negativo por no contar con los ingresos propios suficientes para cubrir sus gastos corrientes; (ii) Aumento
exagerado de la deuda pública en especial para cubrir gastos de funcionamiento, que hoy representa un riesgo a
nivel macroeconómico debido a la debilidad financiera de las entidades territoriales que podría llevar a una
cesación de pagos generalizada; (iii) Existencia de plantas de personal sobredimensionadas que han ocasionado la
acumulación de pasivos laborales y pensionales ocasionando el            no pago de estos compromisos en muchas
entidades territoriales; (iv) Aumento de los pasivos pensionales originado en el inadecuado manejo administrativo
y la falta de provisión para sufragar estos gastos; los esfuerzos se dirigieron a la implantación de un modelo de
fortalecimiento de las administraciones públicas, basado en una mayor eficiencia y equidad en la asignación de los
recursos públicos, el desarrollo de la política fiscal y el acercamiento de los servicios públicos a los ciudadanos,
donde FINDETER participó como actor importante en la financiación de esta política gubernamental.

El Programa de Saneamiento Fiscal constituyó un paso fundamental para inducir el desplazamiento progresivo de
FINDETER desde la "financiación de proyectos" a la "financiación de clientes", considerando el riesgo global de
la entidad territorial en lugar del "riesgo parcial de cada inversión".

7. ACCIONES FUTURAS


7.1 Visión estratégica

Desde la aprobación de la segunda operación con el Banco Mundial, FINDETER ha pasado por tres etapas. Entre
1998 y 1999, FINDETER logró cumplir con su meta de desembolsos y cumplir con las programaciones
establecidas con la banca multilateral. Entre el 2000 y 2002, la crisis macroeconómica del país, sumada a los
problemas fiscales del sector municipal, redujeron abruptamente la demanda para crédito, lo que ocasionó un
exceso de liquidez del mercado y los IFIs ofrecían sus propios recursos a unas tasas igual de competitivas que las
que FINDETER ofrecía. La inflexibilidad en las tasas de FINDETER y a la concentración de clientes tradicionales
generaron la transformación de la Entidad, se logró modificar su objeto social, incorporar nuevos beneficiarios de
crédito y segmentar las tasas de redescuento.

Con este nuevo empuje comercial, y la revitalización de la economía nacional y el fisco local, los desembolsos de
FINDETER crecieron a un ritmo fuerte, donde el indicador de crecimiento de cartera llegó a estar en niveles del
33.25%. Los activos han sido financiados en su mayoría a corto plazo, con pasivos de un año, acarreando la
posibilidad de crear un riesgo de tasa de interés, también FINDETER ha perdido en el hedging de su riesgo
cambiario con la reevaluación del peso colombiano en el 2003.

El Gobierno Nacional ha dado a FINDETER un papel clave en una prioridad urgente, que es la expansión de
crédito de vivienda para familias de bajos ingresos.

Toda esta evolución y las nuevas perspectivas de FINDETER señalaron la necesidad de definir una nueva Visión
Estratégica que se adecue al nuevo contexto.

Es así como el Banco Mundial ha apoyado a través de una Consultoría, la definición de la nueva Visión Estratégica
de FINDETER, de manera que estudie y analice varios aspectos relevantes a saber: (i) Fortalecimiento de la
capacidad municipal y asistencia técnica, (ii) Financiamiento crediticio de servicios domiciliarios y de proveer
liquidez a este mercado, (iii) Papel de FINDETER en crédito para municipios y graduación de municipios a nuevas
fuentes de fondeo, (iv) Diversificación de financiamiento crediticio a nuevos sectores, (v) Microfinanciamiento de
vivienda, (vi) Diversificación de productos financieros, (vii) Gobernabilidad de FINDETER, (viii) Estructura de
segundo piso y relaciones con IFI's, (ix) Manejo de riesgo de plazo, contraparte y cambio, y (x) Análisis
comparativo con otras Bancas de Segundo piso.

7.2 Manual de Procedimientos para manejo de Préstamos con la Banca Multilateral

Es importante que la Entidad cuente con un Manual de Procesos para el manejo de créditos con la Banca
Multilateral, donde no solo este el área encargada de administrar la línea, sino la parte contable, de tesorería,
comercial, jurídica entre otros y establecer los roles que cada uno tiene dentro del diseño, implementación,
ejecución y cierre de Préstamos; de ésta manera se evita la concentración de funciones en una sola persona y
facilitaría el desarrollo del Préstamo a todo nivel.

7.3 Perspectivas y formas de financiamiento

FINDETER para la vigencia 2004 proyectó un monto de redescuentos de $650.000 millones y con corte al 30 de
junio del presente año lleva una ejecución del 61% equivalente a $394.131 millones de pesos.

Actualmente ya se desembolsaron los últimos recursos contratados con el Banco Mundial y el Banco
Interamericano de Desarrollo, pero la mayor parte del endeudamiento necesario para fondear los redescuentos, se
ha obtenido en el mercado local mediante emisiones de Títulos Valores con plazos a 1, 2, 3 y 4 años.

Este fondeo en el mercado local, además de ser de corto plazo, tiene un límite, de tal forma que se hace necesario
contratar algunas fuentes de financiación externas con plazos largos, que nos permitan tener una combinación de
recursos acordes con los plazos de colocación de nuestros redescuentos.

FINDETER elaboró proyecciones de sus Estados Financieros para los próximos 10 años, suponiendo que los
redescuentos aumentan con base en la inflación para determinar sus necesidades de recursos, necesitaríamos entre
el 2005 y 2007, la suma de USD$150 millones, además de cerca de $862.523 millones en promedio cada año, en
títulos en el mercado local.

Debido al acelerado crecimiento de los redescuentos en los diferentes sectores y nuevos beneficiarios, FINDETER
se ha venido fondeando principalmente con recursos de corto plazo, entre uno y cuatro años, a través de emisiones
de Títulos Valores.

La Financiera no puede continuar con este esquema de fondeo por que estaría aumentando el riesgo de
refinanciación y por consiguiente se requiere la necesidad de combinar el endeudamiento de corto y largo plazo.

La Entidad proyecta continuar las buenas relaciones con la Banca Multilateral en la búsqueda de nuevos
empréstitos garantizando así cubrir la demanda de crédito de largo plazo.

A través del análisis que hará la Consultoría contratada por el Banco Mundial para definir la Visión Estratégica de
la Entidad, se definirá además el valor agregado que puede tener una nueva operación del Banco Mundial para la
Entidad, aunque la misma esta soportada con análisis financieros y la necesidad de estos recursos para que de
acuerdo a su resultado, se dimensione y oriente los recursos de la nueva operación con el aval de la Nación.

Project Development Objectives (from Project Appraisal Document)
The overall purpose of the EMRRP is to initiate the long-term process of reconstruction
and economic rehabilitation. The specific objectives are to assist the DRC to (a) start
rebuilding agricultural production and enhance food security, (b) rehabilitate and
reconstruct critical infrastructure, (c) restore essential social services and build
community infrastructure, and (d) strengthen capacity of government to formulate,
implement, and manage medium and long-term development programs.

  The project consists of a Grant of US$ 44 million covering health sector and of a Credit
of US$410 million covering the rest of the project.

1. Project Context, Development Objectives and Design

1.1 Context at Appraisal

1.     Years of economic mismanagement and conflict had devastating effects on the
country's economy and citizens' quality of life. In addition to significant loss of life,
massive internal population displacement, human rights abuses, and violence, virtually all
of the Democratic Republic of Congo's (DRC's) indicators of economic and social
developments deteriorated significantly, placing the country among the world's most
deprived nations. Government revenues amounted to no more than five percent of gross
domestic product (GDP) over many years, resulting in high fiscal deficits. Export
earnings had dramatically declined, resulting in a more than doubling of the current
account deficit financed mainly by accumulation of arrears on external debt.

2.     Agriculture, the country's most important sector in terms of employment and
share of national economic output, saw its production fall significantly, moving from a
surplus to a net deficit. The fall in agricultural production greatly increased food
insecurity and impoverished millions of people, including farmers, many of whom had to
purchase food to survive.

3.      Lack of maintenance and damage from the conflict had led to the steady and
significant deterioration of roads, bridges and other transport infrastructure,
disconnecting farmers and markets for their produce, disrupting the supply of agricultural
inputs and contributing to a significant increase in the price of goods, including in urban
centers such as Kinshasa.

4.      The electricity supply had collapsed because of lack of maintenance of electricity
infrastructure and failure to replace worn and outdated equipment. Moreover, war
damaged distribution networks, cutting off several important areas/cities from electricity.
Water supply systems were not maintained, rehabilitated or repaired, leading to a
significant reduction in access to piped water and a sharp deterioration in the quality of
service in the major urban centers and peri-urban areas, thereby exposing residents to
considerable risks of diseases.

5.      Urban streets were in very poor conditions and drainage channels, where they
existed, were completely blocked or collapsed, tanks and latrines overflowed. This
impeded the movement of goods and people, undermined buildings, contributed to
potholes, provided breeding grounds for mosquitoes and other pests and significantly
increased disease burden. Public health had deteriorated drastically over more than a
decade, leading to a substantial increase in the burden of disease and high levels of death
from malaria, a dramatic increase in Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) infections,
sleeping disease, and the frequency of tuberculosis and diarrhea diseases.

6.     Primary school enrollment had decreased significantly due to inadequate
financing of services and teachers' salaries and decayed infrastructure. Lack of teaching



                                            1
material had also affected the quality of education, while lack of government support had
led to most schools being operated by nongovernmental groups which collected fees from
parents. Poor families could not afford these fees and their children suffered the long-
term consequences of this arrangement.

7.     Finally, public administration in the DRC had deteriorated through long years of
neglect while poor management and low salaries encouraged corruption.

8.       The international donor community was reengaging with the DRC after a long
period of turmoil. In that context, the Bank approved both the Transitional Support
Strategy (TSS) and the Emergency Early Recovery Project (EERP) on July 31, 2001. The
TSS aimed to contribute to the transition to peace and stability in the DRC and sub-
region and had four areas of focus: (a) meeting basic and urgent needs, (b) rebuilding
effective public institutions and policies, (c) revitalizing economic activity, and (d)
rebuilding administrative and implementation capacity. The EERP aimed to: (a)
rehabilitate key infrastructure, (b) carry out institutional strengthening and capacity
building in the areas of management for donor assistance and support to good governance
and economic management, and (c) address urgent needs and pilot implementation
solutions, all in line with the priorities of the TSS. The Bank and other donors'
involvement stemmed from several factors: (a) the DRC is the third largest country in
Africa and strategically located in the heart of Africa with nine neighboring countries, it
has vast natural resources and the potential to be one of the continent's richest countries:
its stability is essential for the promotion of peace in the sub-region; (b) for the first time
in many years, the DRC had a leadership committed to establishing peace, reviving the
economy and alleviating poverty; and (c) the Bank has a comparative advantage that can
make it play a critical role in supporting the development efforts of the government,
mobilizing resources from other donors and providing overall coordination to ensure
smooth implementation of the development agenda of which the Emergency Multisector
Rehabilitation and Reconstruction Project (EMRRP) was an important part of. The
project was consistent with this overall framework.

9.     The project was also fully integrated into the macroeconomic framework that the
government has developed with support from the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and
the World Bank through the Economic Recovery Credit which, once approved by the
Board, cleared the DRC's arrears with the Bank. The EMRRP became the basis for the
government to further mobilize donor resources for reconstruction and delivery of basic
services.

10.     The project was part of a program that was among the first to be implemented in
the DRC once the arrears were cleared and the International Development Association
(IDA) could resume its assistance program. The program was countrywide, with
activities, under IDA financing, restricted only where the local security situation would
make supervising the activities difficult. Activities under IDA financing were to be
expanded to the Eastern provinces as soon as the security situation could allow it. The
level, nature, and modalities of development assistance were adapted to the specific
situation of each province (adjusted as the situation evolves) to reflect needs, and



                                              2
capacity to implement, operate and maintain projects. To compensate for weak
government capacity, the project was designed to be implemented within the framework
of a single operation to facilitate implementation and supervision. Considerable intensive,
hands-on technical assistance to help government institutions prepare and supervise
projects, and, over time, to strengthen institutions were critical to the success of the
program and project.

1.2 Original Project Development Objectives (PDO) and Key Indicators

11.     The overall purpose of the EMRRP was to initiate the long-term process of
reconstruction and economic rehabilitation. The specific objectives were to assist the
DRC to: (a) start rebuilding agricultural production and enhance food security; (b)
rehabilitate and reconstruct critical infrastructure; (c) restore essential social services and
build community infrastructure; and (d) strengthen the government capacity to formulate,
implement, and manage medium and long-term development programs and projects.

12.      The key indicators in the Memorandum and Recommendations of the President
approved by the Board are provided in the technical annexes and appendixes to the
Memorandum and focus on: (a) the number of kilometers of both paved and gravel roads
to be rehabilitated or maintained; (b) the expected results from the agricultural sub-
component in terms of the length of rural roads rehabilitation works and the number of
households, small livestock breeders and fish farmers targeted to benefit from market
information systems, improved seeds and planting materials, improved water
management systems, the provision of alevins, productivity improvement support and the
increase in the annual production of vaccine doses following the rehabilitation of the
Kinshasa laboratory; (c) in the health sector, the percentage of targeted health zones that
receive support, the minimum level (percentage) of population to whom access to
essential and acceptable heath care services is provided; and (d) in the education sector,
the number of primary schools rehabilitated, the number of textbooks, teacher guides and
school directors' kits procured and the number of training sessions for the teachers. It
should be noted however that although no indicators were initially provided in the same
structured approach for electricity, water supply and sanitation, sector strategies,
institutional reform and capacity development they were developed during project
implementation and oriented toward achieving results.

1.3 Revised PDO (as approved by original approving authority) and Key Indicators,
and reasons/justification

13.    There was no revision to the PDO and Key Indicators.

1.4 Main Beneficiaries

14.     The main intended beneficiaries of the project were urban and rural dwellers for
whom the project would help prevent further deterioration of health and loss of life by
increasing food security, delivering health care and other basic services and restoring
energy and water supply services. Improved learning conditions for primary schools



                                              3
would benefit students. Farmers would benefit from increased productivity and
production as a result of the assistance provided to them. A more reliable electricity
supply and better roads linking different cities would also benefit the private sector.

15.     Additionally, capacity building and institutional development measures would
benefit government agencies and institutions as well as their employees.

1.5 Original Components

16.    There are four components defined in the Memorandum and Recommendations of
the President of the International Association to the Executive Directors (MOP):

(a) rehabilitation and reconstruction of critical infrastructure (transport including roads,
water supply, electricity and urban infrastructure): (i) for transportation, activities to
improve river, rail and air transportation, including rehabilitation of ports, airports and
bridges, and purchase of equipment for river navigation and aeronautical communications,
(ii) for roads, rehabilitation or reconstruction of main highways and bridges, including
RN1 linking six provinces and the coastal ports, (iii) for electricity, rehabilitation of
electricity supply networks serving major cities, (iv) for water supply, rehabilitation of
water works in cities, including Kinshasa, Lubumbashi, Mbuji-Mayi and Boma, where
works have badly deteriorated, (v) for urban infrastructure, support to selected urban
centers to improve solid waste services, maintain and rehabilitate selected urban streets

1.7 Other significant changes

assessments in the country at the time, and the discovery from further engineering and
survey work that assets conditions were worse than originally assessed.

December 31, 2008, and then to March 31, 2010, in both cases in order to allow for the
continuation of the implementation of project activities taking into account
implementation delays for many sub-components in the infrastructure activities as well as
the scale-up and additional financing. Some amendments were also made to the legal
documents, usually to reflect the reallocations but also covering procurement and other
matters in some cases (such as clearing the site from underwater explosives discovered



                                             5
during construction at the port of Mbandaka, which necessitated special and lengthy
procedures to comply with Bank guidelines and requirements).

22.     The overall result of the reallocations were to transfer funds to the categories of
civil works (SDR 59.1 million, an increase of 25.8 percent from the original allocations,

2. Key Factors Affecting Implementation and Outcomes

2.1 Project Preparation, Design and Quality at Entry

23.      The project was identified from September 9 to October 3, 2001, negotiated on
May 2-4, 2002, approved by the Board on August 6, 2002, and signed on August 14,
2002. The credit became effective on November 8, 2002. Key factors that facilitated such
a rapid processing included; (a) preparatory work on the US$50 million grant for the
Emergency Early Recovery Project approved in July 2001 and effective in December
2001, which included a component to open the key road between Kinshasa and the sea,
institutional strengthening and capacity building, addressing urgent social needs and
piloting implementation solutions; (b) the fact that activities under the project were drawn
from the large multi-sector program of rehabilitation and reconstruction prepared by the
Ministry of Planning and Reconstruction in September 2001 (as agreed between the Bank
and this Ministry at pre-identification of the project) in consultation with various sector
ministries; and (c) lessons learned by the Bank from post-conflict assistance to many
other countries. All these factors, the TSS mentioned earlier (Section 1.1), the fielding of
a strong and experienced task team and the commitment by management to provide
needed resources provided a sound basis for the project design and processing on a fast
track basis consistent with the guidelines for Emergency Recovery Assistance (OP/BP
8.50, dated August 1995). There was no Quality Assurance Group review of the project.

24.      The selection of sectors and activities for the project was based on the following
criteria: (a) consistency with the strategies of the government and the Bank and relevance
with the overall objective of initiating the reconstruction process; (b) ownership by the
government as evidenced by the prior work of the Ministry of Planning and



                                             6
Reconstruction with the involvement of sector ministries in the preparation of a multi-
sector program; (c) relevance to the most urgent needs and priorities for social services
and economic recovery; (d) potential for direct and rapid impact on the social and
economic situation of the people; (e) contribution to poverty alleviation; (f) current
involvement of other donors in the sectors and activities of the project and the potential to
mobilize more donor support; (g) consideration of the degree to which activities are
relatively simple and easy to implement; and (h) possibility to hire adequate
implementation support given the weak capacity of the government.

25.     The main risks identified and the mitigations identified were: (a) Failure of the
peace process: Peace process collapse and resumption of conflict could lead to
destruction of rebuilt infrastructure. To mitigate this risk, it was decided to start
reconstruction activities in areas where authorities could assure security as confirmed by
the United Nations and to carefully monitor developments with a flexibility to either
scale back, suspend or shift focus elsewhere if warranted; (b) Lack of meaningful
progress on good governance: Failure to advance the process of building an effective,
stable and transparent government could lead to the neglect of reconstructed and
rehabilitated infrastructure and to mismanagement and theft of funds. Mitigating
measures included the project implementation mechanisms adopted and the concerted
efforts of the donor community to support key reforms, while recognizing that such
reforms take time to bear fruit; (c) Lack of government capacity: Weak government
capacity could result in implementation delays. Mitigation measures included the project
implementation arrangements with the hiring of an external firm, the use of IDA grant
funds from the Emergency Early Rehabilitation Project to ensure that contracts are
awarded as soon as possible after effectiveness and the use of the Bureau Central de
Coordination (BCECO) for the implementation of social, community development and
agricultural activities; and (d) Financing gap: Failure by the donors to fill the financing
gap in the program could stop activities implementation. This was mitigated by adopting
parallel, not joint financing mechanisms and identifying free-standing sub-projects that fit
in the program so that donors can choose among them. Further, more efforts were to be
put in mobilizing donors' funding.

2.2 Implementation

26.     The DRC was emerging from one of the worst, largest and most complex
conflicts in post-colonial Africa which had destroyed public institutions and the capacity
of the government to execute development programs and projects. This was confirmed by
a May 2001 Country Procurement Assessment Review (CPAR) which revealed that the
main agencies and ministries concerned with the EMRRP did not have the technical
capacity to implement the project. The compelling need for rapid implementation of
donor assistance and experience in other post-conflict situations suggested the need for
special arrangements to implement procurement, disbursement and financial management.
The implementation arrangements for the project were therefore designed to compensate
for government's weaknesses and seek to lay the foundations for strengthening its
capacity to implement future development projects.




                                             7
27.     Overall project oversight was the responsibility of an inter-ministerial steering
committee chaired by the Minister of Planning and Reconstruction and comprising
ministers (or their delegates) of finance and sector ministries involved with the project.
Project activities were sub-divided into two sub-programs: (a) the rehabilitation and
reconstruction of critical infrastructure, benefiting from about 70 percent of project
financing, to be executed by an international executive firm specializing in the
implementation of large infrastructure projects. This project management and
implementation firm (PMIF, later on called Bureau de Coordination des Marchés
d'Infrastructure, BCMI) was to be responsible for planning activities, carrying out
procurement and financial management, carrying out evaluations, and monitoring and
supervising contracts on behalf of the government in accordance with Bank procurement,
financial management and disbursement guidelines (and working closely with sector
ministries and national entities involved); and (b) the cluster of activities made of
agriculture and social services and community development, was to be executed by the
BCECO, a unit established under the ministry of finance to administer donor-financed
projects, working closely with sector ministries involved.

28.     The ministry of finance would sign all contract for works, goods and services
prepared by BCMI for critical infrastructure following no-objection from the Bank. Two
high-level international procurement and financial advisors would assist the minister by
reviewing on behalf of the government all procurement and financial management
aspects of the project, including those handled by BCECO. As implementation proceeded,
the government set up a project implementation coordination unit (Cellule d'Appui au
Pilotage (CAP)) in the ministry of plan and reconstruction to support the inter-ministerial
steering committee.

29.     The project promoted and reinforced commitment and ownership by the
respective sector ministries and agencies through the establishment of Memoranda of
Agreement (Protocoles d'Accord): (a) between BCECO and the ministries responsible
for agriculture, health, education, community development and social protection and (b)
between BCMI and the 14 principal infrastructure entities involved with the project as
well as the Ministry of Environment.

30.     A key feature worth noting was the use of non government organizations (NGOs)
for the delivery of some social services such as health in close coordination with local
authorities, a model first piloted under the EERP. Despite some problems encountered
along the way (such as initial lack of knowledge of Bank procedures by the NGOS), most
of them manageable and addressed in the course of implementation, these arrangements
were effective in ensuring that the project was implemented adequately.

31.    Implementation of the critical infrastructure activities experienced delays because
of the need for preparation of detailed design and tender documents, the complex
procurement requirements and, to some extent, the difficulties with attracting reliable
contractors given the overall situation in the DRC.




                                            8
32.     The mid-term review took place on schedule (18 months after effectiveness) and
its conclusions were that all the ratings were "satisfactory" and the project was on target
to achieve its development objectives. It was agreed that the government would prepare a
detailed action plan for the remaining implementation period, submit its requests for
additional financing and credit reallocation and amendment to the Development Credit
Agreement. Aspects of the project to which the government attention was drawn were:
(a) the need to reinforce PMIF, which was later called Bureau de Coordination des
Marchés d'Infrastructure (BCMI), in preparation of the expected increase in the
implementation load of infrastructure works, (b) the need for capacity strengthening
measures and a greater involvement of the sector ministries and agencies concerned by
the project, and (c) the need to provide adequate recurrent budget funding to BCECO,
CAP and the environmental unit. All these were achieved during the rest of project
implementation period.

2.3 Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) Design, Implementation and Utilization

33.     Monitoring and Evaluation under the project had two aspects: (a) intensive review
and supervision of the project by the Bank relying on detailed and well structured activity
reports prepared by the implementation entities in DRC and including the mid-term
review; and (b) a set of key indicators agreed with the government. There were 21
supervision missions from the November 2002 project effectiveness to the March 2010
closing, including the mid-term review mission in May 2005. Key outcome indicators are
set out in section 1.1 above. In most cases, especially for infrastructure, the baseline is
simply the starting situation where nothing was in place in terms of road maintenance and
rehabilitation, the number of hours of available electricity per day, and the existing level
of electricity and water supply. In the health sector, after it was determined that where
baseline information for the 64 initially targeted health zones was not available, the
project would undertake and complete a baseline Knowledge, Practice and Coverage
survey within a few months of project effectiveness using the same questionnaire,
adapted as needed, as the baseline survey being implemented by the University of
Kinshasa School of Public Health in ten clusters of 64 health zones with assistance from
a project (the SANRU III project) supported by the United States Agency for
International Development (USAID). This was done and closely monitored by the
implementing agency and the NGOs. Finally, most community development activities
were expected to start from a zero base for activities such as the provision of improved
seeds and plants. Adequate monitoring of sector strategy studies, institutional reforms
and capacity development and the development of the respective indicators were done
during implementation.

2.4 Safeguard and Fiduciary Compliance

34.     The project was classified as environmental category A under OP 4.01 because of
its size and the wide-ranging nature of infrastructure rehabilitation and reconstruction.
The applicability of the remaining nine Safeguard Policies remained to be determined,
with the possibility also that OP 4.12 (Involuntary Resettlement) could be triggered, as
some acquisition might be required. Because of these uncertainties, including on the



                                             9
location of many sub-projects, it was decided to have a programmatic process or strategic
approach for environmental and social assessment (including screening and
categorization, preparation, public consultation, disclosure, review, approval and
monitoring) put in place and implemented over the life of the project. This was meant to
ensure that the selection, design and implementation of all sub-projects would comply
with World Bank Safeguard Policies. The weakness of local capacity for environmental
and social management led to the decision to select an environmental consulting firm to
manage and oversee the Environmental and Social Management Plan (ESMP), while
providing institutional strengthening and on-the-job training services. It was expected
that this process would take place within one year of effectiveness but that some sub-
projects being designed under an IDA grant before effectiveness would have assessment
and resettlement plans, as appropriate, completed in parallel with the process. They
would subsequently be reviewed, approved and disclosed in-country in the info-shop in
accordance with Bank policies.

35.     An environmental firm was recruited within a year after effectiveness and its
report was finalized and disclosed a few months later. It included the definition of a
framework, guidelines and procedures for environmental management. In addition, the
firm did an environmental review of all the sub-projects in the infrastructure component,
including those that were approved previously. It also undertook verification missions for
sub-projects under implementation and made recommendations based on its findings. The
firm managed the ESMP, worked on institutional strengthening, carried out training and
helped set up an environmental unit at the Ministry of Environment. It prepared a training
program for staff of this unit. The environment firm worked on project activities until
project completion.

36.     The environmental unit at times faced problems of funding for recurrent costs and
training as well as adequate coordination with other project stakeholders (see Aide-
Memoire April 2005, Mid-Term Review Annex 3). However, these problems were
resolved and did not adversely or unduly affect project implementation.

2.5 Post-completion Operation/Next Phase

37.    The current macro-economic situation in the DRC poses some threats to the
sustainability of the physical investments made under the project. Both the government
and the Bank recognized that the sectors supported by the project were essential and
underlined the need to ensure that project realizations would be sustained. Although
sustainability was not explicitly addressed during project preparation and appraisal, both
the Bank and government underlined its importance as soon as implementation started
(Aide-Memoire of April 28-May 5, 2003 Supervision Mission). Subsequently, in
November 2004, the government produced a report by a working group it established
highlighting the weak status of public finances and requesting that donors continue to
provide massive financial assistance to the Government during the transition period and
support its institutional reforms and development program management efforts.

38.    The first step taken by the government was to request the first additional
financing from the Bank and then the second one under the project. The Bank
recommended that it was necessary: (a) to set up a study group to review sustainability
issues and make recommendations after it was discovered later during project
implementation that some infrastructure rehabilitated and rebuilt under the project were
already showing signs of aging and needing maintenance, and (b) to use as much as
possible sector projects either recently approved or under preparation for funding by the
Bank or other donors to secure maintenance and consolidation of EMRRP-supported
investments.

39.     A number of projects approved after the EMRRP and under implementation are in
many of the sectors supported by the project and they finance activities similar to those
financed by the project. In fact, many are the result of the various studies financed under
the project. They include the Private Sector Development and Competitiveness Project
( US$120 million, effective December 2003) which includes support for the reform of
public enterprises in the mining, telecommunications, financial, transport, and energy
sectors; the Emergency Economic and Social Support Project (US$214 million, effective
December 2003) which includes support for institution building and the restoration of
infrastructure and community-led development activities; the Emergency Social Action
Project (US$60 million, effective July 2005) which aims to expand access by the poor to
economic and social services; the Health Sector Rehabilitation Support Project (US$150
million, effective April 2007) which aims to guarantee access in the targeted zones to a
variety of high-quality health services; the Education Sector Project (US$150 million,
effective January 2008) with the aim of avoiding further reduction in the provision of
essential primary education services; the Urban Water Supply Project (US$190 million,
approved in December 2008) whose objective is to increase access to water in selected
urban areas and increase the efficiency of REGIDESO; two electricity projects; one
agriculture project; one roads project; an education project; and a multi-modal transport
project.

40.    The challenge is to have effective coordination within the Bank and between the
Bank, the other donors and the Government to ensure that these different projects are



                                            11
made to consolidate and maintain what has been achieved by the EMRRP. Workshops
were carried out to this effect, during project implementation, to sensitize the different
actors and partners to the many results of the project and the need to consolidate them
and propose solutions for their sustainability including effective government contribution.
It was highlighted during project implementation and during the sustainability workshops,
that it would be more beneficial that the new Bank supported sector projects build on,
consolidate, maintain and sustain the achievements under the EMRRP before including
new activities (especially for main roads and multi-modal transport operations which
would have ensured road maintenance before the road fund becomes fully operational,
dredging of the access channel to the only maritime port of the country, maintenance of
the berths of the port of Matadi, etc.).

3. Assessment of Outcomes

3.1 Relevance of Objectives, Design and Implementation

41.     The objectives, design, and implementation of the project were highly relevant to
the government's priorities and Bank's strategy which was encapsulated in the TSS
approved in July 2001. The Bank, along with other donors, were responding to a window
of opportunity created by positive developments to establish a lasting peace in the DRC,
end decades of devastation and lay the groundwork to tackle the country's economic
development problems, and initiate the long-term process of reconstruction and economic
rehabilitation. The challenges clearly identified and used as the four focus areas of the
TSS were: (a) to meet basic and urgent needs by supporting social service delivery and
social infrastructure, (b) to rebuild effective public institutions and policies to strengthen
transparency, accountability and participation while working on measures to promote
broad-based growth, (c) to revitalize economic activity through reconstruction of
essential infrastructure, assistance to agriculture, incentives for private sector investment
and community-driven development, and (d) to rebuild implementation capacity capable
of effective and efficient administration. The project objectives and design are consistent
with the TSS focus.

42.     The urgency for rapid implementation of donor assistance was also recognized.
The diagnosis of weak government implementation capacity led to the consensus that the
government development programs and projects would be best implemented through the
creation of a temporary unit to implement procurement, disbursement, financial
management and other key functions the government was not in a position to carry out.
The project implementation arrangements reflect this consensus.

43.    The project's objectives and design were also consistent with the government's
own priorities because its activities were drawn from the large multi-sector rehabilitation
and reconstruction program that the government itself prepared.




                                             12
3.2 Achievement of Project Development Objectives

44.     The overall development objective which was to initiate the long-term process of
reconstruction and economic rehabilitation has clearly been achieved with all the
investments planned being realized and capacity built within concerned ministries. More
importantly, the results of the project and their visibility have made a major contribution
to strengthening the involvement and commitment of other donors to help the DRC
pursue its development program. This has led to greater political stability and the
undertaking of many project and program initiatives using the results of the project as a
springboard. For instance, many new projects by the Bank and other donors are based on
the sector strategy and institutional studies carried out by the project. In addition to
sector results (water treatment plant, health zones, classrooms, protection of vulnerable
segments of the population, Matadi-Kinshasa road, Loange and Lovua bridges, Matadi
port, Mbandaka port, Kinshasa port, agriculture, electricity, etc.), there was also the
follow up reconstruction program launched by the government (les Cinq Chantiers du
Président) building on the EMRRP, as well as the infrastructure program supported by
China which also builds on the project and its achievements. Both reconstruction
programs are presently under implementation.

45.      The specific objectives have been achieved for the most part. With regard to
rebuilding agricultural production and enhance food security, a large number of farmers
and livestock breeders received improved seeds, plants and animal stock, several rural
roads and rural bridges were rehabilitated/constructed, and close to two million jobs were
created in the rural areas. The objective of rehabilitating and reconstructing critical
infrastructure was achieved -- all the major investments planned were realized and their
impact has been significant. Ports and key roads and bridges were rehabilitated or built.
Clean water production doubled in Kinshasa, hydro and thermal electricity production
was stabilized and increased substantially while distribution networks were rehabilitated.
Indeed the project initiated ambitious undertakings that are coming to fruition under
subsequent IDA projects. The objective to strengthen the capacity of the government to
formulate, implement and manage medium and long-term development programs have
been achieved to a satisfactory degree through the extensive training programs provided
in multiple areas, the sector reform and institutional reform studies carried out under this
operation, which were used to initiate new programs and activities, as well as the
institutional capacity put in place to implement this project. Details are provided in
Annex 2.

3.3 Efficiency

46.     The project did not lend itself to indicators of efficiency and none were defined.
As an emergency operation, moreover, there was no ex-ante formal economic and
financial analysis. It is however clear that the benefits generated by the investments
(roads rehabilitated, bridges, water, electricity, health, agriculture, classrooms, social
protection, etc.) are in excess of costs. Total Bank preparation, appraisal and supervision
costs were 0.69 percent of the total project costs. At the same time, overall project
operating costs for management, monitoring and evaluation of project implementation is



                                            13
estimated at 2.81 percent of total project costs. Given the complexity of the project, the
recognized weaknesses of a post-conflict situation, the complete lack of familiarity of the
DRC government with the Bank's guidelines and procedures and the fast processing of
the project, the level of project preparation, appraisal and supervision costs is reflective
of an efficient process. Project management costs are also low when taking into account
the lack of familiarity with the Bank's procedures. Other factors that reflect well on the
efficiency are the vast size of the country over which project activities took place and the
length of project implementation. Project implementation costs could have been
substantially higher and fraught with governance risks had a different implementation
arrangement been used with a more direct role for the fledgling government ministries
and agencies.

3.4 Justification of Overall Outcome Rating
Rating: Satisfactory

47.     The satisfactory rating is justified by the discussion above that the objectives, the
design and the implementation were highly relevant, that the PDOs were achieved, that
the benefits generated by the investments were in excess of costs, and by the modest level
of processing costs by the Bank. The results of the different sectors covered were
satisfactorily achieved. Thanks to the number of people trained, institutional change has
taken place. Some ministries have strengthened their capacity to manage projects, and are
beginning gradually to do so (health, education, agriculture, public infrastructure, water
entity, electricity entity, etc.). Policies or laws have been enacted using Bank advice
resulting from the different studies carried out under the project (road fund, different
sector reforms). The objectives were also achieved because of a number of factors: (a)
competent support from the government to the implementation structure put in place, (b)
the flexibility shown by both the Bank and the government to be proactive and agree on
timely reallocations of funds as dictated by the circumstances on the ground and (c)
vigilant and proactive supervision by the Bank which made sure all the needed resources
were available to carry out the task; (d) the stability and continuity of both the Bank and
the government project teams. The overall outcome rating is justified by the
achievements in each of the four components of the project as set out below.

Rehabilitation and reconstruction of critical infrastructure

48.     Water works completed have increased the N'Djili water production capacity
from 150,000 M3 per day before the project to 330,000 M3 per day after the completion
of the project. This now allows an additional population of about 2.3 million to have
access to safe water in Kinshasa. In addition, sector water studies (carried out under the
project) in provincial towns have been used for preparation of projects for Bank or other
donor financing, which has been useful.

49.     In transport, with regard to roads, some 1047 km (about 782 km paved and 265
km gravel) along the country's backbone main national highway (EN1) have been
rehabilitated or reconstructed (the main sections on EN1 concerned were Boma-Kinshasa,
Kinshasa-Kikwit, Kikwit-Tshikapa, Knanga-Mwene Ditu, Kamina-Lubumbashi). The



                                             14
Matadi-Kinshasa road which is the heart of the country for import and export saw its
travel time reduced from eight days to about six hours. Construction of the Loange
Bridge (some 500 m long, now called Independence Bridge as it was inaugurated at the
occasion of the 50th independence commemoration) created for the first time in the
country's history a continuous road link between the Eastern and Western parts of the
country. In other transport sub-sectors works were carried out to rehabilitate the ports of
Kinshasa, Mbandaka and Matadi; to dredge the maritime channel to Boma port and to
rehabilitate the dredger; to supply sleepers, spare parts and telecommunication equipment
to the railway company in the South-East; supply of hydrographic boats and buoys for
maritime and river navigation safety.

50.      Emergency repairs and works to secure electricity production at Inga
hydroelectric power station and at major provincial towns stations; rehabilitation of
selected transmission lines and distribution networks in Kinshasa and major provincial
towns. The project procured turbines for group 12 at Inga, installation is being done
under an IDA supported electricity project. This will restart group 12 for the first time
since 1996 when it collapsed. The EMRRP supplied and installed new thermal power
stations at the provincial towns of Mbandaka (1.25 MW), Mbuji Mayi (2 of 1.25 MW
each) and Kikwit (0.75 MW) which restored electricity production in these important
provincial towns (in Kikwit, for example, the national electric company, SNEL, stopped
producing electricity since major breakdowns several years earlier). High voltage sub-
stations and high voltage transmission lines have been rehabilitated under the project in
the Inga-Kinshasa corridor. This secured transmission to Kinshasa and helped reduce
outages along the line. The project rehabilitated targeted sections of the distribution
networks in Kinshasa (particularly in the industrial area where the water treatment plant
of Ndjili was built under the project), Matadi, Boma and Kimpese.

51.     Finally, urban works to control major erosion in poor quarters of Kinshasa;
rehabilitation and lighting of major urban and commercial urban artery (12 km long
crossing some 10 municipalities in the capital Kinshasa) was also achieved.

Agriculture, delivery of social services and community development

52.     In agriculture, animal production (alevins, poultry, fish), vegetal production
(cassava, rice, improved seeds, etc.), over 2000 km of rural feeder roads rehabilitated,
veterinary laboratory rehabilitated and equipped. Over two million households benefited
from the component and more than 1.8 million jobs were created under labor intensive
methods.

53.     In the health sector 78 health zones in the different provinces of the country
supported and equipped through NGOs providing full health services: some 480 health
centers rehabilitated and equipped. The component benefited some 10 million people
spread over the provinces served. One high medical institute and seventeen technical
institutes were rehabilitated and equipped. More than 265 trainers have been trained at
the central, provincial and district levels in health service management, and in turn, some
650 health worker teams were trained in primary health services. Six drug procurement



                                            15
and distribution centers were set up and made operational. The services were provided by
selected NGOs under service contracts. The NGOs performance was monitored by the
implementing agency BCECO. Baseline knowledge and progress of health indicators
were monitored and reported by the NGOs, using the initial work carried at the project
start under USAID financing.

54.     In education, more than 2,300 classrooms rehabilitated (excluding those under the
community development component), some 28,000 school benches supplied. Close to
180,000 children benefited from this component. The project also supported social
protection for vulnerable groups. Close to 400 small sub-projects to assist vulnerable
segments of the population (orphans, displaced population, young abandoned single
mothers, etc.) were carried out. Close to 80,000 persons benefited from these small
projects. In community development: some 800 sub-projects were completed with the
participation of the communities in different provinces of the country covering small
water schemes, feeder roads, health centers, classrooms, markets, slaughter houses.

Development of sector strategies for the medium and long term and strengthening of
human and institutional capacities

55.      A number of sector strategies and studies have been completed and some are
being used by other sector projects to implement or for preparation of sector operations.
They included a master plan for Kinshasa water supply, health sector strategy, education
strategy, social protection national policy/strategy for vulnerable groups, national
community development policy/strategy, master plan for Kinshasa sanitation, a study on
civil aviation directorate restructuring, update of civil aviation code, urban transport
institutional reform study, transport sector institutional reform study, road safety study,
road fund setting up study and action plan.

56.     With regard to capacity building and institutional strengthening, six ministries and
thirteen government agencies had their office buildings rehabilitated and/or equipped,
and received computers, vehicles and other equipment to facilitate their work. Some 830
staff from five ministries and thirteen government agencies were trained in-country and
abroad in different topics such as procurement, project management, IT, English,
safeguards, financial management, environment aspects. Some 860 staff from five sector
ministries and nine government agencies benefited from on-the-job training through
pairing of national counterparts with international experts of consulting firms and
contractors.

3.5 Overarching Themes, Other Outcomes and Impacts

(a) Poverty Impacts, Gender Aspects, and Social Development

57.    Because of the emergency nature of the project, no poverty impact, gender or
social development impact were undertaken. Nonetheless the project had important
impacts on poverty alleviation, gender aspects and social development, and made major
contributions to the MDGs in the country. As noted elsewhere, clean and safe water in



                                            16
quality and quantity according to WHO standards was provided to over three million
people living in the poorest quarters of Kinshasa. Large scale construction/equipping of
classrooms is allowing some 164,000 children to benefit from education in decent
conditions. Over two million households have benefitted from improved agriculture
production. About 10 million people living in poor areas in different provinces are
benefitting from the supported integrated health zones operated by well regarded NGOs.
More than 77,000 vulnerable persons (orphans, displaced persons, handicapped, single
young mothers, etc.) were assisted and supported under the social protection component
of the project.

(b) Institutional Change/Strengthening

58.     The main thrust of the project was not to address long term institutional or sector
reform, or capacity building issues, although activities and funds were included to that
effect. However, a number of results were achieved as evidenced by the many training
programs offered to staff of various ministries and agencies in areas such as procurement,
project management, Information Technology, financial management, and environment
management both in the DRC and overseas. In addition, close to a thousand staff from
four ministries (agriculture, rural development, health, education and social affairs),
while staff from five ministries and nine agencies (infrastructure sectors) were able to
learn on the job by working as counterparts to the team of experts recruited for project
implementation and return to apply the lessons learned in their respective
ministries/agencies.

59.     Institutional strengthening was also carried out through the supply of essential
materials and equipments such as computers and vehicles to at least six ministries
(finance, plan, rural development, primary and secondary education, environment, social
affairs and health) and a number of national agencies (water, roads, meteorology,
railways, electricity, etc.). Finally, a number of institutional reform and sector strategy
studies financed by the project led to the initiation and actual implementation of reforms
by the government in key areas such as civil aviation, urban transport, roads, railroads,
environment management, river transport, electricity, and urban water and sanitation.
One notable example is the creation by the government of the road fund (under a road
sector project, but based on the recommendations of the studies carried out under the
EMRRP). Studies were also done on social protection of the vulnerable groups,
community development, and specific agricultural sub-sectors such as cocoa, coffee,
palm trees and rubber.

5. Assessment of Bank and Borrower Performance

5.1 Bank Performance
(a) Bank Performance in Ensuring Quality at Entry

Rating: Satisfactory

62.     The Bank was very receptive and responded expeditiously to the country's urgent
needs for reconstruction and rehabilitation, processing the project from identification to
Board approval in less than a year under the Rapid Response to Crises and Emergency
guidelines (OP8.50 at that time). It worked diligently with other donors to help the
government define the contours of a vast emergency multi-sector rehabilitation and
reconstruction program which the project is part of. The Bank also played an important
role in the efforts to mobilize donors to provide financing, including during
implementation when one donor stopped support for education activities. In expediting
the reopening of a country office, the Bank helped strengthen the in-country dialogue and
coordination with both the government and the donors. This was further reinforced by the
inclusion of field office staff with expertise in the critical areas of financial management,
procurement and infrastructure in the project task team as project implementation
intensified. Finally, in addition to making sure the staff resources were available to work
closely with the government during implementation, great flexibility was demonstrated
with the willingness to work proactively with the government to find a speedy and
satisfactory conclusion to the requests made for extensions, amendments and
reallocations during implementation. The project (given its scope and magnitude and the
size of the country) helped demonstrate that OP8.50 needed to be revised (which was
ultimately done with the new OP8.00 which replaced the previous one).

63.    Preparation of the project was well focused and preceded by important work on
the overall macro-economic environment and the prevailing conditions in a country
emerging from years of conflict. This preliminary work, underscored by the TSS and
other planned activities, helped define the key parameters that were critical to an
appropriate project design. The rationale for Bank involvement was articulately presented
and the criteria for the selection of the project components well defined. The teams were
properly staffed to address the sector issues. The problem of key indicators was properly
addressed through the design of steps to obtain a reliable base case scenario where none




                                              18
existed. The project provided a framework for other emergency projects that followed in
other provinces of the country.

64.      The inclusion of institutional strengthening, capacity building and sector strategy
preparation in an emergency project meant to focus on short term critical infrastructure
rehabilitation and meeting of social needs was a judicious recognition that in a post-
conflict situation emergency, unlike the case of a disaster-caused emergency operations,
the weakness of public institutions and governance and the absence of long-standing
sector policies and strategies required that a first step be taken to start the process of
institution and capacity building as well as strategy development to lay the ground work
for future development initiatives. The project did this well and has contributed to the
creation of a rich and relevant project and program pipeline that is now being exploited
by all the donors, including the Bank itself.

(b) Quality of Supervision
(including of fiduciary and safeguards policies)

Rating: Satisfactory

65.      The Bank's performance during supervision was satisfactory. There was strong
management support throughout implementation. Supervision missions were carried out
on a regular basis during the long implementation period. As the multi-sectoral nature of
the project made it difficult to always have all the expertise needed to organize complete
missions covering all sectors, missions in the early implementation period were made at
regular intervals. Despite senior management changes over the implementation period
that saw three vice-presidents, four country directors and two sector managers,
institutional memory and commitment continuity were preserved, the key factor being
that the same task team leader was kept.

66.     The task team was very proactive in working with the government to identify
most critical issues and to find a solution within an appropriate time frame and in a
flexible and responsive fashion so as to avoid major disruptions to project activities. This
is evidenced by the way the extensions of the closing dates, the reallocations of funds, the
processing of additional grants and the amendments to the financing agreements were
handled. The skills mix in the staffing of the missions reflected the build-up of the
implementation of the largest and more complex infrastructure component. Missions
were scheduled flexibly so that staff dealing with a given sector could undertake their
missions separately without waiting to be part of a big overall mission, although multi-
sector missions also took place whenever feasible and became more frequent after the
mid-term review. Likewise, responsibilities for the important areas of financial
management, education and procurement were transferred to the country office staff,
which made it possible to address issues that came up more rapidly. The task team
realized early on that because of the complexity with the technical studies and the
procurement process of the infrastructure component, its pace of actual implementation in
terms of commitment and disbursement was slower than that of the social component. It
subsequently worked with the government to extend the implementation period over a
more realistic time frame.

6. Lessons Learned

73.    The key lessons learned can be broken into the following areas: the preparation
phase and the implementation phase.

The Preparation Phase

       Even in emergency situations, an effort should be made to address sustainability
       issues at the project or program design stage. If not done, the investments
       made could be jeopardized over the long term. If this is not discussed during
       project preparation and appraisal, it should be included as part of early
       implementation activities as it was done in the project case.
       Related to this is the extent to which such projects should focus on policy and
       institutional reform as well as capacity building. An effort should be made to
       include institutional and capacity building skills in the preparation phase so that
       appropriate groundwork can be laid even with a modest level of funding.
       Agreement should be reached on how knowledge and sector development
       strategies would be addressed during implementation, and this can be the
       precursor for follow up operations as was the case in this project.
       Because of the uncertainties concerning cost estimates in an emergency situation,
       flexibility needs to be built in the financing plan so that processing of adjustments
       or reallocations can be simplified and the transaction costs minimized as further
       studies provide more reliable cost estimates. Under the project, the unallocated
       category was on average around five percent of total costs. This was found to be
       inadequate.
       The implementation period initially envisaged was too optimistic given the
       complexities of infrastructure implementation. Even with the provision of
       effective and efficient technical assistance, a more realistic timeframe for project
       completion needs to be considered in emergency situation; particularly for
       operations with multi-sectoral dimension since normal Bank implementation
       guidelines and procedures still need to be followed. On average a five-year
       implementation period (against an initial three-year implementation period) to


                                            21
      meet its reconstruction and longer-term institutional capacity building objectives
      could be considered.
      A distinction needs to be made between emergency operations caused by natural
      disasters and those in a post-conflict context. In the first case, institutional
      capacity and policy tend not be major issues. In the second case, because of the
      destruction caused by conflicts, the process starts off with much lower levels of
      capacity and knowledge. This argues for the need to include institutional reforms,
      capacity building and sector strategy formulation as was done in this project in
      order to start laying the foundations for the future.

Implementation and Supervision Phase

      Adoption of simple implementation mechanisms aided by appropriate
      technical assistance is a good approach which could however be supplemented
      by an early discussion of long term capacity building measures after
      implementation starts. Every effort should be made to discourage the use of such
      "enclave" implementation mechanisms as the normal practice for future
      operations. Temporary implementation arrangements used in this project have
      ended, and capacity built under the project has been integrated in the ministries
      and national entities which are now gradually taking the responsibility for
      execution of new operations financed by Bank and other donors.
       Sustainability issues need to be part of the dialogue early during project
      implementation, especially when this is not explicitly considered during the
      lending phase. Provision for adequate maintenance of investments was
      thoroughly discussed with the government during implementation. In this respect,
      some more recent Bank-financed sector projects are consolidating and supporting
      the achievements of the project. Workshops on results and sustainability aspects
      of the project were held prior to project completion in order to sensitize the
      government and other partners to sustainability and to make practical
      recommendations to the government for further implementation and for the
      transition from emergency to sector operations.
      Effective harmonization is critical to maximize the development impact. Even
      in post-conflict situations for low income countries, important results can be
      achieved when a program/project is well designed and the adequate execution
      arrangements are put in place for its implementation. The project greatly
      benefitted from the: (a) design closely aligned with overall objectives, (b) lessons
      learned from previous and similar emergency operations; (c) guidelines for
      Emergency recovery assistance OP/BP 8.50; (d) government's ownership; (e)
      concerted efforts from the donor community, (f) experienced multi-sector Bank
      team (g) special arrangements to implement fiduciary aspects;(h) strengthening of
      capacity for the existing implementing agencies; and (i) close on-the- ground
      supervision for learning and monitoring.
      Strong commitment from management in terms of resources and continuity
      within the task team is a key determination of successful implementation.




                                          22
       The use of NGOs proved to be an effective and efficient way of implementation
       the social activities at the grass root level. This should serve as a lesson and good
       precedent.

74.     To sum up, should this type of project in similar emergency situations (post
conflict, reconstruction after a natural disaster, etc.) be repeated by the Bank? The project
has achieved its objectives and the results it yielded are important. There are however
many prerequisites and "ifs" to be fulfilled before undertaking a similar project: if the
right institutional arrangements are put in place, the right skills mix and competencies are
deployed, the government at high level is fully supportive and on board, the
responsibilities and roles are clear and well defined (ministries, national entities,
executing and implementing agencies), the program and its components are well designed
and well monitored with all necessary resources, the skills mix and the leadership can be
maintained during implementation, then the operation could be repeated and can be
successful.

75.      After DRC there have been similar openings in fragile countries such as Liberia,
and South Sudan where IDA has been active. In these two latter cases, the decision was
taken to proceed with more sectorially focused emergency operations, rather than a broad
multi sectorial operation. While it may be too early to judge the relative outcomes in the
two cases, it should also be noted that given the geographical complexity of DRC at the
time with regard to access and stability, and the urgency to move quickly into the country
along a broad swath of sectors and areas, the multi-sectorial approach may have been the
only practical way forward. And in either case it has to be emphasized that sustained
support from donors over an extended period of time, to support recovery and build
institutions is essential for fragile countries to emerge into an era of peace and sustained
economic development.

I.     BACKGROUND

The Emergency Multisector Rehabilitation and Reconstruction Program (EMRRP) is
the largest visible program that was launched right after cooperation between the
Government of the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) and the international financial
community resumed. The program was designed in a post conflict and emergency context.


The program focuses on large infrastructure works (transport, roads, water, electricity,
railroads and sanitation) and also includes priority actions to provide social services
(education, health, social safety protection, community development) and support for the
agricultural sector to ensure food security for targeted populations in government-controlled
provinces, with priority to those living along RN1 [national route 1].


The IDA-financed project ended on March 31, 2010. The objective of this workshop is as
follows:
       (i)         Evaluate the framework for Project preparation and implementation ;
       (ii)        Draw lessons from current and future projects for the World Bank or any
                   other donor ;
       (iii)       Hear from beneficiaries ;
       (iv)        Respond to specific questions about the Project.

of lectures followed by testimonies by both beneficiary institutions and direct beneficiaries.
To enrich the discussions, a results matrix for the entire project was presented.

workshops.

1. Assessment of the development objective and design and quality at inception
1.1.   Project evaluation background (excerpt of TA of MOP)

1.     The years of economic mismanagement and the recent conflict have had a devastating effect on the
economy of the country and the quality of life of citizens. This period is marked by the loss of very many
lives in combat and the large displacement of people. Practically all the DRC's indicators ranked the
country's economic and social development among the lowest in the World.

2.    Agriculture. This is the country's largest sector in terms of employment and economic production.
Agricultural production has declined steeply, from surplus production to deficit production. The fall in
agricultural production has considerably increased food insecurity.

3.    Transport infrastructure. The lack of maintenance and damage caused by conflict has led to the
continuous deterioration of roads, bridges and other transport infrastructure.

4.     Water supply. As a result of lack of funds, the national water distribution company, la Régie de
Distribution d'Eau (REGIDESO), has not been able to restore, repair or ensure minimum maintenance of
its water installations.

5.     Electricity services. The electricity system has simply collapsed because the national electricity
company, la Société Nationale d'Electricité (SNEL), has been unable to maintain electricity infrastructure
or replace obsolete or worn out equipment.

6.     The cities' streets, particularly those of Kinshasa are in a very poor condition. Consequently, not
only stagnant water accumulates often on the roads resulting in overflows of septic tanks and latrines but
also creating favorable conditions for the reproduction of mosquitoes and other parasites.

7.   Education. The enrolment rate has dropped considerably, from 72 percent of eligible children in
1978-79 to 59 percent in 1995.

8.     Health. Public health has deteriorated significantly in the past ten years. Malaria, Aids, sleeping
sickness, tuberculosis and other parasitic and infectious diseases are responsible for 80 percent of diseases
in the DRC.

9.    Public administration. Public administration in the DRC has deteriorated over many years of
neglect. Mismanagement and low salaries have led to corruption.

10. Donors have gradually started to resume their support for economic recovery in the DRC. The
United Nations agencies are assisting the government with a variety of programs designed to create the
conditions for lasting peace.

1.2.   Indicators and initial goals

11. The overall goal of the project was to respond to the Government's efforts and strategy aimed at
overcoming the several challenges that the DRC was facing. The goal reflected the willingness expressed
by the donors to resume assistance for reconstruction and development. It is consistent with the IDA's
transitional assistance strategy approved by the Board of the World Bank at the end of July 2001.

12. As defined in § A of Section III of the « Memorandum and Recommendation of the President »
(MOP), the overall goal of the EMRRP project is to start the reconstruction and economic recovery
process.

2.1.      Quality at the inception of the project

25. As defined and conceptualized at its inception, in order to (i) meet the desired objectives and (ii)
reduce the possible effects of the risks, the project, with the totality of its components, was maintained
and carried on to completion following the structure and strategy initially developed and implemented in
agreement with all the parties.

26. The emergency procedure (OP. 8.50) used by the Bank to develop the project allowed it to be
implemented very quickly, because only one (1) year elapsed between pre-appraisal (December 2001) and
effectiveness (November 8, 2002).

29.        Finally, identifying the various needs proved to be very prudent.

environmental firm.

2.3.       Implementation

       32. The implementation challenge was how to address the weaknesses caused by the post conflict
           situation in the DRC and by the intrinsic characteristics of the multi-sector project.

       33. The government and the Bank therefore agreed to create temporary bodies for the management,
           monitoring and evaluation and execution of the Project.

       34. An "Inter-Ministerial Steering Committee" headed by the Minister in charge of Planning was
           established. It comprises the Ministries (or their representatives) of Finance, Budget, and
           technical ministries involved in the project. It is supported by a Technical Coordination
           Committee (CTC) and the Project Steering Supporting Unit (CAP) is its Technical and Executive
           Secretariat.

       35. Two implementing agencies are responsible for the technical and financial management of both
           programs, namely :

       -    BCMI (Markets Coordination Office, initially called PMIF), run by an international company
            recruited for this purpose.

       -    BCECO (Central Project Coordination Office), a national agency supported by three international
            experts.

the phases and made some recommendations which led to :
    - The formalization of supplementary financing and a postponement of the closing date.

    - Strengthening of the BCMI and preparation of action plans.

5. Lessons Learned.

      Evaluation Phase and Loan Agreement

70. The project was expected to be carried out over a short time. The first installation maintenance
works would thus not be necessary during the project execution period.

71. Thus, there is no need to expect maintenance at the design stage. However, there needs to be a
synergy between the different projects studied by the Bank so that the sector projects which are still being
studied, take into account the temporary maintenance of rehabilitated facilities if necessary.

72. The nature of the emergency Project made the designers lose sight of the reality of what could be
the actual delays in execution; (i) from the Bank's side; the procedures remain identical to all other
Projects at the execution stage; and (ii) from the implementing agencies side, the processing, procedures
and responsiveness do not necessarily respond to these emergency requirements.

73. The lesson to be drawn is that it is necessary to anticipate these constraints and not use overly tight
deadlines although they could be an incentive to mobilize the various stakeholders.

      Implementation Phase

74. The implementation procedures have remained the same for as for traditional projects. This
approach led to time being lost on both sides - the Bank and implementing agencies.

75.    Measures should be envisaged to address this concern.

76. Évaluation de l'objectif de développement et de la conception et de la qualité à l'origine.
76.1. Contexte à l'évaluation du Projet (extrait des TA of MOP)
1. Les années de mauvaise gestion économique et le récent conflit avaient eu des effets dévastateurs sur
l'économie du pays et la qualité de vie des citoyens. Cette période est marquée par la perte de très
nombreuses vies dans les combats et des déplacements très importants de personnes, dont beaucoup ont
fui vers les villes. Pratiquement tous les indicateurs de la RDC classifiaient le développement économique
et social du pays parmi les plus démunis dans le monde. Le produit intérieur brut avait chuté, Le revenu
par habitant avait diminué de façon constante. La RDC était alors l'un des pays les plus pauvres dans le
monde. Les recettes publiques ne s'élevées à pas plus de 5 pour cent du PIB. Le déficit (près de 18 pour
cent du PIB en 2000) a été financé principalement par l'accumulation d'arriérés sur la dette extérieure.
2. Agriculture. Est le secteur le plus important du pays en termes d'emploi et de production
économique. La production agricole a chuté fortement et d'excédentaire est devenu déficitaire. La chute
de la production agricole a considérablement augmenté l'insécurité alimentaire et appauvrie millions
d'agriculteurs et de gens qui doivent acheter de la nourriture pour survivre.
3. L'infrastructure des transports. Le manque d'entretien et les dommages causés par le conflit a conduit
à la détérioration constante de routes, ponts et autres infrastructures de transport. Les marchés ne sont plus
connectés et les agriculteurs ne peuvent ni acheter des intrants agricoles, ni facilement vendre leurs
produits. La détérioration des infrastructures entraîne une augmentation conséquente des prix des produits
sur les marchés.
4. Approvisionnement en eau. Par manque de fonds la Régie de Distribution d'Eau (REGIDESO), n'a
pas été en mesure de remettre en état, réparer ou de fournir un minimum d'entretien de ses ouvrages
hydrauliques. Cette situation a conduit à la forte détérioration de la qualité des services dans les grands
centres urbains exposant les populations qui font appel à des solutions palliatives à de nombreuses
maladies.
5. Les services d'électricité. Le système de l'électricité n'a fait que s'effondrer car la Société Nationale
d'Electricité (SNEL), n'a pas maintenu les infrastructures d'électricité, ni remplacé les équipements
obsolètes ou usés. En outre, en raison du conflit, plusieurs zones du pays et villes importantes ont été
coupées du réseau d'approvisionnement.
6. Les rues des villes et particulièrement celles de Kinshasa sont en très mauvais état. En conséquence,
l'eau stagnante s'accumule souvent dans les rues et les trottoirs, entravant la circulation des personnes et
des biens, portant atteinte à des bâtiments, contribuant à la formation de nids de poule, ce qui, associé au

                                                     79
débordement des fosses septiques et des latrines favorise les zones de reproduction pour les moustiques et
autres parasites., ce qui favorise la création de zones de reproduction pour les vecteurs de maladies
nuisibles et expose les habitants à des risques importants de maladie.
7. Education. Le taux de scolarisation a fortement chuté. Il est passé de 72% des enfants admissibles en
1978-79 à 59% 1995. La détérioration des infrastructures, des matériels, le manque de support du
Gouvernement ont favorisés le développement de structures non gouvernementales, payantes, excluant les
enfants des plus pauvres qui en subissent les conséquences à long terme.
8. La santé publique s'est considérablement détériorée au cours des dix dernières années. Le paludisme,
le sida, la maladie du sommeil, la tuberculose et autres maladies parasitaires et infectieuses sont
responsables de 80 pour cent des conséquences de la maladie en RDC. Le nombre de personnes
nouvellement infectées par les infections à VIH a augmenté d'un facteur 16 entre 1990 et 2000
9. L'administration publique. L'administration publique en RDC s'est détériorée au cours de longues
années de négligence. La mauvaise gestion et les bas salaires ont encouragé la corruption.
10. Progressivement les donateurs ont commencé à reprendre leur soutien à la relance économique en
RDC. Les Organismes des Nations Unies aident le gouvernement avec une variété de programmes conçus
pour créer les conditions d'une paix durable. Au début de 2001, le gouvernement a négocié un programme
avec le FMI, qui devait conduire à un programme de développement pour la réduction de la pauvreté et
pour la croissance. La Banque a élaboré une stratégie d'aide transitoire (SAT), approuvé par le Conseil
d'administration, pour orienter ses programmes d'assistance en RDC pendant la période précédant
l'effacement des arriérés.

76.2. Objectifs d'origine et indicateurs.

11. L'objectif d'ensemble du projet était de donner une réponse aux efforts et stratégie que le
Gouvernement était en train de mettre en place pour relever les nombreux défis qui se posaient à la RDC.
Il entrait dans le cadre de la volonté de reprise des activités d'assistance à la reconstruction et au
développement que les bailleurs avaient témoigné. Il s'articulait avec la stratégie d'aide transitoire (SAT)
de l'IDA, approuvée par le Conseil d'Administration de la Banque fin juillet 2001, qui avait été conçue
pour permettre à la RDC et à la sous-région de faire la transition vers la paix et la stabilité pour générer
l'assistance économique qui accompagnerait les efforts déployés par le Gouvernement, pour assurer la
relance de l'économie et sa remise sur la voie de la croissance.
12. Les mécanismes de mise en oeuvre prévus devaient permettre de (i) lever les principaux obstacles à la
reprise économique, (ii) mobiliser et générer des ressources et des investissements, (iii) assurer la
restructuration des entreprises publiques et répondre aux besoins les plus urgents (démobilisation,
réinsertion, services sociaux essentiels, infrastructures, VIH/SIDA) qui risquaient d'entraver les
perspectives de développement à long terme de la RDC.
13. Comme définis au § A de la section III du "Memorandum and Recommendation of the President"
(MOP), L'objectif global du projet PMURR est d'engager le processus de reconstruction et de
relèvement économique.
14. Les objectifs spécifiques consistent à aider la RDC à :
    a. Commencer à reconstruire la production agricole et améliorer la sécurité alimentaire ;
    b. La réhabilitation et la reconstruction des infrastructures essentielles ;
    c. Rétablir les services sociaux et construire des infrastructures communautaires ;
    d. Renforcer la capacité du Gouvernement à formuler, mettre en oeuvre et gérer des programmes de
       développement à moyen et long terme.

15. Comme l'avait montré la revue à mi-parcours du projet conduite en avril 2005, le PMURR projet

76.3. Composantes initiales.

16. Pour atteindre les objectifs et plus particulièrement ceux, rappelés ci-dessus au 3.1, spécifiques au
programme, l'accord de Crédit (DCA) en date du 14 août 2002, dans son annexe 2 comprend quatre
composantes principales ; elles même réparties en plusieurs sous-composantes pouvant être réparties en
plusieurs secteurs d'intervention.
17. Bien que le Programme soit à l'échelle nationale, le Projet sous financement IDA a du se consacrer
aux zones ou la situation de sécurité locale rendait possible les activités de supervision. C'est ce qui
explique que les activités sont largement concentrées dans les zones contrôlées par le Gouvernement.
18. Le programme et plus spécialement le Projet financé par l'IDA trouve ses sources dans le
"Programme multisectoriel de réhabilitation et de grande reconstruction" préparé en septembre 2001 par
le Ministère du Plan en consultation avec les Ministères sectoriels et les Entités concernées.

   A- Réhabilitation et Reconstruction des infrastructures de base;
           Infrastructures clés dans le domaine des transports
                   - Infrastructures ferroviaires ;
                   - Ports fluviaux et maritimes ;
                   - Navigation fluviale ;
                   - Navigation maritime ;
                   - Météorologie nationale (sécurité navigation aérienne).
                   - Circulation routière;
                   - Définition des politiques sectorielles.
           Routes principales
           Énergie électrique,
           L'eau potable,
           Infrastructures et services urbains.
19. Cette composante finance la réhabilitation et la reconstruction des infrastructures essentielles dans
chacune des sous composantes.
20. La sous composante transport finance des activités afin d'améliorer le maritime, le fluvial, le
ferroviaire, et même l'aérien ;
21. La sous composante routes principales appui la réhabilitation ou la reconstruction de la (RN1) qui
relie six provinces aux ports côtiers.
22. La sous composante énergie électrique finance la réhabilitation des réseaux de distribution
d'électricité desservant les grandes villes et la fiabilisation ou restauration de la production.
23. La sous composante eau appui la réhabilitation d'ouvrages hydrauliques dans la ville de Kinshasa où
les services de l'eau sont fortement détériorés et prépare la restauration pour les villes de l'intérieur.

24. La sous composante des infrastructures urbaines permettra à la ville de Kinshasa de réhabiliter un
axe primordial et traiter un site d'érosion particulièrement critique et préparer un service des déchets
solides et une stratégie de drainage.
   B- Agriculture, secteurs sociaux et développement communautaire;

26. La sous-composante agriculture finance la réhabilitation des routes rurales et pistes d'accès; aidera à
établir un système d'information agricole pour les agriculteurs, aide à renforcer les opérations des
organisations de producteurs, ONG, et les principaux prestataires de services publics, et soutient le
développement d'un cadre politique qui améliore l'investissement pour l'agriculture et la sylviculture.
27. La sous-composante santé aidera le gouvernement à (i) renforcer le partenariat et la participation
communautaire et à rétablir les structures de la santé; (ii) réhabiliter et d'équiper les centres de santé et
autres établissements de santé dans de nombreuses zones de santé; (d) accroître le nombre et améliorer les

de distribution pour améliorer la disponibilité des médicaments et autres fournitures médicales
essentielles dans tout le pays; (iv) renforcer les programmes de prévention et de traitement des maladies
les plus graves de la RDC, y compris le VIH / sida, le paludisme, la tuberculose et la maladie du sommeil,
et (v) fournir des services de soins de santé destinés à des groupes spécifiques, notamment les mères, les
enfants, les orphelins et victimes de guerre.
28. La sous-composante éducation finance la réhabilitation des écoles primaires, l'achat d'équipement
des classes.
29. La sous-composante protection sociale soutient les activités pilotes visant à tester des approches de la
conception et l'exécution des programmes pour aider les personnes extrêmement vulnérables. Les
activités peuvent comprendre l'éducation informelle et accélérée pour les enfants des rues, des
programmes visant à réintégrer les enfants soldats dans la société, et autres actions.
30. La sous-composante développement communautaire sert à financer des projets à petite échelle pour
améliorer l'éducation, la santé, les transports, l'eau potable et d'assainissement, l'approvisionnement en
énergie, protection de l'environnement, et la prestation des services sociaux de base et économique. Les
projets sont identifiés, préparés et exécutés par les communautés avec l'aide des ONG, afin d'assurer
qu'ils reflètent les priorités des communautés, et que les communautés prennent en charge l'entretien des
infrastructures et des services.
   C- développement des stratégies sectorielles, réformes institutionnelles et renforcement des
   capacités et des institutions;
31. Cette composante financera la préparation des stratégies de développement pour les secteurs les plus

activités préparatoires pour les futurs programmes d'investissement.
   D- gestion, suivi et évaluation de la mise en place du programme et du projet IDA.

        développement communautaire)
    -   D'apporter un appui financier pour compléter l'ensemble des opérations dont les coûts entre la

        surcoûts de la sous-composante électricité en ce qui concerne le deuxième financement
        complémentaires.
    -   D'une meilleure gestion des intervenants extérieurs. Afin d'assurer une totale transparence dans

pris sur certaines opérations à bénéficié du report de la date initiale de clôture (30 juin 2005) au 31
décembre 2008 puis 31 mars 2010.
36. Trois réallocations des fonds pour, répondre aux réalités du Projet, notamment dans les
infrastructures (par exemple la sous composante électricité initialement prévue en fournitures, la mise en
place devant être faite par la SNEL a finalement été réalisée sous la forme fourniture et montage) ont été
faites par la Banque à la demande du Gouvernement ; (i) en même temps que le 2ème financement
additionnel en janvier 2008 ; (ii) la deuxième en avril 2009 et (iii) la troisième en décembre 2009.

identifiés
    - La mise en place d'un gouvernement efficace, stable et transparent ne réussit pas à progresser
    - Les agences gouvernementales et leur partenaires non gouvernementaux manquent des capacités
         pour contrôler le programme
    - Les bailleurs ne fournissent pas les fonds nécessaires à palier au manque de financement

Devait pouvoir répondre aux attentes.

    -   Gestion et exécution du volet A ­ infrastructures - par une firme internationale PMIF, sous la

gestion environnementale et sociale et des évaluations environnementales des sous-projets établi par la
Firme internationale spécialement recrutée pour l'ensemble de la gestion environnementale et sociale du
Projet.
44. Le caractère d'urgence du Projeta permis que les opérations soient lancées avant l'établissement du
cadre du processus environnemental et social.

    - L'établissement du cadre du processus environnemental et social ;

77.3. Mise en oeuvre

47. Le défi lancé pour la mise en oeuvre était celui de palier aux faiblesses dues à la situation post-conflit
de la RDC et aux caractéristiques intrinsèques du projet multisectoriel.
    -   Faiblesse des institutions en charge des programme et des projets,
    -   Cadre réglementaire et législatif non en phase avec celui des principaux partenaires au
        développement, dont l'IDA,
    -   Ressources humaines dans les Ministères et Entités non mises à jour aux faits des procédures
        courantes de la Banque
    -   Cohésion et l'unité nationale en cours de parachèvement.
    -   Gérer et coordonner un Projet impliquant un très grand nombre d'institutions, neuf (9) Ministères
        et treize (13) Entités nationales
48. Ainsi le gouvernement et la Banque ont convenu de mettre en place des structures transitoires de
gestion, suivi et évaluation de l'exécution du Projet et parallèlement renforcer les capacités tant humaines
qu'institutionnelles des structures administratives pérennes pour qui, à terme, seraient appelées à gérer les
futurs projets.
49. Conformément aux orientations de la commission gouvernementale Economique et
Financière(ECOFIN), il a été mis en place un `'Comité interministériel de pilotage'' (décret présidentiel
No 036/046 du 08 Décembre 2003) conduit et présidé par le Ministre en charge du plan et par essence
coordinateur du programme de reconstruction nationale. Il comprend les Ministres(ou leurs représentants)
des Finances, du Budget, et les Ministres Techniques concernés par les opérations du Projet, ainsi que des
Responsables Techniques du Bureau du chef de l'Etat. Il est appuyé par un Comité Technique de
Coordination (CTC) composé des experts des Ministères, Organismes et Entités impliquées dans le
Projet. La Cellule d'Appui au Pilotage du projet (CAP) en est le Secrétariat Technique et exécutif.
50. Deux Agences d'Exécution ont la charge de la gestion technique et financière des deux sous
programmes à savoir :
    -   BCMI (Bureau de Coordination des marchés des Infrastructures, initialement dénommé PMIF),
        animé par une firme internationale recrutée à cet effet.
    -   BCeCo (Bureau Central de Coordination des projets), une structure nationale appuyée par trois
        experts internationaux.
51. Une cellule spécifiques a été crées pour faciliter et accélérer l'exécution du Projet dans le cadre
réglementaire des accords de Crédit.
    -   La cellule fiscale dont les modalités de fonctionnement sont définies par les arrêtés du 6 mars et
        29 mai 2003. Elle permet d'accélérer les procédures de dédouanement et d'admission temporaire
        pour les Contrats financés par le PMURR (ou autres Projets financés par les bailleurs).

    -   Favoriser une plus grande implication des Ministères sectoriels et Organismes concernés par le
        projet.
    -   Donner les moyens financiers nécessaires a BCECO, CAP, cellule environnementale.
53. Ces recommandations ont bien entendu été suivi d'effets et notamment des dispositions ont été prises
pour accentuer et formaliser l'implication des Ministères et Entités impliquées dans le PMURR.
    -   Des points focaux ont été nommés dans chaque structure.
    -   Des homologues ont été désignés dans les Ministères et Entités et les structures de suivi pour être
        immergés de façon participative dans les agences d'exécution.
    -   Pour les Infrastructures (BCMI) des homologues ont été désigné pour être immergés dans toutes
        les missions de contrôle.
54. Ces dispositions ont été consacrées et formalisées par :
    -   La création de la Cellule Focale Santé et celle du Ministère de l'Agriculture, pêche et élevage
        (Secrétariat Exécutif du Comité d'Orientation Agriculture - SECO).
    -   Etablissement et signature des protocoles d'accord entre le BCeCo et les ministères ayant en
        charge l'agriculture, l'enseignement primaire, le développement communautaire, la protection
        sociale.
    -   Etablissement et signature des protocoles d'accord entre le BCMI et les quatorze (14) principales
        Entités impliquées dans le Projet et, le Ministère de l'Environnement.

77.4. Pérennisation des réalisations/phase suivante

55. Les objectifs du projet, du fait de sa nature d'urgence réhabilite les infrastructures et remet les
services en état sans nécessairement s'occuper des politiques sectorielles, des réformes institutionnelles et
sectorielles ni de la pérennisation des acquis. Compte tenu du délai assez long pendant lequel s'est
exécute le Projet il a pu être constaté un début de dégradation de certains ouvrages.
56. Le problème appréhendé par tous les intervenants, une réflexion et une sensibilisation au sein de tous
les acteurs était nécessaire. Un atelier de sensibilisation sur la pérennisation des acquis PMURR
réunissant les ministres ou vice-ministres, les dirigeants et les experts des divers ministères, entités
nationales, agences d'exécution concernées par le PMURR, a été organisé.
57. Compte tenu des disponibilités financières encore faibles de la RDC le support des bailleurs s'avère
encore nécessaire. La prise en compte de l'entretien des infrastructures réhabilités ou de la continuité des
actions dans le domaine social par des projets sectoriels reste une priorité. Les nombreux projets en cours
ou en préparation dans les secteurs des infrastructures, services ou secteurs sociaux gagent, avec les
efforts menés par le Gouvernement (par exemple concession de l'axe routiers Matadi ­ Kinshasa), de la
réussite du processus.
58. Dans le même temps le Gouvernement qui à déjà mis des mécanismes en place (fonds d'entretien
routier par exemple) devra veiller à une utilisation prioritaire et appropriée des ressources à l'entretien des
infrastructures réhabilitées ou construites par le PMURR.

78. Réalisation des objectifs et résultats

78.1. Atteinte des objectifs.

59. Le démarrage du projet a été lent pour la plupart des composantes. La conception et mise en place
très rapide du Projet (procédure des projets d'urgence de la BM ­ O.P. 8.50) n'a pas permis de procéder
aux études préliminaires à travers un préfinancement (PPF) dans une phase préparatoire, l'essentiel des
dossiers nécessaires à la réalisation des diverses opérations, bien que, certaines d'entre elles ont fait
l'objet d'un début d'exécution avec le don H05. Par exemple pour le volet A (infrastructures) les délais
pour mobiliser la structure du BCMI devant l'exécuter ont été très longs, pratiquement un an entre la mise
en vigueur de l'accord de Crédit et la mobilisation des personnels. La plupart du temps la préparation n'a
pu se faire qu'après la signature des accords de Crédit et la phase de préparation, notamment celle des
études et établissement des documents d'appel d'offres "DAO", a pris un retard conséquent. De plus,
l'assimilation de l'esprit d'urgence du Projet et la nécessité de relancer très vite l'économie n'a pas
toujours animé les équipes qui, dans bien des cas, ont continuées de gérer avec une réactivité semblable à
celle affichée pour des opérations se déroulant dans un environnement classique, mais non dans celui d'un
pays en situation post-conflit ou tout est à reconstruire.
60. Toutefois dans le cadre du développement de chaque composante on verra que les objectifs ont été
très largement atteints. En ce qui concerne les objectifs globaux, le Projet a grandement répondu aux
attentes.

Objectif (a) : Commencer à reconstruire la production agricole et améliorer la sécurité alimentaire

61. Cet objectif a largement été atteint avec les nombreuses actions de développement de la production
végétale et animale sur plusieurs saisons avec des actions pérennisées compte tenu de du report de la date
de clôture du Projet qui a permis de renforcer les actions sur plusieurs saisons supplémentaires. La
restauration de d'infrastructures de base (abattoir et laboratoire vétérinaire de Kinshasa) et la
réhabilitation de plus de 2 000 km de pistes rurales interconnectées au réseau principal (RN 1) y a très
fortement contribué.
Objectif (b) : La réhabilitation et la reconstruction des infrastructures essentielles

62. Cet objectif en ce qui concerne le projet a été globalement atteint compte tenu du nombre important
des infrastructures réhabilitées ou reconstruites dans tous les domaines et les impacts immédiats comme
peut en témoigner, entre autres (i) la continuité et le fonctionnement de la liaison multimodale (ouverture
de l'accès au port de Matadi, amélioration du port, reconstruction de la liaison routière Matadi Kinshasa)
entre l'océan et Kinshasa et la réhabilitation d'une grande partie de la liaison routière Kinshasa ­ Kikwit -
Lubumbashi en assurant notamment la continuité de l'itinéraire routier par la construction de deux grands
ponts sur les rivières Loange et Lovua ; (ii) plus que le doublement de la production en eau potable sur la
ville de Kinshasa ; (iii) La fiabilisation ou rétablissement de la production d'énergie électrique aussi bien
hydraulique que thermique, de la chaîne de transport ou de distribution de l'énergie électrique ; (iv) la
réhabilitation d'une liaison intra urbaine primordiale de Kinshasa et le traitement d'un site érosif
particulièrement important.

Objectif (c) : Rétablir les services sociaux et construire des infrastructures communautaires

63. Cet objectif, globalement, était déjà atteint par l'exécution même du volet social du projet. En effet,
ont été développés tous les mécanismes d'identification, de financement et de mise en oeuvre de projets,
essentiellement dans les volets développement communautaire ou protection sociale. Ces outils (manuels


                                                     87
de procédures, définition des politiques etc. ...) ont non seulement étés définis, puis adoptés par les
Ministères concernés, puis vulgarisés auprès des populations cibles mais surtout testés et utilisés et
confirmés à travers de nombreuses réalisations, attestant de la relance et la vivacité de ce secteur.

Objectif (d) : Renforcer la capacité du Gouvernement à formuler, mettre en oeuvre et gérer des
programmes de développement à moyen et long terme.

64. Cet objectif, globalement, a également été atteint en ce qui concerne le projet. En effet a travers (i)
un programme ambitieux de formation aussi bien des cadres que de beaucoup de personnels d'exécution,
de nombreux ministères et Entités nationales aussi bien que d'autres structures parapubliques ou
villageoises et associatives ; (ii) Une association permanente à travers une participation active de toutes
les parties prenantes au Projet (Ministères et Entités nationales) à sa gestion à travers les agences
d'exécution, et une formation dynamique par compagnonnage d'un grand nombre de personnels de ces
ministères au sein de toutes les instances d'exécution des diverses opérations (maîtrise d'ouvrage ou
maîtrise d'oeuvre) ; (iii) les études institutionnelles importantes élaborées dans nombre de domaines
(transport, énergie, routes, agriculture ...) ainsi que le développement de procédures et méthodologie
d'action dans beaucoup de domaines (social notamment), (iv) l'appui institutionnel très important
apportés à toutes les Entités et Ministères (immobilier, informatisation, matériel roulant etc. ...) ; c'est
tout un ensemble qui a permis de redéfinir et rendre opérationnel la quasi-totalité des secteurs de l'activité
économique et sociale du pays à travers toutes les parties prenantes du projet. Il en est comme illustration
le nombre maintenant important de projets sectoriels pris directement en charge par les Ministères ou
Entités responsables qui pérennisent les actions du PMURR.

70. La gestion complète du Projet, y compris la surveillance, la gestion environnementale (gestion et
opérations d'atténuation environnementales et sociales) et audits est d'environ 7 % du total des

des programmes de la sous-composante santé par les MOD n'a pas dépassé les 12 % comme il l'avait été
défini en début de Projet et celle de la gestion pour les projets communautaires n'a pas dépassé 10%
quand les processus se sont normalisés avec les ALEs.
71. Les deux agences d'exécution, malgré les difficultés de mise en oeuvre dans le contexte post-conflit
du Pays ont livré au Gouvernement et ses Agences, ainsi qu'à la société civile des réalisations et supports
conformes aux attentes suscitées par les documents descriptifs du projet.

79.1. Performances de la Banque.

        (a) qualité à l'évaluation.

Note : très satisfaisant.

76. La banque dés le processus de paix engagé a réagi très rapidement pour soutenir le Gouvernement
intérimaire dans ses efforts aux côtés des institutions des Nations unie du FMI et la communauté
internationale. Elle a été le moteur essentiel pour mobiliser les bailleurs, définir avec le Gouvernement un
Programme multi-bailleurs et multisectoriel pour la relance de la RDC et la faire émerger de sa situation
post-conflit.
77. Le PMURR premier Projet conséquent et basé sur la reconstruction (dans tous les sens du terme)
après de nombreuses années d'aide humanitaire focalisait toutes les attentes et était appréhendé comme
l'élément essentiel de la reprise des activités en RDC.
78. La Banque n'a pas déçu ces attentes en mobilisant très rapidement une équipe pluridisciplinaire pour
la conception du Projet (et du Programme). Pour la mission de pré-évaluation en décembre 2001 cette
équipe était composée de quatorze personnes. A la mission d'évaluation en février 2002 ce sont 18
personnes mobilisées (y compris les mêmes qu'à la mission précédente.
79. L'équipe a travaillée en étroite collaboration avec le Gouvernement et les Entités ce qui a permis de
très rapidement structurer le Projet (et le programme) et le faire très rapidement approuver par le conseil
d'administration de la banque.
80. Le Projet a bien été identifié, de façon cohérente et suivant une stratégie limitant les risques et
favorisant un impact maximum des investissements ­b développement des activités dans les zones sous
contrôle Gouvernemental (avec possibilité d'extension si le processus de paix évolue) avec comme axe
directeur la RN1 route structurante principale du pays.
81. La nécessité et l'urgence à rétablir les infrastructures et les services de bases dans un contexte
d'urgence ne permettaient pas d'aborder parallèlement les réformes institutionnelles et les questions de
maintenance des réalisations dans le cadre du Projet. Toutefois la prise en charge des études de réflexion
étaient pertinentes et ont apportés les outils nécessaires au Gouvernement pour préserver l'avenir (ex ;
l'étude et la mise en place du fonds d'entretien routier).
82. Les choix du montage institutionnels ont étés judicieux compte tenu de l'impossibilité qu'il y avait à
reconstituer des capacités d'experts nationaux dans le laps de temps très court entre l'évaluation et la
phase de mise en oeuvre, au risque de prendre un retard considérable dans l'exécution du projet ou une
dérive dans la mise en oeuvre.
83. Enfin, la répartition des fonds entre les diverses composantes a totalement répondu aux exigences
financières de chacune pour répondre au minimum des besoins recensés dans l'environnement général de
tous les bailleurs. La communication lors du groupe consultatif de Paris en 2003 confirme ce choix
puisque à cette date, sur le programme initial les financements étaient pratiquement assurés dans les
composantes sociales (sauf éducation) et à environ à 50 % pour les infrastructures.

de fonds dans l'économie du pays élément primordial à la relance.

des personnels ou d'implications aléatoires. Le dialogue avec le Gouvernement, le Comité de Pilotage et
la CAP et les agences d'exécution en charge de la mise en place en a été fortement facilité et a permis

86. Par ailleurs, sous cette impulsion, la réactivité de la Banque aux problèmes d'exécution ou aux
demandes d'ajustements formulés par le Gouvernement a été déterminante. Il en est pour exemple les
réponses aux demandes de financement complémentaires, de report des dates de clôture et aux
réallocations des fonds.
87. Cette réactivité a aussi été déterminante pour les ajustements mineurs sollicités par le Gouvernement

à des demandes de non objection (NO) pour la dévolution de certains contrats importants (Ex Sous-

coût des prestations.

93. Dans la quasi-totalité des cas il a su aplanir et apporter les éléments nécessaires à régler les
problèmes rencontrés lors de l'exécution de diverses opérations en mobilisant les autorités administratives
adéquates (gouvernorats etc. ...).

        (b) Agences d'exécution.

Note : satisfaisant.

94. Les agences d'exécution que ce soit BCECO ou BCMI ont chacune été confrontées à des problèmes
considérables dans la mise en place du Projet. Que ce soit l'étendue des zones parfois très difficile d'accès
ou étaient réalisées les opérations, le nombre considérables de dossiers à traiter (notamment BCECO dans
le domaine social), les problèmes inhérents à leurs nombreux interlocuteurs et leurs exigences, les
nombreuses défaillances des entreprises et plus généralement un environnement administratif et technique
national défaillant.
95. Dans cet environnement difficile elles ont réussi à répondre aux exigences du projet, à le mettre en
place dans son intégralité dans les règles fiduciaires et administratives de la Banque (les audits successifs
n'ont pas fait ressortir de problème spécifique).
96. Elles ont travaillées en parfaite collaboration avec le Comité de Pilotage d'une part et la Banque en
participant activement à toutes les missions de supervision, fournissant en temps voulus les informations
relatives à l'avancement du Projet (physique n financier). Elles ont répondu aux exigences des rapports
d'information mensuels trimestriels et annuels.
97. Enfin elles ont participé activement à la formation sur le tas de cadres des divers Ministères et
Entités nationales. Cet effort ayant très fortement contribué à la reconstruction des capacités dans ces
institutions maintenant en position de gérer elles mêmes les projets sectoriels à venir.

    (c) Performance générale de l'Emprunteur.

Note : satisfaisant

98. L'engagement du Gouvernement et tous ses Ministères et Entités et notamment le Ministère du Plan
et celui des Finances Maître d'ouvrage délégué de part les accords de Crédit, tout au long du Projet
(évaluation, exécution) a été un gage de réussite. Cet engagement a créé les conditions et le climat
nécessaire à la conduite d'une opération aussi importante et complexe dans les règles de bonne gestion
administrative et fiduciaire.

80. Enseignements retirés.



                                                     93
99. Deux étapes prépondérantes marquent la vie d'un Projet. Celle de la préparation et évaluation puis
celle de l'exécution. Les deux étapes sont traitées séparément bien que le même esprit les ait animé, à
savoir, répondre en urgence à une situation post-conflit et un environnement des plus dégradés dans tous
les domaines.
100. Beaucoup de commentaires peuvent être formulés quand aux dispositions et choix faits pour
chacune des composantes durant ces deux phases mais, est-on certain qu'ils ne sont pas influencés par les
dispositions et conditions de travail qui leur sont habituelles dans des Projets courants (notamment
sectoriels en situation sociale, politique et financière dite "normale").

    Phase évaluation et accord de prêt.

101. Le Projet étant initialement projeté pour être réalisé sur une période très courte coïncidant la plupart
du temps à la clôture du Projet (par exemple, les travaux des routes devaient être exécutés sur une période
de 21 mois cf. appendice 3 à l'annexe technique au MOP) les premiers travaux d'entretien des ouvrages
ne seraient donc pas nécessaires pendant la période d'exécution du Projet. C'est pourquoi la Banque en
discussion avec le Gouvernement a projeté les futurs besoins et fait les recommandations nécessaires et
définis les besoins à venir à mettre en place (cf. aide mémoire de la mission d'évaluation en février 2002
alinéa 21) pour assurer maintenir en l'état les sections de routes non traitées et assurer l'entretien de celles
réhabilitées. Malheureusement compte tenu des conditions d'exécution déjà évoquées le projet s'est
réalisé sur 7 années au lieu de 3 années initialement prévues couvrant alors le début de l'entretien des
premiers ouvrages remis.
102. En tirer la leçon qu'il y a nécessité de prévoir l'entretien à la phase de conception dans un tel projet
n'est certainement pas réaliste mais par contre il doit y avoir une synergie entre les différents Projets
étudiés par la Banque de façon que les projets sectoriels, en cours d'étude (ce qui a été le cas pour
l'entretien routier, l'électricité, la Santé, l'Education etc. ...) prennent en compte la maintenance
transitoire des ouvrages réhabilités.
103. La notion d'urgence à l'établissement du Projet imposée par les conditions au moment de son
évaluation fait perdre aux concepteurs qui veulent répondre à l'urgence la réalité de ce que pourront être
les délais réels à l'exécution. En effet (i) coté Banque les procédures sont accélérées (OP 8.50) pour la
conception et l'approbation des Projets, mais restent identiques au stade de l'exécution ce qui bien
souvent engendre des délais trop importants (exemple NO pour contrat sous-composante eau) ; et (ii) coté
agences d'exécution le traitement, les procédures et la réactivité ne répondent pas nécessairement à ces
exigences d'urgence.
104. Il faut en tirer la leçon d'anticiper sur ces contraintes et moins s'enfermer dans des délais trop exigus,
bien qu'ils puissent être un moyen incitatif à la mobilisation des divers intervenants.

    Phase exécution.

105. A l'exécution comme il l'a été abordé ci-dessus on a pu noter que les procédures d'exécution sont
restées les-mêmes que pour des Projets traditionnels. Cette approche à conduit a des accumulations de
perte de temps certains que ce soit par le traitement des dossiers coté Banque ou côté agences
d'exécution.
106. Des modalités devraient être prévues pour répondre à cette préoccupation ce pourrait être :
           Côté banque :
       -     Imposer des règles aux sectoriels et leur donner des délais dans le traitement des dossiers
             (notamment aux demandes de NO)
       -     Eviter le remplacement trop fréquent des responsables (sectoriels notamment).

                                                      94
       -     Eviter les interférences de responsables divers de la Banque.
           Côté Agences d'exécution.
       -    Dans le cas du recrutement d'une firme internationale, systématiquement prévoir des contrats
            une partie de rémunération à la performance et à la responsabilité. La rémunération au seul
            temps passé est non adaptée et aberrante dans ce contexte.
-      Les structures Gouvernementales si leur fonctionnement n'est pas prévu d'être assuré dans le cadre
       de la contrepartie gouvernementale doivent avoir une base de rémunération au pourcentage
       (modulé) des fonds gérés.

                1.4.3. Facteurs majeurs spécifiques dans l'exécution de la sous composante routes
                       principales

        21. Deux opérations ont été marquées par des faits exceptionnels.
            -   Les lots 6 & 7 Batshamba ­ rivière Loange ­ Tshikapa avec la construction des deux
                ouvrages les plus importants du Projet sur les rivières Loange (440 ml) et Lovua (80 ml).
                Après constat de l'incapacité de l'entreprise titulaire des contrats à réaliser les travaux, la
                résiliation a été prononcée. Un nouveau contrat a été négocié avec l'entreprise classée
                2ème au premier AOI (Appel d'offre international) compte tenu que les délais n'étaient
                plus suffisants pour relancer une consultation. La dernière demande de report de la date
                de clôture a pratiquement était dictée par les délais nécessaire à finir cette opération. Les
                travaux ont étés achevés dans les temps.
            -   Le lot 9 Lubumbashi ­ Likassi ­ Nguba. L'Entreprise a abandonné le chantier fin août
                2008 car elle contestait un redressement financier suite à une surfacturation et
                l'application des pénalités de retard dûment constatées, en laissant une section non
                terminée d'environ 8 km sur un itinéraire de 122km. Le refus de l'entreprise de reprendre
                les travaux a entrainé la résiliation du marché. Compte tenu des délais restants avant la
                date de clôture il n'a pas été possible de procéder (soit par négociation directe, soit par
                AOI) à la signature d'un nouveau contrat. Les travaux seront complétés en dehors du
                Projet.
        22. Ces chantiers d'importance, ont nécessité la mobilisation d'entreprises, soit internationales
        qui n'avaient pas de base dans le pays compte tenu de la période de conflit, soit nationales, ayant
        en théorie les capacités de répondre aux exigences de tels contrats, mais qui avaient perdues la
        plupart de leur potentiel et savoir faire suite à la très longue période d'inactivité due au conflit.
        23. Dans les deux cas les temps de réaction ou de restructuration ont été longs, les dispositions à
        prendre décidées ou appliquées trop tardivement.
        24. Les retards constatés sont généralement supérieurs à un an pour un délai d'exécution moyen
        de l'ordre de 2 ans. Ils peuvent être imputables à plusieurs facteurs et notamment (i) la nécessité
        de mettre en place des bases vies pour les chantiers de l'intérieur du pays, (ii) procéder à
        l'élaboration des plans d'exécution définitifs par les entreprises alors que les ouvrages s'étaient
        fortement détériorés entre la période d'évaluation et celle de réalisation ; (iii) difficultés dans
        l'acheminement des matériels et matériaux par des entreprises pas assez organisées compte tenu
        du contexte.

groupes extrêmement vulnérables, et à aider les personnes vulnérables à travers des projets
communautaires.
76. Ces objectifs ont étés complètement atteints à travers la mise à disposition du Ministère des
affaires sociales du Document de politique et stratégie nationale de protection sociale des groupes
vulnérables en RDC pour le volet institutionnel et le financement et exécution de Projets dans 11
provinces.
        2.5.2. La composante

77. Les Projets limités à 25000 USD chacun répondent aux besoins des groupes vulnérables. Ils
sont identifiés et réalisés avec leur active participation à travers les organismes d'encadrement
des bénéficiaires (OEBs) qui signent des conventions de maîtrise d'ouvrage délégué (MOD) avec
le BCECO agence d'exécution.
78. La sous-composante comme toutes celles de volet santé cantonnée à l'origine aux zones sous
contrôle du Gouvernement à son évaluation a été étendu aux Provinces de l'Est après la revue à
mi-parcours et la mise en place du 1er financement additionnel.
79. Cette sous-composante s'est soldée par :
    -   Le financement de 383 sous projets ayant permis l'assistance d'environ 77 000 personnes
        vulnérables.



                                               135
    -   Le renforcement des capacités de la Direction des études et planification (DEP) du
        Ministère des affaires sociales en charge de ce domaine n(véhicules, ordinateurs,
        photocopieuses, connexion internet).
    -   La formation des experts du Ministère des affaires sociales.

        2.5.3. Facteurs majeurs spécifiques dans l'exécution de la sous composante
               protection sociale.

80. Les difficultés rencontrée compte tenu de la non maîtrise de la gestion des projets dans les
normes imposées à nécessité une implication forte de l'agence d'exécution (BCECO)

    2.6. Le Développement communautaire.




        2.6.1. Objectifs spécifiques à la sous composante Le Développement communautaire.

81. Ils sont définis à l'annexe technique au MOP, et sont de renforcer la capacité des
collectivités à améliorer la qualité et la disponibilité des services de base, et d'augmenter
rapidement les infrastructures collectives, la qualité et la disponibilité des services de base dans
tout le pays.
82. Objectifs atteints a travers la définition d'une politique d'intervention en ce domaine (mise à
disposition du Ministère du développement rural du Document de politique et stratégie nationale
du développement communautaire) et en l'appliquant à la réalisation d'environ 955 projets dans
11 provinces et pratiquement tous les domaines d'activités communautaires.

                                                136
          2.6.2. La composante

83. Les Projets au montant plafonné à 25 000 USD répondent aux besoins des communautés
initiatrices des opérations. Ils sont identifiés et réalisés avec leur active participation à travers les
agences locales d'exécution (ALEs), qui sont pour la plupart des ONGs reconnues pour leur
savoir faire et leur sérieux, qui signent des conventions de maîtrise d'ouvrage délégué (MOD)
avec le BCECO agence d'exécution. L'ensemble étant exécuté avec l'étroite participation du
Ministère du développement rural.
84. La sous-composante comme toutes celles de volet santé ou encore protection sociale
cantonnée à l'origine aux zones sous contrôle du Gouvernement à son évaluation a été étendu aux
Provinces de l'Est après la revue à mi-parcours et la mise en place du 1er financement additionnel.
85. Cette sous-composante s'est soldée par :
     -    La réalisation de 955 sous projets dans des domaines aussi variés que l'éducation, la
          santé, la fourniture d'eau, le transport, les marchés et abattoirs, l'environnement et
          l'énergie ont permis d'aider environ 43 000 élèves, à porter un soutient à la santé à près
          de 1 750 000 personnes, l'au à 230 000 personnes, refaire 23 ponts, installer près de
          2 700 vendeurs sur les marchés, etc. ...
     -    Le renforcement des capacités de la Direction communautaire du du Ministère du
          développement rural en charge de ce domaine (véhicules, ordinateurs, réhabilitation du
          bâtiment des bureaux).
     -    La formation des cadres en statistiques communautaires.

          2.6.3. Facteurs majeurs spécifiques dans l'exécution de la sous composante Le
                 Développement communautaire.

86. Quelques difficultés ont été rencontrées dans l'exécution des projets :
     -    Ceux liées à l'absence de maîtrise de gestion des projets par les ALEs.
     -    La difficulté de réaliser les missions de supervision par BCECO compte tenu de la
          multiplicité des opérations pour permettre l'accélération des paiements des acomptes des
          factures successives introduites par les Ales et ne pas les mettre en difficulté.
87. Pour palier à ces difficultés BCECO à procédé à des ateliers de vulgarisation du document
de stratégie et des séminaires de formation, spécialement auprès des AlEs pour les renforcer dans
leur gestion et les sensibiliser aux exigences des procédures (notamment fiduciaires) du bailleur
de fonds.

3.       Composante C ­ développement des stratégies sectorielles, réformes
         institutionnelles et renforcement des capacités et des institutions.

88. Cette composante avait pour but (i) la préparation des stratégies de développement pour les
secteurs les plus importants de la RDC, agriculture, électricité, santé, transport, l'eau,
l'assainissement, la protection sociale ; (ii) le renforcement des capacités humaines et
institutionnelles pour travailler avec succès au sein du système réformé ; et (iii). Réaliser les
principales études et les activités préparatoires pour les futurs programmes d'investissement.
89. Toutes ces activités ont été accomplies, mais pas totalement, en imputant les dépenses à cette
composante mais aux composantes A ­ infrastructures ­ et B agriculture, social et développement
communautaire -. En effet ces deux composantes ont assurés la majeure partie des études
institutionnelles et préparatoires aux nouvelles activités (cf. § A1 & B1). Il en est de même avec

                                                  137
le programme de formation dont l'identification des financements propres a été réduit suivant les
réallocations compte tenu qu'une grande part de celle-ci a été effectuée au sein des deux agences
d'exécution, de la firme environnementale et au sein de certains contrats (formations spécifiques).
90. La formation a été (i) 561 cadres et agents des ministères et entités formés localement et à
l'étranger à des formations basiques et pointues ; (ii) prés de 300 hommes/an formés en
compagnonnage ; (iii) 1 900 hommes/jour en formation dans le cadre des contrats des firmes
infrastructures et environnement ; (iv) 620 hommes/jour en voyages d'étude et séminaires
internationaux et (v) 720 stages de formations diverses.

4.       Composante D ­ gestion, suivi et évaluation de la mise en place du programme
         et du projet IDA.

91. Cette composante fà financé la gestion du Projet, le suivi, l'expertise, le service de
consultants, les coûts de fonctionnement, le soutient logistique et équipements.
92. Les hypothèses de départ pour cette composante étaient :
     -    Exécution du Projet sur une période de 3 années.
     -    Un investissement de base à mettre en place d'environ 450 millions USD,
93. Elle s'est soldé par :
     -    Exécution sur une période de pratiquement 7 années (plus que doublé)
     -    Un investissement réellement mis en place de prés de 700 millions USD compte tenu des
          financements complémentaires et des gains de change.
94. Ces deux facteurs expliquent l'augmentation substantielle de cette composante (cf. § 3.2
"introduction" alinéa 57).

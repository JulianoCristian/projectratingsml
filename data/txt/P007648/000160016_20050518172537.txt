3. Assessment of Development Objective and Design, and of Quality at Entry

3.1 Original Objective:

The project's main objectives were to: (i) improve the quality and efficiency of urban transport systems in
medium-size cities in Mexico; (ii) assist the Federal Government in decentralizing responsibility for urban
transport management; (iii) provide financing for urban transport improvements, and (iv) help to control
the environmental impacts of urban transport operations in such cities.

3.2 Revised Objective:

Project objectives were not revised during implementation. However, the Government decided to restructure
the project in 1997 due to slow implementation, requesting the cancellation of US$23 million of the Local
Component (defined below). The loan was amended several times to make the project more responsive to
current conditions, especially in terms of addressing the transportation requirements of low and middle
income communities living in the peripheral zones.

3.3 Original Components:

1. National Component. This component was designed to enhance the role of the Federal Government in
providing policy and technical guidance to the municipalities, through: (a) strengthening of SEDESOL's
institutional capacity to appraise, supervise, monitor, and evaluate subprojects; (b) strengthening
BANOBRAS´ institutional capacity to administer subloans; (c) training of federal, state and local public
sector staff to increase their capacity regarding urban transport, traffic planning, project evaluation and
execution, and traffic operations and maintenance through intensive four month post-graduate level
courses; and (d) assistance towards the preparation of Integral Transport Plans (ITPs) in the participating
municipalities.

2. Local component. This component included the provision of a credit line to medium-size cities for urban
transport improvement subprojects, in one of the following interrelated activities: (a) the studies needed to
prepare the ITPs; (b) immediate actions, if the delay in waiting for the completion of the ITP would result
in higher costs and/or losses of significant benefits; and (c) the implementation of the physical and
institutional responsibilities for urban transport activities, civil works, traffic management and
environmental control measures, further studies, training, and final design of civil works and equipment
specification.

The design of the project was relatively innovative, and somewhat program based, as the contents of its
components were not fully pre-set at loan approval. Instead, under the National component, candidate
cities had to prepare an ITP which contained adequately screened investment proposals and institutional
strengthening programs that focused on addressing both transport supply and demand issues in an integral
manner. In this way, the project would help to mainstream planning and analytical procedures that were
new to the candidate cities. The cities would then request sub-loans for financing parts or all of the ITP
recommendations under the project's Local component. This cycle was to be introduced and followed in
eligible cities until all of the Bank's loan proceeds assigned to the Local component had been on lent by the
expected closing date.

Hence the project's design permitted the parallel or sequential financing of improvements in different cities,
entering the program at varying times, but all being guided by the same integral planning framework (ITP).
As explained later in this report, the ITP and project cycle procedures were followed but all of the funds
were not disbursed due inter-alia to the major administrative, political, and economic changes that Mexico
experienced from the mid-1990's onward. Ironically, this medium cities quasi-programmatic framework



                                                       - 2 -

could ideally be applied now that interest rates are low, and local agencies have gained significant capacity
and want to improve transport services in their cities, especially via the provision of high capacity bus
systems.

3.4 Revised Components:

The project's local component was revised to include the paving of streets in poor neighborhoods and the
replacement of buses.

3.5 Quality at Entry:

The project predates the introduction of the QAG process at the Bank, hence no official quality at entry
data are available. Nonetheless, the evidence available suggests that the quality at entry can be rated
marginally satisfactory.

The project was consistent with the main objectives identified in the Bank's Urban Development Strategy,
as outlined in the Decentralization and Urban Management Study, July 1991, which supported the policy of
progressive and functional devolution of responsibilities to municipal governments as well as the
improvement in quality and efficiency of urban transport systems in medium-size cities. The project was
designed to provide financial assistance, in the form of Federal grants and access to loans through
BANOBRAS, to states and municipalities meeting subproject loan eligibility criteria. The project was
aimed originally at about a dozen medium-sized cities.

While the project had been well-prepared and had adequate information at appraisal, a shortcoming was the
overestimation of the demand from candidate cities for sub loans for transport infrastructure investments.
Sub-project preparation for the first three cities, based on proposed ITPs, indicated at appraisal that the
short-term investments were of the order of US$40 million to US$50 million per city. The Bank team
assumed that if all of the cities were to participate in the Program at this level of infrastructure investments,
the total program cost would exceed US$2 billion. However, this was a somewhat unrealistic borrowing
expectation level for the municipalities. During implementation, the economic crisis led to very high
on-lending rates, reaching 30% at times, and caused the demand for borrowing to be lower than expected;
thus subproject loans were of a smaller nature, ranging from US$1 million to US$5 million. In addition, the
completion of an ITP usually provided cities with lower cost alternatives, such as better traffic
management, for meeting transport demand and they were therefore able to postpone larger investment
programs. As a result, the project disbursed only US$33 million by 1998 and accordingly a portion of the
loan proceeds was cancelled.

On hindsight, a program of US$100 million would have been appropriate given the changes in investment
strategy fostered by ITPs, the economic crises, the advent of decentralization and the size of the typical
candidate city.

It is important to mention that the key concept of the project, i.e the preparation of the ITPs to help
municipalities determine investment priorities was widely accepted and was adopted by the GOM for its
own overall program (PCC) for developing and implementing urban transport improvements in 146
municipalities.

4. Achievement of Objective and Outputs

4.1 Outcome/achievement of objective:

(a) Improve the quality and efficiency of urban transport systems in medium-size cities in Mexico.
Although the loan did not fully disburse as envisaged, the project met this objective satisfactorily by
improving the ability of the state and municipal local agencies to plan urban transport subprojects through
the development of ITPs. This was the first time in Mexico that the cities developed integrated short,
medium and long term investment plans and that were tailored to their improvement needs. These plans
proved to be very successful and were aimed to improve traffic management, public transport, traffic flow,
road security and access to transport services to low income areas and to reduce congestion. These integral
plans provided the basis for the 54 sub-loans that were approved during the life of the project. There were
only 2 sub-loans granted to States. Since sub-loans for infrastructure improvements were focused mainly
on existing key corridors in the cities, setting up bus priority lanes, traffic safety, concessioning or
restructuring bus routes, acquisition of buses, etc., it can be stated that the project improved the quality and
efficiency of urban public transport operations, which is by far the dominant mode of transport in Mexican
cities. Because of the combination of lower cost options identified by ITP and the then prevailing high
interest rates, the project´s civil works were implemented on a smaller scale than envisaged at appraisal
and, in some cases, the cities carried out some of the ITP recommended investments on their own.

In all, civil works were carried out in 15 medium-size cities during project implementation, and covered the
following: (i) road rehabilitation and repaving; (ii) road maintenance, including the establishment of a
pavement management system; (iii) improvement of ring roads and key corridors in the cities; (iv)
improvement of bike trails; (v) pavement and rehabilitation of roads in low income areas, and (vi)
improvement of road intersections and crossings. Subproject loans for works also supported the
rationalization of existing bus routes that improved efficiency by providing wider coverage using fewer
overlapping routes. It is worthwhile mentioning that subprojects in some of the cities have led to improved
road safety conditions especially for pedestrians via the provision of road crossing bridges and better
signage. In general, these activities have improved the level of service and time savings to users of the
urban transport systems in the cities.

It is important to mention that the ITP provided feasible, phased, lower cost improvement options for the
municipalities. Being presented with rational choices during a difficult macro-climate, the municipalities
chose to finance on average from the Bank´s loan only about 5% of the value of ITP recommendations.
This contrasts strongly with the borrowing assumptions at appraisal. The municipalities delayed, on one
hand, the larger, more costly, investments and, on the other hand, implemented short-term priority
investments with their own budget and took advantage of the transport management options provided by the
ITP. The municipalities similarly leveraged the technical assistance and training inputs provided under the
project.

(b) Assist the Federal Government in decentralizing responsibility for urban transport management. The
project achieved this objective through the strengthening of federal agencies such as SEDESOL, as well as
the capacity of states and municipalities to plan, implement, and manage urban transport projects. It is
important to mention that local capacity, at the municipal level, was very limited at the start of the project
due to the central government responsibility for urban transport. SEDESOL prepared a series of eight
technical manuals ­ now widely used in other Latin-American countries- to guide municipalities and their
consultants in their preparation of ITPs. Before committing to major investments, each municipality
produced a coherent integrated transport plan as well as a definition of the organizational set up for
managing the urban transport sector within the city. Moreover, with the completion of the ITPs, a few of
the municipalities created semi autonomous "transport institutes" with funding from city budgets, thereby


                                                       - 4 -

providing greater project institutional decentralization impact and financial sustainability. Another
important factor was the up-front training of a cadre of core staff of state and municipal agencies, followed
by the training offered to administrative staff during project implementation. This facilitated the
decentralization agenda and the implementation of project components in each city.

In general, with institutional reforms and integrated plans together with project investments, it can be said
that the project has promoted the transfer of operational and managerial responsibilities for urban transport
and infrastructure provision and maintenance from federal to state or municipal government in a greater
number of cities than the twelve envisaged at appraisal.

(c) Provide financing for urban transport improvements. This objective was achieved satisfactorily. At
the start-up and during the first three years of execution, the project had a difficult transition from the
planning phase to the execution phase of infrastructure investments. This slow implementation occurred
mainly due to (i) SEDESOL's budget reduction prevented the timely provision of its 25% contribution to
the preparation of ITPs; and (ii) municipalities' lack of borrowing capacity in the face of high interest rates
and debt ceilings at that time.

The financing of the infrastructure improvements were basically driven by demand from the cities. The
ITPs were used as a planning tool by the states and municipalities, and included a methodology for
economic evaluation of subproject investments. The subproject loans were usually aimed at financing
low-cost solutions to municipal transportation needs, as identified and justified by the respective ITPs.

(d) Control the environmental impact of urban transport operations. This objective was achieved
satisfactorily. For example, in larger urban centers, the emphasis was placed on reducing vehicle based
emissions and/or the implementation of bus fleet renovation (average fleet age > 15 years) and the
reduction of traffic congestion due to the implementation of more efficient traffic circulation arising from
the improved supply and demand management measures. While for settlements on a city periphery, the
paving of streets in many low-income areas contributed to reducing environmental impacts through the
reduction of suspended dust clouds along roadways; this was a major problem particularly in the northern
desert states. Other activities to control environmental impacts included the construction of sound barriers
alongside major highways so as to reduce noise pollution.

4.2 Outputs by components:
Overall implementation of the two project components is rated satisfactory. The National Component
accomplished satisfactorily its outputs, through strengthening SEDESOL and BANOBRAS as well as
developing 42 ITPs with the beneficiary municipalities. With respect to the Local Component, the outputs
achieved were less than expected due to municipalities' limited capacity to borrow because of the
combination of the Tequila crisis and high interest rates, all of which caused significant delays during the
first three to four-year period of project implementation. To overcome this situation, the Bank was very
proactive in modifying procedures and adding investments activities such as the subcomponent for street
paving in poor neighborhoods, among others. A detailed description of the results achieved under each
component is listed below.

1. National Component. The implementation of this component is rated satisfactory and consisted of the
following subcomponents: (i) Strengthening of SEDESOL, (ii) Strengthening of BANOBRAS, (iii)
implementing a National Training Program, and (iv) formulating Integral Transport Plans.

(i) Strengthening of SEDESOL. The project financed technical assistance to the Secretariat of Social
Development (SEDESOL) to strengthen its institutional capacity in urban transport policy formulation in



                                                     - 5 -

order to provide adequate technical assistance to the states and municipalities for the formulation of their
urban transport strategies. This process was undertaken during the initial years of project implementation.
To achieve this, the project provided training to SEDESOL's own staff with outside consultants and the
hiring of additional local professional staff. In addition, the project assisted SEDESOL in the preparation
of eight technical manuals to help guide municipalities and states in the preparation of their ITPs. An
international consultant firm was hired to develop the manuals and to provide training to staff and local
consultants.

(ii) Strengthening of BANOBRAS. Through the First Urban Transport Project (Loan 2824-ME), the
National Development Bank for Public Works and Services (BANOBRAS) gained solid experience in the
supervision of urban transport loans, with emphasis on planning and regional infrastructure solutions.
Based on this, for the project, BANOBRAS responsibilities were for loan administration, procurement
supervision and financial monitoring of credit lines to municipalities and states. In this respect, the project
provided training for BANOBRAS staff in urban transport finance and administration, and Bank
procurement procedures. It is estimated that 80 person/months of training during the initial years of the
project were provided. The technical performance of BANOBRAS staff was adequate throughout the
project implementation period.

(iii) Implementation of a National Training Program. Staff training was provided to the national agencies
and the municipalities through: (i) introductory two-day workshops for mayors and senior administrators
which was achieved successfully; (ii) two four-month full-time courses on transport planning, traffic
engineering, public transport, and transport economics- with more than 130 participants. This was a very
useful training program given the weak local capacity of municipalities' staff; (iii) updating courses and
regional seminars, which were provided upon demand from local agencies; and (iv) overseas post-graduate
training for selected participants. Also, there was a large annual training program in all aspects of urban
transport that was carried out in all participating cities. Training was also delivered via video conferences.

(iv) Integral Transport Plans (ITPs). This subcomponent introduced for the first time a more rational
urban transport planning approach for municipalities. It was a requirement for participating in the project,
through SEDESOL financing or sub-loan agreements between BANOBRAS and each participant
municipality. As mentioned earlier, 42 ITPs were prepared which compares well with the approximately 15
that were expected at appraisal. This strategy was very useful not only for the beneficiaries but also for
rationalizing urban transport investment demands of the cities.

2. Local Component. This component financed primarily the implementation of the investment and
institutional recommendations of the ITPs.

Initially, financing under the local component was requested mostly for road rehabilitation, traffic
management and maintenance, institutional strengthening, and environmental impact studies. However, the
project encountered many difficulties from the mid-1990's onward. Among the difficulties were: (i) the peso
financial crisis of 1995-99, accompanied by very high on lending rates that were aligned with the Mexican
CETES (a proxy for a long term bond). These high interest rates made loans appear onerous for participant
municipalities and helped to limit scope for project financed investments; (ii) difficulties in managing the
procurement cycle; and (iii) the frequent changes in municipal administrations. To overcome this situation,
during the mid-term review, the project was partially restructured to provide more emphasis on supporting
of public transportation improvements and on road paving in poor neighborhoods. As a result, there were
about 27 municipalities that implemented a part of their ITPs with project financing, including the
extensive programs developed in Leon and Ciudad Juarez. A brief description of the achievements in three
cities follows.


                                                       - 6 -

Leon Subproject: Leon municipality has implemented: (i) civil works on 38 corridors, (ii) civil works on 55
access roads to low income areas, (iii) 113 road maintenance subprojects, (iv) construction of 25 km of
bike trails, (v) improvement of 37 road intersections, (vi) installation of a traffic control system (ATC), and
(vii) introduction of bus-priority lanes. Achievements in institutional strengthening were also impressive
due to the implementation of an extensive institutional training program and to the establishment of a
Transport Planning Institute. The city of Leon has definitely been able to develop a transport system that
can meet the demands of a rapidly growing city (20 percent population increase in the last 5 years) and that
is functioning well. The city's capacity has developed to the extent that it has recently managed the
successful introduction of the first phase of a Bus Rapid Transit System.

Puebla Subproject: The implementation of ITP recommendations were only partially achieved mainly due
to the frequent changes in administration of the four key municipalities involved. Some of the physical
components planned were the development of a bus way scheme, including plans to rationalize the bus
transport systems. It was not completed due partly also to the lack of a political consensus for the
scrapping of existing, inefficient mini-buses.

Monterrey Subproject: As part of the project, the following activities were implemented: (i) a
comprehensive traffic control system (ATC), namely the Sistema Integral del Tránsito Metropolitano del
Área Metropolitana de Monterrey (SINTRAM); (ii) rehabilitation of exclusive bus lanes on arterials; (iii)
road maintenance; and (iii) institutional strengthening program. The urban transport system has been
improved considerably in Monterrey.

4.3 Net Present Value/Economic rate of return:

A cost-benefit analysis was used to screen all eligible infrastructure subprojects, and a 12 percent minimum
economic rate of return was required from subprojects with quantifiable benefits that were to be included in
the ITP. Allocations for operation and maintenance activities were also required for subprojects to be
approved.

During the preparation of the ICR, SEDESOL decided to embark on a comprehensive ex-post evaluation of
the Project as a whole. In order to perform such a task, the Bank team reviewed and commented on the
terms of reference and requested an extension of the presentation of the ICR. The justification for the
extension was based on the expectation that the ICR could be enhanced with the findings and
recommendations of the SEDESOL study report. However, as of the date of the presentation of this ICR,
the consultant's report was not completed and thus, the ICR could not benefit fully from the SEDESOL
study.   Hence it is not possible to specify precisely the ex-post rates of return, but by design the
pre-screening of sub-projects for ITP purposes helped to assure their viability. Given the reportedly higher
than expected urban traffic growth, the rates of return probably are at least equal to the appraisal estimate
(35%).

4.4 Financial rate of return:

Not applicable.

4.5 Institutional development impact:

At the initiation of this project, there was no rational planning framework available to secondary cities. The
project therefore provided substantial contributions to institutional development in the urban transport
sector in Mexico in about 27 municipalities. In particular, the project assisted: (i) SEDESOL by improving
its capacity to design and evaluate ITPs from technical, environmental, economic and financial feasibility


                                                      - 7 -

points of view. This was accomplished by training its staff and providing a series of technical manuals to
guide states and municipalities and their consultants in the preparation of the ITPs; (ii) BANOBRAS by the
lessons learned about financing urban transport improvements in a decentralizing environment and on Bank
procurement procedures. Both SEDESOL and BANOBRAS staff took part in National Training Programs;
and (iii) States and Municipalities by supporting the introduction of both transport supply and demand
manangement via the ITPs, as well as the creation of urban transport institutes and by strengthening
directorates or agencies. In particular, the project served to increase substantially the technical capacity of
the cities in the following areas: urban transport planning, traffic management, institutional reforms,
maintenance of existing infrastructure, and cost recovery mechanisms. It is important to mention that this
technical capacity was very limited at the start of the project in most of Mexico's medium-size cities and
local agencies because of the previously centralized administration of the sector. Hence, the project helped
in implementing the federal decentralization agenda and to improve the operational efficiency of urban
transport systems. Therefore, the institutional impact on the participating institutions was substantial.

5. Major Factors Affecting Implementation and Outcome

5.1 Factors outside the control of government or implementing agency:
Deteriorating macroeconomic situation contributing to high interest rates and shortage of local
counterpart funds. The peso crisis of 1995 made even the blended credit/grant financing too costly for
municipalities to participate. This caused slow disbursements, hence, as of July 1998, only 21% of the loan
amount had been disbursed. Also, the difficult economic climate prevailing after the crisis generated a
shortage and untimely provision of counterpart funds to SEDESOL, BANOBRAS and to participating
municipalities, which led to reductions in implementation rates.

Changeover in government administration. Over the life of the project, several administration changes
occurred at the national and local levels, which had adversely affected continuity and resulted in delays in
project implementation.

5.2 Factors generally subject to government control:

Untimely provision of federal counterpart funds. Problems with federal counterpart funding adversely
affected performance throughout the implementation of the project. The untimely release of counterpart
funds compounded by the insufficient amount was a factor that significantly affected the implementation of
the federal component and civil works investments. The transition to a decentralized sector management
proved also to be disruptive in the intermediate term due in part to the need for all parties, federal and local,
to adjust to the new management, planning, and budgeting procedures.

Weak financial and institutional capacity to execute the Program at the municipal level. The
responsibility for urban transport management was transferred during the project to agencies at the
municipal level as decentralization proceeded. However, the weak financial and institutional capacity at the
municipal level, represented a further source of delays in project implementation. There was also a lack of
borrowing capacity in some of the municipalities, which became even more restrictive due to the high
interest rates prevailing at that time. It is important to mention that the original project organizational
design was focused on SEDESOL and BANOBRAS, i.e. the bidding process for civil works were, in
general, performed by BANOBRAS. With the onset of decentralization some of these responsibilities had
to be shifted to municipalities that in many cases were unprepared. However, the project was instrumental
in helping them to adjust to the new reality.

5.3 Factors generally subject to implementing agency control:

BANOBRAS as financial intermediary. BANOBRAS as financial intermediary performed well, although


                                                      - 8 -

it did not have much choice in setting the on-lending rate. The CETES-based financing mechanism was set
up between the Bank and the Secretaría de Hacienda y Crédito Público (SHCP). Hence, with the resultant
high on-lending rates the municipalities partially financed their programs on their own (albeit on a smaller
scale), thus reducing demand for subloans.

5.4 Costs and financing:

As of December 2003, the total cost of the Program implemented with project financing amounted to the
equivalent of US$154.44 million, which is 33 percent lower than the appraisal estimate of US$471.1
million. It was financed by the Bank´s loan (US$102.91 million) while the federal and state governments'
counterpart contributions summed to US$51.53 million. Project loan funds were cancelled in the amount of
US$97.09 million.

Project costs details are given in Annexes 2a (Project Costs by Component), 2b (Project Costs by
Procurement Arrangements) and 2c (Project Financing by Component).


6. Sustainability

6.1 Rationale for sustainability rating:

Sustainability of the project is rated as likely. The institutional support provided through the ITPs in this
project, together with the experience gained from the successful implementation of infrastructure
investments in the medium-size cities, has led to policy changes in the urban transport sector in each city,
which included a heightened emphasis on the provision of public transport, and the creation of coordinating
agencies or strengthening directorates at the municipal level to implement urban transport policies. Given
the prevailing more favorable economic climate, cities are carrying out second rounds of the integral studies
and in some cases implementing further phases of urban transport development, e.g. the new Leon Busway.
The project, therefore, essentially launched cities or municipalities on a more sustainable transport
improvement path.

SEDESOL has launched a comprehensive ex-post evaluation study of the program in order to obtain more
detailed recommendations and lessons learned from this project. The results of this study may prove useful
to all participants in helping to ensure the sustainability of the advances obtained under this project and
also contribute possibly to the design of future initiatives.

6.2 Transition arrangement to regular operations:

No substantial transition arrangements are needed since the project focused on developing the capacity of
staff at local and national levels, who are still largely in place and performing their functions. Still it would
be advisable to: (i) assign a project team on a full time basis within SEDESOL to continue with the federal
advisory and leadership role ; (ii) continue the institutional strengthening progress at the municipal and
state levels; (iii) continue with the technical staff training program for local agencies to develop their
capacity to plan, implement and manage urban transport projects in other cities not covered under the
project. In addition, SEDESOL should also help municipalities to develop key measurable quality and
efficiency indicators and encourage their use by implementing agencies at local governments.

7. Bank and Borrower Performance

Bank
7.1 Lending:

The Bank's performance during the project preparation stage is rated satisfactory. The Bank responded
when the GOM requested support to improve the urban transport services in the medium-size cities of
Mexico because inter-aliathe GOM wanted to provide attractive alternatives so as to reduce growth
concentration along the Mexico city-Puebla axis. The SAR for the project covered most of the sector
issues and incorporated the lessons learned from previous projects executed in Mexico. In particular, the
analysis of the relevant "lessons learned" was undertaken based on fifteen Project Completion Reports
(PCRs) in the urban sector worldwide, addressing a broad range of issues in an integral framework. The
SAR had identified the risks of counterpart funding at Federal and municipal level though not to the degree
of severity that occurred during implementation.

7.2 Supervision:

As mentioned earlier, the project suffered major startup delays. The supervision of the Bank can be
characterized by two phases: the first phase can be rated as unsatisfactory while the second phase, starting
since 1998, rated as satisfactory.         The first phase was characterized by Bank waiting for the
macroeconomic climate in Mexico to improve. While in the second phase, the Bank was more pro-active,
adjusting and restructuring the project in order to achieve objectives. The QAG review had commended the
Bank team for this.

The second phase started at mid 1998 when the project status was changed to satisfactory performance due
to its restructuring and increased disbursements. The project was subjected to two QAG reviews of
supervision because of its size and importance. The QAG complimented the Project's supervision effort for
timely identification of implementation problems, regard for development impact, advice and solutions
proposed to the borrower, and appropriateness and speed of the Bank follow-up. The QAG also stated that
the improved Project performance was due to a number of factors, among them a better promotion of the
project´s benefits to the cities, proactive supervision, and the readiness to modify procedures where possible
to meet and serve client needs.

On average, the project was supervised twice a year and implementation progress and ratings were
adequately reported. During this period, the project was amended several times to respond to the needs of
the borrower. There were three task managers during this phase. The bank conducted 17 supervision
missions and the project supervision took a total of 146.7 staff weeks.

The original loan closing date of June 30, 2000 was extended three times. The first two-year extension to
June 30, 2002 was granted in order to permit: (i) the conclusion of urban transport investment subprojects
in cities already in the program; (ii) the participation of new cities not yet in the urban transport
modernization program; (iii) the paving of roads in low-income communities, for urban poverty alleviation
goals; (iv) the completion of ITPs; (v) the strengthening of the planning departments of the participating
municipalities; and (vi) the provision of technical assistance and financing for the modernization of public
transport systems. The second extension to December 31, 2002 was given at the Government's request in
order to ensure that improvement in urban transport needs continue to be addressed in major cities. Finally,
the loan closing date was extended to allow disbursements of the National Component (Part A) until
December 31, 2003.

8. Lessons Learned

Several lessons can be obtained from the implementation of this project, some of these are:

8.1 Project Design and Policy Implications:

Urban Public Transport is a complex matter, which involves more than just infrastructure
improvements. Public transport projects should be approached from all perspectives in order to increase
their chance of success. For example, this Project was prepared in a historically centralized environment.
This changed dramatically during project implementation and the existing institutions were unprepared for



                                                     - 11 -

managing in the newly decentralized environment.

Assess institutional capacity prior to the start of project implementation. The institutional capacities at
municipal level need to be assessed adequately, in particular the resources allocated to planning,
administration, implementation and training. Successful implementation of a sub project depends to a large
extent on the establishment and operation of an adequately staffed or well managed transport unit or core
team, qualified and fully trained at the outset.

Project design and on-lending instruments. This project was in the end targeted increasingly at the urban
transport improvements that affected the poor (improvement of mass transport corridors, including those
providing access to poor neighborhoods, as well as street paving within some of those neighborhoods).
However, funds were on-lent on the basis of the market interest rates prevailing for the CETES and during
the many years of crisis the interest rates reached at times about 30%.    On hindsight, the project should
have been designed with on lending at the Bank nominal rate (plus the Banobras fee) for major corridor
improvements and matching grants for other project components. This would have represented a more
equitable treatment within the transport sector, especially given the Bank's involvement for Federal
highway projects in the same states on essentially a grant basis. In retrospect, the disbursement at market
interest rates of over US$100 million during the project's implementation period was an impressive
achievement.

8.2 Project Implementation:

Use of monitoring and supervision (M&S) system, and follow up of key outcome indicators. The
project did not focus enough on the development of an appropriate M&S to measure outputs, outcomes and
impacts. Attention should be paid to data collection in terms of urban transport project monitoring
indicators and management. Developing a central data system in each city, as well as detailed
implementation action plans and specific measurable indicators and targets could prove helpful to
monitoring project outputs and outcomes.

Annex 3. Economic Costs and Benefits



Generally, data are not available for the ex-post economic analysis of the project due largely to the lack of
data collection and limited analytical capacity of the local agencies now responsible for urban transport.
SEDESOL is still trying to undertake a special exercise with some of the agencies for this purpose.
However, given the rational screening of road projects for ITP purposes, the targeting at heavily trafficked
zones, the resultant capacity of project cities to handle rapidly growing traffic volumes, and the lower than
the SAR-estimated investment costs, if seems clear that the project proved to be economically viable. The
ERR was probably equal to or higher than the 35% expected typically at appraisal.

The traffic signals, and other transport management initiatives led reportedly to up to 25% higher travel
speeds and to reductions in emissions due to bus replacement and to better vehicle circulation patterns.
These benefits, however, though real and typical of urban transport improvements are very difficult to
quantify.

For the technical assistance and training components, quantitative economic evaluations are not possible
but these activities were indispensable to the development of the institutional capacity of the national and
the then nascent local agencies.

Additional Annex 8. Borrower's Evaluation Report

A.      Datos del Proyecto

El Proyecto de Transporte urbano para Ciudades Medias fue identificado durante la preparación del Primer
Proyecto de Transporte Urbano en 1986 destinado a apoyar a la zona del Valle Cuautitlán ­ Texcoco, en el
Estado de México y la Zona Metropolitana de Monterrey.

El Proyecto fue preparado y revisado en 1989, el cual estaba destinado a apoyar la solución de la
problemática que enfrentaban las ciudades medias del país en materia de vialidad y transporte urbano. Su
evaluación se llevó a cabo en enero y febrero de 1992 y las negociaciones se programaron para noviembre
de 1992.

El contrato de préstamo fue firmado el 18 de junio de 1993 y se declaró en efectividad el 19 de febrero de
1994.

El monto original del préstamo fue de US$200 millones destinados a financiar parcialmente el Proyecto de
Transporte Urbano para Ciudades Medias, cuya ejecución estaría a cargo de BANOBRAS y de la
Secretaría de Desarrollo Social (SEDESOL).

Los objetivos generales del Proyecto son los siguientes:

   l Mejorar la calidad y eficiencia del sistema de transporte urbano en las ciudades medias.


   l Apoyar al Gobierno Federal en la descentralización de las responsabilidades para el manejo del
        transporte urbano.

   l Financiar el mejoramiento de la infraestructura que sea económicamente justificable.


   l Contribuir al control de los impactos ambientales de la operación del transporte urbano.


Los objetivos particulares son los siguientes:

   l Fortalecer la capacidad institucional para la planeación y manejo del sistema de transporte urbano
        en ciudades medias.

   l Mejorar la eficiencia operacional de la red actual del transporte urbano.


   l Preservar las inversiones en la infraestructura del transporte.


   l Incentivar la participación del sector privado en el desarrollo del transporte público.


   l Mejorar la infraestructura a zonas marginales.


   l Mejorar las condiciones ambientes, mediante el fortalecimiento del control ambiental y la
        aplicación de estándares ambientales.




                                                   - 24 -

   l Apoyar el desarrollo de instalaciones para transporte no motorizado.


Para la implementación, el proyecto se dividió en dos partes:

Parte A Nacional. A esta parte se le asignó un monto de US$26,500,000 y su ejecución es responsabilidad
de la SEDESOL. Estos recursos se destinaron para las siguientes acciones:

   l Un programa de fortalecimiento institucional de la SEDESOL.


   l Un programa nacional para capacitación de profesionales en transporte urbano.


   l Preparación de estudios integrales de vialidad y transporte urbano en las ciudades elegibles.


Parte B Local. Esta parte del préstamo tuvo una asignación de US$173,5000,000 y sus ejecución estuvo a
cargo de BANOBRAS. Los recursos de esta parte del préstamo se destinaron a financiar a los estados y
municipios elegibles en las siguientes acciones:

   l Un programa de fortalecimiento institucional de BANOBRAS.


   l Los estudios integrales de vialidad y transporte urbano.


   l Un programa de acciones inmediatas para el mejoramiento de la infraestructura del transporte.


   l El programa multianual de inversiones derivadas de las recomendaciones de los estudios integrales
         de vialidad y transporte.

   l El diseño de proyectos, asistencia técnica y capacitación de profesionales en las ciudades.


   l La adquisición de equipo para manejo y control de tránsito.


B. Evaluación del Desarrollo de los Objetivos del Proyecto de la "Parte B Local"

1. Cumplimiento de objetivos originales


Los objetivos originales del Proyecto no fueron modificados y lograron cumplirse de manera satisfactoria.

Los objetivos fueron claros y completos, ya que proporcionaron un marco adecuado para impulsar el
mejoramiento integral del sistema de vialidad y transporte urbano y contribuir al desarrollo económico y
social de las ciudades participantes.

No obstante lo anterior, las componentes del Proyecto inicialmente no cubrir de manera integral las
necesidades del sector, para alcanzar los objetivos planteados.

El inicio de la implementación de las acciones del Proyecto fue de alta complejidad, debido a que no
estabas dadas las condiciones en estados y municipios para cumplir con los requerimientos técnicos
requeridos para el mejoramiento del sistema de vialidad y transporte urbano, no se contaba con los técnicos
suficientes en este sector y no existían las metodologías técnicas adaptadas para el desarrollo de los
estudios y proyectos.

Aún con las limitaciones técnicas y financieras que se presentaron, cada uno de los objetivos propuestos se

El logro de los objetivos generales del programa, brevemente se describe a continuación:

   l Mejorar la calidad y eficiencia de sistema de transporte urbano en las ciudades medias. Este
        objetivo se alcanzó satisfactoriamente en las ciudades participantes, en virtud de que realizaron un
        estudio integral de vialidad y transporte urbano, que les permitió determinar un plan multianual de

   l Apoyar al Gobierno Federal en la descentralización de las responsabilidades para el manejo del

   l Financiar el mejoramiento de la infraestructura que sea económicamente justificable. Con la

        integraron con proyectos seleccionados con base en los resultados de una evaluación técnica y
        socioeconómica.

   l Contribuir al control de los impactos ambientales de la operación del transporte urbano. Los
        proyectos ejecutados contribuyeron en la reducción de las emisiones contaminantes, en virtud de
        que se disminuyeron congestionamientos viales y fueron sometidos a una evaluación del imparto
        ambiental, generándose en su caso recomendaciones para mitigar posibles impactos negativos.

2. Cumplimiento de ejecución de componentes originales

Los componentes originales no fueron suficientes para alcanzar un desarrollo integral del sistema de
vialidad y transporte y en consecuencia para lograr los objetivos planteados.

en los estudios integrales, fue necesario aumentar las componentes originales del Proyecto y ampliar el
universo de atención de las ciudades elegibles.

Con la ampliación de las componentes, fue necesario hacer una reasignación de recursos entre las diferentes
categorías del Proyecto. Para realizar las modificaciones se formalizaron varias enmiendas al contrato de

3. Logro de los Objetivos del Proyecto

Con base en las acciones realizadas en los diferentes componentes del proyecto, se considera que los
objetivos de dicho proyecto se alcanzaron de manera satisfactoria, ya que se realizaron acciones en
cada uno de ellos con buenos resultados como se indica a continuación:

a. Cumplimiento de objetivos específicos del Proyecto
Los objetivos específicos del Proyecto fueron aplicados de manera particular en cada una de las
ciudades participantes y se lograron a través de las siguientes acciones:

   l "Fortalecer la capacidad institucional para la planeación y manejo del sistema de transporte
        urbano en ciudades medias". Para alcanzar este objetivo, se prepararon e impartieron cursos de



                                                - 29 -

        capacitación y se proporcionó la asistencia técnica para la identificación, preparación y
        evaluación de proyectos, particularmente en las ciudades medias que participaron en el
        Proyecto. Se financiaron además, acciones de asistencia técnica y adquisición de equipo
        requeridos para incrementar la capacidad institucional de las áreas responsables de la gestión
        del sistema de vialidad y transporte.

   l "Mejorar la eficiencia operacional de la red actual del transporte urbano". Con recursos del
        préstamo se financió directamente a 10 ciudades, la elaboración de sus estudios integrales de
        vialidad y transporte. Como resultado de los estudios, se llevaron a cabo proyectos para
        incrementar la eficiencia de la red de transporte urbano, entre las cuales se pueden citar, la
        reestructuración de las rutas de transporte, la construcción de carriles exclusivos para
        autobuses, la reubicación de paradas, la adquisición de unidades de transporte público y la
        construcción de terminales de autobuses.

   l "Preservar las inversiones en la infraestructura del transporte". En las ciudades participantes
        en el Proyecto, se llevaron a cabo programas de mantenimiento de la infraestructura y de
        mantenimiento rutinario. Estos proyectos fueron prioritarios en los programas multianuales de
        inversiones recomendados por los estudios integrales de vialidad y transporte.

   l "Incentivar la participación del sector privado en el desarrollo del transporte público". Una
        parte muy importante del préstamo se destinó a financiar a los concesionarios de los servicios
        público de transporte la renovación de unidades y el incremento del parque vehicular.

   l "Mejorar la infraestructura a zonas marginadas". Para mejorar los servicios públicos y el
        acceso al transporte en zonas marginadas, en el préstamo se etiquetó una categoría específica
        para financiar la pavimentación de calles en colonias populares. La inclusión de esta
        componente fue de mucho éxito, en virtud de que se ejerció un alto porcentaje de los recursos
        asignados.

   l "Mejorar las condiciones ambientales, mediante el fortalecimiento del control ambiental y la
        aplicación de estándares ambientales". Con los proyectos viales y con la renovación de
        vehículos de transporte público financiados, se disminuyeron las demoras de tránsito, se
        redujeron las emisiones contaminantes generadas por los vehículos y se redujo la emisión de
        partículas suspendidas generadas en colonias populares.

   l "Apoyar el desarrollo de instalaciones para transporte no motorizado". Como resultado de los
        estudios integrales de vialidad y transporte realizados en las ciudades participantes, se propuso
        la ejecución de proyectos para impulsar el transporte no motorizado. Este tipo de acciones se
        llevó a cabo principalmente en una ciudad, en la cual se implementó un programa de
        construcción de ciclo pistas con resultados aceptables, también se construyeron calles
        peatonales, mejorando la imagen urbana de las ciudades.

Con base a lo anterior, se puede concluir que los objetivos se alcanzaron de manera satisfactoria.

5. Impactos en el desarrollo institucional

Con base en la implementación de las diferentes acciones realizadas en materia de desarrollo
institucional, se considera que se logró significativamente un cambio estructural en el sector. Ahora
casi la mayoría de los proyectos en las ciudades se eligen con base en los resultados de un estudio de
factibilidad y de una evaluación socioeconómica o financiera según el proyecto. Se cuenta con más
herramientas técnicas para la planeación, diseño, ejecución, operación, normatividad y mantenimiento
de los proyectos. Con los cursos de capacitación y con la asistencia técnica proporcionada a las
ciudades, gradualmente se fue incrementando la capacidad institucional de los gobiernos locales.

6. Principales Factores que Afectaron la Implementación y el Resultado del Proyecto

En la implementación del Proyecto se presentaron diversos problemas, que atrasaron
considerablemente su conclusión y que no se logró desembolsar la totalidad del préstamo.            Los
principales factores que afectaron al proyecto fueron los siguientes:
Factores fuera del control del ejecutor:

   l El Préstamo entró en efectividad en el primer bimestre de 1994 y en diciembre de ese año, el
         país entró en una crisis económica muy severa que provocó un incremento fuerte en las tasas
         de interés y como consecuencia por el incremento en los pasivos los estados y municipios se
         vieron obligados a reestructurar sus créditos, disminuyendo con ello su capacidad de
         endeudamiento y la posibilidad de tomas nuevos créditos.

   l La reactivación de los créditos se empezó a presentar a partir del segundo semestre 1997, es
         decir, tres años después de entrar en efectividad, derivado de la disminución de las tasas y de
         mejores expectativas económicas del país.

   l Los recursos federales originalmente previstos para integrar mezclas de recursos con el
         préstamo, fueron menores a los esperados y a partir de 1999 estos recursos se transfirieron
         directamente a los estados y municipios para que los ejercieran en los sectores que
         consideraran convenientes.

   l Factores internos de participantes en el proyecto:


   l Los recursos del préstamo originalmente estaban destinados a financiar los programas de
         inversión multianual en las doce ciudades más importantes del país. Esto suponía que las doce
         ciudades se comprometerían a ejercer con crédito la totalidad de sus programas; sin embargo,
         este supuesto no se cumplió por lo cambios de administración y por las repercusiones en la
         capacidad de endeudamiento.

   l Los componentes de pavimentación de colonias populares y de financiamiento de unidades de
         transporte público no estaban incluidos en el préstamo, pese a que en muchos casos eran


                                                - 31 -

        prioritarias para las autoridades locales. Ante estas limitantes,      muchas de las ciudades
        decidieron recurrir a otras fuentes de financiamiento para atender sus necesidades de inversión.

   l Hacia finales de 1997 se logró formalizar una de las enmiendas que incorporaba el componente
        de pavimentación de colonias. Para 1998, se formalizó la enmienda que permite finalmente
        darle al préstamo un carácter sectorial, en virtud de que prácticamente se abre el
        financiamiento a todos los componentes del sistema de transporte urbano y se amplía el
        universo a todas las zonas urbanas mayores de 50,000 habitantes, incluyendo las zonas
        metropolitanas. Por ello, se podría considerar que para efectos prácticos, el préstamo sólo
        operó los últimos dos años del período de inversión originalmente definido y que los anteriores
        no tuvieron efectos en la colocación de créditos.

   l En los últimos tres años del período de inversión del préstamo se registró una tendencia
        significativa a la baja en las tasas de interés; sin embargo, el consumo de tiempos sin
        desembolso representó un alto costo para BANOBRAS por el pago de comisiones de
        compromiso. De otro lado, la reducción del plazo de amortización de los subpréstamos hizo
        menos atractivos los recursos, ya que requerían de plazos mayores a 10 años y ya sólo se
        disponía de 7 años.

   l Los problemas presentados para el desembolso de recursos obligaron a que se formalizara la
        cancelación de 48 millones de dólares del préstamo.

   l La instrumentación al inicio del proyecto fue muy difícil debido a que los procesos de
        aprobación fueron complicados, con constantes adecuaciones a los estudios integrales. La
        revisión de los proyectos también fue lenta y atrasó la ejecución de las obras. Estos procesos se
        presentaron porque aún no se tenían las herramientas y metodologías claras sobre el contenido
        de los estudios y de los proyectos. Otros factores que al inicio afectaron el proyecto fueron los
        procesos de licitación que también eran lentos atrasando la contratación e inicio de las obras.

7. Sostenibilidad

El proyecto reforzó las políticas en la planeación, diseño, ejecución, operación, mantenimiento y
financiamiento del sistema de transporte. A nivel nacional ahora existe una dependencia que apoya a
los estados y municipios en la gestión del sector.

A nivel local, el estado cuenta con áreas específicas para emitir la normatividad en la planeación,
diseño, ejecución, operación, mantenimiento y financiamiento del sistema de vialidad y transporte
urbano, en el ámbito de su competencia y de igual manera sucede en los municipios. En algunos
estados y municipios, como producto del Proyecto, se han creado organismos autónomos para la
planeación, diseño y operación del sistema de vialidad y transporte urbano. La SEDESOL continúa
apoyando a los estados y municipios en este sector; mientras que, BANOBRAS conserva programas de
financiamiento y asistencia técnica para la modernización del sector de vialidad y transporte urbano,
aplicando las metodologías técnicas y financieras creadas o diseñadas durante la implementación del
Proyecto.

En los estados y municipios se tienen las áreas técnicas para atender a cada una de las fases de
instrumentación de los proyectos, ya que cuentan con áreas de planeación, con áreas de construcción y
mantenimiento de la infraestructura, y con las áreas para regular y controlar la operación y control del
tránsito y del transporte. No obstante la existencia de dichas áreas, siempre existirá el riesgo de la


                                                 - 32 -

sostenibilidad por la posibilidad de la pérdida del personal técnico que se capacitó en el marco del
proyecto, generada por los cambios de administración municipal o por obtener mejores ofertas en otras
áreas o sectores.

8. Desempeño del Banco y del Prestatario

Banco
No obstante que al inicio fue difícil la instrumentación del proyecto, se considera que fue satisfactoria
la intervención del Banco, tomando como referencia los resultados finales obtenidos en el desarrollo del
sector, aunque éstos no se hayan reflejado en el desembolso de los recursos del préstamo. El proyecto
permitió que se registraran cambios positivos muy importantes en el sector, ya que ahora en muchas
ciudades los problemas se atienden con una visión integral y los proyectos son sometidos a un análisis
técnico, socioeconómico y ambiental, y se eligen los que son rentables.

El Banco tuvo una supervisión constante del proyecto y contribuyó con las dependencias, entidades y
organismos participantes a incrementar la calidad y eficacia de gestión del sector. Puede considerarse
que, al inicio la participación de los expertos del Banco fue superior a la que se había estimado
originalmente, motivada por las necesidades técnicas del sector; sin embargo, gradualmente fue
disminuyendo la supervisión, por los avances que se registraron en dependencias de los estados y
municipios, y en los agentes técnicos y financieros.

La mayor participación se debió principalmente a los problemas presentados para la ejecución del
proyecto y, posteriormente, a las enmiendas en la ampliación del período de inversión del proyecto.

A pesar de los cambios y esfuerzos realizados no se logró desembolsar la totalidad de los recursos
financieros, pero se compensó con el cambio registrado en el sector alcanzando los objetivos técnicos.

El Banco mostró una mayor flexibilidad para modificar algunos criterios del Proyecto, con el fin de
hacerlo más atractivo, lo cual se reflejó en las enmiendas al contrato de préstamo. No obstante la
flexibilidad del proyecto, no se logró desembolsar una cantidad importante de los recursos, debido a
que los cambios se formalizaron casi al final del período de inversión del préstamo, cuando los plazos
para el otorgamiento de créditos a estados y municipios eran reducidos considerando la magnitud de las
obras y con lo cual resultaba menos atractivo.

Por otro lado, pese a que existieron varios cambios en los responsables del proyecto por parte del
Banco, la continuidad no se rompió y mejoró considerablemente la cooperación y el intercambio entre
el Banco y BANOBRAS.

BANOBRAS
Se puede considerar que, técnicamente, para BANOBRAS, el resultado de la implementación del
proyecto fue satisfactorio, pero financieramente no lo fue. En los aspectos técnicos se logró integrar un
área para proporcionar asistencia técnica a los acreditados con instrumentos y herramientas técnicas
que facilitan el análisis y toma de decisiones en la dictaminación de estudios y proyectos. BANOBRAS
ha participado en la modernización del sector de vialidad y transporte, contribuyendo a implantar una
nueva cultura y forma de atender las necesidades del sector, mediante la asistencia técnica y la
capacitación a estados y municipios.

Se lograron establecer y cumplir con las normas y procedimientos del proyecto en materia de
adquisiciones, preparación y evaluación de proyectos, desembolso de recursos, control y supervisión


                                                - 33 -

del proyecto, reportes de avance del proyecto y justificación de enmiendas. Con la asistencia técnica de
BANOBRAS y de la SEDESOL, se logró incrementar la capacidad institucional de las áreas
responsables del sistema de vialidad y transporte en estados y municipios, aunque existieron casos
donde los técnicos capacitados salieron por cambios en las administraciones estatales y municipales.

No obstante que, en los estados y municipios participantes se realizaron estudios integrales de vialidad
y transporte que les permitió identificar las inversiones para el corto, mediano y largo plazo, los
problemas presentados a nivel nacional y local no permitieron contar con los recursos financieros
suficientes para llevar a cabo los planes de inversión en su totalidad.
Por lo que se refiere a los aspectos financieros, cada una de las condiciones establecidas en el contrato
de préstamo y de los subpréstamos se cumplió de manera eficiente; sin embargo, se puede considerar
como insatisfactorio porque tuvieron que pagarse altos costos por los recursos no desembolsados y
tuvieron que cancelarse cantidades importantes del préstamo. Asimismo, del monto total previsto en el
SAR, sólo se pudo ejecutar un 29%, sin considerar los montos en afectaciones.

9. Lecciones Aprendidas

Con la instrumentación del Proyecto se obtuvieron valiosas experiencias que permitieron conocer más
la problemática, las necesidades y los mejores esquemas de atención al sistema de vialidad y transporte,
con lo cual se contribuyó a mejorar su desarrollo. También, con las experiencias obtenidas se
negociaron enmiendas al contrato de préstamo.

Entre algunas de las lecciones aprendidas se pueden mencionar las siguientes:

   l Las dificultades técnicas presentadas en los inicios del Proyecto, se debieron a que se esperaba
        lograr un cambio estructural inmediato y no un cambio gradual para sustituir inercias en la
        aplicación de procedimientos y herramientas técnicas tradicionales y modificar las estructuras
        organizacionales.

   l La implantación de una nueva cultura para la elaboración de estudios y proyectos, y para la
        gestión de este subsector, sólo podría lograrse mediante un intenso programa de asistencia
        técnica y capacitación, y ofreciendo incentivos técnicos y financieros a los estados y
        municipios.

   l La falta de estudios, proyectos, y de programas multianuales de inversión motivaron que, a lo
        largo del tiempo se atendieran los problemas de manera aislada, con lo cual se carecía del
        suficiente sustento y de mejores alternativas para solucionar los problemas y para disminuir
        sus efectos adversos.

   l El personal en las áreas de vialidad y transporte era escaso y las tareas por desarrollar eran
        muchas, por ello fue necesario impulsar la preparación y difusión de herramientas técnicas,
        modelos y procedimientos para simplificar y hacer más eficiente el desempeño de sus
        actividades.

   l Los préstamos externos no deben estar etiquetados a determinados núcleos de población o
        proyectos específicos, debido a que limitan el número de posibles acreditados e incrementan los
        costos financieros. Por ello es recomendable que, en el futuro pudieran negociarse préstamos
        con esquemas innovadores con el fin de contar con líneas de crédito acordes a las necesidades y

  al avance en la preparación de proyectos.

l Los componentes de colonias populares y de unidades de transporte son fundamentales, en
  virtud de que son los que mayores beneficios generan a los estratos de población de menores
  ingresos y permiten complementar la atención integral del sistema de vialidad y transporte.

3. Assessment of Development Objective and Design, and of Quality at Entry
3.1 Original Objective:
The objectives of the project, as defined in the Staff Appraisal Report' (SAR), were to: (a)
increase and diversify output and improve productivity in the rural sector; (b) increase
employment and income of small-farmers and improve their living standards by introducing
higher-value crops, promoting storage and processing of agricultural products and rationalizing
marketing; (c) improve rural infrastructure (feeder roads, irrigation and flood control schemes,
marketing facilities); (d) promote grassroots involvement of rural communities and groups of
beneficiaries giving special emphasis to rural women; (e) strengthen beneficiaries' organizations
and local NGOs; (i) promote a better use and conservation of natural resources; and (g) enhance
institutional capacity for planning and implementing rural development activities. These
objectives were clear but very ambitious, considering the complexity of the project and the
management capabilities of the implementing agency. As unforeseen political and eccnomic
events unfolded, these objectives proved largely unattainable.

3.4 Revised C'onpoenles:
There were a total of five amendments to the Loan Agreement (LA). The first in 1993
reassigned US$1.2 million from unallocated to newly created Categories 8 and 9 to rehabilitate
the Paute Disaster Area. The other four were all to facilitate reconstruction of rural civil works
in the project areas damaged by the El Nifno phenomenon in 1998. Although this was not
considered a major restructuring, the Executive Directors were notified of the change as part of
the Board's approval of the El Nifio Emergency Reconstruction Project (Loan 4259-EC). In
anticipation of the damage to come, the Bank agreed in December 1997 (Second Amendment)
to add a new component to finance these reconstruction activities, and the remaining three
amendments facilitated its implementation. Category 10 was added to Schedule I of the LA to
finance emergency works. With the Third Amendment the amount was increased to US$10
million. This amount was shifted from several components with slow implementation. MBS
proved unable to implement this component on a timely basis, so the fifth amendment in March
2000 moved implementation responsibilities for the reconstruction activities to the El Nifio
Emergency Coordination Unit, COPEFEN, in the Office of President, and postponed the closing
date for this component to December 31, 2000.2
3.5 Qualitn at EnIty: Appropriateness of Project Concep)t, Objectives and Ap7proach:
On balance the project's quality at entry is rated satisfactory by the ICR Review.
The level of poverty in the rural areas of Ecuador was very high -- an estimated 78% of rural
families lived below the "critical poverty"3 level. The administration which took office in
August 1988 assigned a high priority to rural development as one of the key elements to reduce
unemployment and poverty in the country, to decrease inequalities among different regions, and
to slow down migration to urban areas. The project objectives were consistent with the Bank's
lending strategy for the sector, as stated in the SAR. The needs of the commercial farm sector
were believed to be served through the opening up of trade and the development of more
competitive financial markets. In the small farm sector, however, more direct interventions to
develop rural infrastructure, improve technology, and conserve natural resources were identified
as important where poverty alleviation was the main objective.
The administration which took office in 1988 wanted to pursue a new approach to rural
development (RD). Previous, more traditional, supply driven RD projects achieved mixed
results. The new government, and particularly the management of the Ministry of Social
Welfare (MBS), believed that emphasis on promoting community and beneficiary organizations,
outreach to NGOs, and protection of the environment (the objectives d, e, and f in para. 3.1
above) would provide the sustainability to RD investments, which had proven so elusive in the
past. The Bank perceived this focus on beneficiaries and the environment as innovative and
leading edge for the region, and it agreed that MBS should prepare and manage the Project.

MBS designed this project to use the implementation capacities of both the public, private and
nonprofit sectors, coordinated by MBS. MBS believed its value added to be prima3rily in
promoting community participation and coordinating the activities of local groups, NGOs and
government agencies. Emphasis on beneficiary involvement and environmental protection was
consistent with the Bank's safeguard policies. During preparation it was correctly recognized
that MBS would need continuing technical assistance to manage the project, so it si,ned a
Technical Assistance contract with the Interamerican Institute for Cooperation in Agrizulture
(IICA) to provide these services, and this was extended for the life of the project. At the same
time MBS also contracted with the local agent of IBM Corporation to design and install an
integrated management information and accounting system for the Project, and it was
successfully maintained until closing.  At the end of the first two years of implementation,
MBS had signed performance contracts with partners to undertake a third of the project and was
ahead of schedule in its commitments.
A special budget law permitted MBS to control the flow of project resources tc these
participants through the use of performance contracts. It would sign these agreememns with
whichever entity had the strongest capacity to implement an activity within a project area. The
areas were selected using a series of poverty indicators. This approach to implementation
worked very effectively during the first two years of project implementation, until the
govermnent changed. Thereafter the political and economic situation in Ecuador deteriorated,
until it negatively impacted project implementation in many respects. The original conzept of
MBS partnering with other ministries and agencies to implement the Project, proved
unsuccessful in the longer run.
With hindsight the Project could have been designed to more effectively insulate the
management team in SSDR from the changes of political leadership. The undersecretary in
charge of the SSDR, as a political appointee, should not have assumed the position of Project
Director, as well. This arrangement permitted each new administration to needlessly interfere
with management, so that the focus and efficiency was lost over time.

Component 1-Irrigation and Flood Control:
This component financed civil works, equipment, engineering design & supervision and
maintenance of small community irrigation and flood control schemes. A total of US  30.9
million was earmarked to finance the completion and rehabilitation of six irrigation schemes,
two flood control schemes and 43 existing water courses, which at completion, would irrigate an
incremental area of about 26,000 hectares.  This work was originally supposed to be
implemented or supervised by INEHRI, the national irrigation institute. In the fourth s ear of
project implementation, this institution was liquidated, and its responsibilities passed tD new
regional authorities or local user groups. On balance this component is rated unsatisfactory, but
some of the unfinished work and transfer of the systems is being successfully accomrlished
under the Irrigation Technical Assistance Project (Loan 3730-EC).
Component 2 & 3-Validation & Transfer of Technology:
The National Institute for Agricultural and Livestock Research, INIAP, was responsible tor the
research and validation of technology. The Ministry of Agriculture new Extension Division was
to transfer this technology, aimed at increasing investment by small farmers. During appraisal,
a total of US$ 8.2 million was allotted to implement these two components. The actual
expenditure for validation & transfer of technology amounted to a mere US$ 2.9 million. A
total of 725 new technologies were planned to be validated and transferred to 23,000 small
farmers to promote incremental production. The project succeeded in achieving about 90% of
its research and validation target, but failed in transferring the technology or altering cropping
patterns to higher value added products under irrigation. For this reason, it is judged an
unsatisfactory outcome.
Component 4- Forestry Development:
This component relied on MBS using local agents to promote reforestation and better
conservation of natural resources, as a means to contribute in raising rural income (through the
sale of forest products and provision of employment in the plantation and management of
forests), to provide wood for basic needs and to conserve natural resources, and to se:rve as
windbreaks (by establishing 11,300 hectares of plantation and 1,100 kilometers oL row
plantations). To that end, US$ 2.7 million was allocated during appraisal, but only U',$ 1.4
million was spent on this component. About 4,204 hectares of productive plantations were
achieved, and the row plantation of trees achieved was above the targeted level at 4,000
kilometers. However, these forest plantations proved to have a low survival rate, so the
outcome was judged unsatisfactory.
Component 5-On-Farm Investment:
During appraisal, it was agreed that the GOE through its National Development Bank would
provide US$ 10 million in credit to project small farmer beneficiaries. This would allovW them
to take full advantage of the investments in irrigation, flood control, better communications &
marketing and make available new, validated technologies. However, little credit was actually
provided (only US$ 0.6 million), as the public and private banking system gradually collapsed
into insolvency during this period. The component performance is considered unsatisfactory.

Component 6-Post-Harvest Processing & Marketing:
This component was designed to: ensure that the increased production promoted by other
components reaches the market; increase the value of crops by improving quality; promote the
participation of women; and to pilot farmer-owned companies. While most of the feasibility
studies planned were completed in the first three years, only two of the 15 regional marketing
centers foreseen were actually built. As a result, only US$ 0.3 million of US$ 2.8 million
budget was spent. The component is rated unsatisfactory.
Component 7-Community Development Fund:
This component was intended to finance local projects submitted by groups of beneficiaries for
technology transfer or training activities, as well as to fund economically feasible productive
sub-projects. Despite the high demand in rural areas, the component's performance proved
unsatisfactory, as it suffered from political interference and bureaucracy. Only 48% of the
resources planned for its implementation were utilized. However, in the final year of project
implementation a sub-account (FONLOCAL) with US$1 million was created to pilot new
activities to rebuild financial intermediaries in selected rural areas. Lessons learned from this
may be applied in the PROLOCAL Project under preparation.
Component 8-Rural Roads:
The aim of this component was to ensure all-year-round access to the twelve selected areas in
order to minimize losses of agricultural products and to reduce unit costs of transport &
commerce. It was envisioned to construct 100 kilometers of new and to improve 700 kilometers
of old rural roads at a cost of US$ 24.5 million. Fifty-two kilometers of new and 460 kilometers
of improvement works were completed, but at a cost of US$ 34.2 million. This component was
the primary source of disbursements for the project, as relatively large contracts were easier to
administer. The results were mixed, because MBS had limited capacity to supervise them and
to obtain community support for carrying out maintenance. Some works contracts remain
unfinished and are still awaiting judicial settlement. On balance, the component is rated
unsatisfactory.
Other Components:
With the exception of land tenure regularization, the components of water harvesting,
environment (no targets), training & communication, and institutional development all lost their
momentum after the first two years of implementation. so they failed to achieve most of the
goals set during the appraisal. In 1998 a new component - the El Nino Reconstruction- was
added to this project which is still under implementation and executed by another project, thus
its progress is not evaluated here.
The issuance of land titles to small farmers is considered successful. The absolute numbers
exceeded the target, but the corresponding area of land was much less, because the target group
had smaller holdings that assumed.
Performance targets for training and communication with community groups exceeded appraisal
expectations, but the high turnover of project staff and an apparent politicization of the process
calls into question the quality of accomplishments. Target groups were often chosen on the
basis of preferential treatment or political affiliation rather than an inclusive local development
plan.

The Economic Rate of Return in the SAR was estimated to be 15% with a Net Present Value
(NPV) of US$16.3 million at the assumed 12% Opportunity Cost of Capital (OCC). The
benefits used in economic rate of return were expected to be realized from:
(a) Increased Agricultural Production: More than 90% of total quantifiable benefits were
expected to result from increased agricultural output. The incremental production was expected
to come from expansion of cropped areas, the introduction of higher value crops, and double-
cropping patterns. Improved technologies & increased availability of irrigation water conmbined
with flood protection and drainage works were believed to contribute to raising agricuiltural
production.
(b) Benefits from Forestry, Post-Harvest Processing & Marketing and Comrnunity
Development: The combination of these three components were expected to raise by 10% the
total quantifiable benefits.
A new estimation of the economic results was made during the last supervision and project
completion mission. As in the original estimate, output market prices for basic grains were
corrected in order to reflect border prices. In the case of unskilled labor, the new estimate made
no adjustment, because unsustainable price distortions caused by the economic crisis show wage
levels significantly below the marginal productivity of labor. The ex-post economic evaluation
indicated that, when all project costs are compared with the estimated benefits derived from
increased agricultural production, the ERR is only 3% with an NPV of minus US$24.8 million.
Benefits from forestry, post-harvest investments and community development subprojects were
considered negligible given their extremely low implementation levels and impacts.

5. Major Factors Affecting Implementation and Outcome
5.1 Factors outside the control of government or imipletmtenting agenc y'
Decreasing international agricultural prices during the project's implementation period,
combined with reduced levels of nominal and effective protection, resulted in lower local prices
at the farm level. In addition, severe floods in the coastal areas during the 1997-98 El Nifio
phenomenon negatively affected the production of project beneficiaries. It also damaged a
number of unfinished irrigation and flood control structures, rural roads and on-farm
infrastructure.
5.2 P'actors general/V subject to government conthol:
Most of the period covering the implementation of this project was characterized by political
instability. Between effectiveness (1992) and project completion in December 2000, there were
six GOE administrations and only the first two finished their constitutional term. During the
same time there were ten changes in the management of SSDR and MBS.
Poor macro-economic policies, weak prudential regulation, and external shocks caused the local
financial system to collapse into insolvency. This situation starved the project's beneficiaries of
commercial credit to make and sustain their investments. The continually worsening fiscal
situation caused Government to cut services and reduce staff, thereby weakening the capacity of
project implementing agencies to carry out their roles.
5 3 Faclor.s genertll/v stubject to implementing agency control:
At the beginning PRONADER benefited from a high degree of commitment and competence in
the staff of the SSDR. However, due to increasing political involvement in managerial and staff
appointments, occupational uncertainty and low motivation reigned. Increasingly, new
appointees exhibited lack of experience in project and personnel management. As fiscal
budgets were cut, MBS lost control of the logical balance among the different components
foreseen during appraisal. On several occasions staff in the field were not paid their salaries for
up to six months. The service activities of the project were not given the attention they
deserved. Gradually emphasis shifted to simple infrastructure investments like feeder roads and
related bridges.
5.4 COs.s and linuncing-
All components except rural roads and technical assistance (IICA) used less funding than
expected at appraisal. The large cost overruns on the rural roads contracts were due primarily
to: (a) unrealistic appraisal estimates of costs and contingencies; (b) additional works not
originally contemplated; (c) delays in completing contracts due to late payment of GOE
counterpart; and (d) contract disputes that went to legal action.
In the last three years of implementation, disbursements for local costs were negatively
influenced by a severely devalued local currency. Counterpart funds were a continuous problem
given the profound economic crisis that has and still is affecting Ecuador. The shortage of
counterpart funds became a most binding constraint for the credit component, which was to be

7. Bank and Borrower Performance
Batik
7.1  Lending Bank.
During identification, preparation and appraisal, the Bank's performance was rated satisfactory
by the ICR Review. Project's design was driven by the government's vision for drarnatic
change, and the Bank perceived it as innovative and leading edge. In many ways the Pr .ject
turned out to be a transition between two paradigms. The old "top down, integrated rural
development" approach previous to this project had largely been discredited. PRONADER. was
seen as an innovative break with the past. It envisaged the involvement of the private sectoi and
NGOs in the process of institution building. Strong involvement of grassroots organizations of
beneficiaries through diverse mechanisms and activities was also part of the strategy. Howwver,
the demand driven, direct contracting of services and infrastructure by the commurnities
themselves, as called for in the new paradigm, was not contemplated. The PRONADER design
I0



was still weighted down by a great deal of complexity in its implementation arrangements. It
was explicitly recognized by the Borrower and the Bank that success would depend on MBS
developing and maintaining a strong management capacity. Consequently TA to support this
objective was arranged through the IICA and IBM contracts.
The appraisal recognized the risk that the very strong government commitment observed at
project preparation and appraisal might not be sustained throughout the long implementation
period of the project. However, the SAR argued that the beneficiary participation in the design
and preparation, which ensured strong political momentum at the grassroots, could immunize
the project against possible disruptive political changes in the central administration. In fact
beneficiaries participation during implementation was not achieved at the level anticipated at
appraisal.
Changes in political leadership proved much more common than assumed at appraisal. There
were six, instead of the three mandated by the constitution. With hindsight, a very short
implementation period would have been more prudent. Additionally the Bank accepted the
MBS assertion that the Subsecretary of SSDR, a political appointee, should be the project
manager. This arrangement turned out to be a two edged sword, in that later, less committed
appointees would have more direct political influence over the project management.
IICA had a contract over the life of the project to undertake independent monitoring and
evaluation. The appraisal provided reasonably clear numerical outputs for project components,
but was weak on indicators to measure attainment of project impact objectives.

During the first three years of the project through 1995, implementation was ahead of schedule.
Thereafter as the economic and political environment deteriorated, the Bank can be faulted for
not fully recognizing its negative impact on project objectives. The continuing changes in
project management after 1993 not only demoralized local staff, but also created a situation in
which Bank staff lost interest in pushing for solutions. For several years supervision missions
were able to rate performance as satisfactory because disbursements continued to meet forecasts
until 1996 even in a very difficult environment. During the final years of implementation the
Bank took a more proactive stance in recognizing implementation problems and addressing
them. In 1997 QAG completed a Rapid Quality of Supervision Assessment of the Project and
rated supervision of last year satisfactory. In 1998 Project funds were reallocated to emergency
reconstruction of El Nifo flood damage in project areas, which was a higher priority of the
government at that time. After additional attempts to reactivate the Project had limited success,
the project's performance rating was downgraded in early 1999. Finally when MBS proved
unable to implement the El Nifio Component, responsibility for implementing this was shifted to
another agency. On balance Bank supervision is rated unsatisfactory.

8. Lessons Learned.
The following lessons learned are the result of the ICR team analysis and a two days workshop
held in Quito, Ecuador, with the participation of the MBS Minister and staff, the IICA, NGOs,
indigenous groups, universities and donors.
> Beneficiary participation is an essential feature of the identification, planning and
implementation process. Participation helps to ensure that  investments which have
prospects of being sustained, are financed.
> Rural development problems are by nature complex and few developing countries have the
capacity to manage integrated solutions, so simplified demand driven models are more likely
to succeed.
>  Continuing ownership of the project objectives and strategy  by stakeholders is a
prerequisite for final success of such a project.
> Community maintenance arrangements for rural roads and other infrastructure should not
assume that the availability of surplus labor will assure success. Viable incentives should be
identified to gain the participation of local stakeholders.
' Effective monitoring of expected project impacts must rely on appropriate indicators being
agreed during appraisal. Evaluation should address specific issues (e.g., technology
adoption, stakeholder commitment) and should be designed to assist in solving problems and
assessing the project's impact.
> If political or economic instability are identified at appraisal as a substantial risk for a
project not to attain its primary objective, then shortening the project implementation period
could mitigate the risk.
>  Project design should seek to insulate project management from day to day political
interference, which might dilute objectives or cause unnecessary management and staff
turnover.

alimentos, al tiempo de promover la participaci6n democratica del campesino en los procesos de

consumo popular y el ingreso campesino a traves del incremento de la producci6n y la productividad y la

de Tecnologia; Desarrollo Forestal; Credito; Rehabilitaci6n y complementaci6n del Riego; Vialidad;

PREFACE
This is the Implementation Completion Report (ICR) for the Morazan Darn Emergency
Project in Honduras, for which Credit 2417-HO in the amnount of SDR 8.83 million was
approved on August 4, 1992, and made effective on March 9, 1993.
The credit was closed on September 30, 1995, one year after the original closing date.
Final disbursement took place on February 7, 1996, at which time a balance of SDR 1,239
equivalent was canceled. Cofinancing for the project was provided by the IDB.
This Implementation Completion Report was prepared by ILF Consultants and reviewed
by Marcelo Osorio, Task Manager, Sector Leadership Group, and Luis Cosenza, Acting
Infrastructure Sector Leader, Central America Country Department, Latin America and the
Caribbean Regional Office.
Preparation of this ICR was begun during a field visit to the project site in August 1995.
The report is based on material in the project file and on discussions with Bank staff The
borrower contributed to the preparation of this ICR by commenting on the draft ICR, and by
preparing its own evaluation of the project which is included as Appendix B.

EVALUATION SUIMMARY
Introduction
1. The Morazan Dam Emegency Project was prepared to finance urgent remedial measures
to reduce potentially dangerous reservoir leakage, which had been detected downstream of
the dam and high hydrostatic pressures at the dam foundation. These conditions were
considered potentially dangerous to the structural integrity of the Morazan Dam. The
project was consistent with the Bank strategy to assist the Government in preventing a
potential economic and environmental disaster, and in ensuring the continued availability of
the country's key source of electricity supply. The US$12.00 million Bank credit financed
two basic components: 92% was used for a program to seal the rock mass at the dam
foundation and to impermeabilize the power plant; and 8% was used for consultant
semices to support project execution.
Project Objectives
2. The project had three basic objectives: i) maintaining the structural integrity of the
Morazan Dam; ii) increasing the reliability of the power plants auxiliary system, and
thereby minimizing the risk of interruption of the principal source of the countrys electricity
supply, and iii) strengthening BEE's emergency preparedness planning and its capacity to
monitor the dam performance.
3. The technical performance indicators established in the SAR were:
* A reduction in the seepage water in Pozo Norte from 850 ISec to 250 IJSec.
* A reduction in the hydrostatic foundation pressures from 50m to 30m head of water.

PART I:         PROJECT IMPLEMENTATION ASSESSMENT
A.    Introduction
1.    The construction of the Morazan Dam project started in 1980 and finished in
December of i984. The dam is a high (226m, among the ten highest in the world)
double-curvature concrete arch which impounds a reservoir of 5,700 million cubic
meters. The power station is located about 200 meters below the ground on the left
abutment and contains four units, each generating 73MW. The station is connected to
the national grid by a double circuit transmission line at 230 kV: the Northem branch
to El Progreso substation (40km) and the Southern branch to Tegucigalpa (140km).
2.    The dam is located about 3 km downstream of the confluence of the Humuya and
Sulaco rivers in a gorge of limestone formation covered by volcanic rock. The
limestone outcroppings at the site have four main vertical faults running parallel to the
river. To waterproof, the dam foundation design consisted of a relatively novel sealing
scheme combining shallow vertical and wide horizontal grouting zones forming a tub-
shaped seal with its downstream edge under the heel of the dam.
3.   By 1986, two years after the initial filling of the reservoir, hydrostatic pressures at the
dam foundation had increased abnormally (by about 20 meters), indicating breaches in
the impermeabilized zone. Deep drain holes were drilled at the base of the dam and
downstream of the originally treated grouting zone which relieved the pressures, but
the resulting seepage was excessive. Moreover, the seepage water contained clay,
indicating that it was eroding it from the faults. The location of renewed higher
pressures and evidence of clay in the discharges pointed to erosion of zones in three
out of the four existing faults. A grouting program was carried out in 1987-1989 at an
estimated total cost of US$ 5.5 million to seal the major leaks and control the erosion
they were causing. This program was successful in halting leakage along one of the
faults. Pressures and total seepage were reduced to acceptable levels, but a side effect,
noticed first in late 1989, was the increase in underground water seepage into the
power plant, from new channels formed between or at faults.
4.    The Panel for the Security of the Dam (PSD) made up of international experts and,
which periodically reviewed the performance of the dam structure since its completion



in 1985, alerted ENEE to the potentially dangerous reservoir leakage (800 L/Sec) and
high hydrostatic pressures at the darn foundation (50 meters). Water seepage at the
dam, if left untreated, could have resulted in serious damage to the dam structure or
even its collapse. High hydrostatic pressures could have eventually reduced the
strength of the abutments and the structural integrity of the dam. Compounding the
problem, the plant's auxiliary electrical system, which operates the sump pumps of the
dam and the underground plant, failed once for about 15 minutes. This event almost
caused flooding of the generating units floor.
B.    Project Objectives
5.    The project had three basic objectives:
*  maintaining the structural integrity of the Morazan Dam by controlling
excessive seepage and hydrostatic pressures under the dam foundation
*  increasing the reliability of the power plant's auxiliary system thereby
minimizing the risk of interruption of the principal source of the country's
electricity supply; and
e  strengthening ENEE's emergency preparedness planning and its capacity to
monitor dam performance and to manage possible similar future seepage
problems at the Morazan Dam.
6.    The technical performance indicators established in the SAR were:
A reduction in the seepage water in Pozo Norte from 850 L/Sec to 250 L/Sec.
* A reduction in the hydrostatic foundation pressures from 50m to 30m head of water.

integrity of the dam were exceeded, the power plant's auxiliary system is working
satisfactorily, and ENEE has a qualified team to monitor the Morazan dam and prevent
emergency situations.

overall dam, the seepage water was reduced from 1,629L/Sec in February 1993 to
88L/Sec in 1995, a very substantial reduction of 1,541 L/Sec. The second target
for ensuring the stability of the dam, the reduction of the hydrostatic pressure.

D.    Implementation Record and Major Factors Affecting the Project
11.   The Project included the following components:
(a) underground investigation with deep rock borings (about 46,1000 meters) and
corresponding instrumentation, followed by high pressure grouting of sealant (about
14,000 metric tons) into the rock mass;
(b) impermeabilization of the power plant sump, construction of additional drainage
galleries (about 300m) to evacuate filtration by gravity, lining of the inspection gallery,
and plugging of the diversion tunnel;
(c) purchase and installation of additional electrical and mechanical equipment to
substantially increase service reliability of the auxiliary electrical system of the power
plant;
(d) purchase and installation of new and complementary instrumentation to monitor
foundation pressures and water filtration;
(e) consulting services to: (i) supervise the impermeabilization works; (ii) carry out a
structural and electromechanical safety review of the dam; and (iii) assist in the
preparation of an emergency preparedness and evacuation plan; and
3



(f) training of ENEE staff in underground investigations, pressure sealing of
foundations, monitoring of hydrostatic foundation pressures and water leakage in high
dams and electrical system stability.
12.    Given the extremely complex geological conditions in the area surrounding the dam
and the dam foundation, it was impossible to predefine the extent of grouting
program and exact areas to be grouted. The complex geological conditions included
the presence of karstic limestone, four geological faults with caves of various
dimensions, clay pockets, and unusually deep pervious zones (between 170-180
meters from the lowest access gallery). The conditions of the reservoir made the
drilling and grouting operations extremely difficult due to the presence of high
hydrostatic pressures, high water flow, and high water velocity. Limited working
space, high humidity and air temperature, and the presence of karstic phenomena
made the task even more daunting.
13.    Due to the above circumstances, an exploratory program consisting of several drill
holes reaching deeply into the foundation was required. These drillings were necessary
in order to identify the areas of the dam foundation to be treated and the volume of
grouting required. As a consequence, the initial contract amount had to be revised to
be able to reflect the true underground conditions as they became known through the
progress of the work.
14.    During the drilling and grouting process, a large cavern was discovered in one of the
faults in the left flank under the dam, at about 70 meters below sea level (about 170-
180 meters below the foundation of the dam and well below the grout curtain).
Tracer tests found that water flowed from the reservoir to the cavern below the grout
curtain. To fil the cavern, approximately 7,800 M3 of grouting was necessawy. This
volume far exceeded the volume estimated in the contract. As shown below, the
overal volume of grouting required was more than three times the volume anticipated
in the SAR, and the extent of drilling was about three times greater than estimated in
the SAR:

the US$ 17.4 million estimated in the SAR. The additional cost was financed mostly
by ENEE, which increased its financial contribution from US$3.8 million (SAR
estimate) to US$15.2 million. This additional financing, four times more than
expected, shows ENEE's strong commitment to the project. The IDB also increased
its financing by about US$3.7 million (from US$1.6 million, as originally estimated, to
US$5.4 million). Bank financing remained the same at US$12.00 million. Table 8B

of the Statistical Annex shows project financing. The additional works required to
achieve the project's objectives were the result of the following circumstances, and
corresponding works, which were impossible to predict at the time of appraisal:
(a)    water flow much deeper than expected with the geological information
available prior to the works;
(b)   large quantities of soft materials at great depth, the opposite of what has
been experienced with other dams built in sites with similar geological
conditions, where the soft material diminishes with the depth;
(c)   the need to build a barrier thicker than foreseen at the initial design to

(d)   large underground sections exposed to high speed water flow, which

additives;
(e)   increase of leakage at unlined sections of one of the drainage tunnels and
the old access tunnel to the power house. This required lining these
tunnels with concrete.
E.    Project Sustainability
16.    The substantial reduction of the hydrostatic pressure and water filtration have
increased the safety and useful life of the dam by securing its structural integrity. The
dam, the stability of which was seriously threatened by the pressure buildup and
subsequent erosive flow in the abutments, is now safe. A static analysis of the dam
carried out in January 1996 confirmed its stable condition. However, the works
executed under the project do not eliminate the possibility of similar leakage problems
in the future. Given the karstic nature of the site where the dam was built, it can be
expected that with the passage of time, the underground flow of water will affect the
rock and leakage might again increase.  Therefore, the monitoring practices
established by ENEE  must continue.   The characteristics of the geological
surrounding of the dam are such that constant surveillance and rapid remedial action
will be necessary to guard against a return to the circumstances that prompted the
emergency project.
F.     Bank Performance
17.    During preparation, the Bank reacted quickly and effectively as soon as the PSD
alerted ENEE and IDA to the potentially dangerous reservoir leakage and high
hydrostatic pressures at the dam foundation. IDA contracted the services of a
consulting firm specializing in hydraulic structures to assess the problem. In April

1992, the consultants presented their recommended remedial measures, and in May
1992, the Bank appraised the project.
18.    During supervision, the Bank closely followed project implementation and was
present during the difficult stages of the project, where adjustments had to be made to
achieve the objectives. The Bank's presence at key meetings with the PSD and the
Project Engineer contributed to the development of a credible and cooperative
environment for the execution of the project. Throughout project preparation and
supervision, the Bank also closely coordinated its efforts with the 1DB, which
provided cofinancing for the project. This was a very positive experience, as both
banks joined forces to provide timely advise and financial support.
G.   Borrower Performance
19.   ENEE established a knowledgeable and dedicated engineering team at the project site
to deal with the complex nature of the project. The team's previous involvement in the
project, from the early 1980s, contributed a great deal to the implementation success.
The team deserves most of the credit for achieving/exceeding the targets in the SAR.
20.   Responsible on-site supervision throughout the duration of the project contributed a
great deal to the quality of the work. It also showed the advantages of allowing
flexibility to experiment with new and innovative grouting methods.
21.   The project engineering team established by ENEE has shown dedication, capability,
professionalism, and efficiency in dealing with difficult tasks throughout project
implementation. The support of a competent security panel (PSD) and engineering
consultant (Colenco), together with the contractor (Conrodio), contributed a great deal
to the achievement of the project objectives.
22.   During the course of repair work ENEE's project team successfully experimented
with new and innovative methods for injecting grout into the caverns with high water
pressure and velocity. These included combining grouting materials with wooden and
plastic balls (5 and 7 centimeters), and the use of rolled-up plastic sacks, normally used
to hold animal feed or grains, as temporary plugs to allow the successful injection of
grouting material.
23.   Two additional problems that appeared during project implementation have not been
solved yet. One was a major slide of the road that occurred about 9km west of the
Morazan plant. ENEE's lack of funds has not permitted a permanent solution to this
problem, which will require a new route for that section of the road. So far, only minor
provisional works have been carried out to keep the vehicular traffic going. The other
is a problem with the elevator installed at the core of the dam. The original supplier
was not interested in repairing the elevator, and instead suggested the installation of a
new one. ENEE plans to deal with these problems in the near future.

H     Assessment of Outcome                      I
24.   The project outcome was satisfactory. It fully achieved its objectives despite the
complexity of the difficult and unpredictable geological conditions. The stability of the
Morazan Dam, which was the main project objective, has been substantially increased.
In addition, ENEE has trained a competent engineering team to deal with the
monitoring of the dam and its foundation.
L      Future Operations
25    ENEE has defined a monitoring program based on the actual configuration of the grout
curtain. ENEE wil observe and monitor the current flows and detect any changes.
ENEE wil also continue with hydrogeochemical testing; this is an important part of
the monitoring program to identify seepage sources and possible changes in their paths.
This close monitoring would allow ENEE to respond properly and timely to an
increase in flows, and therefore control any possible erosion as early as possible.
26.   The Panel of Experts will continue visiting the project site once a year to review the
records on seepage and hydrostatic pressures to be obtained by ENEE's monitoring
team. ENEE will maintain a minimum of equipment on site to be able to act
immediately should it become necessary to follow up with some grouting to control
the situation. ENEE is also negotiating a contract with the company responsible for
the grouting program under the project to make available drilling and grouting
equipment in case a new emergency situation were to arise again.
27.   The reservoir has not reached its maximum level since November 1993.  It is
conceivable that an increase in the head, and therefore pressure, may create new
seepage paths or increase the flow in the existing ones. If equipment is on site, costs
can be minimized and treatment could commence immediately. ENEE will carry out a
training program for the people involved in grouting and electromechanical
maintenance and the members of the PSD will continue to assess the on site situation at
least once a year.
J.     Key Lessons Learned
28.   The main lessons are project related. The first lesson is that a qualified and motivated
project team on the borrower side is the key factor for the successful implementation of
a project.  ENEEs on-site engineers contributed substantially to the successful
achievement of the project objectives.
29.   A second lesson is that flexibility of all parties and development of mutual trust and
respect among all actors involved in the project are prerequisites for successful project
implementation. In the case of the Morazan Project, various factors contributed to the
development of these conditions: i) ENEE's project team gained confidence and
professional respect from consultants and contractors; ii) ENEE assigned qualified and
7



motivated staff to the project; mii) the consultants gained the respect of ENEE and the
contractor because of their knowledge, qualifications and behavior; and iv) the
contractor, once it found a credible team on the owner's side, was able to effectively
perform the job.
30.   A third lesson is the confirmation of the importance of having a panel of qualified
experts to provide advice and guidance in the implementation of technically complex
projects. Borrowers are often reluctant to pay the high fees that these experts usually
charge. However, considering that they usually make just a few short visits, the total
cost of the experts is outweighed by the potential cost savings derived from their
technical advice. In the case of the Morazan Dam, the PSD was composed of the most
qualified experts in the world.
31.   A fourth lesson, applicable to projects with geological conditions similar to this one is
that the project design should be kept flexible enough to allow new and innovative
technologies to be applied. Additionally, a larger than usual allocation for physical
contigencies should be applied to these cases. The unique characteristics of the
Morazan Dam Project, led the project supervision team to utilize the grouting materials
described in the contract combined with new materials such as plastic sacks, concrete,
mortar, wooden balls, and wire mesh. The combination of conventional methods with
these on-site innovations was the key to achieving the objectives.

5.    DESARROLLO DE LOS TRABAJOS Y SU EFECTO EN EL PRESUPUESTO
Los trabajos enmarcados bajo el Contrato 070-92 fueron planificados para superar
las condiciones anormales de filtraciones excesivas que ilegaban al Pozo Norte de
las Casa de Maquinas Subterrinea y las altas presiones en la fundaci6n de la Presa
Francisco Morazan, con enfasis en el tratamiento de las Fallas Geol6gicas HI y IV.
Originalmente los trabajos comprendian la perforaci6n de 52,000 metros lineales,
la inyecci6n de 12,800 toneladas de cemento, revestir galerias (615 m3 de
concreto), y la perforaci6n de un tuinel de drenaje de aproximadamente 100 metros
de longitud. Esta parte se denomina Etapa I del Proyecto.
Por la inforrnaci6n obtenida mediante los sondeos de investigaci6n y la evaluaci6n
continua del desarrollo de los trabajos por parte del personal de ENEE y sus
consultores, se encontr6 una condici6n de roca muy deteriorada principalmente en
las zonas de las fallas geol6gicas, a traves de las cuales circulaba la mayor parte de
los flujos desde el embalse erosionando grandes cantidades de arcilla, por tanto,
para contrarrestar este fen6meno y lograr el objetivo propuesto se concluy6 que
era necesario modificar el esquema del tratamiento mediante la perforaci6n e
inyecci6n de varias filas superpuestas para formar un bloque resistente y mas

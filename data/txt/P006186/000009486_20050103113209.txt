3. Assessment of Development Objective and Design, and of Quality at Entry

3.1 Original Objective:

The objectives of the Environment, Industry & Mining Project for US$11 million (SDR 7.4

million) approved by the World Bank Board on December 21, 1995 were, according to the Staff
Appraisal Report (SAR): (i) to improve the regulatory framework of the mining and industrial
sectors in Bolivia with respect to the environment, and to support the implementation of such
improved framework; (ii) to assist the Borrower in the remediation of pollution stocks in the
mining sector; and (iii) to assist Municipalities in obtaining financial resources to improve
environmental conditions and to alleviate poverty in the mining communities located within the
jurisdiction of such Municipalities.

The Nordic Development Fund (NDF) supported the initiative and joined the project as a
co-financier. The NDF contributed US$7.5 million (SDR 5 million).

Originally, the project complemented another loan that was being prepared by the Inter-American
Development Bank (IDB). However due to a decision to change its business priorities in Bolivia,
before the WB loan became effective, the IDB decided not to pursue its project and some of the
elements of this loan were incorporated into the WB's project design.

Country and Sectoral Context: In order to understand the overall project objectives, it is
necessary to describe the context in which the project originated:

Mining is an important sector in Bolivia: Since the sixteenth century Bolivia has been a
commercial producer of mining products, predominantly silver and tin. Mining production, in
value terms, reached a peak US$756 million in gross metal value in 1980. As international
markets slowed among other factors, production value fell to US$139 million in 1986; it later
recovered reaching US$367 million in 1991. Mining was Bolivia's main export and in 1991 it
accounted for more than 40 percent of the country's total export earnings.

Since the 1950s, production has been dominated by the State-owned Bolivian Mining Corporation
(Corporación Minera de Bolivia - COMIBOL). Early in this project's preparation, the Bolivian
Government decided to restructure its participation in the mining sector.        COMIBOL was
withdrawn from production and began focusing exclusively on the administration of mining
concessions. COMIBOL began opening its mines and smelters to private sector participation and
the share of the private sector expanded significantly. Simultaneously, the Government launched
its Capitalization Program (supported by the WB's Capitalization Program Adjustment Credit --
Credit No. C2761) to attract private investment to key state-owned enterprises and to distribute
Government owned shares to privately-managed pension funds for the benefit of the Bolivian
people at large.

This project was a continuation of Bank involvement: During project preparation, the Mining
Rehabilitation project [financed by an IDA loan (Credit No. C2013)] and the Capitalization
Program were underway. Accordingly, the Environmental, Industry and Mining project consisted
of an ongoing cross-sectoral involvement in industrial, mining, and environmental issues in Bolivia
that placed the Bank in a unique position to mobilize efforts to address the complex issues
involved with pollution abatement.       In addition, mining sector restructuring was aimed at
establishing an enabling environment for private development in the sector through the reform and
modernization of the legal and institutional framework.



                                                - 2 -

Consistency with the Country Assistance Strategy: The project was fully consistent with the
1994 Country Assistance Strategy (CAS) for Bolivia, which focused on eliminating the remaining
structural constraints to growth and expanding the availability of resources for poverty and human
capital development in a sustainable environment. In its proposed lending program, the CAS
contemplated three areas of initiatives: (i) rationalizing the role of the State, (ii) poverty and
human resource development and (iii) natural resource management. This Environment, Industry
and Mining project supported CAS objectives by assisting the Government in addressing its
environmental liabilities arising from privatization, and fostering private development of the
mining and industrial sectors in a more environmentally sustainable fashion.

Weak Environmental Management: Historically, Bolivian economic activities focused mainly
on extraction, agricultural and forestry industries, textiles and light manufacturing grew with little
concern for environmental protection or workers health and safety. The manufacturing sector
was relatively undeveloped, which further complicated its environmental impact.            Pollution
originated from the discharges of untreated effluents to rivers or already-taxed municipal sewage
systems.    Localized air pollution was also present in mining and industry -- from smelters,
refineries, or cement plants. Mining activities, especially those chosen as project subjects (i.e.
those under COMIBOL) were pollution sources that affected surface and ground water, soil, and
air. The mining sector was the source of conflicts between mining and other civil communities in
places like Potosi and Oruro, threatened aquatic life as in Lake Poopó, and contaminated drinking
water in La Paz from abandoned tailings, posing a major threat to the well-being of miners and
their families, in particular and Bolivians in general. Tailings from mills, waste rock, underground
and open-pit mine works, roads, wastewater ponds, landfills, and smelters were among the
potential sources of mining-related pollution identified during project preparation.        Bolivian
mining had a major negative impact on the natural as well as human environment through: (i)
consumption of scarce or non-renewable natural resources, (ii) altering the landscape, (iii)
contamination of surface waters, (iv) accumulation of solid wastes, (v) dust and gases emissions ,
and (vi) occupational health and safety hazards.

Government Strategy: The Government did not have an explicit strategy for the environment and
the mining sector. A governmental Environmental Assessment had been commissioned that laid
the foundations for preparing the project. It and was published as the "Secretaria Nacional de
Medio Ambiente Ministerio de Minería y Metalurgia."

There was an institutional weakness and responsibilities among organizations were not clearly
demarcated:      The project followed on the footstep of environmental policies and laws
promulgated in 1992 including a National Environmental Action Plan. The 1992 environmental
law created the National Secretariat for the Environment (SENMA), which later was subsumed
under a new Ministry of Sustainable Development and Environment (MSDE). MSDE had a small
staff with limited practical experience. Also, the 1992 overarching environmental law lacked a
clear definition and demarcation of institutional responsibilities.       The mining code lacked
environmental provisions. Most mining activities were supervised by the Ministry of Mines and
Metallurgy (MMM). However, exploration and exploitation rights, as well as permits, were
allocated by the Superintendence of Mines, which was part of the separate Court of Mining.



                                                 - 3 -

MMM had an environmental unit and were in the process of drafting a regulation during project
preparation.

3.2 Revised Objective:

During the midterm review in August 2000, the Government and the Bank introduced changes in
the original project objectives and reframed objective (iii) to assist Municipalities in obtaining
financial resources to improve environmental conditions and alleviate poverty in the mining
communities located within the jurisdiction of such Municipalities. The new objectives focussed
on supporting mining communities by addressing urgent environmental problems through
CDD-type initiatives. The two other objectives were deemed achievable under a narrower scope.

3.3 Original Components:

The Staff Appraisal Report (SAR) identified four components to achieve the stated project
objectives.

Component A -- Regulatory Framework:              (US$4.0 million) Component A was aimed at
improving environmental management, both at the national level and in major regions by financing
two studies. One important output from the various studies would be a set of agreed action plans
for Santa Cruz, Cochabamba and El Alto, which could form the basis for future environmental
improvement projects in each region.

The first study involved data collection and analyses to identify mines and industries that have
significant environmental impacts and to establish baseline water quality data for selected surface
and subterranean water bodies. Also, this study was planned to include an evaluation of the
potential costs and benefits of pollution prevention measures in selected industries. Thus, the first
study's activities were planned to include:

(i)     design and implementation of data collection systems that will supply environmental
information to the Borrower's general environmental management information system in order to
identify, mines and other industries with a potential negative environmental impact and the
provision of training;
(ii)    design and implementation of a strategy to monitor the quality of water in Bolivian river
basins, and
(iii)   an evaluation of the potential costs and benefits of measures to prevent pollution in
Bolivia, including, in leather-tanning, meat processing, brick-making, sugar refineries, breweries
and distilleries, and cement plants.

The second study was aimed at policy development and implementation, which included:

(i)     provision of technical assistance and training to the Borrower in order to assist the
Ministry of Sustainable Development and Environment (MSDE) and the Ministry of Economic
Development (MED) to develop and implement policies and procedures to carry out
environmental impact assessments in Bolivia;
(ii)    design of a system of incentives to prevent or reduce pollution in mines and other



                                                 - 4 -

industries in Bolivia, including the provision of training required for the implementation of such
system;
(iii)   design and implementation of a system to finance remediation of pollution stocks from
mines or located on mine sites under the responsibility of State-owned entities in Bolivia,
including the provision of training;
(iv)    identification of hazardous waste sources and characterization of hazardous waste, and
thereafter, design and implementation of a framework to regulate the treatment of such hazardous
waste, including the provision of training required to implement such framework;
(v)     a review of the ambient quality and discharge standards for the mining and industrial
sectors, and thereafter, if necessary, improvement of such standards, including the provision of
training required to apply such improved standards;
(vi)    design and implementation of a strategy to monitor and enforce environmental laws and
regulations in Bolivia, including the provision of training; and
(vii)   design and implementation of a strategy to convey information to the public in Bolivia
concerning environmental and health risks in the mining and other industrial sectors, including the
provision of training.

Component B -- Laboratory Upgrading: (US$2.0 million) This component was aimed at
implementing a program to foster the development of credible laboratory testing services in
Bolivia. The component was planned to finance:
(i)     consulting services, study tours, and short training courses;
(ii)    participation in proficiency testing programs;
(iii)   studies to assist the achievement of international accreditation;
(iv)    preparation of market surveys, feasibility studies, business plans, and related facility
improvement programs;
(v)     training to improve the quality of laboratory operations or field sampling methods;
(vi)    capital improvement programs for IG (Geological Research Institute at the University of
San Andres ­ Instituto de Ecología Investigaciones Geológicas), IE (Ecological Institute at
University of San Andres ­ Instituto de Ecología), or IBNORCA (Bolivian Institute of Standards
and Quality ­ Instituto Boliviano de Normalizacion y Calidad) including services, goods and civil
works; and
(vii)   activities to develop a professional association of laboratories in Bolivia.

Component C -- Remediation Investments: (US$6.0 million) Component C was developed to
finance environmental improvement interventions in selected mining communities. Two main
activities were identified under this component:

(i)     Financing eligible subprojects: Eligible subprojects that were to be financed under this
subcomponent included potable water, sanitation, urban paving for dust control, remediation of
mine tailing piles, control of acid mine drainage flows, among other things, which were
interventions in environmental management measures.
(ii)    Provide technical assistance (TA) to assist communities, including Municipalities, in
accessing non-project sources for priority investments which did not satisfy the eligibility criteria
established by the project in selected mining communities.




                                                 - 5 -

Component D -- Technical Assistance and Training to Support Environmental Management
in Mining: (US$4.5 million) Component D was aimed at strengthening MED's environmental
management capacity in the mining sector. Activities under this component included:

(i)     the design of engineering plans for remediation of pollution stocks in mines or mines sites
owned by the Borrower or State-Owned Entities;
(ii)    the design and implementation of monitoring programs that would collect data on air,
water, soil and health conditions;
(iii)   the design and dissemination of guidelines to enable mine operators to comply with
environmental regulations;
(iv)    the design of procedures to be followed in connection with the issuance of mine permits,
inspection of mines, enforcement of environmental regulations and closure and reclamation of
mine sites; and
(v)     carrying out of hydrological studies in COMIBOL's (Bolivian Mining Corporation ­
Corporación Minera de Bolivia) mines.

3.4 Revised Components:

In January 1998 based on the experience gained after two years of slow project implementation,
Components C and D were amended and a new Component E was added to the project:

Component C -- Remediation Investments: The scope was reduced to finance eligible activities
and related training. The technical assistance subcomponent became a new component E.

Component D -- Environmental Management in the Mining Sector: The component was
redefined to strengthening the Vice-Ministry of Mining and Metallurgy (VMMM) through:

(i)     preparation and dissemination of technical guidelines to enable mining operators to comply
with Bolivia's environmental policies,
(ii)    computer processing of environmental impact assessments and environmental permits,
(iii)   generation of a baseline environmental permits,
(iv)    generation of base-line environmental data,
(v)     provision of environmental training to staff of mining operators and Bolivia's authorities
involved in environmental issues in the mining sector,
(vi)    preparation of specific prevention and control programs in different regions and mining
sub-sectors in Bolivia, and
(vii)   introduction of environmental management systems in Bolivia's mining sector.

Component E -- Technical Assistance to Municipalities affected by mining (US$330,000):
This component intended to provide technical assistance to Municipalities for obtaining financial
resources to improve environmental conditions and for alleviating poverty in mining communities
located within their jurisdiction.

The design or the components was linked to the project's development objectives and
incorporated some of the relevant lessons from previous Bank projects in the country and in the



                                                - 6 -

mining sector. The components were in line with the objectives proposed in the SAR and if they
had been fully implemented they would have allowed the project to fulfill its objectives. However,
their design was, as the whole project, overambitous and demanded at the end extensive redesigns
and amendments in order to achieve at least some of the objectives. It is worth mentioning that
although the SAR did mention some of the risks working in Bolivia, it could not foresee the
political and economic instability the country would be facing.

Midterm Review August 2000 (MTR):
The MTR was scheduled for 1999, but due to project implementation delays it took place in
August 2000. Despite changes introduced in 1998, project execution was slow and generally
unsatisfactory. In particular the following reasons lead to a set of changes in the project's
components as a result of the MTR:
(i)     Slow implementation and poor quality of products delivered under component A by a
Nordic Firm, SGAB, contracted to implement this component.             SGAB did not deliver all
products, and some of the deliverables were of very poor quality. After several supervision
missions and requests from the TTL to the Bolivian Government the payments under the contract
were suspended and cancelled;
(ii)    Slow implementation under Component C. Moreover, the proposed remediation of San
José mine met several difficulties including cost estimates far beyond the project's budget, lack of
community agreement on the proposed solutions, failure to close the mine and concession of a
disposal site to a private operator under COMIBOL's control.

Component A -- Regulatory Framework: The scope of this component was reduced to focus on
local municipalities. The borrower and the NDF decided that the activities under this component
would be financed exclusively by the Nordic Development Fund.

Component B -- Laboratory Upgrading: During the midterm review, it was agreed that the
construction of four laboratories would commence based on their business plans, approved
construction plans, and a clear agreement on the exact obligation of each of the parties in the
completion and operation of the laboratories. The parties agreed that UNDP would manage
procurement.

Component C -- Remediation Investments:            In light of the difficulties encountered in the
remediation of the San José mine attempts to fully implement this component were abandoned and
US$1.8 million transferred to Component B and US$250,000 to Component E. The remaining
US$1.7 million were cancelled.

Component E -- Technical Assistance to Municipalities affected by mining:                This new
component would benefit from activities previously undertaken in Component C in particular
those related to the assessment of remediation options and design to finance pollution abatement
and remediation actions in selected sites affected by past or present mining activities. In the
context of the MTR process it was also agreed that it would be useful to have this Component
implemented through a CDD approach and handed over to an NGO.

The revision undertaken as a result of the Midterm Review tried to introduce a more realistic



                                                - 7 -

approach to the project's goals and objectives by limiting the scope of the components,
complementing some of the activities with others (upgrading with construction) and by providing
the project with the means necessary to achieve its objectives, for example through the
introduction of a new Component E with a strong CDD oriented focus.


3.5 Quality at Entry:

Overall quality at entry was unsatisfactory. Although the SAR was well prepared and correctly
identified the relevant risks (notably institutional ones), project design was too ambitious and
optimistic about the mitigation of these risks, which have proven intractable. Even though the
project design acknowledged some of these weaknesses/risks, it failed to fully incorporate
mechanisms to mitigate against them during implementation.

Furthermore overall political stability, national economic trends and ownership of the project by
the Government, which are three important ingredients of success, were not considered as
potential sources of risk. The project did not have enough Government ownership. The design
of the project failed to reflect the lessons learned, especially on the institutional weakness in
implementing projects in the mining sector, as the implementation of the previous Mining Sector
Rehabilitation.


4. Achievement of Objective and Outputs

4.1 Outcome/achievement of objective:

The first objective to improve the regulatory framework of the mining and industrial sector in
Bolivia with respect to the environment and to support the implementation of such a framework
would have required achievements on many fronts, including commitment and actions at the
national and local level, as well as a clear commitment of the private sector to take such a
framework seriously and comply with it. This objective was not achieved since the commitment
needed in the public and private sector to implement such a framework never materialized.

The second objective to assist the Borrower in the remediation of pollution stocks in the mining
sector, would have needed a clear and decisive action from the Government to advance in the
clean up. However, the Government delayed crucial decisions that would have supported the
implementation of actions needed to achieve this objective, thus impeding its satisfactory
completion .

The third objective to assist Municipalities in obtaining financial resources to improve
environmental conditions and to alleviate poverty in the mining communities located within their
jurisdiction, needed the communities' ownership to be successful. In this context, during the MTR
the objective was reformulated and a system to deal with contaminated areas through CDD
approaches under Component E was incorporated into the project. The involvement of local
communities and stakeholders was key the achievement of this objective.

4.2 Outputs by components:



                                                - 8 -

Component A -- Technical Assistance for Regulatory Framework:

This component is rated: Unsatisfactory.              This component originally aimed at financing
consulting services, and equipment and training to assist the government to improve
environmental management in the mining and industry sectors at the national and local level. This
component suffered also from low quality of its deliverables as well as conceptual design flaws.
For example, it intended to extrapolate local data collected in only two river basins to the whole
country. Due to these difficulties and delays in the implementation of the component it was
restructured and its scope reduced to local activities during the midterm review. The pretended
outputs at the national level were not achieved, however some of the Component's actions to
improve local capacity for environmental management were finalized such as:
(i)     Developing municipal environmental regulations
(ii)    Equipment for municipal environmental agencies
(iii)   Preparing printed guides

Component B -- Laboratory Upgrading:

This component is rated: Unsatisfactory. The aim of this component was to implement a
program to foster development of credible laboratory testing services in Bolivia.             This
component's main outputs were:

(i)     upgrading of four environmental analysis laboratories and one quality control laboratory;
(ii)    accreditation the Bolivian Institute of Standards and Quality (IBNORCA),
(iii)   training of laboratory technicians,
(iv)    strengthening of the National System of Laboratory Accreditation and the Association of
Bolivian Laboratories.

Upgrading of Laboratories: Originally the project did not envisage construction works but only
the upgrade of existing laboratories.      Because the existing facilities were old and largely
inadequate as deemed by the 2000 midterm review, it was agreed that four laboratories would be
built [two in La Paz, and one each in Cochabamba and in Santa Cruz]. The laboratories have
been constructed with project funds and counterparts from the hosting Universities. However,
the acquisition of equipment and furniture for these laboratories suffered from excessive delays
and was not executed at the project's closing date. In the context of the closing mission in
November 2003 it was agreed that the NDF would keep the project open until December 2004
and use the remaining funds to pay for equipment.

Capacity building and accreditation (subcomponents (ii), (iii) and (iv)): IBNORCA has been
accredited under ISO 9000. This component also included capacity building for and support to
the IBNORCA with spillovers to the Bolivian Accreditation Organization (OBA), the Bolivian
Institute of Metrology (IBMETRO). Support was also provided in training, computers and light
laboratory material. Funds have been used for a variety of activities including training in ISO
14000.




                                                 - 9 -

Based on the achievements, or lack thereof, it can be said that this component did only produce
part of the expected outcomes.

Component C -- Remediation Investments:

This   component      is    rated:  Unsatisfactory.   The    government's   response     in  crucial
decision-making was extremely slow. This caused delays that made it impossible to execute the
originally planned remediation investment for mining activities.        For example, a technical
remediation proposal presented in early 1999 was only accepted by VMMM and COMIBOL in
December 2000. Meanwhile, the main area designated for the remediation project in Oruro had
been conceded by COMIBOL to a private entrepreneur. There was also opposition to cease the
highly polluting water pumping from the San José Mine, which was taking place since mine
closure.    Moreover, the political manipulation around this component created unrealistic
expectations among the Oruro population on the potential impacts and benefits of the proposed
investment.

COMIBOL has been held responsible for planning and implementing remediation measures at San
José mine, an old historical mine close to Oruro. Although this component was cancelled during
project restructuring, it financed some preparatory activities such as:
(i)     a study to determine the different alternatives for remediation of several sites in San José
mine, which were considered sources of contaminations, and
(ii)     a study which elaborated and determined the hydrological condition of San José mine and
the hydrological connection between the San José mine and potable water contamination in Oruro
city.

These studies should have provided the basis for actual remediation to be undertaken in the future
by COMIBOL with its own resources and eventual support from other donors, which has not yet
materialized.

Component D -- Technical Assistance for Environmental Management in Mining:

This Component is rated: Marginally Satisfactory.             This component has produced some of its
expected outcomes:
Preparation and distribution of Technical Guidance Documents: Three guides have been
prepared, printed and distributed to stakeholders: Guide for Obtaining and Updating
Environmental Licenses for Mining and Metallurgical Activities, Environmental Guide for
Tailings Dams and Environmental Guide for Water Management in Mining and Metallurgical
Activities.

These guides have been presented in many workshops. Reportedly, interest was high in those
departments where mining is an important economic activity. These documents have been posted
on the Ministry's of Mining and Metallurgy web site and are available for download.

Environmental information system: This subcomponent was aimed at acquiring, storing, and
managing information related to mining leases, environmental licenses and environmental quality



                                                - 10 -

using geographical information systems. At present, the system is only partially available to the
public through Internet, but a much bigger part is stored internally in the VMMM.

Generation of baseline environmental information on Salares de Uyuni and Coipasa: A
detailed report is available, containing baseline data, analysis and several maps. It can be useful
for future decisions on the management of the salares, including zoning for mining, tourism,
conservation and other uses.

Institutional strengthening of the Vice-Ministry of Mining and Metallurgy:               The main
objective was to institutionalize the Environment Sectoral Unit within VMMM. During the
project, technical staff involved in reviewing environmental studies and in preparing and managing
the environmental information system was paid by the project. Institutionalization has been
achieved and these tasks are now performed by civil servants within the Vice-Ministry.

Comparing the proposed outcomes with the achievements of this component, once again the
component fell short of expectations and only marginally produced the expected outcomes.

Component E -- Technical Assistance to Municipalities affected by mining:

This component is rated: Satisfactory. This component focused on community-based
environmental management in mining watersheds and has been satisfactory. It involved the
identification of a number of degraded areas affected by past or present small scale mining
operations and the selection of a few areas for investment in environmental control measures to be
implemented by the local communities and administrations.

The proposed rehabilation of Laguna Pampa tailings and the construction of 3 acid mine water
treatment facilities have been finalized and are operating.

The activities under this component benefited from a stong CDD oriented approach and have
been managed through a NGO, Fundación Medmin that involved local communities in the
selection of alternatives, design, building and operation/maintenance, thus rooting their
commitment to the project's success. Local manpower has been used in construction, providing
temporary income to unemployed miners.

4.5 Institutional development impact:

The project resulted in limited institutional development within Bolivia. However, it had some
beneficial institutional impacts such as:

- Establishment of an environment unit within the Vice-Ministry of Mining and
Metallurgy. The VMMM established a small office in charge of reviewing environmental impact
statements and other environmental studies for the approval of new mining projects. The unit acts
as the "competent sectoral organ" under the environmental law of 1992.

- Capacity building within local governments. Eight municipalities benefited from specialized
consultancy to develop environmental regulations for industry and mining.           The proposed
regulations have been established in consultation with local civil servants and other interested
parties.

- Guidelines and manuals were produced, printed, distributed and are being used to direct
the implementation of regulatory environmental requirements. The project contributed to develop
common concepts among government officials, managers, consultants and technical staff.

- The environmental information system supports the mines register. Environmental
information can be systematically used in making decisions about mine leases.

- New partnerships were established for the environmental management in the mining sector:
for the first time ever, the Vice-Ministry of Mining and Metallurgy was able to work through a
specialized NGO (Fundación Medmin) to implement small projects in collaboration with the local
administration, miners' communities and cooperatives, and small/medium mining enterprises.
This experience created a demand for further actions, and about 20 localities have already
expressed their interest in such investment partnerships.

5. Major Factors Affecting Implementation and Outcome

5.1 Factors outside the control of government or implementing agency:


- Loss of momentum due to political and institutional change. Conflicts between legislators
and the government delayed the project's approval by Bolivia's Congress for more than one year,
prior to effectiveness. Crucial momentum was lost in the final phase of the project due to political
unrest and mass civil disobedience that engulfed Bolivia especially in 2002 and 2003. Faced with
these problems the Government was unable to timely conclude agreed actions and thus it



                                                     - 12 -

requested the project's closing date to be extended twice.

- Prices of Bolivian mineral commodities went down since project preparation and during
project implementation. From 1997 to 2000, the total value of mining exports from Bolivia fell
about 20 percent; an even steeper decline occurred in 2001-2003 for tin and silver. This created
large unemployment and slowed investment in the mining sector almost to a halt by 1998.
Perceptions among public officers, private investors, politicians and miners that environmental
management would add costs to an economic sector already in a competitiveness crisis might
have contributed to eroding support for the project.

- Limiting Procurement Conditions from the Nordic Development Fund (NDF). In line with
its mandate and its internal procurement rules the project's co-financier, NDF, imposed tough
conditions for the acquisition of goods and services, by demanding for example significant Nordic
content (usually 80 percent). These conditions required the international bidding of equipment,
such as computers and software that could otherwise have been acquired locally at better prices
and with better technical support.         There was also little interest from Nordic firms in the
procurement packages for laboratory equipment, either because they were too small or too
diversified; moreover, they did not have a comparative advantage in providing specialized lab
equipment such as the one needed for the laboratories.


5.2 Factors generally subject to government control:


Variable ownership and lack of political will by changing Administrations.                        The
administration that prepared the project left office before the project's effectiveness and there was
a lack of ownership by the succeeding administration. Frequent changes of high officials caused
extreme instability in the Government's public policies. Ministers changed up to five times in one
year, with consequent changes in lower positions. Some officials have shown a genuine interest in
the outcomes of the project; others have tried to obtain political benefits by interfering in the
project's decisions and staffing. These changes compromised project implementation for the
good part of the project implementation.

Institutional capacity of implementing agencies. Originally, the project was to be implemented
by the then Ministry of Sustainable Development and Environment, and the Ministry of Economic
Development. At effectiveness, the task was delegated to two newly created vice-ministries ­
namely the Vice-Ministry of Natural Resources and the Environment under the Ministry of
Sustainable Development and the Vice-Ministry of Mining and Metallurgy under the MED. By
taking away political visibility the institutional redesign had a negative impact on project
implementation. In a highly volatile political environment the issue was taken out of the radar
screen of the decision makers. As a consequence the reforms needed to implement the objectives
agreed in the project lost ground and became relegated in Bolivia's political agenda. Last but not
least, competition for scarce budgetary funds within each Ministry left the vice-ministries with
limited technical and financial resources for project implementation and the project without
counterpart funding.




                                                    - 13 -

5.3 Factors generally subject to implementing agency control:


Inadequate institutional arrangements. The two parallel arrangements for project
implementation (under Ministry of Economic Development and MSDE) were a bureaucratic
response to the uncoordinated and insufficiently defined institutional responsibility in
environmental management. The problem was compounded by the lack of political will to ensure
effective cooperation and communication between the project coordinators. The two ministries
were finally able to agree on a consolidated coordination to upgrade the laboratories under
Component B, during the second extension period. However, deteriorating country conditions
hampered the successful implementation of this agreement.

Contracting an NGO (Fundación Medmin) to handle component E. This strategy led to the
successful implementation of this component, with public participation, and compliance with
timetables and budget.

Extreme weaknesses in procurement.                In light of the weakness shown by the Bolivian
authorities in procuring the upgrades for the laboratories under component B, it was decided
during the midterm review in August 2000 to ask UNDP Bolivia to manage the contracting
process for the construction of the laboratories. However, remediation through UNDP was not
adequate due to UNDP's own lack of capacity in Bolivia to handle specialized procurement
processes under Bank rules, as well as lack of technical expertise in investigating procurement
irregularities.

Financial Management (FM): The project did not require complex financial management
arrangements. However, both PCU in charge of the execution of the project did not perform at
the same level. While the PCU in charge of components A and B overall showed and acceptable
performance, high staff rotation and a serious lack of technical capacity and counterpart funding
plagued the PCU in chage of the implementation FM of components C, D and E. At restructuring
and with the support of the Bank's team, the PCU for these components was able to put
procedures in place and to take appropriate corrective measures to overcome some of its
shortcomings. Since project restructuring FM improved slightly.

Audit Reports: In general terms, audit reports were acceptable for both PCUs. However,
Bolivia's General Comptrollers Office observed statements for project expenditures for US$1.1
million to pay consultant fees from COMIBOL's own funds for components C, D and E. The
results of this investigation are still pending.

Due to the difficulties encountered by the MED to get its budget approved during its FY 2004, it
became impossible for Bolivia's authorities to comply with the financial provisions regarding the
preparation of the closing audit. As a consequence the borrower was not able to present the audit
in time for project closing and will have to cover the cost of the audit from its own budgetary
resources. The borrower indicated that it was willing to do so and is expected to be in a position
to submit the project audit at the latest at the end of March 2005.

6. Sustainability

6.1 Rationale for sustainability rating:

The project's sustainability is unlikely. However the probability of maintaining some of the few
project's achievements is different for each of its component.

Component A -- Regulatory Framework:

Some achievements may possibly be maintained because different stakeholders have been involved
in drafting municipal regulations and have manifested a certain degree of commitment towards its
effective implementation.         In this context official agreements have been signed between the
Ministry of Sustainable Development and beneficiary municipalities, thus providing the basis for
further decentralization of responsibilities.

Component B -- Laboratory Upgrading:

At this point it is not possible to make a final judgment, because the laboratories have been built,
but their equipment has not been procured. In an optimistic scenario funds provided by NDF will
be successfully used to acquire all equipment and materials. Once finished the laboratories will be
owned and operated by the beneficiary universities under approved business plans. However, if
this procurement is unsuccessful, expensive unfurnished labs might be designated for other uses or
simply left empty.       Thus, the success of this component is now entirely dependent on the
satisfactory procurement of lab equipment under NDF and the financial and political commitment
of the Bolivian Government to continue to maintain them.

Component C-- Remediation Investments:

The inherent difficulties in the implementation of this component and its limited results and
cancellation make the sustainability of its few achievements unlikely.

Component D -- Technical Assistance for Environmental Management in Mining:

Although some of its proposed results were achieved, the overall sustainability of this component
is unlikely. The forces against long-term sustainability are the lack full ownership of project's
outcomes on the part of new Government. The recurrent changes of ministers and vice-ministers
contributed to the lack of ownership and created political pressures on staff of the environmental
unit in the VMMM. Although the environmental unit created in the Vice-Ministry of Mining and



                                                   - 15 -

Metallurgy was incorporated as part of this project to its structure in January 2003, continuous
funding and political commitment continue to be a problem.

Component E -- Technical Assistance to Municipalities affected by Mining:

This component features likely sustainability because it has been planned, developed and
implemented under a participatory approach. Local partners have made strong commitments to
the maintenance of the four pollution abatement projects; moreover, there is popular support
towards the long-term operation and maintenance of these pollution control structures.

6.2 Transition arrangement to regular operations:

Transition for outcomes under Component A: Regulatory Framework has been arranged through
agreements signed with each beneficiary municipality. Agreements have also been signed with the
Universities involved in the upgrade of laboratories in Component B. Appropriate arrangements
for some of the outcomes of Component D: Technical Assistance for Environmental Management
in Mining consisted in the incorporation of the environmental unit into the Ministry's regular
services. Transition of the four pollution control installations built under component E to operate
on an ongoing basis was negotiated during design.               The latter takes advantage of a
participatory-community-based process, empowering key stakeholders by assuming symbolic
ownership, thus enhancing the prospect of long-term sustainability.

7.1 Lending:

8. Lessons Learned


 The project having taken nearly a decade from design to closing has shown problems (as many
 other projects in Bolivia) related to the interference of political cycles in project implementation
 as a consequence of the change in administrations and political, economic and social unrest.
 The long implementation cycle suffered from a loss of the authorities' interest and commitment
 in project objectives and execution. This loss of interest became evident when approval of
 counterpart funding was substantially delayed, in particular during the last two years of project
 implementation.

Project Preparation and Appraisal:

   Design: Project design should be in line with country's priorities and needs, but also should
   take into account the policy environment and institutional context. Project objectives and
   components should be simple and focused. A complex and ambitious project design such as
   this should have a clear commitment from the local authorities and stakeholders. It should
   also take into account the political, economic, policy realities together with implementation
   capacity. Also, trying to incorporate elements from other loans, such as the one from the
   IDB, should only be done if it complements the original proposal and does not take away the
   focus from its original objective.

   The institutional arrangements put in place for this project were simply to cumbersome. Two
   Viceministries implementing different components of one project through two separate PCU
   created confusion and inefficiencies in project implementation.

   Risk Assessment and Management: A comprehensive, rigorous and realistic assessment of
   risks, in terms of country capacity, institutional effectiveness, fiduciary aspects, economic and
   financial viability, as well as other exogenous factors should be completed, and mitigating
   strategies and action plans to address them as/if they occur, should be incorporated to the
   project design.

   Fiduciary Aspects: Project preparation team should properly assess the procurement and
   financial management capacity of the implementing agencies, as well as its readiness for
   implementation. The team should put in place mechanisms to strengthen weak areas and
   make sure that the World Bank Procurement, Financial Management, Disbursement guidelines
   and procedures are fully understood by the Borrower. As it was the case in this credit, project
   implementation could be delayed significantly if the project implementation unit staff is not
   equipped with knowledge, resources and skills required to access, use and control loan funds.

   Focus on Results: A good monitoring and evaluation system with focus on results and
   outcomes should be an integral part of the project design. Baseline data for implementation
   and impact indicators, that will enable meaningful comparisons and refocusing of project



                                                - 19 -

  activities as needed during implementation, should be collected at the preparation stage.

Project Implementation:

  Staff Turnover, both from the Government's the Bank's sides, was a major problem for this
  project.    Bank management should ensure continuity in supervision teams to avoid
  interruptions or delays with implementation. Similarly, the task team (and management, as
  needed) should be in continuous communication/coordination with the Borrower to ensure
  that managerial and technical appointments at the project implementation unit are based on
  qualifications (and are free of political considerations) and continuity of staffing is maintained
  throughout implementation to maintain institutional knowledge and memory, and continue
  capacity building.

  Bank Supervision of the project should be continuous, supported by proper budgets and skill
  mix. Supervision missions should be properly planned and executed and interaction with the
  counterparts should continue outside the formal missions to be able to provide timely and
  appropriate support as needed. Follow up of missions should be made through appropriate
  channels and at appropriate levels, raising potential issues and seeking guidance from the
  management. As mentioned in the 2001 QAG review in cases in which irregularities are being
  uncovered, project supervision should be intensified, regardless of the department's budgetary
  constrains.

  Candor in supervision reporting (including implementation performance, progress towards
  achievement of objectives, overall risks and other performance indicators) and an adequate
  filing system and procedure are critical to ensure proper supervision and follow up.
  Management should remain involved and be proactive in spotting and addressing problems.
  Supervision and project management have to be closely coordinated with partners, such as the
  NDF, in particular in a case in which the partner, in this case the NDF does not have enough
  capacity to supervise project implementation.

  Project Extension should be based on clear and realistic action plans that will contribute to
  the project achievements, and count on a firm commitment from the authorities towards
  project implementation (counterpart funding etc) and closing (timely presentation of audit,
  financial management and procurement).         The first extension was justified based on the
  commitment of the borrower to accelerate implementation and take necessary actions to close
  the project. Despite the agreements reached during the closing workshop in November 2003 it
  is not clear how the second extension of the project for a period of 6 months would have
  contributed to its success, considering that the counterparts had already shown difficulties in
  procuring the necessary counterpart funds necessary for project closing.

  Fiduciary Specialists should be an integral part of the project team (as opposed to one-off
  involvement as needed) to provide required technical support to the project unit, pinpoint any
  potential issues and act on them before they become major problems. This will ensure
  continuity of implementation and prevent risks to the Bank's reputation.




                                               - 20 -

    Focus on Results, rather than inputs and activities, would maximize project benefits.
    Continuous monitoring and assessment of achievements against desired outcomes would
    provide the proper direction for the project (in terms of scaling up/down, restructuring) when
    changes occur in the environment within which the project is operating.

    The project team should work with the counterparts to ensure that the beneficiaries and
    stakeholders at large remain involved at the project execution stage, so that the project does
    not lose its priority and visibility. With stakeholder support, implementation could remain on
    track and achievements could be sustained, even at times of political/economic turmoil.

Annex 3. Economic Costs and Benefits


Mining is an important sector in Bolivia's economy. However due to its dismal environmental
record, mining activities in Bolivia had a major negative impact on its natural and human
environment. The project's economic benefits are thus a function of its capacity to create
conditions to support the improvement in human health, economic infrastructure and the removal
of impediments to fresh private investments. If fully implemented, the establishment and
upgrading of laboratories will have contributed to increasing the credibility of Bolivia's capacity to
monitor the environmental impacts of its mining industry, thus contributing to the overall
economic benefits outlined before. Indirectly the project has also contributed to help improve the
worker's health and safety conditions in existing and future mining and industrial enterprises. In
the longer term, the project has encouraged the establishment of programs to help prevent
pollution at the firm level, thus creating a more efficient and sustainable framework for the mining
and industrial sector. Through its training and capacity building programs implemented in the
context to the preparation of various studies, the project has contributed to strengthen the
national manpower base in environmental management.

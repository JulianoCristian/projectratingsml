3. Assessment of Development Objective and Design, and of Quality at Entry

3.1 Original Objective:
The main objective of the project was to increase access to high-quality, up-to-date and cost-effective
training for public and private decision-makers and implementers so as to increase their capacity to design,
plan and manage economic and social development policies. A secondary objective was to establish a center
of excellence in the region for distance learning and exchange of information and experience. The project
was to accomplish these objectives by providing access to high-quality training and information on the
latest advancements through distance learning using state-of-the-art communications technology.

The Project Development Objectives (PDO) were relevant because they were well aligned with the Country
Assistance Strategy (CAS). The two main objectives of the CAS (January 1998) were to (i) ensure
continued rapid and sustainable economic growth, and (ii) maintain social sustainability of the reform
program.

The first objective would be achieved by (i) helping to sustain macroeconomic stability, (ii) improving the
environment for the efficient operation of the private sector, (iii) alleviating infrastructure bottlenecks, and
(iv) ensuring long-term sustainable management of the environment.

The second objective would be achieved by (i) making the provision of education and health services more
widely accessible at a better quality level, and (ii) directly focusing on social protection. The Global
Development Learning Network (GDLN) would directly contribute to improving the environment for the
efficient operation of the public and private sector by building more effective capacity through in-service
training of professionals in the public and the private sectors.

After 5 years of implementation, the project DOs are still in line with the objectives of the current CAS
(March 5, 2003) to support the PRSP Pillar No. 2: Capacity-building and Promotion of Basic Social
Services (pp. 25 and 60).

3.2 Revised Objective:
The original objectives of the project remained unchanged.

3.3 Original Components:
The project as described in the Project Appraisal Document (PAD) consisted of the following:

Project Component I - Distance Learning Center Retrofitting (US$1.1 million)

1. Building facilities on the site of the ENAM (National School of Administration) for the establishment of
the Distance Learning Center (DLC), including:
(i) a videoconference room with a 30-person capacity;
(ii) a computer room outfitted for 30 computer stations (hardware, software and Internet hook-ups);
(iii) a technical and administrative center; and
(iv) installation and/or upgrading of electrical and telephone wiring and other structural requirements.

2. Equipment of the DLC, including:
(i) a Very Small Aperture Terminal (VSAT) (satellite communication terminal);
(ii) video, telecommunications and microprocessor equipment;
(iii) office and classroom furniture; and
(iv) various other equipment for DLC operation and security.



                                                       - 2 -

Project Component 2 - Support for DLC Operation (US$1.0 million)
The following sub-components aimed to assist the DLC to begin its operation and become financially
viable:
(a) financing of DLC operating costs on a decreasing basis over the first three years of operation;
(b) purchase of a vehicle to facilitate transport and marketing of DLC services; and
(c) technical assistance for the training of Center staff, periodic evaluation of Center operation and
management effectiveness, and establishment of financial accounts and their annual audit.

The design of both components was clearly linked to the objectives described above. To provide access to
high-quality training and information on latest advancements, it was essential to build relevant facilities
followed by support for start-up and smooth operation of activities. Six months after Board approval, the
construction of all facilities was completed, staff were hired and equipment was installed, and the DLC
became fully operational.

3.4 Revised Components:
The two components of the project remained unchanged.

3.5 Quality at Entry:
The quality at entry is rated unsatisfactory because the project design contained several weaknesses:

(a) Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E): In a LIL, M&E is a critical instrument to assess the success of
the pilot project and draw lessons for future operations. However, this was one of the first GDLN
operations in Africa and was placed on a fast-track preparation cycle . As a result, there was not sufficient
time to design a complete M&E plan including quantitative and qualitative instruments. The impact
indicators proposed in the PAD (training cost, utilization rate, operating cost/income ratio and number of
course providers) were only quantitative tools, used to assess the impact of the GDLN services in terms of
productivity of senior civil servants, quality of policy formulation and implementation, and timeliness of
economic and sector strategy implementation. However, a qualitative approach would have enabled the
project to also assess the impact on beneficiaries. Measures might have included the reporting of success
stories following trainings and/or systematic surveys similar to what was done during the 2004 GDLN
Evaluation (see para. 4.5). However, such non-financial qualitative data was not specified and detailed in
the project design.

(b) Construction Design: Due to the fast-track preparation, a full analysis of the physical facility
requirements was not done. As a result, the construction of additional areas for break-out rooms,
preparatory meetings and storage was not inculded in the original architectural design.

(c) Institutional Setup: The DLC was built in ENAM's premises in view of complementing and building a
partnership between the two institutions. Moreover, the proximity of the center was to facilitate access to
the DLC for the public sector. But during appraisal, following a misunderstanding between the Director of
ENAM and the Bank mission, the Director of ENAM was convinced that the DLC would be part of his
institution. This misunderstanding generated a conflict between the two entities which led the President of
Senegal to issue a new Decree creating the DLC as autonomous and part of the Ministry of "tutelle". This
was a strong indicator of the importance of guaranteeing the autonomy of the DLC both in terms of
physical location and an appropriate institutional setup.

The DLCs currently under construction (in Mali, Malawi, Madagascar, and Kenya) have significantly
improved the quality of their design, based on the earlier experience with the Senegal DLC.

4. Achievement of Objective and Outputs

4.1 Outcome/achievement of objective:
Overall achievement of objectives under the project is satisfactory.

The first development objective is rated satisfactory : increase access to high-quality, up-to-date and
cost-effective training for public and private decision-makers and implementers to increase their capacity to
design, plan and manage economic and social development policies.

         (i) The high quality of training was achieved through the excellent and various course providers
         which included World Bank Institute (WBI), HEC-Paris/Centre d'Etudes sur le Blanchiment et la
         Corruption (CEBC), Institut International d'Administration Publique de Paris (IIAP), Ecole
         Nationale d'Administration de Paris (ENA), Institut FORHOM/BDPA de la Rochelle in France,
         Centre d'Etudes et de Recherches sur le Développement International de l'Université de Clermont
         Ferrand (CERDI), SOPEL International, Centre de Formation de la Profession Bancaire de Paris
         (CFPB), Centre d'Etudes Financières, Economiques et Bancaires (CEFEB) de l'Agence Française
         de Développement, Agence EDUFRANCE, GAPSET de France, ISTOM de Paris, the Maastricht
         University in the Netherlands, and the British Council. In total, there were over 50 providers which
         offered training, conferences, seminars and roundtables.

         (ii) Clients mainly coming from public sector expressed great interest in the training programs
         offered by the DLC and expressed their satisfaction in the quality of the courses through regular
         assessments that were made after each training event. The positive impact was further
         demonstrated by the fact that the number of clients was constantly increasing and they gave
         positive feedback on the contribution of the training programs on their work.

The secondary development objective is rated satisfactory: establish a center of excellence in the region for
distance learning and exchange of information and experience. The facilities were relevant and adequate
despite a few shortages in the facility design. The management of the Senegal DLC is cited as an example
in the Africa region and plays a mentoring role for other DLCs. For example, some DLC managers,
especially from the West African region, often asked for advice from the Senegal DLC Management to
adress current issues. Moreover, officials of countries which were preparing to have their own DLC
(Mauritania, Burkina Faso, Kenya and Malawi) actively sought information on how the Senegalese
experience eveolved, with the objective to learn from it and to replicate the model.

4.2 Outputs by components:
Project Component I - Distance Learning Center Retrofitting (US$1.1 million)

The overall output for this component is satisfactory. The PPF allowed fast financing of the retrofitting as
well as VSAT and other equipment installation on the premises of the National School of Administration.
It took about six months to complete the work. This enabled the DLC to participate in the inaugural video
conference chaired by Mr. Wolfensohn on June 21, 2000, and begin operating fully. In the beginning, the
main technical problem during operation was related to the generator. Inadequate voltage and power
failures were affecting the training sessions. This problem was rapidly solved.

Project Component 2 - Support for DLC Operation (US$2.3 million)




                                                      - 4 -

The overall output for this component is satisfactory. The DLC team was recruited in a timely manner and
the DLC Director conducted a market study and developed a Business Plan in the first year. Based on this,
three types of training were pursued ­ video conferencing, E-learning, and local training ­ in addition to the
use of the multimedia room for internet connections. When all types of training were included, the number
of student-days logged was nearly double the PAD targets ­ 6582 versus a target of 1000 in Year 1; 6950
versus a target of 2500 in Year 2; and 8757 (from January 1, 2003 to April 30, 2004, closing date)versus
a target of 3750 in Year 3 and 5000 in Year 4 (full year basis). The DLC established itself as a center of
high quality in Senegal. Among trainees that used videoconferencing training, 63 percent came from public
sector agencies, 28 percent from private companies, 5 percent from NGOs, and 3 percent from
international organizations. Furthermore, the DLC made regular progress towards self-sufficiency over the
project period (see para. 4.4 for details).

4.3 Net Present Value/Economic rate of return:
The project was cost-effective (Annex 3 on Economic Costs and Benefits provides more details). The main
conclusion is that the average cost of training delivery through the DLC is far less than the training and
course delivery system for the "target market" in Senegal (high-level government officials and private sector
and community leaders). Specifically, the DLC average cost was estimated at US$110/student-day by end
of project as compared to $296/student-day for the current system for training (i.e. training abroad of at
least ten days duration). In fact, the DLC actual average cost of training reached US$27/student-day at
project completion.

4.4 Financial rate of return:
During project implementation, the DLC achieved a financial coverage rate of 36 percent in year 1; 61
percent in year 2; 76 percent in year 3 (ending April 30, 2004); and 98 percent as of October 31, 2004.
This is compared to the projected PAD rates of 20 percent, 50 percent, 75 percent and 100 percent as of
December 31, 2004. The project performed well in terms of facility utilization rate (91 percent by Year 3
compared to the 60 percent originally envisaged), and in other performance indicators. The quantitative
targets were achieved and surpassed by the project (see more details in Annex 8).

4.5 Institutional development impact:
The institutional development impact of the project is rated substantial. The project had developed an
innovate model of learning through its high technology training services. At project completion, the DLC
was recognized as a high performing tool to strengthen capacity among public/private decision-makers.
More than fifty (50) different organizations against thirty (30) that were initially targeted benefited from
training offered through the DLC's high standard facilities.

The positive assessment of the Senegal DLC is in line with the main findings of the 2004 Evaluation on the
Quality and Impact of Programs Facilitated by the Global Development Learning Network (GDLN). A
sample of past participants in four countries (Brazil, Senegal, Tanzania, and Vietnam) completed a survey
which identified their level of satisfaction with a GDLN event or series and how they used the experience in
the weeks, months, or years following such events. Participants reported a high level of satisfaction and
assigned high ratings for the quality and relevance of the events they had attended. When asked how they
used their GDLN experience, 57 percent reported that they transmitted new knowledge or skills to others in
their organization, 47 percent applied what they had learned to develop plans and continued to work with
others at learning events, and 41 percent formally implemented approaches/strategies learned. The
respondents also indicated that the GDLN activity had enhanced their own professional development, and
to a lesser extent, the capacity of their organizations and the development of their countries.

NB: The survey involved four countries (Brazil, Senegal, Tanzania, and Vietnam). The 2004 Evaluation on the Quality and

Impact of Programs Facilitated by the Global Development Learning Network reported only aggregate data. The separate report
for each country is kept confidential. Therefore, the ICR author had a discussion over the phone with the authors of the

5. Major Factors Affecting Implementation and Outcome

5.1 Factors outside the control of government or implementing agency:
None.

5.2 Factors generally subject to government control:
(a) Training budget allocated to DLC services for the training of civil servants

The Government showed its commitment to the DLC by disbursing timely counterpart funds. Nevertheless,
the annual training budget allocated by the Government to the "Délégation au Management Publique" (in
charge of coordinating training programs within the administration) was very small (below US$60,000).
Accordingly, the last Bank supervision mission encouraged the Government to strengthen its support either
by increasing this global envelope for training or providing each ministry with a specific allocation
dedicated for the use of DLC's facilities.

(b) Institutional

The country's political context affected the DLC. The Government transition in 2000 led to several cabinet
reshuffles between 2000 and 2004. The Ministry of "tutelle" for the project changed three times within the
last four years, which affected the ability of the Borrower to provide consistently high visibility for the
project.

5.3 Factors generally subject to implementing agency control:
(a) Role of DLC Board

The DLC is governed by a Board comprising representatives of Government ministries, Senegalese
academic institutions, the private sector and civil society. The Board is chaired by an official elected from
among its members. During project implementation, the Board faltered in its role to monitor DLC
management, which included approving the annual business plan and program of activities, assessing the
Center's financial statements,         providing recommendations to help the Center become financially
self-sustaining, and deciding on the DLC's development policy. The DLC Board met only once during the
project lifetime. Despite this weakness, the outstanding performance of the appointed Director and his staff
played a crucial role in the generally good performance of the DLC. Due to the strong commitment of the
DLC staff, the DLC was successful in meeting its objectives and the absence of the Board's involvement in
the Center's activities did not have a negative impact. However, it would be important for the Board to
participate more actively in DLC management and to provide strategic guidance and support to its
activities. This would enable greater monitoring capabilities and provide added visibility to the Center since
Board members constitute a sample representation of the targeted beneficiaries.

(b) Marketing activities

After two years of operation, there was a need to update the initial Business Plan which was based on the
results of a `market survey` / needs assessment, carried out in 2000. The initial survey had focused
primarily on the potential needs of senior public sector personnel. Based on the experience of the first two
years, it appeared that the main audience for the DLC shifted from senior officials to the mid levels of the


                                                          - 6 -

public sector hierarchy and, from policy-making to policy implementation. In addition, there was a need to
broaden the target audience to include the donor sponsored activity. Accordingly, the DLC Management
Team updated the initial Business Plan and discussed the new DLC strategy with its Steering Committee
prior to adoption. Such plans are now updated yearly and contribute to enhancing the DLC's activities.

5.4 Costs and financing:
The project was approved for an amount of SDR 1.6 million (US$ 2.1 million). The Government provided
the required counterpart funds of US$120,000 over the life of the project. As of April 30, 2004, 97.95
percent of credit proceeds were disbursed and the remaining SDR 32,78 was cancelled. Disbursements
were satisfactory during project implementation.

6. Sustainability

6.1 Rationale for sustainability rating:
The sustainability of project activities is likely for the following reasons:

(a) Performance: The DLC has demonstrated that it can play a key role in the country's efforts to enhance
capacity. As described in Section 4 abaove, DLC's performance has surpassed the benchmarks specified in
the PAD both in terms of development objectives and performance indicators.

(b) Technical: The DLC is well equipped to host quality training events. The issues relating to poor
equipment during the starting period have been adequately addressed (see para. 4.2). Before the project
closed, videoconferencing equipment and computers were renewed. The DLC is now operating with
enhanced equipment.

(c) Financial: The DLC is progressively reaching financial viability. Over the period January 1, 2003 to
April 30, 2004 (the closing date of the project), the DLC was recovering a significant part (76%) of its
operating costs. The trend has continued after the project closing date. During the period May 2004 to end
of October 2004, the DLC was recovering 98%, taking into consideration that July to September was
holiday season. Based on this momentum, the financial coverage is expected to reach 100% by December
2004.

(d) Market: Market prospects seem promising in the light of the following facts :

       - The initial development objectives of the GDLN Program focused on building the capacity of
decision-makers from the public and the private sectors in developing countries. However, in the course of
the project implementation, the DLC had to broaden its target audience for several reasons: (i) the local
demand increased significantly in terms of number of participants interested in the GDLN Program, as the
DLC was becoming well-known in Senegal; (ii) the DLC being part of a global network, each GDLN
Center benefits from the experience of the others. In addtion, the local DLC benefited from partnerships
developped by the GDLN Network. For example, partnerships signed in Washington, DC, with the British
Council generated local partnerships with the same agency at the country level; moreover, the Senegal DLC
signed a contract with Japan International Cooperation Agency (JICA) and shared this information with the
other Centers to have them sign similar partnerships in their respective countries; and (iii) the profile of
GDLN activities shifted from purely "training" sessions to "knowledge-exchange" type of sessions
including informal discussions. This possibility broadened the scope of learning at DLC facilities.

     - The facilities rental service represents a significant part of the revenues and this market is growing
rapidly. The DLC is currently planning the extension of the building to offer a bigger cafeteria and



                                                        - 7 -

additional meeting/breakout rooms in order to be competitive on a local market largely controlled by the
hotels.

    - The market of the Bank-financed projects is another target of the DLCs business strategy. It represents
a large potential source of revenues. Iit may be possible to include the use of DLC facilities for training
programs in the design of new operations. For example, such an approach has been included in the
Mauritania MAP project.

      - Other future proposals may include setting up a secondary Network outside the capital city. This
would be appropriate as there is little evidence of DLC penetration beyond Dakar. Such a secondary
network would have potentially a great impact on the quality of skills of the development practioners
working on development projects and programs.

(e) Operational Strategy: Regarding its operating costs, the DLC Management has put in place a plan to
ensure future viability: (i) payment of the anticipated bandwidth costs two years ahead and of the
maintenance costs by six months; (ii) reduction of variable operating costs where possible (utilities,
trainings abroad for staff, etc.); (iii) staffing strategy to reduce the current work overload on staff - this
would include the creation of a commercial unit to exclusively handle marketing activities in order to bring
more business to the Center; and (iv) building up of stronger partnerships with development partners as
possible sources of funding.

(f) Technology: The DLCs in Africa communicate with each other through a satellite link which ensures
good quality videoconferences and high-speed internet for on-line discussions and e-learning. The cost of
this technology has decreased by about 50 percent for the Senegal DLC since the beginning of the project
(from CFA 89.5 million per year in October 2000 to CFA 45.0 million in end October 2004). This
contributed to the capacity of the DLC to recover its operating costs. Moreover, it is expected that in the
very near future the DLCs will have the possibility of shifting to much cheaper technologies. At the time of
project preparation, the choice of the technology depended on existing telecom services in Africa. The
Project Team concluded that there were no other effective alternatives aside from using a VSAT link to
ensure quality services at a reasonable price. However, over the past few years several telecom operators
have invested in modern infrastructure on the African continent (e.g., fiber optic along the African Coast
with contact points in Dakar, Abidjan, Accra, Cotonou, and linked between ECOWAS countries). Using
the upcoming cheaper technologies will reinforce the viability of the GDLN Centers.

6.2 Transition arrangement to regular operations:
DLC staff underlined the need for an additional space for face-to-face meetings and break-out sessions. The
initial design should have included the construction of such a space (see section 3.5). The DLC is now
aiming to complete the construction of this additional room in FY 2005. Financing is sought from
Government or from development partners. In the worst case scenario, the DLC would be willing to finance
this construction out of its own funds.

7. Bank and Borrower Performance

Bank
7.1 Lending:
The Bank's lending performance was unsatisfactory. The preparation team guided the Government in
setting up a new institution and promoting a new development concept. The assumptions made in the PAD
addressed most of the issues that could arise during project implementation. The critical risks were
correctly assessed. However, because of time constraints for completing the construction, the project
design included several weaknesses (see para. 3.5 for details). Moreover, during project implementation,


                                                       - 8 -

IDA procurement procedures appeared not to be adequate for the business-oriented management of the
project, and the DLC faced difficulties when having to respond to the very "urgent" demands of clients. On
several occasions, clients requested office supplies with very short notice, which did not leave enough time
for the DLC to follow the standard Bank procurement procedures.

7.2 Supervision:
The Bank's supervision performance was satisfactory. Regular supervision missions allowed for proper
reports on the DLC's overall progress. The Bank supervision team (Task Team Leader, Procurement
Specialist, and Financial Management Specialist) was available to the DLC Management and kept
informed of the project issues. Particular attention was given to procurement and financial management
aspects. Assistance was also provided through GDLN, WBI and ISG on technical matters and
videoconferencing. Issues which were under the control of the Bank supervision team were dealt with in due
course. These efforts had a positive impact on project performance and helped the DLC to achieve all
targets despite the short timeframe for implementation.

7.3 Overall Bank performance:
      Because the good supervision performance compensated the weakeness during the lending phase, the
overall Bank's performance is rated marginally satisfactory.

Borrower
7.4 Preparation:
Borrower performance during preparation was satisfactory. The Government showed strong commitment
by (i) establishing and staffing the DLC in a relatively short timeframe; (ii) placing it initially under the
administrative supervision of the Prime Ministry (this was an appropriate decision to ensure high visibility
of the project, especially in view of the objective to improve public policy formulation and implementation);
and (iii) organizing an excellent opening ceremony under the chairmanship of the Prime Minister.

7.5 Government implementation performance:
Government implementation performance was satisfactory. It met its financial commitments in terms of
counterpart funds. These were disbursed on time. In general, Government ministries benefited from the
training courses. They represented 63% of the participants for the GDLN activities from 2000 to 2004.
During the conflict with ENAM, the President and the Cabinet protected the DLC by issuing a decree to
ensure the autonomy of the Center. However, the successive changes of "tutelle" did not allow the
Government to provide the needed institutional support to effectively promote the DLC.

7.6 Implementing Agency:
 Implementation by DLC is rated as highly satisfactory. It was successful in introducing the distance
learning concept in the country, and it established a positive reputation for quality training, despite some
logistical constraints in organizing the courses and the chronic power supply problems during the intitial
implementation phase. It had a very dedicated and competent staff, who addressed the technical issues in a
timely manner. Outstanding management by the DLC' s Director and his staff allowed the Center to
surpass all the project targets, and establish a model operation in the Africa region, which has served as a
good practice case for subsequent DLCs.

7.7 Overall Borrower performance:
The Overall Borrower performance is satisfactory.

8. Lessons Learned




                                                       - 9 -

Project Design : A LIL focuses on experimentation, learning and piloting in search of possible
developmental solutions, prior to potential larger-scale operations. It should therefore, incorporate
monitoring and evaluation systems to understand the effectiveness of the approach being tried.With a
well-designed M & E system, one learns what has worked, what has not, and why. Without it, it is not
possible to answer these questions or to assess a LIL's pilot or experimental activities. The M&E for the
Senegal DLC focused on the quantitative targets, but failed to take into account the qualitative assessment
of the impact of GDLN services on beneficiaries. The lesson learned from the Senegal experience has been
applied in the design of subsequent Centers, namely in Burkina Faso and Kenya.

Importance of the DLC Management Capacity : For a distance learning center to be effective, the
management capacity is one of the most critical factors. In the case of the Senegal DLC, the strong
performance of the Director and his staff was a significant factor in the performance of the Center and the
achievement of all targets. These efforts were reinforced through effective follow up by the Bank's
supervision team, which was based in Dakar. This suggeses that it is important in similar operations to
ensure regular follow up and if possible provide supervision support from the field office.

Greater Proactivity in Marketing Activities : The sustainability of the DLC is to a large extent related to
the development of marketing activities. The DLC attracted a large number of clients but still a lot remains
to be done, especially regarding clients among staff from multilateral and bilateral organizations and
various segments of the country's private sector and civil society. As shown in para. 6.(d), the market
prospects seem very promising. It would therefore, be crucial for the DLC to emphasize marketing actions.
The Center should also keep regularly informed of the market trends in order to update its strategy as well
as the training content.

 Participation of the World Bank and Involvement of International Organizations : The Borrower felt
that the Bank could have made more use of the DLC's facilities by promoting the DLC to its own staff, to
the Project Implementation Units, and to the donor community. This would imply that the World Bank
"revisit" its approach to capacity building (both for the PIUs and the Bank's own work) and conducts much
more of the capacity building/communication work through GDLN than is currently the practice.
Furthermore, the local community of donors and development agencies could have been included at the
preparation stage of the project, to make sure they would promote the use of the DLC.

Comparison of the Senegal GDLN Center with other Centers in Africa: The DLC Centers in Benin and
Senegal were both financed through a US$2.1 million LIL and had exactly the same design. Both Centers
closed on April 30, 2004. The comparison of their performance shows major differences: (i) although the
Benin DLC generated almost twice as much in revenue as the Senegal one, the cost recovery ratio of the
Benin DLC at the end of the project was 58% compared to 76% for Senegal. This was mainly due to a lack
of rigorous financial management in the Benin DLC. However, the Senegal DLC staff managed to keep the
operating costs within reasonable limits; (ii) during implementation, the Benin DLC failed to adjust its
strategy to the local demand and thus become more business-oriented, whereas the Senegal DLC created
new services in response to the local demand, and adjusted its marketing strategy according to the eveolving
new target audiences and potential markets.

Annex 3. Economic Costs and Benefits

According to the cost-effectiveness analysis detailed in the annex 4 of the PAD, there are four categories in
the current training system in Senegal: International Training; Regional Training; Local Training by Local
Staff; and Local Training by International Staff. Combining the four categories gives an estimate of the
average unit cost for the current training system in Senegal. Depending on the length of the course (and
assuming that local courses are delivered by only one international trainer), this yields a range of unit cost
of the current training system in Senegal of US$296 for a 10-day course and US$493 for a 5-day course.

Comparatively, the proposed Global Distance Learning Network (GDLN) training system was much more
cost-effective than the existing system which lowest possible cost (US$296 for10 days course) was higher
than the GDLN start-up unit cost (US$247 in the second year). At end of project, the average DLC
training cost per student-day (TC) was estimated at US$110/student-day. It reached US$27/student-day
(based on the figures in Annex 8, TC = Revenues from Trainings and Room Rental in Year 3 (Table V) /
number of student-day in Year 3 (Table IV) = CFA 58.929.000 + 65.819.000 / 8757 = CFA 14.245 = US$
27 per student-day at the rate of US$ 1 = CFA 530).

In terms of benefits, the increasing success of the DLC in Dakar showed a very high satisfaction level
among the trainees. The number of organizations using DLC and the number of trainees surpassed
significantly expectations.

I- INTRODUCTION

En partenariat avec la Banque Mondiale, le Gouvernement du Sénégal s'est doté d'un Centre
d'Enseignement à Distance (CED/Sénégal), inauguré le 21 Juin 2000 par Monsieur le Premier Ministre. Il



                                              - 26 -

est membre du Réseau Mondial de Formation pour le Développement (GDLN: Global Development
Learning Network), mis en place par la Banque Mondiale.
Ce Centre, situé dans le même site que L'Ecole Nationale d'Administration (ENA) et le Centre de
Formation Judiciaire (CFJ), ex-ENAM, est une structure propre jouissant d'une autonomie administrative
et financière.
Avec la clôture du Projet depuis le 30 Avril 2004, nous saisissons l'opportunité de ce rapport d'achèvement
pour faire une analyse évaluative des principaux résultats et problèmes relatifs au Projet et de façon
rétrospective.
Nous ferons au préalable un rappel des objectifs du GDLN en général et du CED/Sénégal en particulier,
suivi d'une évaluation des résultats globaux et des principaux problèmes rencontrés dans l'exécution du
Projet, avant de terminer par une réflexion sur les perspectives d'avenir et les ambitions du Centre.

II- LE RESEAU MONDIAL DE FORMATION POUR LE DEVELOPPEMENT (GDLN)

L'objectif général du GDLN est, comme l'indique sa devise, de mettre le savoir au service du
développement. Il a été mis en place par la Banque Mondiale et devra regrouper en son sein, dans un future
proche, plusieurs autres Centres disséminés à travers le monde dont près d'une dizaine d'entre eux basés en
Afrique.

2-1- La Vision du GDLN

Le GDLN est un dispositif innovant mis en place pour réduire la fracture numérique entre pays du Nord et
pays du Sud.
Reliés par diverses technologies de communication parmi lesquelles la liaison par satellite et celle par lignes
numériques, les sites du GDLN participent à des échanges, des actions de formation, des séminaires
pratiques ou des discussions sur des thèmes liés au développement.
Ces nouvelles technologies permettent à des professionnels du monde entier de mettre en commun leurs
connaissances et leurs expériences, en s'affranchissant des contraintes de temps et d'espace. L'écart
Nord-Sud en matière de partage des connaissances pourra ainsi être sensiblement réduit avec l'utilisation
de la visioconférence, des classes interactives, des satellites de communication et internet.

2-2- La mission du GDLN

Elle peut être décrite en 3 points:
- offrir aux décideurs et acteurs du développement à travers le monde une plate-forme d'échange de
connaissances et    d'expériences;
- intégrer les expertises dans une large variété de disciplines et favoriser leur diffusion à travers le Réseau;
- permettre à des professionnels de participer localement à des programmes de formation et de leur faire
réaliser ainsi des économies substantielles (déplacement, hébergement, prise en charge, temps, etc).

2-3- Les acteurs du GDLN

Les partenaires du GDLN peuvent être vus comme les pionniers d'un nouveau dispositif de partage en
temps réel de connaissances et d'expertises en matière de développement.
Parmi ces partenaires, figurent des gouvernements, divers représentants de la société civile, des ONGs, des
organisations du secteur privé, des institutions académiques et des centres de recherche.

III- LE CENTRE D'ENSEIGNEMENT A DISTANCE DU SENEGAL (CED/SENEGAL)




                                                     - 27 -

Le CED/Sénégal est équipé d'une salle multimédia modulable dotés de 34 ordinateurs permettant un accès
rapide à Internet, d'une salle de visioconférence entièrement équipée, et pouvant être utilisée en mode
broadcast (simple) ou en mode interactif (deux voies) et d'une caféteriat aussi entièrement équipée.
Le CED/Sénégal gère son propre espace, ses infrastructures et ses programmes de formation (séminaires
longs sur plusieurs semaines ou séminaires courts de quelques heures) en collaboration avec ses partenaires
locaux et internationaux. Cette gestion inclut les actions de promotion locale et l'administration ( de
l'inscription, l'évaluation, au suivi individuel des participants).

3-1- Les principaux avantages de l'Enseignement à Distance

Grâce aux Nouvelles Technologies de l'Information et de la Communication, l'enseignement à distance
offre des avantages importants dont principalement:

-        l'accès immédiat à des connaissances de haut niveau;
-        la réduction substantielle des coûts de formation;
-        l'amélioration des compétences individuelles;
-        la formation et l'information continues par des praticiens et des personnalités de notoriété
mondiale.

3-2- Contenu de la formation au CED/Sénégal

La formation au CED/Sénégal comprend des cours, des table-rondes, des conférences et séminaires sous
forme de visioconférence, d'enseignement en ligne ou en face à face, dispensés par des spécialistes et des
institutions de
développement et aussi des institutions académiques de haut niveau, des discussions et des échanges
d'expériences entre experts et hauts responsables des politiques économiques de divers pays.

Elle est également dispensée à travers un accès direct à des bases de connaissances actualisées et élaborées
par des institutions de renommée mondiale dans le domaine du développement économique et social.

3-3- Les objectifs du CED/Sénégal

Le principal objectif du CED/Sénégal est le renforcement de la capacité des responsables, décideurs et
cadres tout court des secteurs public et privé, de la société civile et des ONGs, entres autres, dans la
conception, la planification, la gestion et la mise en oeuvre des politiques de développement économique et
social à travers l'accès à des connaissances de haut niveau qu'offre l'enseignement à distance utilisant les
Nouvelles Technologies de l'Information et de la Communication (NTIC).
Le CED/Sénégal vise également à développer un partenariat d'échanges d'expériences et de partage de
connaissances entre les institutions d'enseignement, la communauté du développement et les décideurs de
politiques économiques au niveau local et dans différents pays à travers le Réseau Mondial.
Son objectif majeur sur le plan financier reste l'atteinte d'une situation de viabilité et durabilité financières
à partir de la date de clôture du crédit IDA, en d'autres termes, à partir du 30 Avril 2004.

3-3-1- Evaluation de l'atteinte des objectifs du CED/Sénégal

Nous pouvons dire à priori, au regard des différents résultats obtenus par le CED/Sénégal, tant du point de
vue de la participation et de l'impact de ses activités que du point de vue financier, que les objectifs fixés
ont été largement atteints.




                                                      - 28 -

         - Sur le plan académique :
Comme l'indiquent les tableaux en annexe, avec un taux de participation aussi bien aux activités GDLN
que non GDLN de 73,14% pour l'année 1 (Octobre 2000-Décembre 2001), 96,53% pour l'année 2 (2002),
et 91,22% pour l'année 3 (Janvier 2003-Avril 2004), correspondant respectivement à un nombre de
personnes/jour de 6582,31, 6950,05 et 8757,03, il n'y a aucun doute à se faire sur le niveau des
performances réalisées sur cette période relativement courte.
Il faut noter que le secteur public a enregistré le taux de participation le plus élevé, suivi du secteur privé,
des ONGs et enfin des organisations internationales. De la même façon, la participation masculine l'a
emporté sur celle féminine et toutes ces statistiques sont disponibles et consultables au niveau du Centre.

-        Sur le plan de l'impact :
L'impact constitue à mon avis un indicateur capital pour un projet comme le CED/Sénégal mais difficile à
formuler du point de vue mathématique ou statistique comme le taux d'utilisation ou de couverture des
charges. Il n'est d'ailleurs pas prévu comme indicateur cité dans le Document d'Evaluation du Projet
(PAD).
A partir de l'impact d'un tel projet, on peut déduire la justification de contracter un tel crédit de la part de
l'Etat. Mais l'impact ne peut s'apprécier que sur la base de l'expérience capitalisée et des leçons apprises
par surtout l'équipe de direction du Projet.
De par notre expérience capitalisée à la tête de l'équipe de direction du CED/Sénégal, nous avons pu avoir
des exemples concrets de l'impact du Centre sur nos populations.
En effet, le seul fait de voir la majorité des agents de l'Etat disposés d'une adresse E-mail à la suite d'une
première participation à l'une des activités du Centre en est un exemple. C'est systématique pour le
CED/Sénégal de procurer une adresse E-mail à tous ses participants qui n'en ont pas au moment de venir
au Centre et c'est souvent le cas particulièrement pour les fonctionnaires. C'est donc un motif de
satisfaction que de voir après, tous ces cadres communiquer avec d'autres collègues à travers le monde
utilisant Internet et échangeant ainsi leurs expériences et connaissances.
Ce même exemple est valable pour tous ces élèves et étudiants, et autres personnes utilisant régulièrement
les facilités mises à leur disposition par le Centre pour faire des recherches ou pour communiquer avec le
reste du monde.
Un autre exemple sur l'impact est le feed-back reçu d'anciens participants à nos programmes, qui, après
l'obtention de leur certificat de participation, ont pu trouver un emploi grâce à leurs capacités renforcés sur
un domaine bien spécifique.

-        Sur le plan financier :
Les résultats sont aussi satisfaisants que ceux sur le plan académique ou de l'impact. En effet, par rapport
aux prévisions dégagées dans le (PAD), des performances ont été réalisées tant pour les recettes que pour la
couverture des charges.
En ce qui concerne les recettes, un taux de 143,4% a été obtenu pour l'année 1 par rapport aux prévisions,
113,7% l'année 2 et 121,33% l'année 3.

Quant à la couverture des charges, pour une prévision de 20% l'année 1, un taux de 36,20% a été obtenu,
et 50% de prévu année 2 pour 60,68% d'obtenu, 75% de prévu année 3 et 76,57% d'obtenu.

Pour ce qui concerne le chiffre d'affaires, pour une prévision de 153.400.000FCFA pour l'année 3, une
réalisation de 179.169.422FCFA a été obtenue soit un taux de réalisation de 116,79%, contre 143,38%
pour l'année 1 et 113,65% pour l'année 2.

Depuis le 30 Avril 2004, le CED/Sénégal parvient à prendre en charge les 100% de ses coûts d'opération.
En d'autres termes, les recettes du CED/Sénégal parviennent à couvrir l'ensemble de ses charges, ce qui

nous pousse à conclure un état de satisfaction globalement positif des résultats du Projet même si cette
appréciation pourrait être quand même relativisées.

3-4-    Crédit IDA No 3398-SE du 05 Juillet 2000 et contrepartie financière de l'Etat

Le CED/Sénégal a été principalement financé sur la base d'un crédit d'un montant de 1.600.000 DTS soit
2,1 million de dollars US, accordé au Gouvernement du Sénégal par la Banque Mondiale. Le crédit est
entré en vigueur le 20 Mars 2001 et clôturé depuis le 30 Avril 2004. La contrepartie financière de l'Etat du
Sénégal a été de 84 million de FCFA, entièrement libérés.
Au plan financier, il faut noter que l'Etat a scrupuleusement respecté ses engagements consignés dans les
dispositions de l'Accord de Crédit signé avec l'IDA et que le Crédit a connu un niveau de décaissement
plus que satisfaisant, de l'ordre de plus de 95%.
Sur tous ces points, le CED/Sénégal se félicite d'avoir obtenu satisfaction dans l'atteinte de ces objectifs.

3-5-    Principaux facteurs ayant affecté l'exécution du Projet

Ceci nous ramène à dire que quelque soit les bonnes performances du CED/Sénégal, les résultats obtenus
pouvaient être meilleurs si certaines précautions avaient été prises au départ.

        -Durée du Projet : Je reste persuadé que la durée relativement courte du projet a été un facteur de
        contre-performance. Je crois qu'un Projet de cette nature, appelé à être financièrement viable
        devrait pouvoir bénéficier d'un accompagnement d'au moins cinq à sept ans pour s'assurer de sa
        solidité du point de vue de son implantation financière et aussi de sa notoriété avant d'être laissé
        avec son destin. Cela reste donc une lacune de l'équipe chargée de l'évaluation ex-ante du Projet en
        1999. Ce défi me paraît donc surréaliste même s'il n'est pas impossible à relever mais le risque
        d'échec reste néanmoins très élevé.

        -Implantation du Centre dans une institution hôte : Cela a été à mon avis un facteur de blocage
        dans le fonctionnement correct du CED/Sénégal et à contribuer négativement dans l'atteinte des
        objectifs fixés par le Centre. En effet, cet élément de conditionnalité stipulé dans l'Accord de
        Crédit ne se justifie pas au vu des différents problèmes que l'institution hôte a posé au Projet,
        particulièrement durant les sept premiers mois de fonctionnement du Centre.

        En effet, l'objectif de l'autorité de l'institution hôte était tout simplement d'absorber le
        CED/Sénégal et d'en faire une entité complètement dépendante d'elle. Si toutes ces manoeuvres
        avaient abouti, le CED/Sénégal ne serait pas aujourd'hui à ce niveau de performance.
        Un autre aspect reste le fait qu'en domiciliant une telle structure dans une institution hôte, il faut
        aussi tenir compte de l'héritage d'une certaine image déjà obtenue par l'institution hôte. Dans ce
        cas précis, il a été par exemple souvent difficile de convaincre le secteur privé à renforcer les
        capacités de ses cadres utilisant un Centre à l'intérieur d'une école d'Administration publique.
        En plus, certaines réalisations de génie civil prévues depuis la création du CED/Sénégal reste à ce
        jour non exécutées, ayant buté tout simplement sur la mauvaise volonté du responsable de
        l'institution hôte qui règne en maître des lieux. Nous sommes persuadés que le projet d'extension
        des locaux du Centre, qui est actuellement une nécessité vu l'accroissement des activités et les
        sollicitations quant à l'utilisation des facilités du Centre, sera confronté aux mêmes difficultés.
        Je reste convaincu que le Centre aurait pu mieux s'épanouir et faire dans n'importe quel autre
        espace qui lui est strictement propre et obtenir de meilleurs résultas en bâtissant sa propre image
        depuis sa naissance.




                                                       - 30 -

-Engagements du Gouvernement et évaluation de comportement: C'est vrai que l'Etat a
respecté intégralement ses engagements contenus dans l'Accord de Crédit mais il pouvait aussi
mieux apporter son soutien au Centre si des mécanismes de prise en charge de ses agents pour
suivre des formations au CED/Sénégal avaient été prévus par l'Accord de Crédit. Cela pouvait se
faire sous forme d'un pourcentage du budget des départements et agences étatiques à affecter
chaque année au Centre ou suivant tout autre mécanisme. A charge au CED/Sénégal ensuite
d'assurer la formation d'un certain nombre d'agents chaque année dans des domaines variés ou
prédéfinis. Cela aurait permis certainement à un nombre beaucoup plus important d'agents de
l'Etat de participer à des programmes de formation en plus de ceux que nous parvenons à faire
participer utilisant nos propres tuyaux. Les départements ministériels sont souvent confrontés à ce
problème de prise en charge des frais de participation de leurs agents désirant suivre une formation
au CED/Sénégal.
En ce qui concerne le rattachement institutionnel du Centre, une certaine stabilité serait souhaitable
car le CED/Sénégal a connu à quatre reprises une nouvelle tutelle durant ces quatre années
d'existence et q'un rattachement à un niveau décisionnel tel que Primature ou Présidence a toujours
été recommandé à l'issue des missions de supervision et de la revue à mi-parcours en 2002.

Nous pouvons donc saluer le bon comportement des autorités gouvernementales qui n'ont en rien
gêné la bonne marche du Projet avec un soutien moral constant au CED/Sénégal.

-Comportement de la Banque Mondiale : Au niveau local, nous espérions des relations plus
accentuées avec la mission résidente (WBO) de la Banque Mondiale. C'est vrai que nous n'avons
eu jamais de problèmes directement causés par le WBO, mais nous espérions une participation
plus active à nos programmes et à l'effort de marketing, de même qu'une représentation au niveau
le plus élevé à nos différentes cérémonies où le gouvernement a été toujours représenté à un
niveau élevé.
La Banque Mondiale aurait pu contribuer plus largement à la promotion des activités du Centre ne
serait-ce qu'au niveau des projets avec financement IDA utilisant le canal de ses chargés de projet,
ce que nous avons toujours sollicité.
En ce qui concerne aussi le partenariat que le CED/Sénégal aurait pu avoir avec les organismes
internationaux et autres bailleurs de fonds, ce qui peut contribuer pour beaucoup à la poursuite de
l'objectif de viabilité financière du Centre, nous avons toujours avancé l'idée auprès de l'équipe
chargée du partenariat au sein de l'Institut de la Banque Mondiale, du GDLN, pour attirer leur
attention sur le fait que la Banque devrait jouer un rôle plus déterminant dans la stratégie de
conviction de nouveaux partenaires pour leur participation au financement des activités des CED
en particulier et du GDLN en général. Nous pensons que cet aspect particulier n'a pas été une
réussite et que le CED/Sénégal a utilisé sa propre stratégie pour nouer des partenariats avec
certains organismes internationaux avec qui il travaille. La Banque Mondiale devrait jouer un plus
grand rôle dans ce domaine.

Durant les phases d'identification et de préparation, il faut saluer les performances de la Banque
Mondiale quant au démarrage rapide et sans heurts du Projet. Les travaux et équipements du
Centre ont été correctement diligentés et installés. Les missions de supervision et d'évaluation ont
été normalement et efficacement menées. Mais pour ce qui concerne les relations entre la Banque et
l'Emprunteur, ces relations ont été plus existantes et soutenues entre la Banque et le CED/Sénégal
en tant qu'entité chargée de l'exécution du Projet.
En définitive, nous pouvons dire que la Banque Mondiale a eu une très bonne performance et un
comportement encourageant de bonne partenaire durant cette phase d'exécution du Projet.

        Quant à l'Unité de Projet, il est constitué de l'équipe dirigeante du CED/Sénégal composée de
        neuf puis onze membres de staff. L `équipe a toujours veiller sur la bonne exécution des activités
        pour l'atteinte des objectifs du Projet. Elle s'est singulièrement attelée au respect des Accords de
        Crédit et de Projet et particulièrement au respect des procédures de gestion administrative et
        financière.
        Il faut néanmoins noter la nécessité de réadaptation de certaines procédures surtout financière ne
        collant pas à la réalité de gestion d'une structure de formation devant obéir principalement à une
        certaine flexibilité surtout dans le domaine des marchés d'acquisition de fournitures de bureau.
        Il faut aussi noter que la non existence d'une personne, à défaut d'une équipe, chargée
        spécifiquement de l'aspect commercial et du marketing des activités du Centre, en plus du
        Directeur, s'est fait ressentir sur les performances. La création d'une section commerciale et
        marketing au sein du Centre s'avère indispensable.

        -Evaluation de la pérennité du Projet :
        Le CED/Sénégal a fait des performances incontestables et intéressantes. Mais l'autre grand défi à
        relever est de maintenir ce cap de performance. Les acquis et résultats devront être maintenus et
        pourront l'être avec l'engagement et la détermination de tous les acteurs, particulièrement, à ce
        stade, l'unité de gestion du Centre et le gouvernement et aussi la Banque Mondiale à moindre
        échelle.

        L'Etat devra, pour cela, plus marquer son engagement avec un renouvellement et un maintien des
        faveurs accordées au CED/Sénégal, consistant en une série d'exonérations diverses et aussi trouver
        les vois et moyens de participer au financement des activités du CED/Sénégal par un mécanisme de
        prise en charge des frais de participation de ses agents plus approprié et plus souple.
        L'appui financier de l'Etat serait aussi indispensable pour les travaux d'extension du CED/Sénégal
        dont les locaux s'avèrent aujourd'hui exigus au vu de son développement, pour le renouvellement
        de ses équipements, de même que pour son projet de mise en place de son réseau secondaire dans
        les principales villes de l'intérieur du pays.

        Pour ce qui concerne la Banque Mondiale, elle devrait systématiquement orientée toutes les actions
        de formation interne à la Banque et les composantes formation de tous les projets sur financement
        IDA vers le CED/Sénégal avec instructions écrites aux chargés de projet et même inscription dans
        les Accords de Crédit comme conditionnalité à chaque fois que c'est possible.
        La Banque devra aussi toujours veiller à la bonne qualité des services sur le plan technique et aussi
        s'évertuer à toujours obtenir des coûts compétitifs en ce qui concerne les frais de location de la
        bande passante et la maintenance des installations VSAT.

        Quant à l'équipe de Projet, elle devra être toujours plus déterminée et engagée pour faire face aux
        nouveaux défis de pérennité et une politique motivationnelle adaptée à la nouvelle situation doit
        prendre place de façon conséquente surtout quant aux rémunérations et autres avantages sociaux
        auxquels elle aspire, de même que le renforcement constant et permanent de ses capacités dans cet
        environnement compétitif et concurrentiel.

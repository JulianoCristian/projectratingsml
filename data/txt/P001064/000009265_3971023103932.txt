Preface
This is the Implementation Completion Report (ICR) for the National Agriculture and
Extension Project, for which IDA Credit 1955-GUI in the amount of SDR14.2 million (US$18.4
million equivalent ) was approved on August 15, 1988, and became effective on May 26, 1989.
The Credit was closed on December 31, 1995, after a one-year extension of the original
closing date. It was fully disbursed and the last disbursement took place on March 25, 1996.
Co-financing for the project was provided by the Fonds Europeen de Developpement
(US$6.1 million); the Fonds d' Aide et de Cooperation (US$1.6 million); and the International
Fund for Agricultural Development (US$1.9 million).
The ICR was prepared by an AFTA3 tearn led by Mr. Mathurin Gbetibouo and
comprising Messrs. Amos Ben Mayor and Joseph Toledano, and Mrs. Guillemette Rohan.
Preparation of this ICR was begun during the Bank's final supervision/completion
mission conducted by Mr. Amos Ben Mayor (mission leader) and Joseph Toledano, September
11-19, 1996. The ICR is based on materials in the project files, field visits, and interviews with
Government officials and staff members of the National Agricultural Extension Service and the
Institute of Agronomic Research of Guinea, who worked closely with the mission. The
mission's aide-memoire is attached as Appendix A. The Borrower contributed to preparation of
the ICR by providing comments on the draft ICR and preparing its own evaluation of the project,
a summary of which is attached as Appendix B.

EVALUATION SUMMARY
1.    The Government of Guinea launched a far-reaching economic and financial reform in late
1985 and made major progress over the next two years toward liberalizing the economy, giving
priority to the agricultural sector. This resulted in a significant supply response, but policy
changes were not sufficient to realize the sector's full potential. Growth was constrained by the
farmers' lack of confidence in the meager support services, lack of suitable technical advice for
farmers, absence of credit and input supply mechanisms, and a dilapidated infrastructure.
2.    Recognizing these problems, the Government decided to give priority to the improvement
and rehabilitation of rural infrastructure and agricultural support services. It sought to produce
and transfer messages by developing local capacity to conduct decentralized adaptive research
and mass extension programs with the full participation of farmers.  This strategy was
implemented with assistance from IDA for the National Seeds Project and the Extension and
Research Pilot Programs. The National Agricultural Research and Extension Project (NAREP)
was approved by IDA to provide further support to the Government's strategy.
Project Objectives
3.    The NAREP's objectives were to: (a) strengthen research stations to identify, select, and
test crop varieties and production technologies; (b) establish an off-station research network to
test the adaptability and acceptability of these varieties and technologies with farmers; (c) train
local staff in commodity and cropping systems research, research and extension methodologies,
and management; (d) strengthen and reorganize extension services including activities aimed at
improving women's agricultural productivity; (e) strengthen the capacity of the State Secretariat
for Decentralization (SED) for developing cooperatives through the creation of a National
Cooperative Support Agency (SNATC); (f) strengthen the capacity of the Ministry of Agriculture
and Animal Resources (MARA) and the State Secretariat for Scientific Research (SERS) to
oversee project impact through the creation of small monitoring and evaluation cells; (g) fund the
preparation of projects and start-up activities needed to achieve sector development; and (h)
strengthen the capacity of the research and extension structures for planning and management.
4.    Project objectives were appropriate given the conditions prevailing in Guinea at that time.
They were in line with the Government's strategy for agriculture and were important to promote
food security, increase agricultural exports, and raise the living standards of the rural population.



iii
They were timely in seeking to improve the organization and management of research and
extension and create institutional capacity in MARA for planning, strategy formulation,
investment selection, and monitoring and evaluation. They were consistent with IDA's strategy
in the rural sector to raise smallholder incomes while creating durable institutions in both the
public and private spheres. But the objectives were also ambitious and demanding, especially in
the early years of project implementation. The risks of ineffective management and coordination
of research and extension programs and the failure to adhere to agreed priorities and balance in
research programs, correctly identified during appraisal, proved real.
Implementation Experience and Results
5.    Most of NAREP's objectives were achieved. The involvement of IDA was considered
key in helping develop the national agricultural services, harmonize various donors' approaches,
and prevent the emergence of programs outside the national strategy The project helped create a
national agricultural research and extension system, an effective National Rural Development
and Agricultural Extension Service (SNPRV), and strengthened local capacity for planning and
management. By project closing, the SNPRV was operating in 20 prefectures (25 percent more
than the target), with over 900 field extension workers, each serving more than 300 farm
families. Farmers now willingly collaborate with extension agents in trying out new crop
varieties and agronomic practices on demonstration plots on their fields. Although qualitative
research improvements lagged mainly because of inadequate management capacity and the low
technical level of researchers, the Institute of Agronomic Research of Guinea (IRAG), which was
created under the project, is now a relatively better equipped problem-solving research
institution, managing 16 research programs in seven research centers. Placing IRAG within the
structure of MARA, rather than under SERS, helped to strengthen the links between research and
extension and made agronomic research more focused on problem solving at the farm level.
6.    Other project objectives, however, were not met. This was the case with the institutional
strengthening in finance and management that was to be achieved through technical assistance
for IRAG. The cooperative development support did not materialize and SNATC was not
established. The gender focus in extension services to increase women farmers' participation
and productivity was initiated only after project closing. Monitoring and evaluation (M&E) cells
to oversee the impact of the project were not created. The internal M&E unit in SNPRV took too
much time to develop appropriate tools and methodology for its operation.
7.    Actual project costs are roughly in line with appraisal estimates, US$30.9 million
estimated versus US$29.0 million actual. Financing was provided by IDA (US$20.0 million
versus US$18.4 million estimated), the European Development Fund (US$6.0 million versus
US$6.1 million estimated), the Fonds d'aide et de cooperation (US$1.6 million actual and
estimated) and Government (US$1.4 million actual versus US$2.9 million estimated). IFAD was
to provide US$1.9 million to the project, but this funding did not materialize. The ex-post
economic rate of return estimate of 29 percent compares favorably with the appraisal estimate of
20 percent.

Summary of Findings, Future Operations, and Key Lessons Learned
8.    Performance of the Bank and Borrower was satisfactory overall. The legacy of three
decades of Government control and a command economy had left a deep sense of mistrust
among farmers vis-a-vis public institutions and service. The SNPRV, therefore, had an uphill
task to build trust among farmers to enlist their cooperation, and this took some time. The lack
of an implementation manual and of staff trained in Bank procurement and disbursement
procedures caused implementation delays. The Government was slow in appointing project
financial staff and in making counterpart funds available. Pressures on IRAG and SNPRV to
share resources with fund-starved units in MARA were intense. The Bank's assistance to the
Borrower during project implementation should have given more emphasis to the research
component, which was somewhat neglected compared with the extension component.
9.    Project achievements are likely to be sustainable in light of the Borrower's strong
commitment to increasing productivity of smallholders, achieving food security, and alleviating
broad-based poverty. Implementation of the follow-up IDA-assisted National Agricultural
Services Project, agreement on the Medium-Term Expenditures Framework, and follow-up of
the recommendations of the Public Expenditure Review should also help assure sustainability by
rationalizing public expenditures and fostering private sector initiatives in the agricultural sector.
The overall outcome of the NAREP is judged satisfactory.' While farmers benefiting from
existing extension and research results have realized substantial increases in foodcrop yields, the
project did not fully achieve the expected qualitative improvement in technology development
and research management nor in setting up the institutional framework for farmers organizations
envisaged through the cooperative component.
10.   The key lessons learned from the implementation of the NAREP are: (i) a project design
which combines research and extension within one agricultural services project, as in this
instance, is more likely to succeed because it builds on the synergy of the two services; (ii) the
institutional relationship between research and extension needs to be clarified early on, so that
one service does not overshadow the other; (iii) the Bank needs to assess more realistically the
Borrower's capacity to provide counterpart funds in a timely manner and involve the Ministry of
Planning and Finance early on in project preparation to ensure timely availability of counterpart
funds; and (iv) the Bank should be more even-handed in its supervision of project components.

PART I. PROJECT IMPLEMENTATION ASSESSMENT
A. INTRODUCTION
1.    The Government of Guinea launched a far reaching economic and financial reform in late
1985 and made major progress over the next two years toward liberalizing the economy, giving
priority to the agricultural sector. This resulted in a significant supply response, but the policy
changes were not sufficient to realize the sector's full potential. Growth was constrained by
farmers' lack of confidence in the meager support services which existed, lack of suitable
technical advice for farmers, absence of credit and input supply mechanisms, and a dilapidated
infrastructure. Even though the country had some capable staff and certain research stations with
infrastructure and a stock of suitable genetic materials, the lack of equipment and operating funds
and a national strategy for research and extension meant that agricultural services were not
operational and were donor-driven. In addition, the Government's former direct involvement
had created serious mistrust among farmers' organizations.
2.    Recognizing these problems, the Government decided to give priority to the improvement
and rehabilitation of rural infrastructure and agricultural support services. Its strategy for
agricultural support services was to produce and transfer technical messages to farmers by
developing local capacity to conduct decentralized adaptive research and mass extension
programs with the full participation of the stakeholders. It was aware of the need to develop
farmers' confidence in these services and to encourage the emergence of farmers' organizations
to facilitate input supply, credit, and marketing.
3.    This strategy was implemented with assistance IDA for the National Seeds Project and
the Extension and Research Pilot Programs. The National Agricultural Research and Extension
Project (NAREP) was approved by IDA to provide further support to the Government's strategy.
The involvement of IDA in this project was considered key in helping develop the national
agricultural services, harmonize various donors' approaches, and prevent the emergence of
programs outside the national strategy. The project was to be the first five-year phase of a long-
term plan for developing and strengthening research, extension, and farmers' organizations in the
country. A Project Preparation Facility was awarded for a Pilot Extension Program.



2
B. STATEMENT/EVALUATION OF OBJECTIVES
4.    The major objectives of the NAREP were to: (a) strengthen research stations to identify,
select, and test crop varieties and production technologies; (b) establish an off-station research
network to test the adaptability and acceptability of these varieties and technologies with
farmers; (c) train local staff in commodity and cropping systems research, research and extension
methodologies, and management; (d) strengthen and reorganize extension services including
activities aimed at improving women's agricultural productivity; (e) strengthen the capacity of
the State Secretariat for Decentralization (SED) for developing cooperatives through the creation
of a National Cooperative Support Agency (SNATC); (f) strengthen the capacity of the Ministry
of Agriculture and Animal Resources (MARA) and the State Secretariat for Scientific Research
(SERS) to oversee project impact through the creation of small monitoring and evaluation cells;
(g) fund the preparation of projects and start-up activities needed to achieve sector development;
and (h) strengthen the capacity of the research and extension structures for planning and
management.
5.    Project objectives were appropriate given the conditions prevailing in Guinea: low
technology level, limited crop diversification, slow emergence of the private sector in input
distribution, and the lack of formal credit and organized farmers' groups. They were in line with
the Government's strategy and were important to promote food security, increase agricultural
exports, and raise the living standards of the rural population. They were timely in seeking to
improve the organization and management of research and extension and create institutional
capacity in MARA for planning, strategy formulation, investment selection, and monitoring and
evaluation. They were consistent with IDA's strategy in the rural sector to raise smallholder
incomes while creating durable institutions in both the public and private spheres. They
complemented the objectives of other IDA assisted operations in the sector: the Agricultural
Services Project, Livestock Sector Rehabilitation Project, and the National Seeds Project.
6.    But the objectives were also ambitious and demanding, especially in the early years of
project implementation. The risks of ineffective management and coordination of research and
extension programs and the failure to adhere to agreed priorities and balance in research
programs, correctly identified in the Staff Appraisal Report (SAR), proved to be real. Donor
coordination posed a problem at times because of different rules and procedures applicable to the
release of funds, especially as counterpart funding by the Government was frequently delayed.
Project design reflected over-optimism about donor coordination and co-financing for the
research component. It also underestimated the effects of a legacy of three decades of a
command economy.
C. ACHIEVEMENT OF OBJECTIVES
7.    The project's overall institutional development objectives were substantially achieved.
The NAREP helped create a national agricultural extension system, now effectively run by the
National Rural Development and Agricultural Extension Service (SNPRV). It strengthened local
capacity for planning and management of extension activities; SNPRV is now the sole medium
of contact with the farming community. Extension activities under other donor-financed projects



3
are being executed by SNPRV. By project closing, the SNPRV was operating in 20 prefectures
(25 percent more than planned), with more than 900 field extension workers, each serving over
300 farm families. Farmers willingly collaborate with extension agents in trying out new crop
varieties and new agronomic practices on demonstration plots on their fields. SNPRV now
covers all of the country's 33 prefectures. The results of the research component were less
successful. Although qualitative research improvements lagged mainly because of inadequate
management capacity and the low technical level of researchers, the Institute of Agronomic
Research of Guinea (IRAG), which was created under the project, is now a relatively well
equipped problem-solving research institution, managing 16 research programs in seven research
centers. The institute was placed within the structure of MARA rather than under SERS. This
helped to strengthen the link between research and extension and made agronomic research more
focused on problem solving at the farm level. The project's preparation fund successfully
completed preparation of three projects:  the Forestry/Environment Project, the Rural
Infrastructure Project, and the Export Crops Rehabilitation Project.
8.    Other project objectives, however, were not met. This was the case with the institutional
strengthening that was to be achieved through technical assistance for two man-years of a
financial controller and three man-years of a research manager at IRAG. The cooperative
development support did not materialize because the Government failed to revise the
cooperatives law in a manner satisfactory to IDA. The draft texts of the law were deemed
inappropriate by IDA, as none precluded Government control over cooperatives, which was
contrary to the Development Credit Agreement. Consequently, SNATC was never established.
The gender focus in extension services to increase women farmers' participation and productivity
was initiated only after project closing. A unit for gender issues, Appui aux Femmes Rurales was
created in SNPRV, but it is not yet operational. Monitoring and evaluation (M&E) cells to
oversee the impact of the project were not created at the ministerial levels in MARA and SERS .
The internal M&E unit in SNPRV took too much time to develop appropriate tools and
methodology for its operation during the project life.
D. MAJOR FACTORS AFFECTING THE PROJECT
9.    Factors not subject to Government control. The legacy of three decades of Government
control and a command economy had left a deep sense of mistrust among farmers vis-A-vis
public institutions and services. The SNPRV, therefore, had an uphill task to build trust about its
mission among farmers and to enlist their cooperation, and this took some time. The lack of a
project implementation manual at the beginning of the project led to delays in implementation
and disbursements.
10.   Factors subiect to Government control. Inadequate training of local personnel in the
Bank's procurement disbursement procedures caused delays in implementation. The evaluation
of MARA personnel, as a condition for the Structural Adjustment Program, followed by many
personnel changes, had the effect of diverting the attention of employees for 34 months, or a
whole agricultural season in 1988-89. Furthermore, the hiatus between the termination of the
Pilot Extension Program and project effectiveness, which was delayed until the end of May 1989,
resulted in the loss of a second agricultural season. It was only with strong training and



4
supervision support of the Bank and a newly recruited consulting firm that NAREP was put back
on track. A rigid framework of unified financial management affected implementation of the
project's research and extension components adversely. It created tensions and led to delays by
tying the progress of one service's activities to that of the other. The Government was slow in
taking actions to appoint the project's financial staff and in depositing counterpart funds into the
project account. It failed to exercise leadership and was torn between major donors' approaches
to research and extension. Frequent changes in field personnel interfered with the smooth
implementation of project activities. The decentralization within the extension service was
hindered until 1994 by the excessive control exercised by the Project Manager, little contact with
staff in the field, and slow-decision making. The appointment of a new manager, the creation of
the SNPRV in MARA, the establishment of a decentralized management system, and more
intensive Bank supervision remedied this situation. Pressures on IRAG and SNPRV to share
resources with the recurrent-funds starved units in MARA was intense and affected several
project-specific activities negatively, such as service delivery and training. Some extension
workers even had to advance their own funds in order to continue extension work with farmers,
while hoping that Government funds would be forthcoming soon.
E. PROJECT SUSTAINABILITY
11.   Project achievements are likely to be sustainable. First, the Borrower is committed to
increasing the productivity of smallholders as the key to its strategy for achieving food security
and broad-based poverty alleviation. The past and current sector strategy documents (Letter of
Agricultural Development Policy in 1991 and 1996) clearly reflect this commitment. Second,
from the technical point of view, the Training and Visit method of agricultural extension is now
widely tested and, by and large, mastered by most agricultural services personnel, and is also
accepted by smallholder farmers in Guinea, as it is in many other African countries. Third, with
respect to financial sustainability, the fiscal demands of the Guinean agricultural extension
service compare favorably with international norms. This strengthens the prospects of the public
sector being able to provide continued financial support for agricultural services. During the
period 1989-94, public expenditures for agricultural extension as a share of agricultural Gross
Domestic Product amounted to 0.7 percent, slightly higher than the 0.4 percent to 0.5 percent in
most developing countries2, but much lower than the 4.8 percent in Kenya.  The prospects for
sustainability are further enhanced with implementation of the ongoing IDA-assisted National
Agricultural Services Project (NASP) and the Medium-Term Expenditures Framework, and the
follow-up to the recommendations of the Public Expenditure Review, which assure a thorough
review of sectoral public expenditures aimed at eliminating waste and inefficiency from existing
programs, seeking cost recovery, and the transfer of responsibilities from the public to the private
sector and non governmental organizations.

F. BANK PERFORMANCE
12.   Bank performance was satisfactory overall. The Bank's in-depth dialogue with the
Government in identifying and analyzing the key problems in research and extension led to a
clear definition of the issues that needed to be addressed within the project: technical, financial,
economic, and institutional, and sociological. Supervision was satisfactory in general, although
it suffered from a lack of continuity in the composition of specialists in the missions. Less
attention appears to have been devoted by Bank staff to the research and cooperative
development components of the project than to the extension. Co-financiers participated in
several supervision missions. The Bank, along with the co-financiers, helped to resolve quickly
the crises that resulted from frequent delays in counterpart financing.
G. BORROWER PERFORMANCE
13.   The Borrower's performance was satisfactory for the most part. While MARA followed
the recommendations made by Bank supervision missions, the Government's complex
administrative procedures led to some serious delays in project implementation. MARA
frequently changed or redeployed project staff, both at the central and field levels. This was
done more for political than professional reasons and often not in a transparent manner. The
result was loss of trained and capable personnel, which slowed down the project's impact on
long-term capacity building in public services and their clients. All audits were unqualified,
although most of them were delivered late. The 1991 audit was 16 months overdue, partly
because all the supporting documents were not kept properly or were lost. The Borrower made
good use of technical assistance and implemented a majority of the supervision missions'
recommendations.
H. ASSESSMENT OF OUTCOME
14.   The overall outcome of the project is judged as satisfactory.4 While farmers benefiting
from existing extension and research results have realized substantial increases in foodcrop
yields, the project did not fully achieve the expected qualitative improvement in technology
development and research management nor in setting up the institutional framework for farmers
organizations envisaged through the cooperative component.
15.   Cultural practices have improved with the introduction of the training and visit system of
agricultural extension. Based on the feedback from farmers who had direct exposure to the
system, they achieved much higher crop yields than farmers who had no direct contact with the
system. These results were confirmed by a formal impact study based on field surveys and
actual expenditures on extension. The estimated marginal rate of return for investing additional
resources in extension services in Guinea was about 70 percent. Yield increases realized under
the project were as follows: rice 44 percent, cassava 50 percent, groundnuts 33 percent, and
maize 11 percent, equal to an ex-post economic rate of return (ERR) of 29 percent, which

extension were strengthened and the technical messages better adapted to farmers' needs. An
exercise to prioritize research was recently completed and is expected to lead to a research

16.   Actual project costs are roughly in line with appraisal estimates, US$30.9 million

I. FUTURE OPERATIONS
17.   The project led to the creation of a national service which now executes the agricultural
extension components in all donor-funded projects throughout the country. The NASP (Credit
2839-GUI) is expanding nationwide the geographical coverage of extension services that was
started under the NAREP. The NASP constitutes the first step in a series of time slices to be
financed under a long-term program. Given that building agricultural research and extension
capacity takes a long time, additional Bank support would be provided if this program continues
to remain a priority in the Country Assistance Strategy. Under the NASP, it was agreed that the
Government and donors would work as partners to strengthen and provide agricultural services.
The Government would be responsible for identifying and preparing its priority programs and
would work with donors to improve the efficiency and sustainability of these programs. The
operation gives more attention to research, focusing on research management and organization
issues that were insufficiently addressed during the first project. The project will seek to: (i)
improve the legal context in which the organization operates, focusing on conditions of service
for researchers, as well as obtaining some degree of financial and administrative autonomy for
the Institute; (ii) research management by introducing formal programming, monitoring and
evaluation procedures and support the regionalization of research programs so that they can be
more responsive to the users' conditions of production; (iii) financial management, setting in
place a cost-accounting system; (iv) human resources management, setting in place a system for
performance review.
18.   The Ministry has been actively working on support to farmer organizations with French
bilateral aid.
KEY LESSONS LEARNED
19.   The following key lessons can be drawn from the implementation of the NAREP:
(i) A project design which combines research and extension within one agricultural
services project as in NAREP is more likely to succeed because it builds on the synergy of the
two services. Although the NAREP did not achieve all the institutional strengthening on the



7
research side and in building support for the farmers' organizations, it succeeded in emphasizing
and reinforcing the essential complementarity between research and extension and built a base on
which the two services could be expanded nationwide under a follow-up project.
(ii) The institutional relationship between research and extension, however, needs to be
clarified early on, so that one service does not overshadow the other. Under the NAREP, the
research component was neglected in the early stages.
(iii) The Bank needs to assess more realistically the Borrower's capacity to provide
counterpart funds in a timely manner. Implementation of several NAREP components was
delayed severely in the first two years because of frequent shortages of counterpart funds. For
example, this curtailed the mobility of extension workers to reach farmers for two cropping
seasons. To gain a better assessment of the Borrower's capacity to provide counterpart funds, in
the future the Bank should engage representatives of the Ministries of Finance and Planning early
on in the preparation and appraisal of the project. These ministries were involved in the
appraisal of NASP and thus far the Government has fulfilled counterpart funding requirements in
a timely manner.
(iv) The Bank should be more even-handed in its supervision of project components,
research and extension in this instance. It was the Borrower's perception that the Bank favored
the extension component. In retrospect, the research component required more assistance during
implementation as it had to be built almost from the ground up, as opposed to the extension
component which has been well-tested over time and has a clear methodology and strategy
(Training and Visit) attached to it. The Bank showed flexibility in making up the deficit in
Government counterpart funding for the extension component by revising the financing schedule
and amending the Credit Agreement. It did not do the same for the research component, with the
result that the training of subject matter specialists by researchers was disrupted.

2.    L'objectif de la mission etait d'entamer l'evaluation retrospective du Projet National de
Recherche et Vulgarisation Agricole (PNRVA) pour lequel IDA a accorde un credit d'un
montant 14.2 million de DTS (soit environl 8.4 million de $EU) au Gouvemement Guineen.
Preparation du Rapport d'Achevement du Projet (RAP)
3.    La mission IDA a aide le MAEF dans la preparation a l'elaboration de la contribution du
Gouvemement au RAP en expliquant aux membres du groupe de travail la structure et le contenu
du rapport et en les guidant pour la collecte des documents et informations necessaires a la
confection du rapport. Un expos6 des directives de r6daction ainsi qu'un modele de RAP avait
dejA e remis a deux cadres du MAEF a titre d'exemple lors de I'elaboration du RAP du Projet
Semencier National.
Evaluation a posteriori des Resultats
4.    Recherche Agronomique: Avant le PNRVA, la recherche agronomique en Guinee etait le
fait d'embryons divers et disparates de recherches d'accompagnement entrepris dans quelques
projets de developpement rural. L'IRAG fut le premier etablissement public a caractere
administratif (EPA) en Guinee. Grace au PNRVA, l'on a pu mettre en place sept centres de
recherche et une direction nationale operationnelle avec le minimum d'equipements necessaires.
Tout le personnel de recherche (environ une centaine) a benefici6 d'une formation de base. En
effet malgre le niveau professionnel relativement bas des chercheurs, grace au programme de
formation, de formation-action, il a ete possible d'obtenir un resultat minimum de recherche dont
les activites se situe a trois niveau: dans les stations, dans un environnement semi-contr6le en
points d'essai (PE), et en milieu paysan, dans les unites experimentales paysannes (UEP), avec la
mise en place d'equipes pluridisciplinaires r6gionales de recherche et de vulgarisation. Ces

paysans des besoins de recherche et de la vulgarisation et A l'amelioration de la qualite des tests
en milieu paysan, ainsi qu'A la formation pratique des chercheurs et des vulgarisateurs.
5.    Des varietes hautement productives de riz, mais, et manioc ont ete selectionnees ou
creees, lesquelles furent largement vulgarisees. Des progres tangibles ont et accomplis dans le
domaine de l'amelioration varietale des fruitiers, des cultures maraicheres, du cafe, des
tubercules et legumineuses alimentaires.
6.    Parmi les domaines dans lesquels les objectifs de la composante recherche n'ont pu etre
atteints, on peut signaler:
a) Le non fonctionnement du Conseil Scientifique (CS) ainsi que le Conseil National
d'Orientation (CNO) qui bien que crees n'ont pas ete operationnels;
b) La non regularite dans la tenue des Sessions periodiques du Conseil d'Administration;
c) L'absence d'un veritable plan de formation ayant entraine un developpement insuffisant des
competences humaines.
7.    Vulgarisation Agricole: Le PNRVA a permis de mettre en place un systeme national de
vulgarisation agricole fonctionnel avec une bonne capacite de gestion du systeme. Ceci a ete
possible grAce a la decentralisation de la gestion avec la creation de huit directions regionales et
la mise en place du dispositifjusqu'A l'echelon " villages". L'execution du projet a aussi permis

recherche, les paysans, les ONG et autres partenaires de developpement du secteur rural.
L'extension de la couverture geographique du dispositif aux trente-trois prefectures a facilite la
diffusion et I'adoption A grande echelle de technologies appropriees (semences de varietes

6levage; tres peu de resultats ont ete developpes et diffuses aupres des eleveurs.

formation de grande envergure de facon a rendre la Guinee mieux a meme de concevoir, gerer et

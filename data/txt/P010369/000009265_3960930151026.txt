MEM4ORANDUM AND RECOMMENDATION OF THE PRESIDENT
OF THE INTERNATIONAL DEVELOPMENT ASSOCIATION
TO THE EXECUTIVE DIRECTORS
ON A PROPOSED CREDIT
TO INDIA FOR A
MAHARASHTRA RURAL WATER SUPPLY AND ENVIRONMENTAL SANITATION PROJECT
1.          The following memorandum and recommendation on a proposed
development credit to India for SDR76.4 million (US$109.9 million equivalent)
is submitted for approval. The proposed credit would be on standard IDA terms
with 35 years maturity and would help finance a rural water supply and
environmental sanitation project in the state of Maharashtrs.
2.          Background.  Over the past ten years, India has made a major
investment in expanding access to rural water supply. As a result, it is
estimated that about 95Z of all villages in India now have access to at least
one safe water source. The supply of water, however, has not been adequate to
meet the demand within villages. A large percentage of the rural population
are still using unsafe water to a varying extent. Related to this, water-
?elated diseases are a major health problem in India, affecting over 802 of
infants and children annually. Small programs have been initiated to promote
latrine usage among the lower income groups in rural areas but these programs
have been unsuccessful due primarily to low demand. lever than 32 of the
rural population use latrines and public facilities for drainage and solid
waste removal are poor. Inadequate water supply coverage, problems with
maintenance and underutilization of existing water supply sources due te
social factors, combined with poor environmental sanitation, have underined
thc lealth benefits of the government's programs. Under the draft Eighth Five
Year-Plan (1990-1995), the central government is emphasizing the consolidation
of rural water supply coverage to improve service levels, increase community
participation in operation and maintenance (O&M) and improve water quality
surveillance. The draft plan also would adopt a new strategy linking health
oommunications, sanitation and water supply. The proposed project would
support the new approaches being adopted by the central government under the
draft five year plan.
3.          Previous Bank Experience.  The Bank has not had any previous
experience in India with implementing a project V, Ach combined rural water
supply, health communications and environmental sanitation in one investment
package; nor has it financedsany free standing rural water supply projects in
India. The Bank has financed seven projects in India with rural water or
rural low cost sanitation components. Three of the projects in the states of
Maharashtra, Uttar Pradesh and Rajasthan have been completed and Project
Performance Audit Reports have been prepared (PPAR Report No. 7202, March
1988, PPAR Report No. 6242, June 1986, and PPAR Report No. 8090, September
1989, respectively). In all three projects, it has been found that the
institutional and financial objectives had been overly ambitious and, thus,
had not been-achieved.   The main lessons learned from these projects are:
(a) adequate project preparation before appraisal, including detailed
engineering designs, is essential; (b) financial and institutional
strengthening targets need to be more realistict (c) the number of contracts



-2-
should be reduced to simplify the procurement processg (d) to achieve
improvements in construction qualitye contractors should be prequalified and
construction supervision should be monitored; (e) adequate funds for OM must
be provided; (f) N&Os can play an Important role in promoting low cost
sanitation and hygiene education; and (g) adequate counterpart funding is
Imperative. These lessons have been incorporated Into the proposed project.
4.          Rationale for IDA Involvement.  IDA support for the proposed
project would assist Maharashtra in giving higher priority to environmental
sanitation and promote increased investment in rural water supply,
environmental sanitation facilities and related health communications
programs. IDA involvement is likely to strengthen the state government's
-emphasis on cost recovery for O0M and community participation. It would
encourage more NGO Involvement in the sector through their participation in
project planning and lmplementation. Improved engineering design standards
would result in more equitable access to rural water supply.
5.          The project wculd improve the health and productivity of the rural
poor in Maharashtra. Women, in particular, would benefit from the project
through improved access to domestic water supply sources. This, in turn,
would give them more time to participate in economically productive
activities. Although other donors are lnvolved in the sector, and have
undertaken pioneering efforts in promoting the linkages between rurel water;
supply and environmental sanltation, the scale of their financial investment
has been small. IDA involvement wouXd provide additional support to
strengthen appropriate policies, stiatigies, design standards and institutions
at the state and district levelsy,-
6.          Proiect Objectivesi  The main objoctives of the project are to
raise the standard of living n rural areas through  improved-health and
-productivity, by expanding access to potable rural water supply systems and
environmental sanitatiou facilities. These improvments  ould be achieved
through the following measurest (a) more equitable access to rural water
supply sources; (b) improved reliability of water supply and drainage ichemes;
(c) increased public and private investment in environmental sanitation; and
(d) greater communlty awarenes of the causes of water-related health problems
and the need for a cleaner environment.
7.          Proiect Description.  The proposed project would be implemented                4
over a period of six years in about ten districts in the state of Maharashtr.a.
There are four main components in the proposed project; rural water supply.
environmental sanitation, health communications and institutional
strengthening.  Inc-tasing the supply of potable water in rural areas is the
largest component in terms of investment coats, and wouli.Sbe about 702 of the
total project costs. The component is expected to include a total of about 75
regional piped water supply schemes and about 170 small individual schemes.
The villages to be served under this component are selected on the basis of
those with the most serious water supply problems. The schemes are selected
on the basis of design criteria agreed with the Association. 'Because of the
low levels of existing environmental sanitation facilities,, the low demand



-3w
relative to water supply and the low unit cost, the environmental sanitation
component would be small in terms of investment costs. Combined with the
health communications component, it would have the objective of developing new
strategies to increase demand and usage, and to implement those strategies
through innovative programs on a district wide basis in a flexible manner to
allow the implementing agencies to adjust the program on the basis of lessons
learned during implementation. This component would include sall scale
surface drains for sullage, soak pits, individual latrines and street bins.
The health communications component would be the smallest in terms of
investment costs, but would constitute one of the most important activities
required to meet the project objectives. This component would include
development of messages to create greater community awareness and demand for
improved hygiene, latrine usage, delivery of potable water, and drainage. The
role of the field staff of the Department of Public Health in ensuring a
cleaner environment would be enchanced. This component would also strengthen
the water quality monitoring system in rural areas. The institutional
strengthening component would involve support to project management and
community participation, and training to support the implentation of the
other three components. The water supply schemes for the first two years of
construction are in an advanced state of preparation. Detailed designs have
been completed for 18 regional and 45 small piped water supply schemes. Draft.
bid documents and bid packaging have been approved and agreed. Sufficient
progress has been made toward land acquisition for almost all land required
for the-first two years of construction for-project implementation to begin in
a timely manner.

a condition of effectiveness; (b) GOM would appoint consultantt for

supply schemes to be financed under the project would be designed according to
criteria satisfactory to the Association; (d) Maharashtra woul4 prepare and

action program for the following year for investments in rural water supply,
environmental sanitation, health iducation, training and technical assistance,
and the status of land acquisition; (e) Maharashtra would prepare and furnish

10.         Women's Participation.  As women have the primary responsibility
for collecting and storing water for household use in India, they will be the
main beneficiaries under the project. As a result of the project, women will
serve increasingly as members on the village water and sanitation committees.
In addition, the GOM has recently introduced a requirement that women hold 30
percent of all seats on the district councils. This will further increase the
likelihood that women will benefit from Improvements effected under the
project. Women have the main responsibility for family hygiene and
sanitation. Accordingly, their input will have a profound impact on the
demand for latrines and their maintenance. Because of their key role in the
sector, the health communications strategy will focus on women as a main
target.
11.         Benefits.  The project is expected to benefit about 1,100 villages
or about 1.1 million persons who would be-covered by all three components.
Through the implementation of the environmental sanitation and health
education components, which will cover villages with piped water s4pply
systems other than those included in the rural water supply component, the
project could benefit an additional 1,000 villages with an estimated-
population of about 3.5 million persons. About 402 of the population served
would be below the poverty line. The main benefit would be the reduction In
the incidence of water-related diseases which would lead to reduced morbidity
and mortality,  azd more water available for day-to-day domestic use.  The
project would also resultein time saved by women in collecting water. The
project would improve the lives of the rural poor.and give communities more
responsibility for services provided.
12.         Risks.  One of the main risks to this project is that the
environmental sanitation and health communications components may be given low
priority in implementation and, thus, will not have their full impact. This
risk will be minimized by the agreement reached with the Gc-arnment of
Maharashtra over the final strategies to be implemented, the involvement of
NGOs during implementation, and the monitoring role of the PPNU.  Another risk
is the lack of experience with NGOs of those implementing the project. This
risk has been reduced by the estnblishment of a Government-NGO Task Force.
The sustainability of investments in rural water supply would be at risk if
there were inadequate institutional and funding arrangements for O&M. This
risk would be minimized through measures tot (a) upgrade local skill levels
through training; (b) strengthen administrative, arrangements by which
communities assume responsibility for O&M; (d) maximize revenues through-
design mprovements which provide for house connections and reduce oW -costs;
and (e) clearly identify subsidies required in the poorest areas.
